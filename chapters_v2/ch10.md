# Chapter 10: Which Way Does Water Flow?

Water obeys gravity. Given a landscape of hills, valleys, and plains, every raindrop that does not evaporate will find its way downhill, joining other droplets until a rivulet becomes a stream and a stream becomes a river. The question "which way does water flow?" seems almost trivially simple when you stand on a hillside and watch a puddle drain. But answering it for every point across an entire landscape --- millions or billions of grid cells in a digital elevation model --- is the single most consequential computation in DEM-based hydrology. Get the flow directions right, and everything else follows: drainage areas, stream networks, watershed boundaries, flood maps, contaminant pathways, erosion patterns. Get them wrong, and every downstream analysis inherits the error.

In Chapter 9 we prepared the digital landscape by filling sinks and enforcing drainage. The result was a hydrologically conditioned DEM --- a surface with no spurious depressions, where every cell has a downslope path to the domain edge. Now we put that surface to work. This chapter introduces the two principal algorithms for computing flow direction (D8 and D-infinity), the concept of flow accumulation that transforms a direction field into a drainage area map, and a family of generalized accumulation functions that extend the basic idea to problems ranging from contaminant transport to sediment delivery. We close with the topographic wetness index and a brief preview of the Height Above Nearest Drainage (HAND) concept that will become central to flood mapping in Chapter 14.

## 10.1 The D8 Flow Direction Model

### 10.1.1 The Core Idea

The **D8 algorithm** --- short for "eight-direction pour point" --- is the oldest and still the most widely used method for computing flow direction on a grid DEM. Published by O'Callaghan and Mark in 1984, it embodies a beautifully simple principle: for every cell, look at the eight neighbors (four cardinal, four diagonal), compute the slope to each, and assign flow to the single neighbor in the steepest downslope direction.

Why eight directions? A regular square grid cell touches four neighbors along its edges (north, south, east, west) and four more at its corners (northeast, northwest, southeast, southwest). These eight neighbors are the only cells that share either an edge or a vertex with the center cell, so they represent all possible immediate destinations for water leaving the cell. The D8 algorithm evaluates every one of them and picks the winner --- the direction of steepest descent.

The slope from the center cell to each neighbor is computed as the elevation drop divided by the distance between cell centers:

$$S_i = \frac{z_0 - z_i}{d_i}$$

where $z_0$ is the elevation of the center cell, $z_i$ is the elevation of neighbor $i$, and $d_i$ is the distance between cell centers. For the four cardinal neighbors (north, south, east, west), this distance equals the cell size $\Delta$. For the four diagonal neighbors, the distance is $\Delta\sqrt{2}$, because the path from center to center crosses the diagonal of a square.

This distinction matters more than it might seem. Consider a center cell at elevation 100 m with two neighbors: the east neighbor at 98 m and the northeast neighbor at 97 m. A naive approach might send water to the northeast neighbor because it is the lowest. But the correct computation reveals:

$$S_{\text{east}} = \frac{100 - 98}{30} = 0.0667$$

$$S_{\text{northeast}} = \frac{100 - 97}{30\sqrt{2}} = \frac{3}{42.43} = 0.0707$$

In this case, the northeast neighbor does have the steeper slope as well as the lower elevation, so it receives the flow. But change the northeast elevation to 97.5 m and the result flips:

$$S_{\text{northeast}} = \frac{100 - 97.5}{42.43} = 0.0589$$

Now the east neighbor has the steeper slope (0.0667 > 0.0589) even though the northeast neighbor is lower in absolute elevation. The steepest descent is not necessarily toward the lowest neighbor --- the $\sqrt{2}$ penalty on diagonal distance can reverse the ranking. This is a subtlety that catches many students (and some practitioners) off guard, but it reflects the physics correctly: a ball rolling across a surface accelerates in the direction of maximum gradient, not necessarily toward the lowest adjacent point.

### 10.1.2 Encoding Conventions

Once the steepest downslope direction is identified, the algorithm records it as a numeric code. Two encoding conventions dominate the field, and confusing them is a perennial source of error.

**Table 10.1**: ArcGIS (Esri) flow direction encoding --- powers of 2, clockwise from east

| Direction | Compass | Code | Binary |
|-----------|---------|------|--------|
| East | E | 1 | 00000001 |
| Southeast | SE | 2 | 00000010 |
| South | S | 4 | 00000100 |
| Southwest | SW | 8 | 00001000 |
| West | W | 16 | 00010000 |
| Northwest | NW | 32 | 00100000 |
| North | N | 64 | 01000000 |
| Northeast | NE | 128 | 10000000 |

The Esri convention uses powers of two, starting from east and proceeding clockwise. The power-of-two scheme is not arbitrary: each direction occupies a single bit in an 8-bit byte. In principle, this means that multiple simultaneous flow directions could be encoded by setting multiple bits (for example, a value of 3 would encode flow to both east and southeast). In practice, the standard D8 algorithm sets only one bit per cell, but the encoding leaves room for multi-direction extensions.

**Table 10.2**: TauDEM (Band) flow direction encoding --- sequential integers, counterclockwise from east

| Direction | Compass | Code |
|-----------|---------|------|
| East | E | 1 |
| Northeast | NE | 2 |
| North | N | 3 |
| Northwest | NW | 4 |
| West | W | 5 |
| Southwest | SW | 6 |
| South | S | 7 |
| Southeast | SE | 8 |

The TauDEM convention, developed by David Tarboton and sometimes called the "Band" convention, uses sequential integers 1 through 8, starting from east and proceeding counterclockwise. This scheme follows the mathematical convention of measuring angles counterclockwise from the positive x-axis: east is 0 degrees (code 1), northeast is 45 degrees (code 2), north is 90 degrees (code 3), and so on.

The practical consequence is straightforward but critical: a cell coded as "2" means southeast in ArcGIS but northeast in TauDEM. When migrating results between software packages, or when reading flow direction rasters produced by someone else, confirming the encoding convention is essential. A flow direction raster without metadata about its convention is a recipe for confusion.

Other software packages have their own conventions. GRASS GIS uses powers of two but with a different starting direction. WhiteboxTools follows the Esri convention by default but offers options. SAGA GIS uses yet another arrangement. The lesson is universal: always check the convention before interpreting a flow direction raster.

### 10.1.3 The Grid Network

When D8 flow directions are computed for every cell in a DEM, the result is a **grid network** --- a directed graph in which each cell has exactly one outgoing edge pointing to its downstream neighbor. This structure is mathematically a forest (a collection of trees), where each tree is rooted at an outlet cell on the domain boundary. If the DEM has been properly conditioned so that every cell has a downslope path to the edge, the grid network forms a single spanning tree rooted at the watershed outlet (or a forest of trees if the domain contains multiple outlets).

The grid network is the backbone of all subsequent hydrologic analysis. By following flow directions downstream from any cell, you trace the flow path that water would take to reach the outlet. By tracing upstream from an outlet, you identify every cell that contributes flow to that point --- which is exactly the definition of a watershed. The grid network implicitly encodes the complete drainage structure of the landscape: every stream channel, every ridge, every confluence.

A useful mental model is to imagine the flow direction raster as a vast collection of dominoes, each one tipped to point toward its steepest downslope neighbor. If you placed a marble on any domino, it would roll to the next, and the next, eventually reaching the edge of the table. Every marble, no matter where it starts, follows a unique path to the outlet. No paths branch (each cell points to exactly one neighbor), and no paths loop (the DEM is conditioned to be loop-free). This tree-like structure is what makes D8 so powerful for watershed delineation and stream network extraction, as we will see in Chapter 11.

The grid network also has a computational property that is essential for efficiency: it defines a topological ordering of cells. A cell can only be processed after all cells upstream of it have been processed. This ordering --- from ridgelines to outlets --- governs the sequence in which flow accumulation and all generalized accumulation functions are computed. Modern implementations like TauDEM exploit this ordering to parallelize the computation across multiple processor cores, dividing the domain into horizontal stripes that can be processed simultaneously with boundary information exchanged between iterations.

### 10.1.4 Limitations of D8

For all its elegance and utility, D8 has a fundamental limitation: it constrains flow to one of eight discrete directions, spaced at 45-degree intervals. On real terrain, water flows perpendicular to contour lines regardless of how the grid happens to be oriented. The continuous reality of flow direction is quantized into just eight possibilities, and this quantization introduces systematic artifacts.

The most visible artifact is the **staircase pattern** in flow paths. Because D8 can only route flow in cardinal or diagonal directions, a flow path that should follow a smooth curve instead zigzags between adjacent grid directions. On a hillslope with a true flow direction of, say, 30 degrees from east, D8 alternates between eastward (0 degrees) and northeastward (45 degrees) cells, producing a characteristic sawtooth path. The path is correct on average --- it does trend at roughly 30 degrees --- but the individual steps are wrong.

A second artifact appears in contributing area maps. On planar or gently sloping surfaces, D8 produces **parallel flow streaks** --- long lines of cells with identical flow directions that create artificial concentrations of drainage area. These streaks arise because a flat or uniformly sloped surface offers no local variation to break the symmetry, and all cells in a row receive the same flow direction code. The resulting contributing area map shows narrow bands of high values separated by bands of low values, an artifact with no physical basis.

The third limitation is conceptual: D8 routes all flow from a cell to a single neighbor, which means that divergent flow is impossible. On a convex hilltop, water in reality disperses in all directions. D8 forces it into a single channel from the very first cell. This makes D8 a poor choice for representing sheet flow on hillslopes, where the spatial distribution of contributing area matters for erosion modeling and soil moisture estimation.

Despite these limitations, D8 remains the standard algorithm for stream and watershed delineation. The reason is precisely the property that makes it poor for hillslope flow: the single-direction routing creates unambiguous, non-branching drainage paths. Every cell connects to exactly one downstream cell, producing a clean tree structure that can be decomposed into stream segments and sub-watersheds. For defining "where is the stream?" and "what area drains to this point?", D8 is not just adequate but optimal --- its discrete, deterministic nature is a feature, not a bug.

## 10.2 The D-infinity Flow Direction Model

### 10.2.1 Moving Beyond Eight Directions

The limitations of D8 motivated David Tarboton to develop the **D-infinity** (D$\infty$) model, published in a landmark 1997 paper in *Water Resources Research*. Where D8 forces flow into one of eight discrete directions, D-infinity represents flow direction as a continuous angle --- any value from 0 to $2\pi$ radians (0 to 360 degrees). This seemingly small change has profound consequences for the accuracy of contributing area computations and the physical realism of hillslope flow representation.

The key insight behind D-infinity is geometric. The eight neighbors of a grid cell define eight triangular facets, each formed by the center cell and a pair of adjacent neighbors. Consider the center cell and its east and northeast neighbors: these three points define a triangular facet. A plane can be fitted through any three points (three points uniquely define a plane in 3D space), and the direction of steepest descent on that plane can be computed analytically. By evaluating all eight facets and selecting the one with the steepest downslope direction, D-infinity determines the true direction of steepest descent, unconstrained by the grid alignment.

The result is a flow direction angle that can point in any direction, not just multiples of 45 degrees. If the steepest descent on the winning facet points at 32.7 degrees from east, that is the recorded flow direction. This continuous representation eliminates the staircase artifacts and parallel streaking that plague D8, producing smooth contributing area fields that better approximate the true physics of overland flow.

### 10.2.2 The Algorithm in Detail

The computation proceeds facet by facet. For a triangular facet defined by the center cell (elevation $z_0$) and two adjacent neighbors (elevations $z_1$ and $z_2$), the slope components along the two edges of the facet are:

$$S_x = \frac{z_0 - z_1}{\Delta}$$

$$S_y = \frac{z_1 - z_2}{\Delta}$$

where $\Delta$ is the grid cell size. The component $S_x$ represents the slope along the edge from the center cell to the first neighbor, and $S_y$ represents the slope perpendicular to that edge within the facet plane.

The flow direction angle within the facet (measured from the edge toward the center cell--first neighbor direction) is:

$$\alpha = \arctan\left(\frac{S_y}{S_x}\right)$$

The overall slope magnitude on this facet is:

$$S = \sqrt{S_x^2 + S_y^2}$$

A critical check follows: the computed angle $\alpha$ must fall within the angular range of the facet. Each facet spans 45 degrees (the angle between two adjacent neighbor directions). If $\alpha$ falls within this range, the flow direction on this facet is valid --- it points outward from the center cell through the body of the triangle. If $\alpha$ falls outside the range (pointing into an adjacent facet or back toward the center cell), the algorithm assigns the flow along whichever of the two bounding edges has the steeper slope, effectively collapsing to D8 for that particular facet.

After evaluating all eight facets, the algorithm selects the one with the greatest slope magnitude $S$. The flow direction for the cell is the angle from that winning facet, reported as a continuous value in radians (or degrees), measured counterclockwise from the positive x-axis (east). If all facets produce negative slopes (the cell is a local minimum), the DEM conditioning from Chapter 9 has failed and needs to be revisited.

### 10.2.3 Flow Proportioning Between Neighbors

The continuous flow angle generally falls between two neighboring grid directions. For example, a flow direction of 32.7 degrees falls between east (0 degrees) and northeast (45 degrees). This means the flow exits the cell through the facet bounded by these two neighbors. D-infinity proportions the flow between them based on how close the angle is to each bounding direction:

$$p_1 = \frac{\theta_2 - \alpha}{\theta_2 - \theta_1}$$

$$p_2 = \frac{\alpha - \theta_1}{\theta_2 - \theta_1}$$

where $\theta_1$ and $\theta_2$ are the angles of the two bounding grid directions, and $\alpha$ is the computed flow angle. For our example with $\alpha = 32.7°$, $\theta_1 = 0°$ (east), and $\theta_2 = 45°$ (northeast):

$$p_{\text{east}} = \frac{45 - 32.7}{45 - 0} = \frac{12.3}{45} = 0.273$$

$$p_{\text{northeast}} = \frac{32.7 - 0}{45 - 0} = \frac{32.7}{45} = 0.727$$

So 27.3% of the flow goes to the east neighbor and 72.7% goes to the northeast neighbor. The proportions always sum to 1.0, ensuring mass conservation. If the flow direction happens to fall exactly on a grid direction (e.g., exactly 45 degrees), all flow goes to that single neighbor --- D-infinity collapses to D8 for that cell.

This proportioning is the fundamental innovation of D-infinity. Rather than forcing all flow into a single neighbor (as D8 does), it distributes flow between two neighbors in a physically meaningful way. The result is smoother, more realistic patterns of contributing area, particularly on hillslopes where dispersive flow is the norm.

### 10.2.4 A Worked Example

Let us trace through the complete D-infinity computation for a concrete case. Consider a center cell at elevation 45 m on a 10-meter grid, with the following neighbor elevations:

**Table 10.3**: Neighbor elevations for the D-infinity worked example

| Direction | Angle | Elevation (m) |
|-----------|-------|---------------|
| East | 0° | 43 |
| Northeast | 45° | 38 |
| North | 90° | 44 |
| Northwest | 135° | 47 |
| West | 180° | 48 |
| Southwest | 225° | 49 |
| South | 270° | 46 |
| Southeast | 315° | 44 |

We will evaluate the facet formed by the center cell, the east neighbor, and the northeast neighbor (spanning 0 to 45 degrees).

**Step 1: Compute slope components.**

$$S_x = \frac{z_0 - z_{\text{east}}}{\Delta} = \frac{45 - 43}{10} = 0.200$$

$$S_y = \frac{z_{\text{east}} - z_{\text{northeast}}}{\Delta} = \frac{43 - 38}{10} = 0.500$$

**Step 2: Compute the flow angle within the facet.**

$$\alpha = \arctan\left(\frac{0.500}{0.200}\right) = \arctan(2.5) = 68.2°$$

**Step 3: Check if the angle falls within the facet range (0 to 45 degrees).**

The computed angle of 68.2 degrees exceeds 45 degrees --- it points outside this facet. Therefore, the flow direction on this facet is assigned along the steeper of the two bounding edges.

The slope along the east edge (0 degrees): $S_x = 0.200$.

The slope along the northeast diagonal: $(45 - 38) / (10\sqrt{2}) = 7 / 14.14 = 0.495$.

The northeast edge is steeper (0.495 > 0.200), so the flow direction from this facet is assigned at 45 degrees with slope 0.495.

**Step 4: Evaluate remaining facets and select the steepest.**

We would repeat this process for all eight facets. In this configuration, the facet between northeast (45 degrees) and north (90 degrees) yields:

$$S_x = \frac{45 - 38}{10} = 0.700, \quad S_y = \frac{38 - 44}{10} = -0.600$$

$$\alpha = \arctan(-0.600 / 0.700) = -40.6°$$

This angle is negative, falling outside the facet range (0 to 45 degrees), so the flow is assigned along the northeast edge at 45 degrees with slope $(45 - 38) / (10\sqrt{2}) = 0.495$. After checking all facets, the northeast direction at 45 degrees with slope 0.495 emerges as the overall steepest. Since the winning direction falls exactly on a grid direction, all flow goes to the northeast neighbor --- D-infinity collapses to D8 for this particular cell.

This collapse is not unusual. On steep terrain with strong directional gradients, D-infinity often aligns closely with D8. The algorithms diverge most on gentle slopes, broad ridges, and planar hillslopes --- precisely the places where D8's artifacts are most problematic and D-infinity's continuous representation is most valuable.

### 10.2.5 Advantages Over D8

The practical differences between D8 and D-infinity become strikingly visible when you compare contributing area maps computed from the same DEM. D8 maps exhibit characteristic horizontal and 45-degree streaks --- bands of artificially high contributing area where flow has been concentrated along grid directions. D-infinity maps show smooth, continuous patterns that follow topographic contours more faithfully.

Tarboton (1997) quantified this advantage using idealized test surfaces: an outward-draining cone (flow diverges from a peak), an inward-draining cone (flow converges to a pit), and a planar surface (uniform flow). For each test case, the analytical contributing area can be computed exactly, providing a ground truth for comparison. D-infinity consistently produced smaller root-mean-square errors than D8, with the most dramatic improvements on the divergent cone, where D8's single-direction routing completely fails to represent the radially spreading flow.

The consequences extend beyond aesthetics. The topographic wetness index (Section 10.5) depends on specific catchment area, which is directly derived from contributing area. In TOPMODEL and related distributed hydrologic models, the wetness index controls the spatial pattern of variable source area runoff generation. Smoother, more accurate contributing area maps translate directly to better model parameterization and more realistic predictions of where saturation-excess runoff occurs.

For stream and watershed delineation, however, D8 remains the preferred algorithm. The reason is that D-infinity's flow proportioning creates a distributed, many-to-many mapping between cells, which does not produce the clean tree structure needed for defining discrete stream segments and watershed boundaries. In practice, most hydrologic workflows use both algorithms: D8 for network definition and watershed boundaries, and D-infinity for contributing area and hillslope characterization.

## 10.3 Flow Accumulation

### 10.3.1 From Direction to Area

A flow direction raster tells you where water goes from each cell, but it does not tell you how much water arrives. That is the role of **flow accumulation** --- the operation that transforms a flow direction field into a map of contributing area. Flow accumulation at a cell represents the total area (or number of cells) that drains into that cell, following the flow direction network.

The concept is intuitive: imagine it rains uniformly across the landscape, with one unit of water falling on each cell. Each cell passes its water to its downslope neighbor (for D8) or proportions it between two neighbors (for D-infinity). The water cascades downhill, accumulating at every cell along the way. Cells on ridgelines receive water only from themselves (accumulation = 1). Cells near the outlet of a large watershed receive water from potentially millions of upstream cells. The flow accumulation value at any cell is simply the count of all cells whose flow paths pass through it.

Mathematically, for D8:

$$A(c) = 1 + \sum_{\text{all } i \text{ that flow into } c} A(i)$$

Each cell starts with a value of 1 (representing itself), then adds the accumulated values of all upstream neighbors that flow directly into it. This recursive definition is computed efficiently by processing cells in topological order --- from ridgelines to outlets --- so that every cell's upstream contributors have already been computed when the cell is processed.

For D-infinity, the formula accounts for flow proportioning:

$$A(c) = 1 + \sum_{\text{all neighbors } i} p(i,c) \cdot A(i)$$

where $p(i,c)$ is the proportion of flow from cell $i$ directed to cell $c$. This produces fractional contributions --- if a cell sends 30% of its flow to the east and 70% to the northeast, the east neighbor receives 0.3 times that cell's contributing area.

A note on conventions: TauDEM includes the cell itself in the count (a ridgeline cell has accumulation = 1), while ArcGIS counts only inflowing cells (a ridgeline cell has accumulation = 0). This one-cell difference is trivial for large accumulation values but matters when applying small thresholds for stream definition.

### 10.3.2 Conservation and the Directed Acyclic Graph

Two mathematical conditions must hold for flow accumulation to be valid:

**Conservation of flow.** For each cell, the fractions of outflow directed to all downstream neighbors must sum to exactly 1.0. In D8, this is automatically satisfied because all flow goes to one neighbor (fraction = 1.0). In D-infinity, the proportioning formula guarantees that $p_1 + p_2 = 1.0$. This ensures mass conservation: no water is created or destroyed during accumulation. If the outflow fractions summed to more than 1.0, the system would create phantom water; if less than 1.0, water would vanish.

**No circular flow paths.** The flow direction network must be a directed acyclic graph (DAG) --- there can be no loops. If cell A flows to cell B, and cell B flows to cell C, and cell C flows back to cell A, the accumulation algorithm would enter an infinite loop, adding water that circulates forever. Proper DEM conditioning (Chapter 9) guarantees this property: since flow always goes downhill, and the conditioned DEM has no flat areas or depressions, no cell can be part of a cycle.

Together, these two conditions ensure that flow accumulation is well-defined and computable. The directed acyclic graph structure also provides the topological ordering needed for efficient computation: process cells from highest to lowest, and every cell's upstream contributors will have been processed before it.

### 10.3.3 Properties and Visualization

Flow accumulation values span an enormous range. A ridgeline cell has an accumulation of 0 or 1 (depending on convention). A cell at the outlet of a 10,000 km$^2$ watershed on a 30-meter grid has an accumulation of over 11 million. This range --- from single digits to millions --- makes visualization challenging. A linear color ramp will paint 99% of the map in the lowest color, with only the main river channel visible.

The solution is to use either a logarithmic scale or a classified symbology with approximately geometric breaks. A useful set of breaks might be:

**Table 10.4**: Suggested flow accumulation classification for visualization

| Class | Accumulation range | Interpretation |
|-------|--------------------|----------------|
| 1 | 0--10 | Ridgelines and hilltops |
| 2 | 10--100 | Upper hillslopes |
| 3 | 100--1,000 | Concentrated hillslope flow |
| 4 | 1,000--10,000 | Headwater channels |
| 5 | 10,000--100,000 | Small streams |
| 6 | 100,000--1,000,000 | Medium rivers |
| 7 | >1,000,000 | Major rivers |

With this classification, the drainage network structure becomes clearly visible: a branching tree of progressively wider channels, exactly mirroring the stream network that would appear on a topographic map.

To convert flow accumulation (cell count) to actual drainage area, multiply by the area of one cell:

$$A_{\text{drainage}} = A_{\text{cells}} \times \Delta x \times \Delta y$$

For a 30-meter grid, each cell represents $30 \times 30 = 900 \text{ m}^2$. A cell with accumulation of 100,000 drains $100{,}000 \times 900 = 90{,}000{,}000 \text{ m}^2 = 90 \text{ km}^2$. For DEMs in geographic coordinates (degrees of latitude and longitude), the cell area varies with latitude, and a cosine correction or local area computation must be applied.

### 10.3.4 Stream Definition by Threshold

One of the most immediate applications of flow accumulation is stream network extraction. The principle is simple: cells with flow accumulation above a chosen threshold are classified as stream cells.

$$\text{Stream}(c) = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } A(c) \geq T \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

The threshold $T$ controls the density of the resulting stream network. A low threshold (say, 10 cells on a 30-m grid, representing 0.009 km$^2$) produces a very dense network that includes every tiny swale and gully. A high threshold (say, 5,000 cells, representing 4.5 km$^2$) produces only the major streams. There is no single "correct" threshold --- the appropriate value depends on the application, the resolution of the DEM, and the scale of analysis.

In practice, the threshold is often calibrated by comparing the extracted network to a reference stream map. In the United States, NHDPlus provides a reference network with roughly 2.7 million stream reaches. In Hungary and the European Union, the EU-Hydro dataset (derived from Copernicus satellite imagery and the EEA-10 DEM) provides a comparable reference. Globally, HydroSHEDS offers stream networks derived from SRTM data at multiple scales. The threshold that best reproduces the density of the reference network is often a good starting point. Chapter 11 explores this calibration process in detail.

### 10.3.5 Interpreting Accumulation at Confluences

At stream junctions, flow accumulation values increase abruptly. This additive property provides a powerful consistency check and an intuitive way to read the drainage structure of a landscape. If a tributary with a contributing area of 900 cells joins a mainstem carrying 6,195 cells, the cell immediately below the confluence will have an accumulation of approximately 7,096 (900 + 6,195 + 1 for the junction cell itself, in TauDEM convention). Any significant departure from this expected sum indicates an error in the flow direction computation or the DEM conditioning.

This additive property also means that flow accumulation is a natural ranking system for streams. The Strahler stream order, Shreve magnitude, and other stream ordering systems (discussed in Chapter 11) all build on the branching structure encoded in the flow accumulation field. The accumulation value at any point along a stream is a proxy for the stream's "size" in terms of contributing area --- a quantity that correlates with mean discharge, channel width, and many other hydrologic and geomorphic variables.

### 10.3.6 Weighted Flow Accumulation

The standard flow accumulation assigns each cell a weight of 1, effectively counting cells. But the concept generalizes naturally to **weighted flow accumulation**, where each cell contributes a specified weight rather than unity:

$$W(c) = w(c) + \sum_{\text{all } i \text{ that flow into } c} p(i,c) \cdot W(i)$$

where $w(c)$ is the weight at cell $c$. The weighted accumulation replaces the uniform rainfall assumption with a spatially variable input. The applications are numerous and powerful.

**Precipitation accumulation.** If $w(c)$ is the precipitation depth at cell $c$ (say, from a radar-rainfall grid or an interpolated gauge network), then $W(c)$ gives the total precipitation volume per unit cell area draining through cell $c$. Multiplying by the cell area converts this to an absolute volume. This is a simple way to estimate runoff volume --- admittedly crude because it ignores infiltration, evapotranspiration, and storage, but useful as a first approximation or screening tool.

**Contaminant loading.** If $w(c)$ represents a pollutant loading rate (kilograms per year per cell, for instance), then $W(c)$ integrates the total loading arriving at cell $c$ from its entire contributing area. This allows rapid identification of stream reaches that receive the highest cumulative pollutant load, even if no individual source is large. The spatial pattern of agricultural nutrient application, for example, can be accumulated along the flow network to map the downstream concentration of nitrogen or phosphorus.

**Land-use weighting.** If $w(c)$ is the imperviousness fraction of cell $c$ (from a land-use classification), the weighted accumulation produces a map of effective impervious area contributing to each stream reach --- a key parameter for urban stormwater modeling and water quality assessment.

In ArcGIS, weighted accumulation is performed by supplying a weight raster to the Flow Accumulation tool and setting the output data type to floating point (integer output truncates the fractional contributions from D-infinity routing). In TauDEM, the D-infinity Contributing Area tool accepts an optional weight grid. In QGIS, the SAGA and WhiteboxTools providers offer equivalent functionality.

## 10.4 Generalized Accumulation Functions

### 10.4.1 Beyond Simple Accumulation

Standard flow accumulation, whether weighted or unweighted, assumes that whatever enters a cell passes through unchanged and continues downstream. This is equivalent to assuming perfect conservation: no decay, no transformation, no capacity limits. For many hydrologic and environmental applications, this assumption is too simplistic. Contaminants decay. Sediment deposits. Infiltration removes water from surface flow. The D-infinity framework enables a family of **generalized accumulation functions** that incorporate these processes, transforming the flow direction field from a simple accounting tool into a flexible environmental modeling platform.

The insight underlying all generalized accumulation functions is that the flow direction field provides a transportation network --- a graph along which quantities can be routed, combined, and transformed according to specified rules. The D-infinity proportioning ensures physically realistic routing, and the topological ordering ensures computational efficiency. The only thing that changes from one application to another is the rule governing what happens as material moves from one cell to the next.

### 10.4.2 The Influence Function

Suppose a contaminant is released at a known location --- a chemical spill, a leaking landfill, a feedlot. The natural question is: "Where does this contamination go?" The **influence function** answers this by tracking material downslope from a source area.

The computation is straightforward. Define an indicator function that equals 1 within the source area and 0 everywhere else. Then accumulate this indicator downslope following the D-infinity flow field. The resulting map shows, for every cell downstream of the source, the fraction of flow originating from the source that passes through that cell. Cells with high values are strongly influenced by the source; cells with zero values are unaffected.

The influence function has applications beyond contamination. In forestry, it can map the area influenced by a timber harvest (where does sediment from exposed soil travel?). In urban planning, it can identify the downstream impact zone of a proposed development. In agriculture, it can trace the pathway of applied fertilizer from field to stream.

Because the influence function uses D-infinity proportioning, it naturally represents the dispersive behavior of flow on hillslopes. Flow from a point source spreads across the hillslope before converging into channels --- a more physically realistic pattern than the single-path routing of D8, which would trace a single line from source to outlet.

### 10.4.3 The Dependence Function

The **dependence function** is the mathematical inverse of the influence function. Instead of asking "where does this contamination go?", it asks "where does contamination at this location come from?" Given a target location --- a water intake, a property boundary, a sensitive wetland --- the dependence function traces flow upstream, quantifying what fraction of flow from each cell in the domain eventually reaches the target.

The computation reverses the direction of traversal. Starting from the target cell (dependence = 1), the algorithm propagates values upstream: each cell receives a value equal to the fraction of its outflow that reaches the target, weighted by the D-infinity proportions along the path. A cell that sends 100% of its flow toward the target (and all intermediate cells also route toward the target) has a dependence of 1.0. A cell on the opposite side of a drainage divide, sending no flow toward the target, has a dependence of 0.0. Most cells fall somewhere in between, with fractional values reflecting the proportion of their flow that eventually reaches the target.

### 10.4.4 Decay-Limited Accumulation

Many contaminants do not survive indefinitely as they travel through the landscape. Organic pollutants biodegrade. Nutrients are taken up by vegetation. Pathogens die off. For these substances, a first-order decay model provides a reasonable approximation: the concentration decreases exponentially with distance traveled.

The **decay-limited accumulation** incorporates this attenuation:

$$C(c) = w(c) + \sum_{\text{all } i} p(i,c) \cdot C(i) \cdot e^{-k \cdot d(i,c)}$$

where $k$ is the decay constant (with units of inverse distance, e.g., m$^{-1}$), $d(i,c)$ is the distance between cells $i$ and $c$, and the other symbols carry their usual meanings. The exponential term reduces the contribution of distant upstream cells, so that a contaminant source far from the target has less impact than a nearby source, even if the distant source is larger.

To illustrate: consider a decay constant of $k = 0.001 \text{ m}^{-1}$. After traveling 1,000 m, a contaminant is attenuated by a factor of $e^{-0.001 \times 1000} = e^{-1} \approx 0.37$ --- it retains 37% of its original concentration. After 5,000 m, the attenuation factor is $e^{-5} \approx 0.007$, or less than 1%. The decay-limited accumulation naturally weights nearby sources much more heavily than distant ones, producing contamination risk maps that reflect the physics of transport and decay.

### 10.4.5 Concentration-Limited Accumulation

Some contaminants are limited not by decay but by solubility. A substance dissolves in water up to a maximum concentration and no further, regardless of how much solid-phase material is present. As flow proceeds downslope, dilution from lateral inflow reduces the concentration, and decay may further attenuate it.

The **concentration-limited accumulation** caps the loading from each source cell at a maximum concentration. The accumulated concentration at any downstream cell is the result of mixing contributions from all upstream cells, each limited by the solubility constraint and diluted by the total flow volume. This produces physically realistic concentration estimates for substances like gypsum, calcium carbonate, or sparingly soluble organic compounds.

The mathematical formulation is more complex than simple decay, as it requires tracking both the mass of contaminant and the volume of water at each cell. The flow algebra framework (Section 10.4.7) provides a general structure for expressing these coupled computations.

### 10.4.6 Transport-Capacity-Limited Accumulation

Sediment transport on hillslopes and in channels is governed not only by the supply of sediment but by the capacity of the flow to carry it. A steep, fast-flowing stream can carry more sediment than a slow, meandering one. When the supply exceeds the transport capacity, sediment deposits; when the capacity exceeds the supply, the flow can erode additional material.

The **transport-capacity-limited accumulation** models this process:

$$T_{\text{out}}(c) = \min\left(T_{\text{supply}}(c) + T_{\text{in}}(c), \; T_{\text{capacity}}(c)\right)$$

$$D(c) = T_{\text{supply}}(c) + T_{\text{in}}(c) - T_{\text{out}}(c)$$

where $T_{\text{supply}}(c)$ is the local sediment production (from erosion), $T_{\text{in}}(c)$ is the sediment arriving from upstream, $T_{\text{capacity}}(c)$ is the transport capacity (typically a function of slope and contributing area), and $D(c)$ is the deposition at cell $c$. Deposition occurs whenever the combined supply exceeds the transport capacity.

This formulation naturally produces the **sediment delivery ratio** --- the fraction of eroded sediment that actually reaches the watershed outlet. As watershed size increases, more opportunities for internal deposition arise (in floodplains, behind obstructions, in low-gradient reaches), and a progressively smaller fraction of the total erosion reaches the outlet. The sediment delivery ratio is a critical concept in soil conservation and reservoir sedimentation planning, and transport-capacity-limited accumulation provides a physically based mechanism for computing it from topographic data alone.

### 10.4.7 The Flow Algebra

The various accumulation functions described above --- simple, weighted, decay-limited, concentration-limited, transport-capacity-limited --- share a common computational structure. In each case, the algorithm:

1. Traverses cells in topological order (either upstream-to-downstream or downstream-to-upstream).
2. Combines values from contributing cells according to specified rules.
3. Applies a transformation (identity, decay, capacity limit, etc.) during transport.
4. Records the result at each cell.

Tarboton and Baker (2008) formalized this shared structure as a **flow algebra** --- a general framework for expressing any terrain-flow-related computation. The flow algebra specifies four components:

- The **flow direction field** $P$ --- the D-infinity proportion matrix that defines how flow is distributed between neighbors.
- The **input quantity** at each cell --- a raster of weights, concentrations, erosion rates, or any spatial variable.
- The **combination rule** --- how upstream contributions are merged (summation, minimum, maximum, or a custom function).
- The **transport transformation** --- what happens to the quantity as it moves from one cell to the next (identity, exponential decay, capacity limiting, etc.).

The general pseudocode for an upstream (accumulating downslope) flow algebra evaluation is:

```
Global P, input, output
FlowAlgebra(i)
    for all k neighbors of i
        if P(k,i) > 0          // k flows into i
            FlowAlgebra(k)     // process k first (recursion)
    next k
    output(i) = FA(i, P(k,i), input(k), output(k))  // combine and transform
    return
```

The function `FA` is the flow algebra rule --- the specific combination of merging and transformation that defines the application. For simple accumulation, `FA` is summation with identity transformation. For decay-limited accumulation, `FA` is summation with exponential decay. For transport-capacity-limited sediment, `FA` is summation capped by a capacity function.

A corresponding downstream flow algebra reverses the direction, accumulating upslope from a target. This supports the dependence function, reverse distance computations, and other upstream-tracing applications.

The power of the flow algebra concept is that it separates the routing logic (which is always the same --- follow the D-infinity proportions in topological order) from the application-specific transformation (which varies). This means that new environmental applications can be implemented by defining a new `FA` function, without reimplementing the routing machinery. It is, in essence, a programming pattern for terrain-based environmental modeling.

**Table 10.5**: Summary of generalized accumulation functions and their flow algebra rules

| Function | Direction | Combination rule | Transport transformation | Application |
|----------|-----------|-----------------|--------------------------|-------------|
| Simple accumulation | Downstream | Sum | Identity (pass-through) | Contributing area |
| Weighted accumulation | Downstream | Sum | Identity | Precipitation, loading |
| Influence | Downstream | Sum | Identity (indicator input) | Contamination footprint |
| Dependence | Upstream | Sum | Proportioned by flow fraction | Source area identification |
| Decay-limited | Downstream | Sum | Exponential decay | Biodegradable contaminants |
| Concentration-limited | Downstream | Sum, capped | Dilution + decay | Solubility-limited substances |
| Transport-capacity-limited | Downstream | Sum, min with capacity | Deposition of excess | Sediment delivery |
| Retention-limited runoff | Downstream | Sum | Subtraction of retention | Run-on/runoff modeling |

## 10.5 Specific Catchment Area and the Topographic Wetness Index

### 10.5.1 Specific Catchment Area

On hillslopes, total contributing area is less meaningful than **specific catchment area** --- the contributing area per unit contour width. The distinction is important because the concentration of flow at a point depends not just on how much area drains to it, but on how wide the "funnel" is at that point. A broad, divergent hillslope may have a large total contributing area but a low specific catchment area because the flow is spread over a wide front. A narrow convergent hollow may have a smaller total contributing area but a much higher specific catchment area because the flow is concentrated.

Mathematically:

$$a = \frac{A}{w}$$

where $A$ is the total contributing area (m$^2$) and $w$ is the contour length (m) through which the flow passes. In grid computations with D-infinity, specific catchment area is approximated as the contributing area divided by the cell width:

$$a = \frac{A}{\Delta}$$

The units of specific catchment area are meters (m$^2$/m = m), which can be interpreted as the depth of water that would result if all the contributing area were concentrated into a strip one cell wide. High values indicate convergent terrain where flow concentrates; low values indicate divergent terrain where flow disperses.

The D-infinity algorithm naturally produces specific catchment area because its flow proportioning distributes flow according to the actual topographic convergence and divergence. D8, by contrast, tends to overestimate specific catchment area on convergent terrain (because all flow goes to one cell) and underestimate it on divergent terrain (because flow cannot spread). This is the fundamental reason why D-infinity is preferred for hillslope characterization, even though D8 is preferred for stream network extraction.

### 10.5.2 The Topographic Wetness Index

The **topographic wetness index** (TWI), introduced by Beven and Kirkby (1979) as a key parameter in the TOPMODEL hydrologic model, combines contributing area and slope into a single indicator of a cell's propensity for saturation:

$$\text{TWI} = \ln\left(\frac{a}{\tan \beta}\right)$$

where $a$ is the specific catchment area and $\beta$ is the local slope angle. The logarithm is needed because the ratio $a/\tan\beta$ spans several orders of magnitude.

The physical interpretation is straightforward. A location with a high TWI is both flat ($\tan\beta$ is small, making the ratio large) and has a large contributing area ($a$ is large). These are exactly the conditions that favor saturation: water from a large upslope area converges on a flat area where it cannot drain away quickly. Valley bottoms along stream channels typically have the highest TWI values, while steep ridgelines have the lowest.

**A worked example.** Consider two cells on a 10-meter grid:

Cell A (valley bottom): contributing area = 500 cells = $500 \times 10 = 5{,}000$ m specific catchment area, slope = 2 degrees.

$$\text{TWI}_A = \ln\left(\frac{5{,}000}{\tan 2°}\right) = \ln\left(\frac{5{,}000}{0.0349}\right) = \ln(143{,}266) = 11.87$$

Cell B (steep hillslope): contributing area = 20 cells = $20 \times 10 = 200$ m specific catchment area, slope = 25 degrees.

$$\text{TWI}_B = \ln\left(\frac{200}{\tan 25°}\right) = \ln\left(\frac{200}{0.4663}\right) = \ln(429) = 6.06$$

Cell A has a TWI nearly twice that of Cell B, indicating a much higher propensity for saturation. In a TOPMODEL simulation, Cell A would be among the first to saturate during a storm event, generating saturation-excess runoff that contributes directly to the stream.

The quality of the TWI map depends critically on the quality of the contributing area input. Because D-infinity produces smoother, more accurate specific catchment area than D8, D-infinity-derived TWI distributions are more realistic. The streak artifacts in D8 contributing area propagate directly into the TWI map, creating artificial patterns of high and low wetness that have no physical basis. For applications where TWI drives model behavior --- distributed hydrologic modeling, wetland mapping, soil moisture estimation --- D-infinity is strongly recommended.

### 10.5.3 International Perspectives on TWI and Contributing Area

The computation of specific catchment area and TWI is universal --- it depends only on having a DEM and computing flow directions. But the interpretation and application vary with the hydrologic setting.

In Hungary and Central Europe, the relatively flat Pannonian Basin presents a particular challenge for TWI computation. Large areas of the Great Hungarian Plain (Alfold) have slopes approaching zero, which causes $\tan\beta$ to approach zero and TWI to approach infinity. This is not a numerical artifact but a reflection of reality: on extremely flat terrain, even tiny amounts of contributing area can cause saturation. However, on the Great Plain, the dominant hydrologic processes are not topographically controlled overland flow (as TOPMODEL assumes) but rather groundwater-surface water interaction and evapotranspiration-dominated water balance. TWI is most useful and most physically meaningful in hilly or mountainous terrain --- in Hungary, the Northern Mountains (Eszaki-koezepheegyseg), the Transdanubian Hills (Dunantuli-dombsag), or the Mecsek region.

## 10.6 Height Above Nearest Drainage: A Preview

### 10.6.1 The Concept

One of the most powerful derivatives of the flow direction field is the **Height Above Nearest Drainage** (HAND). For each cell in the DEM, HAND measures the vertical distance between that cell's elevation and the elevation of the nearest stream cell, where "nearest" is defined not by straight-line distance but by following the D-infinity flow path downhill until a stream cell is reached.

$$\text{HAND}(c) = z(c) - z(s)$$

where $z(c)$ is the elevation of cell $c$ and $z(s)$ is the elevation of the stream cell $s$ that cell $c$ drains to. HAND is always non-negative (since cells drain downhill to streams) and equals zero for stream cells themselves.

The genius of HAND is that it normalizes the landscape relative to the local drainage. Two cells at the same absolute elevation can have very different HAND values if one sits on a high terrace above a deeply incised stream and the other sits on a low terrace above a shallow drainage. The cell on the high terrace has a large HAND value (it is far above its nearest stream); the cell on the low terrace has a small HAND value (it is close to its nearest stream). This relative height is far more informative for flood risk than absolute elevation.

### 10.6.2 Computation

Computing HAND requires three inputs: a conditioned DEM, a flow direction field (preferably D-infinity), and a stream raster (derived from flow accumulation thresholding, as described in Section 10.3.4). The algorithm traces the flow path from each non-stream cell downhill until it reaches a stream cell, records the elevation of that stream cell, and subtracts it from the cell's own elevation.

In TauDEM, this computation is performed by the **D-infinity Distance Down** function, which can compute several proximity measures simultaneously:

- **Vertical distance** (HAND itself)
- **Horizontal distance** (projected distance along the flow path)
- **Slope distance** (actual path length along the surface)
- **Straight-line distance** (Euclidean distance, ignoring topography)

Because D-infinity routes flow proportionally between two neighbors, the HAND value is computed as a weighted average over multiple flow paths, producing smoother results than D8-based HAND. This smoothing is particularly important near ridgelines, where a cell may drain to different stream reaches depending on which neighbor receives its flow.

### 10.6.3 Why HAND Matters for Flood Mapping

HAND has emerged as one of the most practical tools for rapid flood inundation mapping. The basic principle is elegant: flooding occurs when the water depth in a stream exceeds the HAND value of a nearby cell. If a stream reach has a water depth of $h_w$ meters (from a hydraulic model, a real-time forecast, or a design flood estimate), then every cell connected to that reach with a HAND value less than $h_w$ is inundated.

$$\text{Inundation depth}(c) = \begin{cases} h_w(\text{reach}) - \text{HAND}(c) & \text{if } h_w > \text{HAND}(c) \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

This approach is approximate --- it neglects the hydraulics of floodplain flow, channel geometry, and backwater effects --- but it is extraordinarily fast and requires no hydraulic model calibration. For screening-level flood risk assessment, real-time flood mapping, and large-scale flood exposure analysis, HAND provides answers that are often "good enough" when detailed hydraulic modeling is impractical.

In the United States, HAND has been computed for the entire continental US using 10-meter DEMs and NHDPlus stream networks, providing a national-scale flood mapping capability linked to the National Water Model. In Europe, the European Flood Awareness System (EFAS) and its global counterpart GloFAS produce stream flow forecasts that can be coupled with HAND for rapid inundation mapping. In Hungary, HAND can be computed for any watershed using the DDM-5 or Copernicus DEM, linked to the OVF hydrographic network.

### 10.6.4 Proximity Measures on the Hillslope

HAND is just one member of a family of proximity measures that can be computed from the D-infinity flow field. Working both downslope (toward streams) and upslope (toward ridgelines), these measures provide a complete quantitative characterization of any point's position on the hillslope:

**Table 10.7**: Proximity measures computed from D-infinity flow directions

| Measure | Direction | Definition | Application |
|---------|-----------|------------|-------------|
| Vertical distance to stream (HAND) | Downslope | Elevation difference along flow path to nearest stream | Flood mapping, hydrogeomorphic classification |
| Horizontal distance to stream | Downslope | Projected horizontal distance along flow path | Travel time estimation |
| Slope distance to stream | Downslope | Actual surface distance along flow path | Erosion and sediment transport |
| Straight-line distance to stream | Downslope | Euclidean distance (ignoring topography) | Buffer zone delineation |
| Distance from ridge | Upslope | Distance along reversed flow path to drainage divide | Hillslope position |
| Relative hillslope position | Both | Ratio of distance from ridge to total hillslope length | Geomorphic classification |

The combination of these measures enables precise quantitative statements that would otherwise be vague qualitative descriptions. "Halfway down the hillslope" becomes a measurable fraction. "Near the stream" becomes a specific distance in meters. "High above the channel" becomes a HAND value in meters. This quantitative framework transforms geomorphic description from subjective to objective, enabling consistent mapping and classification across landscapes.

## 10.7 High-Resolution Applications

### 10.7.1 What LiDAR Reveals

When D-infinity contributing area is computed on high-resolution LiDAR DEMs with sub-meter cell sizes, an extraordinary level of hydrologic detail emerges. Features that are invisible on 10-meter or 30-meter DEMs suddenly appear with startling clarity:

Individual hiking trails appear as linear features that concentrate and redirect flow. A trail compacted by foot traffic becomes a low-permeability surface that acts as a miniature drainage channel, collecting water from both sides and routing it along its length. The contributing area map shows the trail as a bright line of high accumulation values, revealing its role as an artificial drainage feature that can accelerate erosion on steep slopes.

Roads create even more dramatic flow disruptions. A road cut into a hillslope acts as a linear barrier to downslope flow, intercepting water that would naturally continue downhill. The intercepted water accumulates along the road until it reaches a culvert, where it is concentrated and released downslope as a point discharge. On the contributing area map, the road appears as a sharp boundary with high accumulation values at culvert locations and artificially low values immediately downslope of the road (in the "shadow" of the barrier). This pattern has direct implications for erosion control, road maintenance, and aquatic habitat management.

Microtopographic features on hillslopes --- small swales, rills, depressions left by tree falls, animal burrows --- also appear in high-resolution contributing area maps. These features control the detailed pattern of overland flow and soil moisture at scales relevant to ecology, agriculture, and forest management.

These high-resolution applications, demonstrated for areas such as Teton Pass in Idaho/Wyoming (USA) using data from the OpenTopography repository, illustrate that flow accumulation is not just a watershed-scale tool. At fine resolution, it reveals the hydrologic micro-structure of the landscape, providing insights that are invisible at coarser scales. With the growing availability of LiDAR data --- including Hungary's project-specific surveys for flood-prone valleys and karst areas, and broader European coverage through EU member state LiDAR programs --- these high-resolution applications are becoming accessible to practitioners worldwide.

## 10.8 Software Implementation

### 10.8.1 ArcGIS Pro

ArcGIS Pro (Esri) provides flow direction and flow accumulation as core tools in the Spatial Analyst extension:

- **Flow Direction**: Computes D8 flow directions using the Esri power-of-2 encoding. Optionally outputs a "drop" raster (the percentage drop in elevation in the flow direction). Note: a long-standing implementation detail approximates the diagonal distance factor ($\sqrt{2} \approx 1.414$) as 1.5 in the drop raster computation, producing slightly incorrect slope values for diagonal flow. The flow directions themselves are correct; only the drop values are affected.
- **Flow Accumulation**: Computes D8 flow accumulation with optional weight raster. The output data type should be set to floating point when using weights or when accumulation values may exceed the 32-bit integer range.
- **D-infinity tools**: ArcGIS Pro does not natively implement D-infinity. Users who need D-infinity must use TauDEM (accessible as a command-line toolset or integrated via custom toolboxes) or alternative software.

### 10.8.2 TauDEM

TauDEM (Terrain Analysis Using Digital Elevation Models), developed by David Tarboton at Utah State University, is the reference implementation for D-infinity and the generalized accumulation functions:

- **D8 Flow Directions** (`D8FlowDir`): Computes flow direction (suffix `p`) and slope (suffix `sd8`) using the Band encoding convention (1--8, counterclockwise from east).
- **D-infinity Flow Directions** (`DinfFlowDir`): Computes continuous flow angle (suffix `ang`) and slope (suffix `slp`).
- **D8 Contributing Area** (`AreaD8`): Standard flow accumulation. Counts the cell itself (TauDEM convention).
- **D-infinity Contributing Area** (`AreaDinf`): Proportioned flow accumulation with optional weight grid.
- **D-infinity Distance Down** (`DinfDistDown`): Computes HAND and other proximity measures.
- **D-infinity Distance Up** (`DinfDistUp`): Computes distances from ridgelines.
- **Specialized functions**: Topographic wetness index, stream network tools, reverse accumulation, and the generalized accumulation functions.
- **Parallel processing**: TauDEM uses MPI (Message Passing Interface) for parallel computation, dividing the domain into horizontal stripes processed on separate CPU cores. On a modern workstation, this can reduce computation times for continental-scale DEMs from hours to minutes.

TauDEM is free, open-source software available for Windows, Linux, and macOS. It integrates with ArcGIS via custom toolboxes, with QGIS via the Processing framework, and can be run from the command line for automated workflows.

### 10.8.3 Other Software

- **QGIS**: Provides D8 flow direction and accumulation through the SAGA and GRASS providers, and D-infinity through the WhiteboxTools provider. QGIS is free and open-source, making it the primary tool for many international users.
- **WhiteboxTools**: A high-performance geospatial analysis library (open-source) that implements both D8 and D-infinity, along with many other flow-routing algorithms (including D$\infty$, MD$\infty$, and FD8 multiple-direction methods).
- **GRASS GIS**: Provides `r.watershed` (an efficient memory-optimized D8 implementation) and `r.flow` (a D-infinity-like flow tracing tool). GRASS GIS is particularly well-suited for very large DEMs due to its segmented processing approach.
- **SAGA GIS**: Includes a comprehensive hydrology module with D8, D-infinity, and multiple-flow-direction algorithms.

## 10.9 Putting It All Together: From DEM to Drainage

The sequence of operations covered in this chapter --- flow direction, flow accumulation, stream definition, contributing area, and derived indices --- forms a computational pipeline that transforms a raw (conditioned) DEM into a rich hydrologic information layer. Every step depends on the previous one, and errors propagate forward.

The pipeline begins with the hydrologically conditioned DEM from Chapter 9. D8 flow directions create the grid network --- the directed graph that encodes the complete drainage structure. Flow accumulation identifies how much area drains to each cell. A threshold applied to accumulation delineates the stream network. D-infinity flow directions and specific catchment area provide smooth hillslope characterization. The topographic wetness index identifies saturation-prone areas. And HAND normalizes the landscape relative to the local drainage, opening the door to rapid flood inundation mapping.

This pipeline is not a one-time operation but a reusable analytical framework. Once the flow direction and accumulation rasters are computed, they can be queried and combined in countless ways: extracting the watershed for any point, computing the flow path from any cell, measuring the distance to the nearest stream, estimating cumulative pollutant loading, identifying areas vulnerable to erosion or saturation. The flow direction raster is, in effect, a hydrologic index of the entire landscape --- a compact representation of the drainage structure that enables rapid answers to an enormous range of water-related questions.

Chapter 11 builds directly on this foundation, taking the stream network and flow accumulation field and transforming them into named stream segments, ordered networks, and delineated sub-watersheds --- the spatial units needed for hydrologic modeling and water resource management.
