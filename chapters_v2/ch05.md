# Chapter 5: The Grid: How Computers See Terrain

How does a computer represent a river valley? Not with the sweeping visual impression a human gets standing on a hilltop -- the interplay of ridgelines, the deepening shadow of the channel, the way tributaries branch like the veins of a leaf. A computer sees terrain as numbers arranged in rows and columns: a vast spreadsheet draped over the landscape, where each cell holds a single value representing the elevation of the ground at that location. This seemingly crude approximation turns out to be extraordinarily powerful. From these grids of numbers, we can compute where water flows, how fast it moves, where it accumulates, and which areas will flood when the river rises. Nearly every hydrological analysis described in the remaining chapters of this book begins with a **raster grid** -- a **digital elevation model** stored as a regular array of cells.

This chapter introduces the raster data model: how it works, what it captures, what it loses, and how to make intelligent choices about resolution, format, and interpretation. We begin with the fundamental question of how continuous surfaces can be represented digitally, then examine the anatomy of a grid data structure in detail. We work through the critical distinction between floating-point and integer grids, the role of NODATA values, and the surprisingly subtle question of what a cell value actually means. We then introduce Bloschl's scale triplet -- a conceptual framework that every water professional should carry in their mental toolkit -- and close with a practical survey of raster file formats and storage options that reflects the state of practice in 2026.

The concepts here are foundational. Without understanding raster data, you cannot understand how a DEM becomes a flow direction grid (Chapter 10), how rainfall is distributed across a watershed (Chapter 8), or how a flood inundation map is constructed (Chapter 15). This chapter provides the vocabulary and the mental model for everything that follows.

## 5.1 Representing Continuous Surfaces

The physical world is full of continuous phenomena: temperature varies smoothly through the atmosphere, soil moisture changes gradually across a hillslope, and elevation transitions continuously from ridgeline to valley floor. In geographic information science, these phenomena are called **fields** -- quantities that are defined at every possible position in space. A field has no gaps, no boundaries, no undefined zones. At any point you might choose on the landscape, the elevation has a definite value, even if nobody has ever measured it.

The challenge for a computer is that continuous fields contain infinite information -- there are infinitely many points on any surface, each with its own value. Digital representation requires reducing this infinite reality to a finite set of numbers. The question is how to do this while preserving enough information for the analyses we need to perform. This trade-off between fidelity and tractability recurs throughout the book: in the interpolation of point rainfall measurements to continuous grids (Chapter 8), in the computation of slope from discrete elevation values (Chapter 7), and in the reconciliation of grids at different resolutions for map algebra calculations (Chapter 6).

Three principal methods have been developed for representing continuous surfaces numerically, each with distinct strengths and weaknesses for hydrological applications.

### 5.1.1 The Regular Grid (Raster)

The most widely used representation divides geographic space into a regular mesh of identically sized square cells, each holding a single numerical value. If the field is elevation, each cell stores the height of the terrain at that location. The entire surface is approximated by this mosaic of uniform tiles -- like a bathroom floor where each tile has a number written on it.

The grid representation has several properties that make it dominant in hydrological computing. First, it is computationally simple: every cell is the same size and shape, arranged in predictable rows and columns, so the computer can locate any cell and its neighbors using straightforward arithmetic on row and column indices. There is no need to search through an irregular data structure to find adjacent cells. Second, the grid aligns naturally with the output of many remote sensing instruments -- satellite sensors, weather radar, numerical weather models -- all of which produce data on regular grids. Third, cell-by-cell operations (adding two grids, computing the slope at each cell, finding the maximum of several grids) are trivially parallelizable, meaning modern processors can handle them at enormous speed.

The weakness of the regular grid is its inflexibility. Every cell is the same size regardless of terrain complexity. A flat agricultural plain and a deeply incised mountain valley receive the same spatial resolution, even though the valley contains far more topographic detail per unit area. This leads to either wasted storage in flat areas (if the resolution is fine) or lost detail in complex areas (if the resolution is coarse). Despite this limitation, the regular grid dominates hydrological GIS for a simple reason: the computational advantages far outweigh the storage inefficiency for nearly all practical applications.

### 5.1.2 Triangulated Irregular Networks (TINs)

A triangulated irregular network addresses the inflexibility problem by using irregularly spaced points connected into a mesh of non-overlapping triangles. Points can be concentrated in areas of high terrain complexity -- along ridgelines, in valley bottoms, at breaks in slope -- and spaced widely in flat areas. Each triangle defines a planar surface, and the elevation at any point within the triangle can be computed by linear interpolation from its three vertices.

TINs are mathematically elegant and storage-efficient for terrain representation. A TIN with 100,000 well-placed points can capture terrain detail that would require millions of regular grid cells. TINs are widely used in civil engineering and surveying applications, where high-fidelity terrain representation along specific features (road corridors, dam sites, channel cross-sections) is essential. They are also the natural representation for LiDAR point clouds, which are inherently irregular.

However, TINs have significant limitations for large-scale hydrological analysis. Computing flow direction across an irregular triangular mesh is considerably more complex than on a regular grid. The algorithms for watershed delineation, flow accumulation, and stream network extraction that form the backbone of hydrological GIS (Chapters 9-11) were developed for regular grids and do not transfer straightforwardly to TINs. While TIN-based hydrological algorithms exist, they are not part of standard GIS toolsets, and the vast majority of operational hydrological modeling uses gridded DEMs.

### 5.1.3 The Contour-Flowline Model

A third representation uses contour lines (lines of constant elevation) and flowlines (lines perpendicular to contours, indicating the direction of steepest descent) to define a surface. This approach was developed by hydrological researchers in Australia in the 1970s and 1980s, motivated by the observation that contours and flowlines are the natural language of terrain for hydrologists. The intersection of a contour and a flowline defines a small curvilinear element over which water balance equations can be written directly.

The contour-flowline model has theoretical appeal for hillslope hydrology, where the convergence and divergence of flow paths is central to understanding soil moisture patterns and runoff generation. However, it never achieved widespread adoption because it lacks a standard implementation in commercial or open-source GIS software, and constructing a consistent contour-flowline network from raw elevation data is technically challenging. It remains a research tool rather than a practical data structure.

### 5.1.4 Why the Grid Wins for Hydrology

The dominance of the regular grid in hydrological GIS is not accidental. It reflects a convergence of practical factors that overwhelmingly favor this representation.

The algorithms for hydrological terrain analysis -- computing flow direction, accumulating flow, delineating watersheds, extracting stream networks -- were developed specifically for regular grids during the 1980s and 1990s. The foundational work of O'Callaghan and Mark (1984), Jenson and Domingue (1988), and Tarboton (1997) all assumed a regular grid structure. These algorithms are simple, fast, and well-tested. Decades of use in thousands of studies have identified their limitations and produced effective workarounds (such as pit-filling and stream burning, discussed in Chapter 9). Equivalent algorithms for TINs exist but are less mature, less widely implemented, and less thoroughly validated.

Data availability reinforces the grid's dominance. The major global DEMs -- SRTM, Copernicus DEM, ASTER GDEM -- are all distributed as regular grids. Weather model output, satellite-derived precipitation, soil property maps, and land cover classifications are all gridded products. A workflow built on regular grids can ingest these data products directly, without format conversion.

Finally, the explosion in computing power since the 1990s has largely eliminated the grid's main disadvantage. When a 30-meter DEM of a small watershed consumed most of a workstation's memory, the storage efficiency of TINs was a compelling argument. Today, a standard laptop can handle a 10-meter DEM of an entire country. The computational overhead of uniform-resolution grids is simply not a constraint for most practical applications.

Throughout this book, when we say "raster" or "grid," we mean a regular grid of square cells unless otherwise specified. This is the data structure that underlies virtually all the analyses in Chapters 6 through 16.

## 5.2 Anatomy of a Grid

A grid is deceptively simple in concept but requires precise specification. Six elements define a grid data structure completely, and understanding each is essential for working with raster data correctly.

### 5.2.1 Extent

The **extent** is the total geographic area covered by the grid, defined by its bounding coordinates: the minimum and maximum values in both the x (east-west) and y (north-south) directions. In practice, this is often specified as four values: left (xmin), bottom (ymin), right (xmax), and top (ymax). The extent defines the "bounding box" of the grid -- the smallest rectangle in the coordinate system that contains all the grid cells.

For hydrological applications, the extent must be large enough to encompass the entire study area plus a buffer. If you are delineating a watershed, the DEM extent must extend beyond the watershed divide in all directions; otherwise, the flow direction algorithms cannot determine which cells drain inward toward your study area and which drain outward. A DEM that clips a watershed boundary introduces edge effects that can produce incorrect delineation results. A practical rule of thumb is to buffer the expected watershed boundary by at least 10-20 cell widths on all sides.

The extent also determines the coordinate system context. A grid whose extent spans from 400,000 to 500,000 in the x-direction and from 100,000 to 200,000 in the y-direction is clearly in a projected coordinate system with meters as units (this range is characteristic of the Hungarian EOV system, EPSG:23700). A grid spanning from 16.0 to 23.0 in x and 45.5 to 48.5 in y is in geographic coordinates (degrees) and could represent Hungary's extent in WGS84. Misidentifying the coordinate system of a raster is one of the most common errors in GIS work.

### 5.2.2 Cell Size and Spatial Resolution

The **cell size** (also called pixel size, grid spacing, or resolution) is the length of one side of a square grid cell, measured in the units of the grid's coordinate system. A grid in UTM coordinates with a cell size of 30 means each cell is 30 meters on a side, covering 900 square meters of ground. A grid in geographic coordinates with a cell size of 0.00083333 degrees (1/1200 of a degree, or 3 arc-seconds) has cells that are approximately 90 meters on a side at the equator, but narrower at higher latitudes due to the convergence of meridians.

Cell size is the single most important parameter of a grid for analytical purposes. It determines:

- **Spatial detail**: Features smaller than approximately one cell width cannot be resolved. A 30-meter DEM cannot represent a 10-meter-wide stream channel; the channel is narrower than a single cell. A 90-meter DEM cannot distinguish individual buildings in a floodplain.
- **Data volume**: The number of cells in a grid scales as the inverse square of the cell size. Halving the cell size quadruples the number of cells (and approximately quadruples the storage requirement and processing time). Going from 90-meter to 10-meter resolution increases the number of cells by a factor of 81.
- **Processing time**: Most raster operations visit every cell, so processing time is roughly proportional to the number of cells. Operations that examine cell neighborhoods (slope, flow direction) scale similarly.
- **Appropriateness for the phenomenon**: Precipitation fields vary on scales of kilometers; a 100-meter grid adds no real information compared to a 1-kilometer grid for representing a rainfall event. Stream channels may be only a few meters wide; a 100-meter grid cannot distinguish a stream from its surrounding floodplain.

The relationship between cell size and data volume has immediate practical consequences. Consider a DEM covering Hungary (approximately 93,000 km^2^). At 90-meter resolution, this DEM contains roughly 11.5 million cells -- a manageable dataset that fits in a single GeoTIFF of perhaps 50 MB. At 10-meter resolution, the same area requires approximately 930 million cells and a file size of several gigabytes. At 1-meter resolution (if such data existed nationwide, which it does not), the dataset would contain roughly 93 billion cells and require terabytes of storage. Each tenfold decrease in cell size produces a hundredfold increase in data volume.

### 5.2.3 Rows, Columns, and Cell Indexing

A grid is organized as a two-dimensional array with a defined number of **rows** (running east-west) and **columns** (running north-south). The total number of cells equals rows multiplied by columns. The relationship between extent, cell size, and dimensions is:

$$\text{ncols} = \frac{x_{\max} - x_{\min}}{\Delta x}, \quad \text{nrows} = \frac{y_{\max} - y_{\min}}{\Delta y}$$

where $\Delta x$ and $\Delta y$ are the cell sizes in the x and y directions (usually equal for square cells).

Cells are typically indexed from the upper-left corner, with row index increasing downward and column index increasing to the right. This convention matches the way computer memory stores two-dimensional arrays and the way image formats organize pixels, but it is the opposite of the mathematical convention where the y-axis increases upward. This can cause confusion when translating between grid coordinates (row, column) and geographic coordinates (easting, northing). The geographic coordinates of the center of cell $(i, j)$ are:

$$x = x_{\min} + (j + 0.5) \cdot \Delta x, \quad y = y_{\max} - (i + 0.5) \cdot \Delta y$$

where $i$ is the row index (starting from 0 at the top) and $j$ is the column index (starting from 0 at the left).

### 5.2.4 The Anchor Point

Every grid must be **anchored** to a specific location in the coordinate system. This is done by specifying the coordinates of a reference corner -- typically the lower-left corner (as in ASCII grid files) or the upper-left corner (as in GeoTIFF files). Combined with the cell size, this anchor point allows the GIS to compute the geographic coordinates of every cell in the grid.

The anchor point convention varies by format and software, which can create subtle alignment problems. If one dataset specifies its anchor at the lower-left corner and another at the upper-left corner, failing to account for this difference during overlay operations can result in a half-cell or full-cell shift between the two grids. Most modern GIS software handles these conversions transparently, but awareness of the issue is important when writing custom code or working with unfamiliar formats.

### 5.2.5 Data Type

The values stored in grid cells are of a specific **data type** that determines both the range of values that can be represented and the storage space required per cell. The two fundamental categories are floating-point and integer, each with important implications for how the grid is used and interpreted.

**Floating-point grids** store real numbers -- values with decimal places. They are used for continuous quantities: elevation (217.34 m), temperature (14.7 C), slope (0.0453 m/m), precipitation depth (52.3 mm). Floating-point values are typically stored as 32-bit (single precision) or 64-bit (double precision) numbers. A 32-bit float provides approximately 7 significant digits and can represent values from roughly $-3.4 \times 10^{38}$ to $+3.4 \times 10^{38}$. For virtually all hydrological applications, 32-bit precision is more than adequate -- the measurement uncertainty in any elevation or rainfall value far exceeds the numerical precision of a 32-bit float.

**Integer grids** store whole numbers and are used for categorical or classified data: land cover type (code 41 = Deciduous Forest), soil class (code 7 = Vertisol), watershed identifier (ID 1247), or flow direction (code 4 = East). Integer values may be signed or unsigned, and 8-bit, 16-bit, or 32-bit. An 8-bit unsigned integer can represent values from 0 to 255 -- sufficient for a classification with up to 256 categories. A 16-bit signed integer covers -32,768 to 32,767, which is adequate for most categorical schemes but also for integer-valued continuous data like elevation in meters for most of the world's land surface.

The choice of data type has significant storage implications. A 32-bit floating-point grid requires 4 bytes per cell; a 16-bit integer grid requires only 2 bytes per cell. For a DEM of Hungary at 10-meter resolution (930 million cells), the difference is 3.7 GB versus 1.9 GB -- meaningful but not dramatic on modern hardware. However, at 1-meter resolution, the difference becomes 372 GB versus 186 GB, which matters considerably.

### 5.2.6 NODATA: The Value That Means Nothing

Every grid format reserves a specific value to indicate **NODATA** -- cells where no valid measurement exists. Common NODATA values include -9999, -3.4028235 x 10^38^ (the minimum 32-bit float), 0, or -32768 (the minimum 16-bit signed integer). The NODATA value is stored in the grid's metadata and tells the GIS software to treat cells with that value as absent rather than as carrying meaningful information.

NODATA cells serve several essential functions in hydrological raster work.

**Irregular study areas within rectangular extents.** A grid is always rectangular, but watersheds, countries, and study areas are irregularly shaped. Cells outside the study area but within the bounding rectangle are assigned NODATA. When you clip a continental DEM to a watershed boundary, the cells outside the watershed become NODATA, ensuring they do not participate in subsequent analyses like flow accumulation or zonal statistics.

**Missing measurements.** Satellite-derived DEMs may have gaps where radar shadows, steep slopes, or water surfaces prevented a valid measurement. The original SRTM dataset had numerous voids in mountainous terrain (the Himalayas, the Andes, the Alps), which were filled in subsequent processing versions. These filled areas are sometimes flagged in ancillary quality layers, but in the DEM itself, the original voids appeared as NODATA.

**Water bodies and oceans.** Some DEMs assign NODATA to ocean areas or large inland water bodies, since the radar or optical sensor measures the water surface rather than the underlying terrain. Others assign a constant value (such as 0 for sea level) and leave NODATA only for true data gaps.

**Computational exclusion.** In raster analysis, NODATA propagates through calculations. If any input cell to a map algebra operation is NODATA, the output cell is typically NODATA as well (Chapter 6, Section 6.7 discusses NODATA propagation rules and strategies for managing them). This prevents meaningless results from contaminating analyses. For example, if you compute the average elevation within a zone and some cells are NODATA, those cells are excluded from the average rather than dragging it toward -9999.

A critical practical pitfall involves inadvertent NODATA contamination. If a grid's NODATA value is set to -9999 but the actual data contains legitimate values of -9999 (unlikely for elevation, but conceivable for some derived products), those cells will be incorrectly treated as missing. Conversely, if the NODATA value is not properly recorded in the metadata, cells that should be excluded will be treated as valid data, potentially introducing severe errors -- imagine computing the mean elevation of a watershed and including cells with value -9999 in the average.

## 5.3 What Does a Cell Value Mean?

This question seems simple but conceals a subtlety that affects the correctness of many raster analyses. When a grid cell contains the value 217.3, does that mean the average elevation across the entire 900 square meters of the cell is 217.3 meters? Or does it mean the elevation at the exact center point of the cell is 217.3 meters, with the actual elevation varying across the cell? These two interpretations lead to different analytical approaches and different error characteristics.

### 5.3.1 Three Interpretations

Michael Goodchild (1997) formalized three possible interpretations of raster cell values:

**Interpretation 1: Spatial average over the cell footprint.** The value represents the mean of the quantity across the entire area of the cell. Under this interpretation, the cell value is a spatial average, and the **support** of each measurement equals the cell area. This interpretation is natural for quantities derived by aggregation: the total precipitation falling on a grid cell, the average land surface temperature measured by a satellite sensor whose instantaneous field of view matches the cell size, or the average reflectance of a pixel in a multispectral image.

**Interpretation 2: Point sample at the cell center.** The value represents a measurement taken at a single point -- the center of the cell. The quantity may vary freely within the cell, but only the center value is recorded. Under this interpretation, the support is effectively a point, and the spacing equals the cell size. This interpretation is appropriate for data derived from point measurements or from high-resolution sources that have been resampled to a coarser grid. A DEM created by sampling a high-resolution terrain model at regular intervals follows this interpretation.

**Interpretation 3: Value at grid nodes (cell corners).** The value is associated with the corners (nodes) of the grid cells rather than the centers. This is equivalent to Interpretation 2 with a half-cell spatial shift. It is less common in GIS but appears in some numerical modeling frameworks where variables are defined on a staggered grid.

### 5.3.2 Why the Interpretation Matters

The interpretation determines which **resampling method** is appropriate when changing the grid's cell size or reprojecting it to a different coordinate system.

If the values represent spatial averages (Interpretation 1), then resampling to a finer grid should use a method that preserves the local average -- bilinear interpolation or cubic convolution are appropriate because they create smooth transitions between cells, which is consistent with the idea that the value represents an area-wide average.

If the values are point samples (Interpretation 2), then nearest-neighbor resampling -- which simply assigns each output cell the value of the nearest input cell center -- preserves the original point values without introducing artificial intermediate values. This is essential for categorical data (land cover, soil type) where interpolated intermediate values are meaningless: there is no physical meaning to a land cover value of 21.5, midway between "Developed, Open Space" (code 21) and "Developed, Low Intensity" (code 22).

The interpretation also affects how we assess accuracy. Under Interpretation 1, the cell value should be compared against the spatial average of the true field over the cell area -- a quantity that is itself difficult to measure. Under Interpretation 2, the cell value should be compared against the true field value at the cell center -- a point measurement that is straightforward to obtain with a GPS and a total station, for instance.

For DEMs specifically, the interpretation is often ambiguous. A DEM derived from contour interpolation may best fit Interpretation 1: the gridding algorithm effectively averages nearby contour values over the cell area. A DEM derived from LiDAR, where a statistical algorithm selects a representative value from the hundreds of returns within each cell, is closer to Interpretation 1 as well but with a more complex averaging process. A DEM created by evaluating a mathematical surface model at regular grid points follows Interpretation 2. In practice, the distinction matters most at coarse resolutions. At 1-meter resolution, the difference between the spatial average and the center-point value across a 1 square meter cell is negligible. At 90-meter resolution, the difference can be significant in rugged terrain, where elevation may vary by tens of meters across a single cell.

### 5.3.3 A Worked Example: Resampling Matters

Consider a 30-meter DEM cell in a river valley with a center elevation of 112.4 meters. The true elevation within this cell ranges from 108.2 meters (the channel bottom) to 115.8 meters (the bank top). The spatial average across the cell is approximately 112.0 meters.

If we resample this DEM to 10-meter resolution using bilinear interpolation, the resulting finer cells will have smoothly varying values between 112.4 and the values of neighboring cells. This may produce reasonable results for the upland cells but will fail to recover the channel bottom: the 10-meter cells in the channel area will still show values near 112 meters rather than the true 108.2 meters. Resampling to a finer grid does not create new information -- it merely interpolates the existing coarse values.

If we resample using nearest-neighbor, the nine 10-meter cells within the original 30-meter cell will all receive the same value of 112.4 meters. The result is a staircase pattern that preserves the original values but introduces artificial discontinuities at the boundaries of the original cells.

Neither approach recovers information that was lost when the field was sampled at 30-meter resolution. This is a fundamental principle: **you cannot increase the information content of a raster by resampling it to a finer grid**. Finer resolution requires finer source data. This principle has direct consequences for the cell size reconciliation process described in Chapter 6, Section 6.4, where grids at different resolutions must be aligned before map algebra operations can proceed.

## 5.4 Representing Vector Features on a Grid

Although grids are most naturally associated with continuous fields, they can also represent features that are conceptually vector objects: points, lines, and polygons. This capability is essential for hydrological GIS, where we often need to overlay gridded terrain data with features like stream networks, gauge locations, or watershed boundaries.

### 5.4.1 Points, Lines, and Polygons as Raster Cells

A **point** feature is represented by a single grid cell assigned a designated value. A stream gauge location, for example, might be encoded in a raster by assigning the cell containing the gauge a value of 1 (or the gauge's identifier) and all other cells a value of NODATA or 0. The accuracy of this representation depends on cell size: at 30-meter resolution, the gauge is located to within 30 meters of its true position, which is adequate for watershed-scale analysis but inadequate for engineering-scale work.

A **line** feature is represented by a sequence of connected cells tracing the path of the line. A river, for instance, becomes a chain of cells running through the grid from one end to the other. This rasterization necessarily introduces some geometric distortion: diagonal lines become staircases, smooth curves become angular approximations, and the width of the rasterized line is always at least one cell wide regardless of the true feature width. For hydrological applications, this means a rasterized stream network has an implicit minimum width equal to the cell size. At 30-meter resolution, every stream is at least 30 meters wide in the raster -- a poor approximation for a small headwater channel that may be only a meter across, but acceptable for the large rivers that are the focus of watershed-scale analysis.

A **polygon** feature is represented by a contiguous zone of cells sharing the same value. A watershed boundary becomes a region of cells all assigned the watershed's identifier. The jagged cell boundaries approximate the smooth polygon boundary, with the approximation improving as cell size decreases. For categorical rasters like land cover or soil type, the entire dataset consists of polygon zones represented this way: each cell's integer value indicates which category (and thus which polygon in the original classification) it belongs to.

### 5.4.2 Generalization Rules for Vector-to-Raster Conversion

When converting vector features to raster format, decisions must be made about how to assign values to cells that straddle feature boundaries. Two common rules govern this process.

The **center point rule** assigns each cell the value of whichever polygon contains the cell's center point. This is simple and fast to compute. However, it can produce counterintuitive results: a cell whose center happens to fall in a narrow sliver of one polygon will be assigned that polygon's value even if 90% of the cell's area belongs to a different polygon. Small features that never contain any cell center will be entirely absent from the rasterized result.

The **largest share rule** (also called the majority rule) assigns each cell the value of whichever polygon occupies the largest fraction of the cell's area. This produces a more representative result -- a cell mostly covered by forest will be classified as forest, even if its center happens to fall in a small clearing. However, the computation is more expensive because it requires calculating the fractional area of each polygon within each cell.

In practice, the center point rule is the default in most GIS software because it is fast and deterministic. For hydrological applications where polygon boundaries are critical -- such as watershed delineation or land cover mapping for runoff estimation -- understanding which rule was applied is important for assessing the accuracy of the rasterized result.

## 5.5 The ASCII Grid Format: A Grid You Can Read

Before examining more sophisticated raster formats, it is instructive to study the simplest one: the ASCII grid file (extension `.asc`). This is a plain text file that a human can read, edit, and create with nothing more than a text editor. Its transparency makes it an excellent teaching tool and a useful interchange format for small datasets.

### 5.5.1 Structure of an ASCII Grid File

An ASCII grid file consists of a header followed by the grid values:

```
ncols         6
nrows         4
xllcorner     470000
yllcorner     175000
cellsize      30
NODATA_value  -9999
215.4  217.8  220.1  223.5  225.0  -9999
210.2  213.6  216.3  219.7  222.4  224.8
205.1  207.5  210.8  214.2  217.6  220.3
200.3  202.7  205.4  208.8  -9999  -9999
```

The six header lines specify:

- `ncols` and `nrows`: the grid dimensions (6 columns, 4 rows)
- `xllcorner` and `yllcorner`: the coordinates of the lower-left corner (in this example, the values 470,000 and 175,000 are in the range characteristic of the Hungarian EOV coordinate system)
- `cellsize`: the length of one cell side (30 meters)
- `NODATA_value`: the code used for missing data (-9999)

The body contains the grid values row by row, from top to bottom (north to south). Note that the last column of the first row and the last two cells of the bottom row are NODATA, indicating an irregularly shaped study area within the rectangular grid.

### 5.5.2 Advantages and Limitations

The ASCII grid format has several practical advantages. It is human-readable: you can open it in any text editor to inspect values, verify the header, or hand-edit cells. It is universally supported: every GIS software package (ArcGIS, QGIS, GRASS, SAGA, R, Python) can read and write this format. It is programming-friendly: parsing the header and reading the values is a trivial exercise in any programming language. And it is self-documenting: the header explicitly states the geographic reference information, so there is no ambiguity about cell size, origin, or NODATA encoding.

The format's limitations become apparent with larger datasets. Because every value is stored as text (a character string of digits), the storage is highly inefficient. The value 215.4 requires 5 bytes as text but only 4 bytes as a 32-bit float. For large grids, ASCII files are 3-10 times larger than equivalent binary formats. Reading and writing text files is also much slower than reading binary files, because the computer must convert each text string to a number (parsing). For a DEM of Hungary at 30-meter resolution (~100 million cells), an ASCII grid file would be approximately 1 GB in size and take minutes to read, whereas the equivalent GeoTIFF with compression would be perhaps 200 MB and load in seconds.

Despite these limitations, the ASCII grid remains valuable as an exchange format, a debugging tool, and a way to create small test datasets for algorithm development. The ability to simply type grid values into a text file and import them into a GIS is a capability that no binary format can match.

### 5.5.3 A Practical Exercise: Building a Grid by Hand

To build intuition about grid data structures, consider constructing a simple ASCII grid representing a cross-section of a river valley. Imagine a valley running north-south, with the channel at the center and banks rising on either side:

```
ncols         7
nrows         5
xllcorner     0
yllcorner     0
cellsize      10
NODATA_value  -9999
120.0  115.0  108.0  104.0  108.0  115.0  120.0
120.5  115.5  108.5  103.5  108.5  115.5  120.5
121.0  116.0  109.0  103.0  109.0  116.0  121.0
120.5  115.5  108.5  103.5  108.5  115.5  120.5
120.0  115.0  108.0  104.0  108.0  115.0  120.0
```

This tiny 7-by-5 grid encodes a symmetric valley profile: the outer columns (elevation ~120 m) represent the upland, the intermediate columns (~108-116 m) represent the valley walls, and the central column (~103-104 m) represents the channel bottom. The slight elevation decrease from row 1 to row 3 and back up to row 5 gives the channel a gentle longitudinal gradient, with the lowest point (103.0 m) at the center of the grid. Even in this toy example, the grid structure captures the essential geometry of a river valley: the cross-sectional profile, the longitudinal slope, and the relationship between the channel and its floodplain.

This is, in miniature, exactly what a DEM does for a real landscape. A 30-meter DEM of the Tisza River floodplain in eastern Hungary does the same thing at a vastly larger scale -- hundreds of thousands of cells encoding the subtle topography of levees, oxbow lakes, floodplain depressions, and the river channel itself.

## 5.6 Floating-Point and Integer Grids

The distinction between floating-point and integer grids is not merely a technical detail about data storage. It reflects a fundamental difference in the kind of information the grid represents, and this difference has important consequences for how the grid can be analyzed, displayed, and interpreted.

### 5.6.1 Floating-Point Grids: Continuous Quantities

A floating-point grid stores real numbers (numbers with decimal places) and represents a continuous quantity that varies smoothly across space. The prototypical example is elevation: a DEM stores the height of the terrain at each cell, and these heights change gradually from cell to cell across the landscape.

Other hydrological quantities naturally represented as floating-point grids include:

- **Slope**: the steepness of the terrain at each cell, typically expressed as a ratio (rise/run), percent, or degrees. Values are continuous and can take any non-negative real value.
- **Precipitation**: the depth of rainfall accumulated over a time period, varying continuously across the landscape. A radar-derived precipitation grid might have values from 0.0 to 150.0 mm.
- **Temperature**: air or surface temperature, varying continuously. An ERA5 grid cell might hold a value like 14.73 degrees Celsius.
- **Flow accumulation**: the number of upstream cells draining through each cell. Although conceptually an integer (you cannot have 3.7 cells), flow accumulation is often stored as a float to accommodate very large values and weighted accumulations.
- **HAND (Height Above Nearest Drainage)**: the vertical distance from each cell to the nearest stream channel, used in flood mapping (Chapter 14). Values are continuous and non-negative.

Floating-point grids do not have a Value Attribute Table (VAT) because every cell potentially holds a unique value. There is no finite set of categories to tabulate. Instead, floating-point grids are typically visualized using a color ramp -- a continuous mapping from data values to colors, such as blue for low elevations through green, yellow, and brown for high elevations.

### 5.6.2 Integer Grids: Categories and Zones

An integer grid stores whole numbers and typically represents categorical or classified data, where each integer value is a code for a specific category. The prototypical example is a land cover map, where integer codes identify land cover types: 11 = Open Water, 21 = Developed Open Space, 41 = Deciduous Forest, 82 = Cultivated Crops, and so on.

Integer grids can have an associated **Value Attribute Table (VAT)** -- a lookup table that provides additional information about each integer value. The VAT contains one row per unique value in the grid, along with the count of cells having that value and any number of additional attribute fields.

**Table 5.2**: Example Value Attribute Table for a CORINE Land Cover grid over a Hungarian watershed

| Value | Count | CLC Code | Land Cover Type | Runoff Coefficient |
|-------|-------|----------|----------------|--------------------|
| 1 | 42,310 | 211 | Non-irrigated arable land | 0.35 |
| 2 | 18,750 | 311 | Broad-leaved forest | 0.15 |
| 3 | 12,400 | 112 | Discontinuous urban fabric | 0.65 |
| 4 | 8,200 | 512 | Water bodies | 1.00 |
| 5 | 5,100 | 231 | Pastures | 0.25 |
| 6 | 3,800 | 321 | Natural grasslands | 0.20 |

In this example, the grid itself contains only the integers 1 through 6, but the VAT provides the CORINE classification code, a descriptive label, and even a derived hydrological parameter (runoff coefficient) for each category. This is the power of integer grids: the simple integer serves as a key into an arbitrarily rich attribute table, just as a feature ID in a vector dataset links to its attribute row.

The VAT mechanism is fundamental to hydrological applications of land cover data. When estimating runoff from a watershed, you need to assign a runoff coefficient (or a curve number, or an infiltration rate) to each land cover type. With an integer grid and a VAT, this is a simple table lookup: reclassify the land cover grid using the runoff coefficient column, and every cell in the watershed receives the appropriate hydrological parameter based on its land cover type.

### 5.6.3 The Hybrid Case: Flow Direction

Some grids blur the boundary between floating-point and integer representations. The D8 flow direction grid (covered in detail in Chapter 10) is an integer grid where each cell holds one of eight values (1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64, 128) representing the eight possible flow directions from a cell to its neighbors. These are not categories in the land-cover sense -- they represent a directional quantity -- but they are stored as integers because there are exactly eight possible values. The D-infinity flow direction scheme (Tarboton, 1997), by contrast, stores the flow angle as a floating-point value between 0 and $2\pi$, allowing flow to be distributed between neighboring cells in proportion to the angle.

This example illustrates an important principle: the choice between floating-point and integer representation is not just about storage efficiency. It reflects a modeling decision about the nature of the quantity being represented. D8 says "water flows in exactly one of eight directions." D-infinity says "water flows in a continuous range of directions." The choice of data type encodes an assumption about the world.

## 5.7 The Scale Triplet: A Framework for Understanding Raster Data

In 1995, the Austrian hydrologist Gunter Bloschl formalized a conceptual framework that belongs in the toolkit of every water professional working with spatial data. The **scale triplet** identifies three fundamental properties that characterize any dataset's relationship to the phenomenon it represents: **extent**, **spacing**, and **support**. Understanding these three properties is essential for assessing whether a dataset is appropriate for a given analysis and for interpreting results correctly.

### 5.7.1 Extent

The **extent** is the total length, area, or duration covered by the data. For a spatial dataset, extent is the geographic domain: the area of the map, the bounds of the DEM, the region covered by rain gauges. For a temporal dataset, extent is the period of record: the length of the time series.

Extent determines the scale of phenomena that can be studied. A rain gauge network covering a 100-km^2^ watershed can characterize precipitation patterns at the watershed scale but cannot say anything about continental rainfall trends. A DEM covering a single hillslope provides excellent local terrain information but cannot support regional flood mapping. Inferences are reliable only within the extent of the data; extrapolating beyond the extent introduces substantial uncertainty.

A common analytical error is to draw conclusions that exceed the extent of the data. If you have 20 years of streamflow data and observe an apparent upward trend, you cannot conclude that flow is increasing over centuries -- your 20-year window may capture only part of a longer cycle. Similarly, if your DEM covers only the main valley and not the headwater ridges, you cannot compute the true watershed area.

### 5.7.2 Spacing

The **spacing** is the distance or time interval between successive data values. For a regular grid, the spacing equals the cell size. For a gauge network, the spacing is the average distance between neighboring gauges. For a time series, the spacing is the measurement interval (e.g., 15 minutes, 1 hour, 1 day).

Spacing determines the level of spatial or temporal detail that can be resolved. The **Nyquist sampling theorem** from signal processing tells us that to faithfully capture a feature, we need at least two samples per wavelength -- in spatial terms, at least two cells per feature width. A 30-meter grid can resolve features wider than about 60 meters; features smaller than this will be aliased -- misrepresented or entirely invisible.

**Aliasing** is a critical concept for hydrological raster work. Consider a stream channel 15 meters wide flowing through a landscape represented by a 30-meter DEM. The channel is narrower than one cell, so it cannot appear in the DEM as a distinct topographic feature. The cell containing the channel will hold a single elevation value that blends the channel bottom with the surrounding floodplain, effectively hiding the channel's existence. This is not a flaw in the DEM -- it is a fundamental consequence of the spacing being too coarse relative to the feature size. Resolving this channel requires a finer DEM (e.g., a 5-meter DDM-5 from Lechner in Hungary, or a LiDAR-derived DEM at 1-meter resolution).

The implications for precipitation data are equally important. A rain gauge network with an average spacing of 50 km cannot resolve convective storms that may be only 5-10 km in diameter. The storm may pass between gauges entirely undetected, or be captured by a single gauge while its spatial structure remains unknown. This is one motivation for radar-based and satellite-based precipitation estimation, which provide much finer spatial spacing (1-5 km for radar, 5-10 km for satellite products like GPM/IMERG).

### 5.7.3 Support

The **support** is the area, volume, or duration over which each individual measurement is representative -- the "footprint" of a single data value. For a grid cell interpreted as a spatial average (Interpretation 1 in Section 5.3), the support equals the cell area. For a point measurement, the support is effectively zero. For a rain gauge, the support in space is the area of the gauge orifice (about 200 cm^2^), while the support in time is the accumulation interval (e.g., the previous 5 minutes or 24 hours).

Support determines the degree of smoothing inherent in the data. Large support means the measurement averages out small-scale variability, producing a smoother representation. A grid cell 1 km on a side averages elevation across a million square meters, smoothing out hills, valleys, and channels that would be visible at finer resolution. A satellite pixel 10 km on a side averages precipitation over 100 km^2^, smoothing out the intense core and light edges of a storm cell.

When support is large relative to spacing, the resulting data is over-smoothed: individual measurements overlap and are not independent. This can create a false impression of smooth gradients where the true field may be highly variable. When support is small relative to spacing (as with point measurements on a sparse network), the data is under-sampled: the behavior between measurement points is unknown, and interpolation is required.

### 5.7.4 The Scale Triplet in Practice: A Worked Example

Consider three precipitation datasets available for a watershed in Hungary:

**Table 5.3**: Scale triplet comparison for precipitation datasets covering Hungary

| Property | Rain Gauge Network (OVF) | ERA5 Reanalysis (ECMWF) | GPM/IMERG Satellite |
|----------|-------------------------|------------------------|-------------------|
| Extent (space) | Hungary (~93,000 km^2^) | Global | 67S-67N latitude |
| Extent (time) | ~100 years (since 1901) | 1940-present | 2000-present |
| Spacing (space) | ~20-30 km (avg. between gauges) | ~31 km (0.25 deg) | ~10 km (0.1 deg) |
| Spacing (time) | 10 min to daily | 1 hour | 30 min |
| Support (space) | ~200 cm^2^ (gauge orifice) | ~961 km^2^ (grid cell) | ~100 km^2^ (pixel) |
| Support (time) | Accumulation interval | 1 hour | 30 min |

These three datasets measure the same phenomenon -- precipitation over Hungary -- but with vastly different scale triplets, and this leads to different strengths:

- The **gauge network** has the longest temporal extent (essential for trend analysis and return period estimation) and the finest temporal support when operated at 10-minute intervals, but its spatial support is nearly zero -- each gauge measures precipitation at a single point, and the spatial field must be interpolated. Convective storms between gauges may be missed entirely.

- **ERA5** has the finest spatial support in a sense: each grid cell value represents the physically consistent average precipitation over a 961-km^2^ area, as computed by a numerical weather model constrained by observations. This large support means ERA5 smooths out small-scale precipitation variability but provides a complete, gap-free spatial field. ERA5 is excellent for large-basin water balance studies but cannot resolve individual thunderstorms.

- **GPM/IMERG** provides the finest spatial spacing and the finest temporal spacing, capturing the spatial structure of storms that gauge networks miss. However, satellite precipitation estimates have larger uncertainties than gauges, particularly for light precipitation and in mountainous terrain, and the record begins only in 2000.

No single dataset is "best." The appropriate choice depends on the analysis -- the scale triplet must match the scale of the phenomenon being studied and the requirements of the application. This is Bloschl's key insight: data quality is not a single number but a multi-dimensional concept. Chapter 8 revisits this framework in the context of precipitation interpolation, where the choice between gauge data, radar products, and satellite estimates depends directly on how well each dataset's scale triplet matches the hydrological question.

### 5.7.5 Aliasing, Apparent Trends, and Smoothing

The scale triplet helps diagnose three common pitfalls in spatial data analysis:

**Aliasing from coarse spacing.** If the spacing is too coarse relative to the spatial frequency of the phenomenon, high-frequency features are not just missing -- they may be misrepresented. A temperature field sampled only at noon and midnight will miss the actual daily maximum and minimum, and the reconstructed daily cycle may appear to have a different phase than the true cycle. In spatial terms, a DEM with 90-meter cells may show a broad, gentle valley where the true terrain has a narrow, incised channel flanked by steep banks. The DEM does not merely smooth the terrain -- it potentially distorts the shape of features that are near the resolution limit.

**Apparent trends from limited extent.** If the extent of the data captures only part of a cycle, the data may appear to show a monotonic trend. Monitoring river discharge from January to June in a snowmelt-driven basin would show steadily increasing flow, suggesting a trend. Extending the record through the full annual cycle reveals the seasonal pattern. This pitfall is particularly relevant for climate studies: a 30-year precipitation record may show an apparent increase that is actually part of a multi-decadal oscillation visible only in the 100-year gauge record.

**Smoothing from large support.** When the support is large relative to the spacing, the data is smoother than the true field. This is not always a problem -- for some applications, the smoothed field is exactly what is needed (e.g., basin-average precipitation for a lumped hydrological model). But it can be misleading if the analyst expects the data to capture local extremes. The peak rainfall intensity during a thunderstorm, as measured by a single rain gauge, may be 80 mm/h. The same storm, as represented in a 30-km ERA5 grid cell, may show only 15 mm/h because the intense core occupies only a fraction of the cell area. Both measurements are "correct" given their support, but they tell very different stories about the storm.

## 5.8 Digital Elevation Models: The Raster That Drives Hydrology

Of all the raster datasets used in water resources work, the digital elevation model holds a special position. The DEM is the starting point for nearly every terrain-based hydrological analysis: computing slope, determining flow direction, delineating watersheds, extracting stream networks, and mapping flood inundation. The quality, resolution, and accuracy of the DEM propagate through every subsequent analysis. An error in the DEM -- a pit where there should be a ridge, a flat area where there should be a slope -- produces errors in flow direction, which produce errors in watershed boundaries, which produce errors in flood maps. The DEM is the foundation on which the entire analytical structure rests.

### 5.8.1 What a DEM Is and Is Not

A digital elevation model is a floating-point raster grid where each cell value represents the elevation of the ground surface at that location, referenced to a vertical datum. The key phrase is "ground surface." A DEM ideally represents the bare earth -- the terrain stripped of buildings, vegetation, and other surface objects. In practice, many widely used "DEMs" are actually digital surface models (DSMs) that include the tops of buildings, tree canopies, and other above-ground features. The distinction matters enormously for hydrology: a DSM shows the top of a forest canopy at 25 meters above the true ground, which would completely distort flow direction calculations in a forested valley.

The SRTM dataset, for instance, is technically a DSM because the radar signal reflects off the first surface it encounters -- the tree canopy in forests, the building rooftops in cities. The Copernicus DEM (derived from TanDEM-X) is also a DSM, though its processing pipeline includes some vegetation removal. Only LiDAR-derived products and contour-interpolated DEMs are true bare-earth DEMs, because LiDAR can penetrate vegetation canopy (Chapter 13 discusses this in detail) and contour maps were traditionally drawn to represent the ground surface.

Developed by Dai Yamazaki at the University of Tokyo, MERIT removes major error components from existing spaceborne DEMs -- stripe noise, absolute bias, speckle noise, and tree height bias -- producing a "cleaner" elevation surface at 90-meter resolution. For continental and global hydrological modeling, MERIT has become the preferred DEM because its error correction directly improves the quality of derived products like flow direction and watershed boundaries.

### 5.8.3 Vertical Datums and the Geoid

DEM elevation values are only meaningful relative to a specified vertical datum -- a reference surface from which heights are measured. Two types of vertical datum are in common use:

**Orthometric heights** (also called heights above mean sea level) are referenced to the geoid, an equipotential surface of Earth's gravity field that closely approximates mean sea level. These are the heights shown on topographic maps and used in everyday life. Hungary's official vertical datum is the Baltic Height System (also called Kronstadt 1902, EPSG:5787), defined by the average water level at the Kronstadt tide gauge near St. Petersburg. The United States uses NAVD88, referenced to a tide gauge at Point Rimouski in Quebec, Canada.

**Ellipsoidal heights** are referenced to a mathematical ellipsoid (such as WGS84) and differ from orthometric heights by the geoid undulation -- the vertical distance between the ellipsoid and the geoid at that location. GPS receivers natively measure ellipsoidal heights, which must be converted to orthometric heights using a geoid model.

Most DEMs used in hydrological analysis provide orthometric heights referenced to the EGM96 or EGM2008 geoid model. The Copernicus DEM uses EGM2008. SRTM originally used EGM96 but has been converted to EGM2008 in most distribution channels. The DDM-5 in Hungary uses the Baltic Height System. When combining DEMs from different sources, verifying that they use the same vertical reference is essential to avoid systematic elevation biases.

In Hungary, the geoid undulation (the difference between the WGS84 ellipsoid and the Baltic vertical datum) varies from approximately 40 to 46 meters across the country. If a GPS-measured ellipsoidal height is not corrected for this undulation, the resulting "elevation" will be 40-46 meters too high -- a massive error for any hydrological application.

## 5.9 Cell Size and Resolution: The Trade-Off That Shapes Every Analysis

Choosing the right cell size for a raster analysis is one of the most important decisions in any hydrological GIS project. Too coarse, and critical features disappear. Too fine, and the data volume becomes unmanageable without adding useful information. The optimal choice depends on the application, the phenomena being studied, the available source data, and the computational resources at hand.

### 5.9.1 The Resolution-Information Paradox

A seductive but incorrect intuition holds that finer resolution is always better. In reality, the information content of a raster is determined by the source data, not by the cell size. Resampling a 90-meter SRTM DEM to 10-meter cells does not create a 10-meter DEM -- it creates a 10-meter grid whose information content remains at 90 meters. The 10-meter cells hold interpolated values that fill in the gaps between the original 90-meter samples, but these interpolated values are predictions, not measurements. The true terrain may be completely different from the interpolated surface at the sub-90-meter scale.

This principle has a practical corollary: **the cell size of a derived product should not be finer than the cell size of the coarsest input.** If you compute a flow direction grid from a 90-meter DEM, the result has 90-meter resolution regardless of the cell size you specify in the output. Setting the output cell size to 10 meters creates nine output cells for each input cell, but all nine cells carry information from the same single 90-meter measurement. The computation is nine times slower and the file is nine times larger, with no improvement in accuracy.

### 5.9.2 Matching Resolution to Application

Different hydrological applications have different resolution requirements, driven by the spatial scale of the phenomena being analyzed.

**Continental and global hydrology** (large river basins, climate impact assessment, water balance studies): 90-meter to 1-kilometer resolution is typically appropriate. At these scales, the analysis focuses on major drainage patterns, basin-average quantities, and large-scale spatial gradients. The Copernicus GLO-90, MERIT DEM, and ERA5-Land (9 km) are standard data sources.

**Regional watershed analysis** (medium-sized basins of 100-10,000 km^2^, watershed delineation, stream network extraction, land cover analysis): 10- to 30-meter resolution is the sweet spot. The Copernicus GLO-30 or EEA-10, SRTM 30m, and the Hungarian DDM-5 (5 m) serve this scale well. Most of the watershed delineation workflows described in Chapters 9-11 operate at this resolution.

**Local flood mapping and engineering design** (individual river reaches, urban drainage, infrastructure siting): 1- to 5-meter resolution is needed to resolve levees, buildings, bridge decks, and channel cross-sections. LiDAR-derived DEMs are the standard source. In Hungary, LiDAR coverage is fragmented (available for specific areas like the Aggtelek Karst and certain flood-prone valleys), and project-specific surveys may be needed.

**Detailed channel hydraulics** (bridge scour analysis, habitat mapping, erosion studies): sub-meter resolution is required, typically from dense LiDAR surveys or bathymetric surveys combined with topographic LiDAR.

| Continental flood modeling | 90-250 m | Copernicus GLO-90, MERIT |
| Watershed delineation | 10-30 m | Copernicus GLO-30, DDM-5, SRTM |
| Slope and aspect mapping | 10-30 m | Copernicus EEA-10, DDM-5 |
| Urban flood mapping | 1-5 m | LiDAR DEM |
| Floodplain hydraulic modeling | 1-5 m | LiDAR DEM |
| Infrastructure design | <1 m | Survey-grade LiDAR or total station |

### 5.9.3 A Worked Example: How Cell Size Affects Watershed Area

To illustrate the practical impact of cell size on hydrological results, consider the delineation of a small watershed (approximately 50 km^2^) from DEMs of different resolutions. As cell size increases, the watershed boundary becomes more generalized: fine-scale ridgeline details are lost, and the boundary shifts slightly as the coarser DEM smooths the topography.

Studies have shown that watershed area computed from a 90-meter DEM may differ by 5-15% from the area computed from a 10-meter DEM for small watersheds (under 100 km^2^), with the discrepancy increasing as watershed size decreases. For very small catchments (under 10 km^2^), the error can exceed 20%. This is because the watershed divide is a ridgeline feature whose width approaches the cell size of a coarse DEM, and small shifts in the gridded representation of the ridgeline translate to significant changes in watershed area.

For larger watersheds (over 1,000 km^2^), the effect diminishes because the ridgeline perturbations are small relative to the total perimeter. The watershed area of the Tisza River basin (approximately 157,000 km^2^) computed from a 90-meter DEM will agree with the 10-meter result to within a fraction of a percent.

The practical lesson: use the finest available resolution for small watersheds, and accept coarser resolutions for large basins where the computational savings are significant and the accuracy impact is minimal.

## 5.10 Modern Raster Formats

The ASCII grid described in Section 5.5 is useful for teaching but impractical for operational work. Modern hydrological GIS relies on binary raster formats that are compact, fast to read, and capable of storing rich metadata. The format landscape has evolved significantly, and a 2026-era water professional should be familiar with at least four major formats.

### 5.10.1 GeoTIFF

**GeoTIFF** is the most widely used raster format in geospatial science. It extends the TIFF (Tagged Image File Format) by embedding georeferencing information -- coordinate system, cell size, extent, and datum -- directly in the file's header as special "GeoKeys." This makes a GeoTIFF a self-contained, self-describing spatial dataset that can be opened by any GIS software, programming language, or remote sensing tool.

The advantages of GeoTIFF for hydrological work are numerous. It supports all data types (8-bit through 64-bit integer, 32-bit and 64-bit float). It supports internal compression (LZW, Deflate, ZSTD) that can reduce file sizes by 50-80% without any loss of data. It supports tiling -- dividing the image into square blocks that can be read independently, enabling efficient access to small areas of a large dataset without reading the entire file. It supports multiple bands, so a multi-variable dataset (e.g., a DEM plus a slope raster plus an aspect raster) can be stored in a single file. And it is an open standard maintained by the Open Geospatial Consortium (OGC), ensuring long-term accessibility.

When saving a raster in ArcGIS Pro or QGIS, the `.tif` extension produces a GeoTIFF. This is the recommended default format for any raster output in hydrological analysis.

### 5.10.2 Cloud-Optimized GeoTIFF (COG)

A **Cloud-Optimized GeoTIFF (COG)** is a regular GeoTIFF with a specific internal organization that enables efficient access over the internet. In a standard GeoTIFF, reading a small area of the image may require downloading the entire file because the data blocks are not organized for random access. A COG reorganizes the file so that (a) the header information comes first, (b) the image is tiled, and (c) overview levels (reduced-resolution versions) are interleaved with the full-resolution data. This allows a client to issue an HTTP range request to download only the tiles it needs, without retrieving the rest of the file.

COGs have become the standard distribution format for large raster datasets in cloud environments. The Copernicus DEM is distributed as COGs. The USGS 3DEP data is available as COGs. Major cloud platforms (Amazon Web Services, Microsoft Azure, Google Cloud) host petabytes of COG-formatted raster data that can be accessed directly without downloading entire files.

For a practical example, consider accessing the Copernicus GLO-30 DEM for a watershed in the Zala River basin, Hungary. The entire GLO-30 dataset covers the globe in thousands of tiles. Without COGs, you would need to identify which tiles overlap your study area, download them (potentially several gigabytes), and mosaic them locally. With COGs hosted on cloud storage, you can use a STAC (SpatioTemporal Asset Catalog) API to locate the relevant tiles, then stream only the portion of each tile that overlaps your watershed directly into your GIS software. The difference in workflow efficiency is dramatic.

### 5.10.3 NetCDF and HDF

**NetCDF (Network Common Data Form)** is a self-describing, machine-independent file format for storing multi-dimensional scientific data. It is the standard format for climate and weather data: ERA5 reanalysis, E-OBS gridded observations, CARPATCLIM, and most numerical weather model output are distributed as NetCDF files. NetCDF excels at storing data with multiple dimensions -- for example, precipitation at 1,000 x 800 grid cells x 8,760 hourly time steps in a year, organized as a single three-dimensional array.

For hydrological applications, NetCDF is essential because so many input data sources use it. A typical water balance study might ingest ERA5 precipitation and temperature as NetCDF, extract time series or spatial averages, and use them to drive a hydrological model. Both ArcGIS Pro and QGIS can open NetCDF files directly, displaying individual time slices or spatial layers as rasters.

The related **HDF5** (Hierarchical Data Format 5) format serves a similar purpose and is used by NASA for distributing satellite data products, including GPM/IMERG precipitation and GRACE terrestrial water storage. NetCDF4 is actually built on top of HDF5 internally, so the two formats are closely related.

### 5.10.4 ESRI Grid (Legacy)

The ESRI Grid format (sometimes called ArcGrid or ArcInfo Grid) is ESRI's legacy proprietary raster format. It stores data as a directory (folder) rather than a single file, containing multiple binary files with specific naming conventions. The grid name (directory name) is limited to 13 characters and cannot contain spaces, which leads to cryptic names like `dem10m_clip` or `flow_acc_f`. There is no file extension -- the absence of an extension is what signals to ArcGIS that a folder is an ESRI Grid.

While this format is still supported in current software, it is no longer recommended for new work. GeoTIFF provides all the same capabilities without the naming restrictions, the single-folder limitation, or the proprietary dependencies. Many legacy datasets and tutorials still reference ESRI Grids, so awareness of the format is necessary for working with historical data and older workflows.

## 5.11 Raster Storage in Geodatabases

In Chapter 4, we introduced the geodatabase as the organizational backbone of a GIS project. Rasters can be stored within geodatabases alongside vector feature classes and tables, providing a unified data management framework.

### 5.11.1 Rasters in a File Geodatabase

A file geodatabase (`.gdb`) can contain raster datasets as first-class members. When you drag a GeoTIFF into a file geodatabase in ArcGIS Pro, or use the Copy Raster tool to import it, the raster is stored internally in the geodatabase's proprietary binary format. This approach has several advantages:

- **Long names**: Raster names in a geodatabase can be much longer and more descriptive than the 13-character limit of standalone ESRI Grids. You can name a raster `Tisza_Basin_DEM_CopernicusDEM_30m_2024` rather than `tisza_cop30`.
- **Unified management**: DEMs, flow direction grids, watershed polygons, gauge point features, and parameter tables can all reside in a single geodatabase, making it easy to archive, copy, and share a complete project.
- **Consistent metadata**: The geodatabase manages spatial reference information, extent, and statistics for all its raster contents.
- **Size management**: File geodatabases can handle individual raster datasets up to 1 TB in size with compression.

There are, however, important caveats. Rasters in geodatabases cannot be stored inside feature datasets (the sub-containers that enforce a common coordinate system on vector feature classes). They must reside at the top level of the geodatabase. Additionally, rasters stored in geodatabases are not directly accessible to non-ESRI software without conversion -- QGIS can read file geodatabases using the OpenFileGDB driver, but support for raster content within `.gdb` files is limited. For interoperability, exporting rasters as GeoTIFF files alongside the geodatabase is recommended.

### 5.11.2 Raster Mosaics and Image Services

For large projects that involve many raster tiles -- such as a DEM covering an entire country, distributed as hundreds of individual GeoTIFF tiles -- the **mosaic dataset** provides a way to manage the collection as a single logical layer. A mosaic dataset is a geodatabase object that catalogs the locations and properties of multiple raster tiles and presents them as a seamless, continuous surface to the user. It does not physically merge the tiles (which would create an enormous single file); instead, it references the original tiles and dynamically assembles them on the fly as the user pans and zooms.

This approach is the standard way to manage large raster collections in ArcGIS Pro. The USGS 3DEP, the Copernicus DEM, and most national DEM programs distribute their data as individual tiles that are assembled into mosaics by the end user.

### 5.11.3 Enterprise Geodatabases and Raster Storage

In organizational settings where multiple users need concurrent access to the same raster datasets, enterprise geodatabases (hosted in PostgreSQL, SQL Server, or Oracle with the ESRI SDE technology) provide multi-user raster storage with versioning and access control. A national water agency managing thousands of raster layers -- DEMs, flood maps, land cover grids, precipitation surfaces -- might store them in an enterprise geodatabase accessible to all regional offices.

The practical reality, however, is that raster storage in enterprise databases has become less common as cloud-based solutions have matured. Hosting COGs in cloud object storage (Amazon S3, Azure Blob Storage, Google Cloud Storage) and serving them through STAC APIs or OGC web services provides similar multi-user access with much simpler administration. The trend in 2026 is away from database-centric raster management and toward cloud-native raster services.

## 5.12 Putting It All Together: How a Computer Sees the Tisza Floodplain

To bring the concepts of this chapter to life, imagine the task of representing the Tisza River floodplain in eastern Hungary as a raster dataset for flood mapping purposes. The Tisza is Hungary's second-largest river (after the Danube), and its floodplain is one of the most flood-prone areas in Central Europe. The great floods of 1879, 1970, 2001, and 2006 devastated communities along the river, and accurate terrain representation is essential for predicting where future floods will go.

The Tisza floodplain is remarkably flat. Between Szolnok and Szeged -- a river distance of approximately 200 km -- the terrain elevation changes by only about 10 meters. The floodplain is characterized by subtle features: natural levees along the river channel, oxbow lakes marking former river positions, shallow depressions that fill during floods, and the engineered levee system built during the 19th-century river regulation (the "Tisza szabalyozas").

Representing this landscape on a grid requires careful attention to resolution. The engineered levees are typically 5-10 meters wide at the crest and 3-5 meters above the surrounding floodplain. A 90-meter DEM (Copernicus GLO-90) cannot resolve these levees at all -- they are narrower than a single cell, and their 3-5 meter height is comparable to the DEM's accuracy. A 30-meter DEM (Copernicus GLO-30 or SRTM) may detect the levees as subtle elevation ridges but cannot represent their true cross-sectional shape. A 5-meter DEM (DDM-5) begins to resolve the levees as distinct linear features. And a 1-meter LiDAR DEM, where available, shows the levees in full detail: crest width, side slopes, access ramps, and the scour patterns along the river-facing side.

The choice of resolution for this flood mapping project depends entirely on the question being asked. For a regional flood risk assessment across the entire Tisza basin, the 30-meter Copernicus GLO-30 is adequate -- the analysis focuses on which broad areas are at risk, not the precise extent of inundation. For an operational flood forecast along a specific reach, the 5-meter DDM-5 is necessary to capture the levee system that controls where floodwater goes. For an engineering assessment of a specific levee section -- Is this levee high enough? Is there a low spot that might overtop? -- only a LiDAR DEM at 1-meter resolution provides the necessary detail.

This example encapsulates the central message of this chapter. A raster grid is an abstraction -- a simplification of continuous reality into a finite array of numbers. The art of raster-based hydrology lies in choosing the right level of abstraction for the question at hand: fine enough to capture the features that matter, coarse enough to remain computationally tractable, and honest about what the grid cannot represent.

## 5.13 A Global Perspective on Elevation Data

The revolution in freely available global DEM data has profound implications for water professionals in regions where national mapping agencies have limited resources. As recently as 2000, many countries in Africa, Asia, and the Middle East had no freely available DEM data finer than 1 km -- a resolution utterly inadequate for watershed analysis. Today, the Copernicus GLO-30 provides 30-meter global coverage for free, and the quality is sufficient for most watershed-scale applications.

This transformation has democratized hydrological analysis. A water engineer in Ethiopia, Bangladesh, or Jordan now has access to the same quality of elevation data as their counterpart in Germany or the United States. The analytical workflows described in this book -- watershed delineation, stream network extraction, flood mapping -- can be applied anywhere on Earth using freely available data and open-source software.

However, global DEMs have limitations that are particularly relevant in developing regions. Most global DEMs are digital surface models, not bare-earth DEMs, which means they include vegetation canopy and building heights. In densely forested tropical regions (the Amazon, the Congo Basin, Southeast Asia), this vegetation bias can add 20-30 meters of apparent elevation, severely distorting drainage patterns. The MERIT DEM addresses this to some extent by removing estimated tree height bias, but the correction is imperfect.

In flat, low-lying terrain -- river deltas in Bangladesh, coastal plains in Mozambique, the Nile Delta in Egypt -- even a few meters of DEM error can shift the predicted flood boundary by kilometers. For these areas, the 4-meter vertical accuracy of the Copernicus DEM may be insufficient, and investment in local LiDAR surveys becomes critical for accurate flood mapping.

**Table 5.7**: Global DEM selection guide for different regions and applications

| Region Type | Recommended DEM | Notes |
|------------|----------------|-------|
| Temperate, moderate relief | Copernicus GLO-30 | Excellent general-purpose choice |
| Mountainous terrain | Copernicus GLO-30 or AW3D30 | AW3D30 sometimes better in steep valleys |
| Dense tropical forest | MERIT DEM | Vegetation bias correction essential |
| Flat river deltas | Local LiDAR where available | Global DEMs lack precision in flat terrain |
| Arctic / high latitude | ArcticDEM (Polar Geospatial Center) | Dedicated polar product at 2 m |
| Arid, open terrain | Copernicus GLO-30 or TanDEM-X 90m | All global DEMs perform well in open terrain |
| Historical analysis (pre-2010) | SRTM | Year-2000 snapshot, useful for change detection |

The HydroSHEDS project (developed at McGill University, now at WWF) deserves special mention in this context. HydroSHEDS provides pre-processed hydrological derivatives -- flow direction, flow accumulation, basin boundaries, stream networks -- derived from the SRTM DEM at multiple resolutions. For any location on Earth between 60 degrees north and south latitude, HydroSHEDS provides ready-to-use hydrological datasets that would otherwise require significant processing effort. The HydroRIVERS dataset provides a global stream network, and HydroBASINS provides a nested hierarchy of watershed boundaries at multiple scales. These products save enormous effort for anyone working at regional to global scales.

## 5.14 The Road Ahead: From Grid to Flow

This chapter has established the raster data model as the foundation for hydrological terrain analysis. We have seen how continuous surfaces are reduced to grids of numbers, how the six defining elements of a grid determine its capabilities and limitations, how the interpretation of cell values affects analysis, and how the scale triplet provides a framework for assessing whether a dataset is fit for purpose.

The next three chapters build directly on this foundation. Chapter 6 introduces map algebra -- the framework for performing calculations on grids, cell by cell, neighborhood by neighborhood, zone by zone. Chapter 7 applies map algebra to terrain analysis, computing slope, aspect, and curvature from DEMs. Chapter 8 tackles the problem of distributing point measurements (rainfall, temperature) across a grid through spatial interpolation.

But the true payoff comes in Part III, where the raster DEM becomes the input to a chain of algorithms that trace the path of water across the landscape. Flow direction (Chapter 10) asks: which way would water move from each cell? Flow accumulation (Chapter 10) asks: how many upstream cells drain through each cell? Stream network extraction (Chapter 11) asks: where does enough water accumulate to form a channel? Watershed delineation (Chapter 11) asks: what is the boundary of the area draining to a given point? Every one of these operations takes a raster grid as input and produces a raster grid as output. The grid is the medium through which the computer "sees" water flowing across the landscape.

The grid is, in a real sense, the computer's eye. It sees terrain as a matrix of numbers, not as a sweeping vista. But from those numbers, algorithms can extract information that no human eye could discern from a visual inspection: the precise watershed boundary, the contributing area to a stream gauge, the flood depth at every point in a floodplain. The art of hydroinformatics lies in understanding both the power and the limitations of this numerical vision.
