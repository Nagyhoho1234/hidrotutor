# Chapter 18: Seeing Underground: 3D Subsurface GIS

Water above the surface is visible. We photograph floods from satellites, measure river levels with radar, and track snowpack with LiDAR. The tools described in Parts II through IV of this book all share a common advantage: their subject matter occupies the two-dimensional surface of the earth, or at most sits a few meters above it. Groundwater is different. It moves through rock formations hundreds of meters below the surface, through layers that were deposited over millions of years, folded by tectonics, fractured by stress, and dissolved by chemistry. The aquifers that supply drinking water to half the world's population are invisible, and the geological structures that control their behavior are fundamentally three-dimensional.

This creates a representation problem that two-dimensional GIS was never designed to solve. A polygon on a map can show the surface extent of an aquifer, but it cannot show that the aquifer thins from 200 meters in the east to 30 meters in the west, or that it dips beneath a confining clay layer at a 3-degree angle, or that a fault displaces it by 50 meters halfway across the basin. A contour map of the water table captures the horizontal pattern of hydraulic head, but it says nothing about the vertical gradients that drive water between aquifer layers. To understand groundwater systems -- to model them, manage them, protect them -- we need GIS tools that work in three dimensions.

This chapter introduces those tools. We begin with the question of why three-dimensional representation matters for groundwater, move through the specific data structures that modern geodatabases use to store 3D geometries, examine how volumes are created from surfaces, and explore the specialized world of geoscientific information systems (GSIS) that have evolved to handle the unique demands of subsurface modeling. Along the way, we will visit the thermal aquifer systems of the Great Hungarian Plain, one of Europe's most important geothermal resources, and examine how global initiatives are working to map the world's aquifers in three dimensions for the first time.

## 18.1 Why Three Dimensions Matter for Groundwater

The fundamental reason that groundwater science demands 3D representation is that the subsurface is layered. Unlike surface water, which flows across a single topographic surface governed by gravity and channel geometry, groundwater occupies a stack of geological formations, each with distinct hydraulic properties, each at a different elevation, each potentially connected to or isolated from its neighbors. A complete description of a groundwater system requires knowing not just where aquifers extend horizontally, but how thick they are, how deep they lie, how they connect vertically, and how their properties vary in all three spatial dimensions.

### 18.1.1 The Layered Earth

Consider a simplified cross-section through the Great Hungarian Plain (Alfold), one of the largest sedimentary basins in Europe. At the surface lies a thin layer of Quaternary alluvium -- sands, silts, and clays deposited by the Danube and Tisza rivers over the past two million years. This layer, typically 10 to 50 meters thick, contains the shallow groundwater that supplies domestic wells and irrigates farmland. Beneath it lies a much thicker sequence of Pliocene and Upper Miocene sediments -- the Pannonian formations -- consisting of alternating layers of sand, sandstone, silt, and clay deposited in the ancient Lake Pannon that once filled the Carpathian Basin. These formations can exceed 2,000 meters in total thickness and contain thermal water at temperatures reaching 90 degrees Celsius at depth. Below the Pannonian sediments lies the pre-Neogene basement: Paleozoic and Mesozoic crystalline rocks, carbonates, and metamorphics that form the impermeable floor of the basin.

Each of these layers has different hydraulic conductivity, different storage capacity, different water chemistry, and different temperature. The shallow Quaternary aquifer recharges rapidly from precipitation and river infiltration but is vulnerable to agricultural contamination. The deep Pannonian thermal aquifer recharges slowly -- over thousands to tens of thousands of years -- but contains vast quantities of warm water that Hungary has exploited for bathing, heating, and agriculture for over a century. The confining clay layers between aquifer sands control whether water can move vertically between formations, and the geometry of these layers -- their thickness, continuity, and dip -- determines the degree of hydraulic connection between shallow and deep systems.

A two-dimensional map showing the surface extent of these formations reveals almost nothing about this vertical complexity. What we need is a three-dimensional model: a digital representation that captures the geometry, topology, and properties of every significant hydrogeological unit in the subsurface. Building such a model is the central challenge of subsurface GIS.

### 18.1.2 From Sparse Data to 3D Models

The subsurface presents a data challenge that has no parallel in surface hydrology. We can measure the topography of the earth's surface continuously from satellites and aircraft -- the DEMs discussed in Chapter 5 cover the entire planet at 30-meter resolution. We cannot do anything comparable for the subsurface. Our only direct observations come from boreholes: narrow vertical holes drilled into the earth, from which we extract rock samples (cores and cuttings) and run geophysical instruments (well logs) that measure the physical properties of the surrounding formations.

Boreholes are expensive. A shallow monitoring well might cost a few thousand euros; a deep geothermal well in the Pannonian Basin can cost several million. As a result, borehole density is always far lower than what a hydrogeologist would wish. In the Great Hungarian Plain, which covers approximately 52,000 square kilometers, the national borehole database maintained by the Mining and Geological Survey of Hungary (Magyar Banyaszati es Foldtani Szolgalat, **MBFSZ** -- since reorganized under the Supervisory Authority for Regulatory Affairs, **SZTFH**, as discussed in Chapter 19) contains records for roughly 200,000 boreholes -- an impressive number by international standards, reflecting Hungary's long history of groundwater and geothermal exploration. Yet even this density means, on average, one borehole per 0.26 square kilometers, and many of these are shallow water wells that provide no information about the deeper formations.

The task of constructing a 3D geological model is therefore fundamentally an interpolation problem: estimating the geometry of continuous geological surfaces and volumes from sparse, irregularly spaced point observations. This is analogous to the spatial interpolation of rainfall discussed in Chapter 8, but with the added complexity of a third dimension and the constraint that geological surfaces must respect stratigraphic rules -- a younger formation cannot underlie an older one (unless the sequence has been tectonically inverted), and a layer cannot extend beyond the fault that truncates it.

The data structures and tools described in this chapter exist to support this interpolation workflow: from point observations at boreholes, through cross-sections and surfaces, to fully three-dimensional volume models of the subsurface. Each step in the workflow requires specific GIS data types, and understanding these types is essential before we can build a groundwater model (Chapter 20) or simulate flow through it (Chapter 21).

### 18.1.3 The Vertical Dimension in Groundwater Problems

Three-dimensional representation is not merely an academic exercise -- it is essential for answering the practical questions that drive groundwater management. Consider a few examples that illustrate why the vertical dimension cannot be ignored.

**Wellhead protection zones.** When a municipality drills a production well into a confined aquifer, regulators require a protection zone around the well to prevent contamination. In 2D, this zone appears as a circle or elongated ellipse on the surface. In 3D, the actual capture zone is a complex volume that extends through the confining layer above the aquifer, and its shape depends on the thickness and hydraulic conductivity of that confining layer. A contaminant spill at the surface directly above the well may pose little risk if the confining layer is thick and continuous, while a spill two kilometers away may be dangerous if the confining layer is absent there and the aquifer is exposed. Without a 3D model of the confining layer geometry, the protection zone analysis is fundamentally incomplete.

**Geothermal energy extraction.** Hungary is Europe's second-largest user of geothermal energy for direct heating, after Iceland. The thermal water extracted from the deep Pannonian aquifers must be managed to prevent over-exploitation -- extracting water faster than it recharges leads to declining water levels, reduced temperatures, and eventually to the collapse of the geothermal resource. Sustainable management requires a 3D model of the thermal aquifer system that captures the geometry of the producing formations, the distribution of temperature at depth, and the hydraulic connections between extraction wells and recharge areas. The Hungarian Energy and Public Utility Regulatory Authority (Magyar Energetikai es Kozmu-szabalyozasi Hivatal, MEKH) uses such models when evaluating applications for new geothermal concessions.

**Contamination transport.** When an industrial site contaminates the groundwater beneath it -- as happened spectacularly in the 2010 Ajka red mud disaster, when the failure of a caustic waste reservoir released approximately one million cubic meters of alkaline red mud into the Torna Creek valley -- the contamination migrates through the subsurface along pathways controlled by the three-dimensional geology. The plume may spread horizontally in a permeable sand layer, be blocked vertically by a clay aquitard, and re-emerge at the surface kilometers away where the sand layer outcrops. Predicting the movement of such a plume requires a 3D geological model linked to a 3D transport simulation.

These examples share a common thread: the problems are inherently three-dimensional, and any attempt to solve them in 2D requires simplifying assumptions that may lead to dangerously wrong answers. The remainder of this chapter describes the data structures that make 3D representation possible within the GIS environment.

## 18.2 Z-Enabled Features in Geodatabases

The foundation of 3D subsurface representation in GIS is the ability to store vertical coordinates alongside the familiar horizontal coordinates. Standard GIS features -- points, lines, and polygons -- are defined by their x (longitude or easting) and y (latitude or northing) coordinates. They exist on a flat plane. To move into three dimensions, we need features that also carry a z coordinate representing elevation or depth at every vertex of their geometry.

### 18.2.1 From 2D to Z-Enabled Geometry

Modern geodatabases -- including ESRI's file geodatabase and enterprise geodatabase, GeoPackage, and PostGIS -- support what are called Z-enabled feature classes. When you create a new feature class and enable the Z dimension, every vertex in every feature stored in that class carries three coordinates: x, y, and z. The naming convention in the ESRI ecosystem makes this explicit by appending a "Z" to the geometry type name. A standard Point feature becomes a **PointZ**, a Polyline becomes a **PolylineZ** (or LineZ), and a Polygon becomes a **PolygonZ**. The multipatch geometry type, which we will discuss in detail later, is always Z-enabled by definition.

The distinction between a standard 2D feature class and a Z-enabled feature class is made at creation time. In ArcGIS Pro, the option appears in the New Feature Class dialog: a checkbox labeled "Coordinates include Z values -- Used to store 3D data." In QGIS, the equivalent option appears when creating a new layer, where you can select the geometry type as "PointZ," "LineStringZ," or "PolygonZ." In PostGIS, you specify the geometry type with a Z suffix in the column definition. Once a feature class is created as Z-enabled, the vertical coordinate becomes an integral part of the geometry, stored and managed alongside the horizontal coordinates.

It is important to understand that enabling the Z dimension does not automatically make your data three-dimensional in a meaningful sense. If you create a Z-enabled point feature class and then add points without setting their z values, those points will have z = 0 (or NaN, depending on the software), and they will display at sea level in a 3D viewer. The Z capability is a container; you must fill it with actual elevation or depth values to create genuinely three-dimensional features. In practice, z values come from several sources: direct measurement (GPS with precise vertical positioning, borehole depths), interpolation from a DEM (draping 2D features onto a surface), attribute transfer (reading a depth value from a database field and writing it into the geometry), or manual editing (adjusting vertex elevations in a 3D editing session).

### 18.2.2 PointZ: Three-Dimensional Point Features

The PointZ geometry is the simplest 3D feature type and the most common in groundwater applications. Every borehole in a groundwater database is, at minimum, a 2D point on a map -- it has a location defined by x and y coordinates (or longitude and latitude, or easting and northing in a projected coordinate system). Making it a PointZ adds the z coordinate, which for a borehole typically represents the ground surface elevation at the drilling location.

But a single PointZ at the wellhead tells us nothing about the subsurface. The real power of PointZ features in groundwater work comes from representing the contacts between geological formations as observed along the borehole. If a borehole penetrates five distinct hydrogeological units, we can represent each contact -- each boundary between one unit and the next -- as a separate PointZ feature. The x and y coordinates are the same for all contacts (they all lie along the same vertical borehole), but the z coordinate differs: the top of the Quaternary alluvium might be at z = 87 meters above sea level (the ground surface), the base of the alluvium at z = 55 meters, the top of the first Pannonian sand at z = 55 meters, and so on down the borehole.

This representation -- a vertical stack of PointZ features sharing the same horizontal location but differing in z -- is the fundamental building block of all subsurface models. These points are what we interpolate between to create the surfaces and volumes that define hydrogeological units over an area. The accuracy of the final 3D model depends directly on the accuracy and density of these borehole contact points.

In Hungary, the MBFSZ national geological database stores borehole stratigraphic data in this format. Each borehole record includes the surface coordinates (typically in the EOV projection, EPSG:23700), the ground elevation, and a sequence of stratigraphic contacts with their depths below ground surface. Converting depth-below-surface to elevation-above-sea-level requires subtracting the depth from the ground elevation -- a simple calculation, but one that introduces error if the ground elevation is inaccurate. For boreholes drilled before the era of GPS, ground elevations were often read from topographic maps and may carry vertical errors of several meters. These errors propagate directly into the 3D model.

### 18.2.3 PolylineZ and PolygonZ: Lines and Areas in 3D Space

A **PolylineZ** feature is a line whose vertices each carry a z coordinate, allowing the line to follow a path through three-dimensional space rather than being confined to a flat plane. In groundwater applications, the most common use of PolylineZ is representing a borehole as a vertical line segment (or, for deviated wells in petroleum and geothermal applications, as a curved line in 3D space). Each borehole becomes a PolylineZ feature with vertices at every stratigraphic contact. Unlike the PointZ representation where each contact is a separate feature, the PolylineZ representation captures the entire borehole as a single feature, with the z values of its vertices recording the depths of the contacts.

Which representation you choose -- multiple PointZ features per borehole or a single PolylineZ per borehole -- depends on the analysis you intend to perform. If you need to interpolate surfaces between contact points across many boreholes, the PointZ approach is more convenient because each point can be treated independently by interpolation algorithms. If you need to visualize the borehole as a continuous vertical profile, display it in a 3D viewer, or query the sequence of formations along it, the PolylineZ representation is more natural.

A **PolygonZ** feature extends the same principle to areas. Each vertex of the polygon carries a z coordinate, so the polygon defines a surface patch in 3D space rather than a flat area on a 2D plane. In subsurface work, PolygonZ features are used to represent the top or bottom surface of a geological formation as a set of triangular or irregular polygonal facets -- essentially a triangulated irregular network (TIN) stored as a set of PolygonZ features. They are also used for cross-sections: a vertical slice through the subsurface can be represented as a set of PolygonZ features, each representing a different geological unit within the cross-section plane.

The relationship between PolygonZ and true 3D volumes is important to understand. A PolygonZ feature is a surface in 3D space -- it has area and orientation but no thickness. It defines a boundary, not a volume. To represent a volume -- a body of rock with a top surface, a bottom surface, sides, and an interior -- we need the multipatch geometry type, which combines multiple surface patches into a closed solid.

### 18.2.4 Practical Considerations for Z-Enabled Data

Working with Z-enabled features introduces several practical complications that do not arise with standard 2D features. Understanding these issues before you begin building a subsurface database will save considerable trouble later.

**Vertical datum and units.** Just as horizontal coordinates require a defined coordinate reference system (Chapter 3), z values require a defined vertical datum and unit. In Hungary, the standard vertical datum is the Baltic Height System (Balti magassagi rendszer), which references heights to mean sea level at the Kronstadt tide gauge. In the European context, the European Vertical Reference System (EVRS) provides a unified datum. In the United States, the North American Vertical Datum of 1988 (NAVD88) is standard. If your borehole data records depths below ground surface rather than elevations above sea level -- which is the most common format in drilling records -- you must convert before creating Z-enabled features, and that conversion requires knowing the ground surface elevation at each borehole, which in turn requires a DEM or surveyed elevation.

**Vertical exaggeration.** The subsurface is typically much wider than it is deep. A cross-section through the Great Hungarian Plain might span 100 kilometers horizontally but only 2 kilometers vertically -- a 50:1 aspect ratio. Displaying such a cross-section at true scale makes the geological layers appear as nearly horizontal lines, with all the structural detail invisible. The universal practice is to apply vertical exaggeration -- stretching the z dimension by a factor of 5x, 10x, or even 50x relative to the horizontal dimensions -- to make geological structures visible. Modern 3D viewers (ArcGIS Pro's 3D scene, QGIS 3D view, Paraview, Leapfrog) all support vertical exaggeration as a display setting. It is essential to remember that vertical exaggeration distorts angles: a fault dipping at 60 degrees in reality will appear nearly vertical at 10x exaggeration, and layer thicknesses will appear ten times greater than they actually are.

**Coordinate system compatibility.** When combining surface and subsurface data -- for example, overlaying a geological cross-section on a topographic DEM -- all features must share the same horizontal and vertical coordinate system. This is straightforward in principle but frequently violated in practice. It is common to receive borehole data in one coordinate system (EOV for Hungarian data), a DEM in another (WGS84 geographic coordinates from a Copernicus download), and geological maps in a third (the historical Gauss-Kruger projection used in older Hungarian geological mapping). Defining the coordinate system correctly at the feature class level, and transforming data before loading it into the geodatabase, is a necessary preliminary step.

## 18.3 Multipatch Features: Representing Volumes

Points, lines, and polygons -- even in their Z-enabled forms -- are fundamentally surface geometries. They define locations, paths, and areas, but they cannot represent volumes: enclosed three-dimensional bodies with an interior. For groundwater applications, we need volumes to represent hydrogeological units (aquifers, aquitards), contamination plumes, ore bodies, and any other subsurface entity that has a top, a bottom, and a measurable thickness. The geodatabase geometry type designed for this purpose is the **multipatch**.

### 18.3.1 What Is a Multipatch?

A multipatch is a geometry type that stores the outer surface of a three-dimensional object as a collection of planar patches. Each patch is a flat polygon (a triangle, a triangle strip, a triangle fan, or a ring), and the patches together form the boundary of a 3D solid -- like the faces of a die, but with many more faces and potentially curved boundaries approximated by many small flat facets.

The name "multipatch" comes from the idea that the geometry consists of multiple patches stitched together. Unlike a PolygonZ, which represents a single surface in 3D space, a multipatch can represent a closed volume. If you imagine the six faces of a rectangular box, each face is a flat polygon; together, the six polygons enclose a volume. A multipatch stores all six faces as a single geometry, and GIS software understands that the enclosed space is the "inside" of the feature.

In practice, subsurface multipatches are much more complex than rectangular boxes. A hydrogeological unit in the Great Hungarian Plain might be bounded on top by an undulating surface representing the formation top, on the bottom by another undulating surface representing the formation base, and on the sides by the lateral extent of the formation (which might be defined by a fault, a facies change, or simply the edge of the study area). The resulting multipatch has hundreds or thousands of triangular faces that approximate these irregular surfaces.

Multipatch features are always Z-enabled -- there is no 2D multipatch, because a 3D solid that has been projected onto a plane is no longer a solid. They are stored in a standard feature class within the geodatabase, alongside attribute fields that describe the properties of each volume: formation name, lithology, hydraulic conductivity, porosity, age, and any other relevant characteristics. Each multipatch feature in the feature class represents one geo-object (a concept we will formalize in section 18.5), and the collection of features represents the complete geomodel.

### 18.3.2 Multipatch Construction Methods

Creating multipatch features is considerably more complex than creating points or polygons, because you must define a closed 3D surface rather than a flat outline. Several approaches are used in practice.

**Extrusion.** The simplest method for creating a 3D volume from existing 2D or 2.5D data is extrusion: taking a flat polygon and stretching it vertically (or along some other axis) to create a solid. If you have a polygon representing the surface extent of an aquifer and you know the aquifer's top and bottom elevations, you can extrude the polygon between those two elevations to create a block. In ArcGIS Pro, the Layer 3D To Feature Class tool converts extruded 2D features into multipatch features. In its simplest form, extrusion assumes that the top and bottom surfaces are flat, which is adequate for thin, gently dipping layers but grossly inaccurate for formations with significant topographic relief on their boundaries.

A more sophisticated form of extrusion uses two surfaces -- the formation top and the formation bottom -- and creates a volume between them. Each point on the top surface is connected vertically to the corresponding point on the bottom surface, and the sides are closed where the surfaces terminate. This "between two surfaces" approach produces multipatches that faithfully capture the variable thickness of the formation, and it is the standard method in most subsurface modeling workflows.

**Triangulated surface stitching.** When the top and bottom surfaces of a formation are represented as TINs (triangulated irregular networks) or raster surfaces, the multipatch can be constructed by triangulating both surfaces and connecting their edges to form a closed solid. The top surface provides the upper face of the multipatch, the bottom surface provides the lower face, and a skirt of triangles around the perimeter closes the sides. This is the method used by most specialized subsurface modeling software.

**Direct modeling.** In some applications -- particularly petroleum reservoir modeling and mining -- multipatches are created directly from interpreted cross-sections, geological maps, and borehole data using interactive 3D modeling tools. The geologist draws the formation boundaries on cross-sections, the software interpolates between cross-sections to create surfaces, and the surfaces are combined into closed volumes. This workflow is described in more detail in section 18.5 on geoscientific information systems.

### 18.3.3 Working with Multipatches in Practice

Multipatch features behave differently from standard polygon features in several important ways. Understanding these differences is essential for working with 3D subsurface data in a GIS environment.

**Display.** Multipatch features are only meaningful when viewed in a 3D scene. In ArcGIS Pro, this means the 3D Local Scene or Global Scene view; in QGIS, it means the 3D Map View. In a standard 2D map view, a multipatch feature displays as its 2D footprint -- the projection of the 3D solid onto the horizontal plane -- which provides no information about the vertical dimension. This is analogous to viewing a building from directly above: you see the roof outline but nothing about the building's height or internal structure.

When displayed in 3D, multipatches can be rendered with various visual effects: solid color, transparency (essential for seeing internal structures), texture mapping, and lighting effects that enhance the perception of depth and shape. For subsurface models, transparency is particularly important -- setting the upper geological layers to 50 or 70 percent transparency allows the viewer to see through them to the deeper formations below, creating a layered visualization that communicates the three-dimensional structure far more effectively than any cross-section.

**Spatial queries.** Because multipatches represent volumes, they support spatial queries that are impossible with 2D features. You can query whether a 3D point (say, the bottom of a proposed well) lies inside a particular multipatch volume (does the well reach the target aquifer?). You can calculate the volume of a multipatch feature in cubic meters (what is the total volume of the aquifer?). You can intersect two multipatch volumes (where does a contamination plume overlap with the production aquifer?). These queries require the 3D Analyst extension in ArcGIS Pro or equivalent functionality in open-source tools like the 3D capabilities in GRASS GIS or the volumetric tools in Leapfrog.

**Limitations.** Multipatch features in standard GIS environments have important limitations compared to the volumetric representations used in specialized GSIS software. The most significant limitation is that a multipatch defines only the outer boundary of a volume -- it does not discretize the interior. There is no grid of cells inside the multipatch, no way to assign different property values to different parts of the interior, and no built-in support for the kind of cell-by-cell calculations that numerical groundwater models require. To run a flow simulation, the multipatch geometry must be transferred to a modeling environment (like MODFLOW) that uses its own internal discretization (Chapter 21). The multipatch is a geometric container, not a computational mesh.

## 18.4 Creating Volumes by Extrusion

Extrusion is the most accessible method for generating 3D subsurface volumes from the kinds of data that hydrogeologists routinely work with: surface extent polygons, formation top and bottom surfaces, and borehole contact data. This section walks through the extrusion workflow in detail, from input data to finished multipatch features.

### 18.4.1 Simple Vertical Extrusion

The simplest form of extrusion takes a 2D polygon (the surface footprint of a geological unit) and stretches it vertically between two constant elevations. The result is a flat-topped, flat-bottomed block -- a geological "slab" with uniform thickness. This is appropriate only for the simplest geological situations: a thin alluvial layer over a flat plain, or a first approximation of an aquifer before detailed subsurface data are available.

The workflow in ArcGIS Pro proceeds as follows. You start with a 2D polygon feature class representing the surface extent of the formation. In a 3D Local Scene, you set the polygon layer's elevation properties: the base height comes from one source (say, a constant value of -50 meters representing the formation bottom) and the top height from another (say, the ground surface DEM). The polygon then appears as a 3D block in the scene. To convert this visual extrusion into a permanent multipatch feature, you use the Layer 3D To Feature Class geoprocessing tool, which reads the extrusion parameters and writes a new feature class containing multipatch geometries.

```
# ArcPy example: simple vertical extrusion
import arcpy

# Input: 2D polygon of aquifer extent
input_polygon = "C:/GIS/GreatPlain.gdb/Quaternary_Aquifer"

# Extrusion parameters
base_elevation = -50  # meters, constant bottom
top_surface = "C:/GIS/GreatPlain.gdb/DEM_10m"  # ground surface DEM

# Create extruded multipatch
arcpy.ddd.Layer3DToFeatureClass(
    in_feature_layer=input_polygon,
    out_feature_class="C:/GIS/GreatPlain.gdb/Quaternary_Aquifer_3D"
)
```

This example omits some steps (setting the layer's 3D properties requires working with the layer's display settings before running the tool), but it illustrates the conceptual workflow: 2D polygon plus elevation information equals 3D multipatch.

### 18.4.2 Extrusion Between Two Surfaces

The geologically realistic version of extrusion uses two surfaces -- the formation top and the formation bottom -- rather than constant elevation values. In the Great Hungarian Plain, the top of the Upper Pannonian thermal aquifer is not flat; it varies from about 50 meters below sea level in the central basin to near the surface where the formation outcrops along the basin margins. The bottom surface varies even more dramatically, reaching depths of 1,500 meters or more in the deepest parts of the basin. Extruding between these two surfaces produces a multipatch that captures the full three-dimensional geometry of the formation.

The two surfaces are typically created by interpolating borehole contact data. At each borehole that penetrates the formation, we have a PointZ feature representing the top contact and another representing the bottom contact. We interpolate the top contacts across the study area to create a continuous surface (using kriging, IDW, or spline interpolation -- the methods described in Chapter 8), and we do the same for the bottom contacts. The result is two raster surfaces: formation_top and formation_bottom, each defined over the same grid.

The volume between these two surfaces is the formation. To create a multipatch from it, we use the approach described in section 18.3.2: triangulate both surfaces, connect them at the edges, and close the resulting solid. Several tools support this workflow:

In ArcGIS Pro, the **Multipatch From Raster** approach involves converting each raster surface to a TIN, then using the Extrude Between tool (available through the 3D Analyst extension) to create the volume. Alternatively, the surfaces can be loaded into the ArcGIS for subsurface workflows provided by the Arc Hydro Groundwater toolset, which automates much of this process.

In the open-source ecosystem, **PyVista** (a Python library for 3D visualization and mesh analysis) can construct closed surfaces from paired raster grids. **Gempy**, an open-source geological modeling library written in Python, takes borehole data and geological rules as input and produces 3D geological models directly, bypassing the manual surface-creation step entirely.

### 18.4.3 Worked Example: Extruding the Quaternary Aquifer Layer

To make the extrusion workflow concrete, let us work through a simplified example using data from the Great Hungarian Plain.

**Input data.** We have 47 borehole records in a study area of approximately 400 square kilometers near Hajduszoboszlo -- one of Hungary's most famous thermal spa towns, whose thermal baths are supplied by wells tapping the deep Pannonian aquifer at 1,100 meters depth. Each borehole record includes the EOV coordinates, the ground surface elevation, and the depth to the base of the Quaternary alluvium (which forms the shallow aquifer in this area).

**Step 1: Create PointZ features for the formation base.** For each borehole, calculate the elevation of the Quaternary base:

$$z_{base} = z_{ground} - d_{Quaternary}$$

where $z_{ground}$ is the ground surface elevation (meters above Baltic Sea level) and $d_{Quaternary}$ is the depth to the base of the Quaternary (meters below ground surface). For our example borehole HAJ-42 (Table 18.1): $z_{base} = 87.4 - 32.5 = 54.9$ m asl.

**Step 2: Interpolate the formation top surface.** The top of the Quaternary is the ground surface itself, which we obtain from a DEM. For the Hajduszoboszlo area, we use the DDM-5 (5-meter resolution Hungarian national DEM) resampled to 50-meter cells.

**Step 3: Interpolate the formation base surface.** Using the 47 PointZ features representing the Quaternary base elevation, we run an ordinary kriging interpolation to create a continuous raster surface. The variogram analysis reveals a range of approximately 8 km (the distance beyond which borehole observations become spatially uncorrelated) and a sill variance of 35 m$^2$, indicating moderate spatial variability in the Quaternary thickness. The kriging prediction surface gives us the estimated base elevation at every 50-meter grid cell.

**Step 4: Calculate thickness.** Subtracting the base surface from the top surface (the DEM) gives the Quaternary aquifer thickness at every grid cell:

$$T(x,y) = z_{top}(x,y) - z_{base}(x,y)$$

In our study area, $T$ ranges from 12 meters (on local highs near the basin margin) to 48 meters (in buried channels that incise into the underlying Pannonian formations). The mean thickness is 29 meters with a standard deviation of 9 meters.

**Step 5: Create the multipatch.** Using the top surface (DEM) and bottom surface (kriged Quaternary base) as inputs, we construct a multipatch feature representing the Quaternary aquifer volume. The total volume of the Quaternary aquifer in the study area is:

$$V = \sum_{i=1}^{n} T_i \times \Delta x \times \Delta y = \sum_{i=1}^{n} T_i \times 50 \times 50$$

With 160,000 grid cells (400 km$^2$ / 2,500 m$^2$ per cell) and a mean thickness of 29 meters, the total aquifer volume is approximately:

$$V \approx 160{,}000 \times 29 \times 2{,}500 = 11.6 \times 10^9 \text{ m}^3 = 11.6 \text{ km}^3$$

Assuming a porosity of 0.25 (typical for Quaternary alluvial sands), the total groundwater storage volume is approximately:

$$V_{water} = V \times n = 11.6 \times 0.25 = 2.9 \text{ km}^3$$

This represents roughly 2.9 billion cubic meters of water stored in the shallow aquifer -- enough to supply the city of Hajduszoboszlo (population ~30,000) for several centuries at current consumption rates. This kind of volumetric calculation, which requires knowing the three-dimensional geometry of the aquifer, is impossible with 2D data alone.

## 18.5 Geoscientific Information Systems (GSIS)

Standard GIS tools -- even with Z-enabled features and multipatch geometries -- were designed primarily for surface features: roads, rivers, land parcels, buildings. The subsurface has different requirements. Geological formations are not discrete objects like buildings; they grade into each other, pinch out, are displaced by faults, and are defined by interpretation as much as by measurement. Representing this complexity requires specialized software that understands geological rules, supports interactive 3D editing, and can construct coherent models from sparse, uncertain data. This specialized branch of spatial information science is known as **geoscientific information systems** (GSIS), or equivalently as **geomodeling systems**.

### 18.5.1 How GSIS Differs from Traditional GIS

The distinction between GSIS and traditional GIS is not merely one of dimensionality (3D versus 2D). It is a difference in the fundamental data model, the types of operations supported, and the workflow by which models are constructed.

**Data model.** Traditional GIS represents the world as discrete features (points, lines, polygons) or continuous fields (rasters). GSIS adds a third paradigm: **geo-objects**, which are subsurface entities with distinct boundaries in three dimensions (Fisher 1993). A geo-object might be a geological formation (a volume of rock with measurable top and bottom surfaces), a fault (a planar surface along which rocks have been displaced), a fold (a curved surface defining the deformation of strata), or a vein (a tabular body of mineralized material). Geo-objects are not simply "3D versions of polygons" -- they carry geological semantics. The GSIS knows that a formation is bounded by surfaces that cannot cross each other (the top of a formation cannot lie below its bottom), that a fault displaces all formations it cuts, and that formations must be arranged in stratigraphic order unless explicitly marked as unconformities.

**A combination of geo-objects forms a geomodel** -- an abstract digital representation of a portion of the earth's subsurface (Apel 2006). The geomodel is not a single feature or a single layer; it is an integrated collection of volumes, surfaces, and structural elements that together describe the complete three-dimensional architecture of the study area. A geomodel for the Great Hungarian Plain might contain 15 to 20 geo-objects representing the major hydrogeological units from the Quaternary surface layer to the pre-Neogene basement, plus fault surfaces that displace them, plus the water table surface and the base of the thermal anomaly.

**Operations.** Where traditional GIS excels at spatial queries (what is at this location?), overlay analysis (where do two features intersect?), and network analysis (what is the shortest path?), GSIS excels at operations specific to subsurface modeling. These include: interpolating surfaces from borehole data while honoring geological constraints (a fault must be an interpolation barrier); constructing cross-sections by projecting borehole data onto a vertical plane and interpolating between them; editing surfaces interactively in 3D to match the geologist's interpretation; building volumes between pairs of surfaces; and slicing volumes to generate new cross-sections at arbitrary orientations.

**Workflow.** The GSIS workflow is fundamentally interpretive. Unlike a DEM, which can be created automatically from LiDAR data with minimal human input, a geological model requires expert interpretation at every step. The geologist examines borehole data, draws tentative correlations between formations in different boreholes, constructs cross-sections that express their interpretation of the subsurface structure, adjusts the cross-sections until they are consistent with all available data, generates surfaces from the cross-sections, and builds volumes from the surfaces. This process is iterative and subjective -- two equally competent geologists working with the same borehole data may produce different models, especially where data are sparse. GSIS software supports this interpretive workflow by providing tools for rapid visualization, interactive editing, and immediate feedback on the consequences of each interpretive decision.

### 18.5.2 Major GSIS Software Platforms

The GSIS software landscape in 2026 includes both commercial and open-source options, each with distinct strengths. The following is not exhaustive but covers the platforms most relevant to groundwater applications.

**Leapfrog Geo** (Seequent, a Bentley Systems company) is perhaps the most widely used GSIS for groundwater and environmental applications. It uses an implicit modeling approach where geological surfaces are defined by a mathematical function (a radial basis function interpolant) fitted to contact point data, rather than being explicitly drawn by the geologist. This approach makes model updates fast -- when new borehole data become available, the surfaces automatically recompute -- and reduces the subjectivity of the modeling process. Leapfrog is used by environmental consultancies worldwide and has been adopted by several Hungarian engineering firms for contaminated site investigations and thermal water exploration.

**GMS (Groundwater Modeling System)** developed by Aquaveo bridges the gap between GSIS and numerical modeling. It includes tools for constructing 3D geological models from borehole data and cross-sections, and can export these models directly as input to MODFLOW and other groundwater simulation codes (Chapter 21). GMS is widely used in the United States and is the recommended interface for the US Army Corps of Engineers' groundwater modeling projects.

**GemPy** is an open-source Python library for implicit geological modeling, conceptually similar to Leapfrog but freely available and scriptable. GemPy uses potential-field interpolation based on the universal co-kriging framework to construct geological models from point data and orientation measurements. It can be integrated into Python-based geoprocessing workflows and is increasingly popular in academic research. Its main limitation is the lack of interactive 3D editing -- all model construction is done through code rather than through a graphical interface.

**GOCAD / SKUA-GOCAD** (Aspen Technology, formerly Paradigm) is the dominant GSIS in the petroleum industry. It excels at modeling structurally complex geology -- faulted, folded, and unconformity-bounded sequences -- and includes powerful tools for building reservoir simulation grids. While primarily a petroleum tool, it has been used for large-scale groundwater basin modeling, particularly in projects involving complex structural geology.

### 18.5.3 The GSIS Workflow in Detail

To make the GSIS workflow concrete, let us walk through the steps of building a geological model for a thermal water exploration project in the Great Hungarian Plain -- the kind of project that Hungarian hydrogeological consultancies undertake regularly.

**Step 1: Data compilation.** The first step is to gather all available subsurface data for the study area. This includes borehole logs from the MBFSZ national database, geological maps at 1:100,000 scale from the Geological and Geophysical Institute of Hungary (now part of MBFSZ), existing cross-sections from previous studies, seismic profiles if available from petroleum exploration, and surface geology mapped in the field. For a typical geothermal exploration project covering 100 to 500 square kilometers, the compiled dataset might include 50 to 200 borehole logs, 5 to 10 published cross-sections, and regional geological maps.

**Step 2: Define the stratigraphic framework.** Before modeling, the geologist must define the sequence of geological units to be modeled and the rules governing their relationships. For the Great Hungarian Plain, a typical framework might include (from top to bottom): Holocene alluvium, Pleistocene fluvial and aeolian sediments, Upper Pannonian (including alternating sand and clay members), Lower Pannonian marls and turbidites, and pre-Neogene basement. The framework specifies which units overlie which, where unconformities exist, and which faults are significant enough to include.

**Step 3: Enter borehole data.** Each borehole is entered into the GSIS as a vertical (or deviated) line with contacts marked at the depths where one formation gives way to another. The GSIS assigns each contact to the appropriate unit in the stratigraphic framework. At this stage, the geologist may need to reinterpret ambiguous borehole descriptions -- old drilling records from the 1960s and 1970s may use inconsistent formation names or imprecise depth measurements.

**Step 4: Construct cross-sections.** The geologist selects lines across the study area and constructs vertical cross-sections by projecting nearby boreholes onto the section plane. Between the boreholes, the geologist draws the formation boundaries based on their interpretation of how the geology varies laterally. This is the most subjective step in the workflow, and it is where geological expertise matters most. The GSIS provides a 2D cross-section editor where the geologist can draw, edit, and refine these boundaries interactively.

**Step 5: Interpolate surfaces.** Using the borehole contacts and the interpreted cross-section boundaries as control points, the GSIS interpolates continuous surfaces representing the top and bottom of each formation across the study area. In implicit modeling systems like Leapfrog, this step is automatic -- the software fits a mathematical surface to all available data points. In explicit systems, the geologist may need to guide the interpolation by adding control points where the algorithm produces geologically unreasonable results (such as a surface that intersects itself or a layer that has negative thickness).

**Step 6: Build volumes.** The interpolated surfaces are combined to create closed volumes representing each formation. The GSIS ensures that the volumes are consistent -- they do not overlap (two formations cannot occupy the same space) and they fill the model domain completely (there are no gaps between formations). The result is a complete geomodel: a set of non-overlapping volumes that together represent the entire three-dimensional subsurface structure of the study area.

**Step 7: Validate and export.** The geologist validates the model by checking it against any data not used in construction (a common practice is to withhold 10 to 20 percent of boreholes from the modeling and use them for validation), generating new cross-sections through the model and comparing them to published geological sections, and reviewing the model in 3D for geological plausibility. Once validated, the model is exported for its intended purpose: as input to a groundwater flow model (Chapter 21), as a basis for well placement decisions, or as a component of a geothermal resource assessment.

## 18.6 Geomodels and Geo-Objects

The concepts of geomodels and geo-objects form the theoretical foundation of subsurface GIS. While section 18.5 described the practical workflow, this section formalizes the underlying data model -- the abstract framework that defines what subsurface entities can be represented and how they relate to each other.

### 18.6.1 Defining Geo-Objects

A **geo-object** is a distinctive subsurface feature with measurable spatial boundaries in three dimensions (Fisher 1993). This definition is deliberately broad. A geo-object can be a volume of rock (a geological formation, an aquifer unit, an ore body), a surface (a fault plane, an unconformity surface, the water table), a line (a borehole path, a geological contact trace on a map), or a point (a borehole contact, a sample location). What distinguishes a geo-object from a generic GIS feature is its geological meaning: a geo-object is defined by its geological properties and its relationships to other geo-objects, not merely by its geometry.

The most important type of geo-object for groundwater applications is the **hydrogeological unit**: a body of rock or sediment that functions as a distinct entity in the groundwater system because of its hydraulic properties. Hydrogeological units include aquifers (formations that transmit economically significant quantities of water), aquitards (formations that retard water movement), and aquicludes (formations that are effectively impermeable). These units are defined not by their lithology alone but by their hydraulic behavior -- a formation of fine sand might be an aquifer in one context and an aquitard in another, depending on the hydraulic conductivities of the surrounding formations.

Each geo-object carries both geometric and attribute data. The geometric data defines the object's shape and position in 3D space -- its bounding surfaces, its volume, its spatial relationships to neighboring objects. The attribute data describes the object's properties: formation name, geological age, dominant lithology, hydraulic conductivity, specific storage, porosity, and any other parameters relevant to the application. In a geodatabase, the geometric data is stored in the feature's geometry field (typically as a multipatch for volumetric units or a PolygonZ for surfaces), and the attribute data is stored in the feature class's attribute table.

### 18.6.2 From Geo-Objects to Geomodels

A **geomodel** is a coherent assembly of geo-objects that together represent the three-dimensional structure of a defined portion of the subsurface (Apel 2006). The key word is "coherent" -- the geo-objects in a geomodel must satisfy geological consistency rules: volumes must not overlap, surfaces must not cross each other inappropriately, and the stratigraphic sequence must be honored. A geomodel is more than a collection of independent 3D features stored in the same geodatabase; it is an integrated model where each component is aware of and constrained by the others.

In database terms, a geomodel is typically implemented as a set of related feature classes within a geodatabase, connected by relationship classes or identifier attributes. The Arc Hydro Groundwater data model (Strassberg et al. 2011), which provides a standardized geodatabase schema for groundwater applications in ArcGIS, implements geomodels through a combination of feature classes and relationship classes:

- **GeoSection**: A feature class storing cross-section polygons (PolygonZ features), each representing a geological unit within a cross-section plane. Attributes include the formation name, the cross-section identifier, and display properties.
- **GeoVolume**: A feature class storing multipatch features representing the 3D volumes of geological units. Each feature corresponds to one hydrogeological unit.
- **BoreHole**: A feature class storing borehole paths as PolylineZ features.
- **HydrogeologicUnit**: A table defining the formations in the model, their properties, and their stratigraphic order.
- **GeoRaster**: Raster datasets representing the top and bottom surfaces of formations.

These feature classes are linked by common identifiers (HydroID in the Arc Hydro convention) so that a query on one component -- "show me all cross-sections through the Upper Pannonian sand" -- can retrieve the corresponding features from multiple feature classes.

### 18.6.3 Topological Relationships Between Geo-Objects

The relationships between geo-objects are as important as the objects themselves. In a well-constructed geomodel, the following topological relationships are maintained:

**Stratigraphic order.** Formations are stacked in a defined order, with younger formations overlying older ones (unless the sequence has been tectonically inverted). The top surface of one formation is typically the bottom surface of the overlying formation -- they are shared surfaces, not independent surfaces that might inadvertently separate or overlap.

**Fault relationships.** A fault surface divides the model into blocks that may be offset relative to each other. All formation surfaces and volumes on one side of the fault are displaced by the fault throw (the amount of vertical offset). The GSIS must maintain the relationship between faults and the formations they cut, so that when a fault's geometry is modified, all affected formations update accordingly.

**Lateral boundaries.** Formations do not extend indefinitely. They pinch out (thin to zero thickness), grade laterally into other formations (facies changes), or are truncated by erosion (unconformities). The geomodel must represent these lateral terminations correctly, which is one of the most challenging aspects of subsurface modeling.

**Nesting.** A geomodel may contain nested levels of detail. A regional model of the entire Great Hungarian Plain might represent the Pannonian sequence as a single unit; a local model of the Hajduszoboszlo geothermal field might subdivide the same Pannonian sequence into 15 individual sand and clay members. The GSIS should support this nesting, allowing the user to work at the appropriate level of detail for their application.

## 18.7 Visualization in 3D

A 3D model that cannot be effectively visualized provides little more insight than the raw borehole data from which it was constructed. Visualization is not an afterthought; it is an integral part of the subsurface modeling workflow, used at every stage to check the model against geological expectations, communicate results to non-specialists, and identify areas where additional data are needed.

### 18.7.1 3D Scene Views

The primary tool for visualizing subsurface models in GIS is the 3D scene view. In ArcGIS Pro, this is the **Local Scene** or **Global Scene**; in QGIS, it is the **3D Map View** introduced in version 3.0. Both allow the user to navigate freely through a three-dimensional representation of the data, rotating, zooming, and tilting the view to examine the model from any angle.

For subsurface visualization, the local scene is generally preferred over the global scene because subsurface models cover a limited area (typically kilometers to tens of kilometers) and the curvature of the earth is negligible at this scale. The local scene uses a flat, projected coordinate system and supports vertical exaggeration, which is essential for making thin geological layers visible.

The key display settings for subsurface data in a 3D scene include:

**Vertical exaggeration.** As noted in section 18.2.4, subsurface models are typically much wider than they are deep. A vertical exaggeration of 5x to 20x is standard for regional models; local models of thick formations may require only 2x to 5x. The vertical exaggeration setting is a property of the scene, not of individual layers, so all data in the scene are exaggerated equally.

**Transparency.** Rendering the upper geological layers with partial transparency (50 to 80 percent) allows the viewer to see through them to deeper structures. Without transparency, only the outermost surface of the model is visible -- the ground surface hides everything below it. Graduated transparency, where shallower layers are more transparent and deeper layers are more opaque, can create a striking visual effect that emphasizes the layered structure.

**Lighting.** Directional lighting enhances the perception of surface relief on geological boundaries. A light source placed at a low angle relative to the viewer creates shadows that accentuate folds, faults, and other structural features. Most 3D viewers allow the user to adjust the light direction and intensity interactively.

**Slicing.** An interactive slice plane that moves through the model, revealing its interior along a flat cross-section, is one of the most powerful visualization tools for subsurface data. In Leapfrog and similar GSIS software, the slice plane can be moved and rotated in real time, providing an animated "flythrough" of the model's interior. In ArcGIS Pro, a similar effect can be achieved using the Exploratory Analysis tools to create temporary cross-sections through multipatch features.

### 18.7.2 Cross-Sections and Fence Diagrams

While 3D perspective views are visually compelling, geologists and engineers often need to examine specific vertical slices through the model in detail. A **cross-section** is a vertical profile along a specified line across the study area, showing the geological structure as seen from the side. Cross-sections are the bread and butter of geological illustration -- they appear in every geological report, every borehole completion record, and every environmental impact assessment.

In a GIS context, cross-sections can be generated in two ways. The first is to draw the cross-section line on a map and extract the geological information from the 3D model along that line -- slicing the model with a vertical plane and displaying the result. The second is to construct the cross-section from borehole data by projecting boreholes within a specified distance of the section line onto the section plane and interpolating between them. The first approach requires a finished 3D model; the second is part of the model-building workflow described in section 18.5.3.

A **fence diagram** is a collection of intersecting cross-sections displayed together in 3D, creating a visual framework (like a garden fence, hence the name) that shows the geological structure along multiple transects simultaneously. Fence diagrams are particularly useful for presenting subsurface models to non-specialist audiences -- decision-makers, regulators, the public -- because they provide a tangible, intuitive representation of the underground structure. The viewer can see how geological layers vary from one cross-section to another, and the intersections between cross-sections provide visual verification that the interpretations are consistent.

### 18.7.3 Visualization Tools Beyond GIS

While ArcGIS Pro and QGIS provide basic 3D visualization capabilities, specialized visualization tools offer more advanced features for subsurface data. These tools are worth knowing about, even if they are not strictly GIS software.

**ParaView** (Kitware, open source) is a general-purpose 3D visualization platform originally developed for computational fluid dynamics but widely used in geosciences. It excels at rendering large volumetric datasets, supports advanced visual effects (volume rendering, iso-surfaces, streamlines), and can handle the millions of cells that characterize numerical groundwater models. ParaView reads common formats including VTK, NetCDF, and CSV, and it is freely available on all platforms.

**PyVista** is a Python library that provides a Pythonic interface to the VTK (Visualization Toolkit) library. It is lighter-weight than ParaView and integrates seamlessly into Python-based workflows. For the Python-comfortable user, PyVista offers a rapid way to visualize borehole data, geological surfaces, and model outputs without leaving the scripting environment.

**Leapfrog Viewer** (Seequent) is a free companion to Leapfrog Geo that allows anyone to view (but not edit) Leapfrog geological models. It is particularly useful for sharing 3D models with clients, regulators, and other stakeholders who do not have the full Leapfrog license. The viewer supports all of Leapfrog's visualization features, including transparency, slicing, and cross-sections, and models can be exported as compressed scene files for easy distribution.

**Web-based 3D viewers** are becoming increasingly common for disseminating subsurface models to broad audiences. Technologies like CesiumJS (for global-scale models) and Three.js (for local-scale models) enable interactive 3D visualization in a web browser without requiring the user to install any software. Several geological surveys, including the British Geological Survey (BGS) and the Geological Survey of the Netherlands (TNO), have published national-scale 3D geological models through web viewers.

## 18.8 Hungarian Case Study: Thermal Water Aquifers of the Great Hungarian Plain

Hungary sits on one of Europe's richest geothermal resources. The Pannonian Basin, a structural depression surrounded by the Alps, Carpathians, and Dinarides, is filled with kilometers of Neogene and Quaternary sediments and characterized by an anomalously high geothermal gradient -- approximately 45 to 50 millikelvins per meter, compared to the global average of about 30 mK/m. This means that water at 1,000 meters depth in the Great Hungarian Plain is typically 60 to 70 degrees Celsius, and at 2,000 meters it reaches 90 to 100 degrees Celsius. Exploiting this resource sustainably requires three-dimensional geological models of the kind this chapter describes.

### 18.8.1 The Pannonian Thermal Aquifer System

The primary thermal aquifer in the Great Hungarian Plain is the Upper Pannonian (Felso-Pannonian) formation, deposited between approximately 9 and 4 million years ago in Lake Pannon, the vast brackish lake that filled the Carpathian Basin during the late Miocene. As the lake gradually shrank and was replaced by rivers from the surrounding mountains, the sediment character changed: the deep-water marls of the Lower Pannonian gave way to deltaic and fluvial sands, silts, and clays of the Upper Pannonian. These sandy intervals within the Upper Pannonian are the thermal aquifer horizons.

The thermal aquifer system is not a single continuous layer of sand. It consists of numerous sand bodies -- individual channel fills, delta lobes, and sheet sands -- separated by clay and silt intervals that act as local confining layers. The geometry of these sand bodies is complex: they are lenticular (lens-shaped), laterally discontinuous, and variable in thickness. Two thermal wells drilled one kilometer apart may encounter entirely different sequences of sand and clay, because they penetrate different parts of different depositional systems.

This geological complexity makes 3D modeling essential. A simple layer-cake model (uniform horizontal layers of constant thickness) cannot capture the lenticular geometry of the sand bodies, the lateral facies changes, or the complex connectivity that controls how water flows from recharge areas to production wells. The Hungarian Geological and Geophysical Institute (now part of MBFSZ) has invested heavily in 3D geological modeling of the Pannonian Basin, using tens of thousands of borehole logs from the national database to construct regional models that capture the distribution of permeable and impermeable sediments.

### 18.8.2 Pannonian Basin Stratigraphy: A Multi-Layer Architecture

The subsurface of the Pannonian Basin is not a simple stack of uniform layers but a complex architecture reflecting more than 20 million years of tectonic subsidence, sedimentation, and erosion. For the hydroinformatician building a 3D model, understanding this stratigraphy is essential -- it defines the geo-objects that populate the geomodel, the surfaces that bound them, and the hydraulic properties assigned to each. Marton (2009) provides the most accessible synthesis of the Hungarian stratigraphic column from a hydrogeological perspective, which we summarize here in the framework relevant to GIS representation.

**Pre-Neogene basement (Paleozoic and Mesozoic).** The deepest unit in the Pannonian Basin consists of crystalline metamorphic rocks (granite, phyllite, schist, gneiss, amphibolite) and Mesozoic carbonates. The crystalline basement has negligible permeability and functions primarily as the impermeable floor of the basin, though it serves as an excellent heat conductor, elevating temperatures in the overlying sediments. The Mesozoic carbonate basement -- Triassic limestone and dolomite -- is a markedly different story. Where fractured and karstified, these carbonates form excellent aquifers. The Transdanubian Range (Dunantuli-kozephegyseg) exposes a northeast-southwest trending carbonate sequence reaching 4,000 to 5,000 meters in total thickness, which stores karst water and, at depth, so-called "paleo-karst water" in generally open systems (Marton, 2009; Korim, 1971). In the southeastern Great Plain, Mesozoic carbonates at 3,000 to 4,300 meters depth have yielded high-pressure thermal water at localities such as Fabiansebestyen and Nagyszenas, though these deep carbonate resources remain incompletely explored.

The basement surface is heavily tectonized, with horsts (sasbercek) and grabens (arkok) creating elevation differences that locally exceed 5,000 meters. This rugged paleotopography exerts a first-order control on the geometry of the overlying sedimentary fill and must be accurately represented in any 3D geomodel of the basin.

**Tertiary sediments: the Pannonian sequence.** Approximately 12 million years ago, the Pannonian Basin became isolated from the open sea as the surrounding Alpine-Carpathian-Dinaric mountain chains rose, transforming the former marine embayment into the vast brackish Lake Pannon. Rivers draining the surrounding mountains progressively filled this lake with enormous volumes of clastic sediment -- marls, turbiditic sandstones, deltaic sands, and fluvial deposits -- building prograding delta systems primarily from the northwest and northeast (Juhasz, 1998).

The resulting sedimentary fill, which ranges from 100 meters at the basin margins to more than 7,000 meters in the deepest sub-basins, displays a characteristic vertical progression reflecting the gradual shallowing of the lake. From bottom to top, the sequence comprises:

- **Lower Pannonian (Kunsagi stage, ~11.5--5.5 Ma):** Deep-water marls and turbiditic sandstones. The Endrod Formation consists of calcareous marls forming the regionally continuous *Endrod Aquitard*, with permeability less than 1 millidarcy (approximately 10^--15^ m^2^). This aquitard, found at variable depth between roughly 500 and 5,000 meters below sea level, divides the basin fill into an upper permeable and a lower permeable series (Toth and Almasi, 2001). The Szolnok Formation contains the *Szolnok Aquifer* -- sandstone bodies with permeability of 10 to 100 millidarcys, interspersed with aquitard lenses.

- **Upper Pannonian (Balatoni stage, ~5.5--2.4 Ma):** This is the principal thermal aquifer zone. The Torteli and Zagyvai Formations together constitute the *Nagyalfoldi Aquifer* (Great Plain Aquifer), a multi-storey system of sand bodies interbedded with clay and silt. The depth of this aquifer system ranges from 400 to 2,500 meters depending on the position within the basin's sub-basins (Marton, 2009). Individual sand bodies typically take the form of sheet sands (2 to 25 meters thick with large lateral extent) or lenticular channel fills of highly variable dimensions. Many individual sand bodies merge laterally, creating hydraulically connected composite reservoirs that function as unified thermal water storage units. Thin (1 to 5 meters) sand layers separated by clay interbeds form "sandwich-type" thermal reservoirs (Korim, 1981). In the Transdanubian region, 74 percent of wells draw water from these Pannonian porous formations; on the Great Plain, the figure reaches 95 percent (Urbancsek, 1973).

- **Uppermost Pliocene (Levantei stage):** A distinctive unit of variegated clays, silts, and sands deposited in the final phases of lacustrine-fluvial transition. In the Jaszsag and Koros depression, this unit reaches 300 to 500 meters in uniform clayey facies; along the Danube structural trench, the equivalent deposits are sandier and serve as productive aquifers.

**Quaternary sediments.** The youngest 2.4 million years of deposition produced the surficial layer of fluvial, lacustrine, and aeolian sediments that blanket the basin. Several sub-basins developed, each with its own depositional history. The Lower Pleistocene is dominated by coarse-grained fluvial deposits -- the best shallow aquifer horizons. The Middle Pleistocene saw reduced subsidence rates and increased deposition of clayey and silty layers. The Upper Pleistocene records a renewed tectonic pulse with additional sandy deposition, though well yields from this horizon are lower than from the deeper Pleistocene sands. In the Nyirseg region, recent reinterpretation of well-log data has revised the Quaternary-Pannonian boundary significantly: at Nyiracsad, for example, the Quaternary extends to 130 meters depth, underlain by Pliocene deposits to 250 meters and Upper Pannonian below 250 meters (Marton, 2009). Nationally, 80 percent of drilled wells are completed in Quaternary formations (Urbancsek, 1973).

### 18.8.3 The Geothermal Anomaly: Heat Flow and Temperature at Depth

The Pannonian Basin is one of the world's geothermally most anomalous continental regions. The explanation lies in its tectonic history: during the Miocene extensional phase, the lithosphere thinned dramatically. In the Bekes sub-basin, the crust-mantle boundary (Moho) rises to only 22 kilometers depth, and the lithosphere-asthenosphere boundary lies at approximately 40 kilometers -- far shallower than the continental average of 100 to 150 kilometers. The asthenosphere (the ductile, partially molten zone of the upper mantle) bulges upward beneath the thinned lithosphere, bringing anomalously high heat flow close to the surface (Bada and Horvath, 1998; Marton, 2009).

The consequence for hydrogeology is profound. Marton (2009) reports the geothermal gradient formula used in Hungarian practice:

$$G = \frac{t_h - t_0}{h_a}$$

where $G$ is the geothermal gradient (degrees C per meter), $t_0$ is the temperature at the "neutral zone" (approximately 25 meters depth, where seasonal fluctuations cease), $t_h$ is the temperature measured at depth $h_a$, and $h_a$ is the measurement depth in meters below the neutral zone. In the Pannonian Basin, typical values of $G$ range from 0.045 to 0.055 degrees C per meter -- that is, 45 to 55 degrees C per kilometer of depth. The global continental average is approximately 25 to 30 degrees C per kilometer. Hungary's geothermal gradient is thus roughly **double the global average**.

The reciprocal of the gradient is the **geothermal step** (geotermikus lepcso): the depth interval required for a one-degree Celsius temperature increase. In Hungary, this is typically 18 to 22 meters per degree C, compared to the global average of 33 to 40 meters per degree C.

The practical consequence is that economically useful thermal water is available at much shallower depths in Hungary than in most other countries:

The surface heat flow in the Pannonian Basin averages 80 to 100 mW/m^2^, compared to the global continental average of approximately 65 mW/m^2^. In localized areas -- particularly above basement horsts where the crystalline rocks conduct heat efficiently upward, or along active fault zones where convective heat transport occurs -- heat flow can exceed 120 mW/m^2^.

For 3D GIS, the geothermal gradient adds a critical fourth dimension to the subsurface model. Temperature is not merely a property of interest; it directly affects water density and viscosity, and therefore the hydraulic behavior of the aquifer system. Hot water is less viscous and less dense than cold water, which means that hydraulic conductivity calculated from laboratory permeability measurements at 20 degrees C will underestimate the actual conductivity of an aquifer at 80 degrees C. Marton (2009) demonstrates that a 15-degree C decrease in water temperature reduces the hydraulic conductivity -- and thus the well yield -- by approximately one-third, solely due to the change in viscosity. A 3D geomodel that includes temperature as a spatially variable property allows this effect to be accounted for in well design and resource assessment.

### 18.8.4 The Multi-Tier Groundwater Flow System

The groundwater flow system of the Pannonian Basin is not a single circulation pattern but a superposition of flow systems operating at different depths, driven by different forces, and operating on different timescales. The foundational theoretical framework for understanding these nested systems was provided by Toth (1963), whose concept of local, intermediate, and regional flow systems in topography-driven basins (introduced in Chapter 9 of Marton, 2009) finds its most dramatic real-world expression in the Pannonian Basin.

Toth and Almasi (2001) produced the most comprehensive analysis to date of the Pannonian Basin's flow systems, based on 53,000 pore-pressure and water-level measurements from 1,274 deep boreholes and 16,192 water-level records. Their analysis, synthesized by Marton (2009), reveals a three-tier flow architecture:

**Tier 1: Shallow gravity-driven flow (0 to ~500 m).** In the upper 200 to 500 meters -- the Quaternary and uppermost Pannonian sediments -- groundwater flow is driven by topographic gradients, exactly as Toth's (1963) theory predicts. Water infiltrates at topographic highs (the Danube-Tisza Interfluve ridge, the Godollo Hills, the Nyirseg upland) and flows toward topographic lows (the Danube valley, the Tisza floodplain, the Koros rivers). Potentiometric surfaces at these depths closely follow the land surface topography. This is a classic gravity-driven, regionally open flow system with identifiable recharge areas, discharge areas, and a lateral flow zone at approximately 100 meters elevation along the margins of the Danube-Tisza ridge.

**Tier 2: Intermediate transitional flow (~500 to 1,500 m).** In this depth range, the transition from gravity-driven to overpressured flow occurs. Potentiometric surfaces begin to diverge from the surface topography. In the depth interval between 300 and 600 meters, potential values still generally decrease with depth beneath topographic highs and increase beneath lows, but a new phenomenon appears: isolated potentiometric highs ("pressure islands") that do not correspond to surface topography. These represent the upward influence of the deep overpressured system forcing water through permeable pathways -- faults, fracture zones, and locally permeable sand lenses.

**Tier 3: Deep overpressured flow (below ~1,500 m).** Below approximately 600 to 1,000 meters depth (and deepening toward the basin center), pore pressures become strongly superhydrostatic -- that is, they significantly exceed the pressure expected from the weight of the overlying water column. In the depth range of 1,000 to 1,500 meters, three major overpressured zones are identifiable: near Szolnok (excess hydraulic head of 150 to 200 meters), near Biharkeresztes (elongated pressure ridge with excess head exceeding 500 to 800 meters), and near Battonya (excess head of approximately 300 meters). At depths of 2,200 to 2,500 meters, the entire study area is divided into a strongly overpressured "highland" in the west and northwest (Szolnok, Karcag, Szarvas, Tiszakecske -- excess head commonly exceeding 1,000 meters, locally reaching 2,000 meters) and a moderately overpressured "basin" in the east and southeast. Below 2,500 meters, observed excess hydraulic heads routinely reach and exceed 3,000 meters (Toth and Almasi, 2001; Marton, 2009).

The driving forces behind the deep overpressured system include tectonic compression (the Adria microplate rotates counter-clockwise and pushes northward against the Pannonian Basin at approximately 0.2 degrees per million years), sediment compaction from the enormous overburden load, and possibly clay mineral dehydration at elevated temperatures. The overpressured fluids escape upward along faults and fracture zones that penetrate the Endrodi Aquitard, creating pressure "plumes" and "ridges" that extend into shallower formations.

This multi-tier system has direct implications for 3D GIS modeling:

- The geomodel must include not only the geological formations but also the fault network, because faults serve as hydraulic conduits between the deep overpressured system and the shallow gravity-driven system.
- The potentiometric surface cannot be represented as a single map -- it varies dramatically with depth, and in the deep system it bears no relationship to surface topography.
- Water chemistry varies systematically between the tiers: the shallow system contains fresh, recently recharged water (low chloride, negative delta-18-O values indicating Holocene precipitation); the deep system contains ancient, mineralized water with high chloride content. In the Koros Basin, upward leakage of Pannonian water mixes with pre-glacial Quaternary water to produce characteristic intermediate chemistries (Marton, 2009).

### 18.8.5 Hydraulic Parameters of Hungarian Formations

Populating a 3D geomodel with realistic hydraulic properties requires measured values of permeability, hydraulic conductivity, porosity, and storage coefficient for each hydrostratigraphic unit. The Hungarian hydrogeological literature, synthesized by Marton (2009), provides an unusually rich dataset of formation-specific parameters, reflecting more than a century of drilling and testing in the Pannonian Basin.

**Permeability and porosity by depth.** The most systematic compilation of Pannonian Basin aquifer properties is that of Korim (1981), who established the following depth-dependent relationships for the Upper Pannonian thermal aquifer sands:

- At approximately 1,000 meters depth: permeability of 1 to 2 darcy (approximately 10^--12^ m^2^), porosity of 30 to 35 percent
- Below 2,000 meters depth: permeability drops to tens of millidarcys (approximately 10^--14^ m^2^), porosity decreases to 10 to 20 percent
- In deep Miocene sandstones: permeability of 10 to 50 millidarcys, porosity of 5 to 10 percent

The decline in both permeability and porosity with depth reflects compaction under the increasing weight of the overburden. Stegena (1972) documented this compaction trend for Hungarian sandstones, and Blatt (1979) confirmed a linear porosity-depth relationship in late Tertiary sandstones from Louisiana -- from 38 percent at the surface to approximately 18 percent at 6,000 meters. The Pannonian Basin values are broadly consistent with these global trends but tend toward the higher end, reflecting the relatively young age and incomplete compaction of the basin fill.

**The Endrodi Aquitard** -- the regionally continuous Lower Pannonian marl that separates the upper and lower permeable series -- has permeability less than 1 millidarcy (less than 10^--15^ m^2^). Despite its low permeability, this unit is not perfectly impermeable: slow vertical leakage through it over geological timescales contributes to the overpressured conditions in the deep system.

**Permeability anisotropy.** A critical parameter for 3D flow modeling is the ratio of horizontal to vertical permeability. For Pannonian sands and sandstones, Erdelyi (1966) reported that vertical permeability is 30 to 50 percent lower than horizontal permeability. An oil industry study analyzing "several thousand core-sample measurements" found that the overall anisotropy of the Pannonian marl section was approximately one order of magnitude -- that is, horizontal permeability roughly ten times greater than vertical -- over the depth interval of 1 to 2 kilometers. Below 2.5 kilometers, permeability in all directions drops uniformly below 0.1 millidarcy (Toth and Almasi, 2001; Marton, 2009).

### 18.8.6 Geothermal Utilization and the Need for 3D Models

Hungary has approximately 1,400 thermal wells, of which about 250 are used for balneological purposes (thermal spas), 200 for agricultural heating (greenhouses and animal husbandry), and the remainder for municipal heating, industrial processes, and bottled mineral water production. The total thermal water production exceeds 300 million cubic meters per year, making Hungary one of the world's top geothermal energy users for direct heat applications.

The two principal thermal aquifer rock groups are the Upper Pannonian porous sands (supplying 60 percent of all thermal wells producing water above 30 degrees C) and the Mesozoic fractured carbonate rocks -- Triassic limestone and dolomite (Marton, 2009). The Lower Pannonian sandstones contribute only about 3 percent of the thermal well inventory, reflecting both the low permeability of the Endrodi Aquitard that must be penetrated to reach them and the generally tighter, more compacted character of the deeper formations.

Marton (2009) and Rezessy, Szanyi, and Hamor (2005) note that at 4 to 5 kilometers depth in the Mesozoic basement, high-enthalpy "hot dry rocks" may be suitable for engineered geothermal systems capable of electricity generation -- a resource frontier that remains largely unexplored in Hungary but represents a significant future opportunity.

Several iconic Hungarian thermal facilities illustrate the practical importance of 3D subsurface modeling and demonstrate the range of geological settings that must be captured in a geomodel:

**Hajduszoboszlo.** The Hajduszoboszlo thermal spa -- one of Europe's largest -- is supplied by wells tapping the Upper Pannonian multi-storey aquifer at depths of 1,000 to 1,100 meters, where water temperatures reach 73 to 75 degrees Celsius. The thermal water was discovered accidentally in 1925 during a hydrocarbon exploration drilling campaign. The producing formation consists of multiple sheet-sand bodies (each 2 to 25 meters thick) within the Upper Pannonian sequence, separated by clay interbeds that provide partial confinement. The lenticular geometry of these sand bodies -- laterally discontinuous, locally merging, and variable in thickness -- means that two wells drilled one kilometer apart may encounter entirely different sand-clay sequences, highlighting why a simple layer-cake model is inadequate and why a true 3D geomodel capturing the heterogeneity of the reservoir is essential (Marton, 2009).

Over-extraction from the growing number of production wells has caused water levels in the thermal aquifer to decline by several meters over recent decades, raising concerns about long-term sustainability. The recharge of the deep thermal system is extremely slow -- radiocarbon dating indicates water ages of thousands to tens of thousands of years -- meaning that current extraction rates far exceed natural replenishment. A 3D geological model of the Hajduszoboszlo geothermal field is essential for understanding the aquifer's geometry, estimating its total storage volume, and predicting the long-term response to continued extraction.

**Heviz.** The Heviz thermal lake, located near Lake Balaton in western Hungary, is the world's largest biologically active natural thermal lake (area approximately 4.4 hectares). It is fed by thermal springs that discharge from a deep Triassic carbonate aquifer through a geological fault zone. The water temperature at the spring orifice is approximately 38 degrees Celsius year-round. Unlike the Pannonian sand aquifers of the Great Plain, the Heviz system involves karst hydrogeology -- water flows through dissolution cavities and fractures in the northeast-southwest trending Triassic limestone and dolomite sequence of the Transdanubian Range, which reaches 4,000 to 5,000 meters in total thickness (Marton, 2009; Korim, 1971). The aquifer functions as a generally open karst system, but the flow paths are controlled by fault geometry rather than by porous-media permeability. A 3D model of the fault system and the karst aquifer is essential for protecting the Heviz spring from over-exploitation by nearby thermal wells. The Hungarian government has designated a protection zone around the lake, and any proposed new drilling within this zone must demonstrate, through 3D geological and hydrogeological modeling, that it will not reduce the spring discharge.

**Szeged.** The city of Szeged in southern Hungary operates one of Hungary's largest geothermal district heating systems, supplying thermal water at 80 to 98 degrees Celsius from wells at 1,800 to 2,000 meters depth. At these depths, the Upper Pannonian aquifer sands have permeability of only tens of millidarcys and porosity of 10 to 20 percent (compared to 1 to 2 darcys and 30 to 35 percent at 1,000 meters) -- a direct consequence of compaction under the greater overburden (Korim, 1981; Marton, 2009). The system includes reinjection wells that return the cooled water to the aquifer after heat extraction, maintaining aquifer pressure and reducing wastewater disposal. Planning the placement and operation of production and reinjection wells requires a detailed 3D model of the aquifer geometry and flow paths, to ensure that the cooled reinjection water does not reach the production wells prematurely (a phenomenon called thermal breakthrough that would reduce the system's efficiency). The Szeged system also illustrates an environmental challenge noted by Marton (2009): the mineral-laden thermal wastewater can increase soil salinity to levels that make agricultural land unsuitable for irrigation, requiring careful management of surface disposal.

### 18.8.7 The MBFSZ National 3D Geological Model

The Mining and Geological Survey of Hungary (MBFSZ) has developed and maintains a national-scale 3D geological model covering the entire territory of Hungary. The model is constructed from the national borehole database (approximately 200,000 records), geological maps at various scales, seismic reflection profiles from petroleum exploration, and surface geological observations.

The model divides the subsurface into major geological units based on the national stratigraphic framework. For the Great Hungarian Plain, these units include the Quaternary (subdivided into Holocene and Pleistocene), the Upper Pannonian (subdivided into several members based on lithological character), the Lower Pannonian, and the pre-Neogene basement (subdivided by rock type: crystalline metamorphic, Paleozoic carbonates, Mesozoic carbonates, and Mesozoic flysch). Each unit is represented as a volume bounded by interpolated top and bottom surfaces.

The model serves multiple purposes: identifying favorable areas for geothermal development, assessing groundwater vulnerability, planning infrastructure projects that require deep excavation, and supporting mineral resource assessment. It is also used as the geological framework for the national groundwater flow model maintained by the Hungarian Academy of Sciences, which simulates regional groundwater flow patterns and thermal water circulation throughout the Pannonian Basin.

The MBFSZ model illustrates both the power and the limitations of national-scale 3D geological modeling. Its strength lies in providing a consistent, quality-controlled geological framework covering the entire country -- something that no individual project-level study can achieve. Its limitation is resolution: at the national scale, the model cannot capture the fine-scale heterogeneity (individual sand bodies, local fault splays) that controls groundwater flow at the field scale. For detailed project work, the national model serves as a starting point that is refined with local data.

## 18.9 International Perspective: Global Aquifer Mapping

While Hungary has invested decades in mapping its subsurface, much of the world's groundwater remains poorly characterized. Globally, groundwater provides drinking water to approximately 2.5 billion people and irrigation water to nearly 40 percent of the world's irrigated agriculture. Yet for vast areas of Africa, Asia, and South America, even basic information about aquifer geometry, depth, and productivity is unavailable. This section examines international initiatives to address this knowledge gap using the 3D GIS techniques described in this chapter.

### 18.9.1 The World Hydrogeological Map (WHYMAP)

The World-wide Hydrogeological Mapping and Assessment Programme (WHYMAP), coordinated by the Federal Institute for Geosciences and Natural Resources of Germany (BGR) in cooperation with UNESCO, maintains the global-scale map of the world's major groundwater resources. WHYMAP provides a set of map products at 1:25,000,000 scale showing the distribution of major groundwater basins, areas of complex hydrogeological structure, and regions with limited groundwater resources.

WHYMAP's contribution to 3D subsurface GIS is primarily conceptual: it provides the global framework that identifies which aquifer systems exist and where. The actual 3D characterization must be done at regional or national scale. But WHYMAP's Transboundary Aquifer Map, which identifies groundwater systems that cross international boundaries, has been instrumental in initiating cooperative 3D mapping efforts between neighboring countries. In Europe, the shared aquifer systems of the Pannonian Basin (extending across Hungary, Serbia, Romania, Slovakia, and Croatia) are among the transboundary systems identified by WHYMAP, and their 3D characterization has been a focus of EU-funded research projects.

### 18.9.2 The EU Groundwater Directive and 3D Mapping

The European Union's Water Framework Directive (WFD, 2000/60/EC) and its daughter Groundwater Directive (2006/118/EC) require EU member states to identify groundwater bodies, assess their quantitative and chemical status, and implement measures to prevent deterioration. Compliance with these directives requires a level of hydrogeological understanding that effectively demands 3D models, even if the directives do not explicitly mandate them.

Hungary has delineated 185 groundwater bodies under the WFD framework, grouped into porous (unconsolidated sediment), karstic (carbonate), and mixed types. Each groundwater body is defined by its horizontal extent (a polygon on a map) and its vertical boundaries (top and bottom elevation surfaces). The delineation and characterization of these groundwater bodies for the River Basin Management Plans required precisely the kind of 3D geological modeling described in this chapter.

At the EU level, the European Geological Data Infrastructure (EGDI), maintained by EuroGeoSurveys (the association of European geological surveys), provides access to harmonized geological data across Europe. The EGDI portal includes borehole data, geological maps, and -- increasingly -- 3D geological models from national surveys. The GeoERA programme (Establishing the European Geological Surveys Research Area, 2018--2021) funded several projects specifically aimed at cross-border 3D geological modeling, including the HOVER project on groundwater vulnerability and the RESOURCE project on mineral and groundwater resources.

### 18.9.3 The Africa Groundwater Atlas

In contrast to Europe's data-rich environment, many developing regions face severe data scarcity for subsurface mapping. The Africa Groundwater Atlas, developed by the British Geological Survey (BGS) in partnership with the African Union and national geological surveys, represents a major effort to compile and disseminate the available hydrogeological information for the African continent.

The Atlas provides country-by-country summaries of aquifer types, productivity, depth to water, and water quality, along with hydrogeological maps at the continental and national scales. While the Atlas is primarily a 2D product -- the data density in most African countries is far too low to support 3D modeling at any meaningful resolution -- it lays the groundwork for future 3D efforts by identifying the major aquifer systems and compiling the borehole data that do exist.

The data scarcity problem in Africa is extreme. Many countries have fewer than one borehole per 100 square kilometers, and many of those boreholes are shallow hand-dug wells with no geological logging. Satellite gravity data from the GRACE and GRACE-FO missions (discussed in Chapter 3) can detect changes in total water storage at continental scale, but they cannot resolve individual aquifer layers or provide the kind of structural detail needed for 3D modeling. The most promising path forward combines targeted borehole drilling (prioritized by geophysical surveys), airborne geophysical mapping (which can cover large areas quickly), and the 3D interpolation techniques described in this chapter.

## 18.10 Voxel Models: The 3D Raster

While multipatch features represent subsurface volumes as discrete objects with defined boundaries, an alternative approach represents the subsurface as a continuous three-dimensional grid of cells, each cell carrying a property value. This is the **voxel model** (from "volume element," by analogy with "pixel" for "picture element"), and it is the 3D equivalent of the raster grids discussed in Chapter 5.

### 18.10.1 Concept and Structure

A voxel model divides a three-dimensional region into a regular grid of rectangular cells (voxels). Each voxel is defined by its position (row, column, layer) in the grid and its size (dx, dy, dz). Every voxel carries one or more attribute values: lithology code, hydraulic conductivity, porosity, temperature, contaminant concentration, or any other property that varies through the subsurface.

The relationship between voxel models and 2D rasters is direct. A 2D raster divides a horizontal area into rows and columns of equal-sized cells; a voxel model adds layers in the vertical dimension, creating a three-dimensional array of cells. Where a 2D raster has dimensions (nrows, ncols), a voxel model has dimensions (nrows, ncols, nlayers). Where a 2D raster cell is identified by (row, col), a voxel is identified by (row, col, layer). Where a 2D raster stores one value per cell, a voxel stores one value per cell -- but the cell exists in 3D space.

Voxel models can use either regular spacing (all voxels have the same dimensions in x, y, and z) or variable spacing (voxels have different sizes in different parts of the model). Variable spacing is common in groundwater modeling, where the mesh is refined near wells and coarsened far from features of interest. The MODFLOW grid (Chapter 21) is essentially a voxel model with variable layer thicknesses that conform to the geometry of hydrogeological units.

### 18.10.2 Voxels Versus Multipatches

The choice between voxel representation and multipatch (geo-object) representation reflects a fundamental tension in subsurface modeling that mirrors the raster-versus-vector debate in 2D GIS (Chapter 5).

**Multipatches (geo-objects)** represent the subsurface as a collection of discrete volumes, each corresponding to a geological unit. This representation is natural for geological thinking -- geologists conceptualize the subsurface as a set of formations with distinct boundaries. It is efficient for storage (only the boundaries need to be stored, not the contents of each volume) and supports topological queries (which formation is adjacent to which?). Its limitation is that it assumes sharp boundaries between units and uniform properties within each unit -- assumptions that are often violated in reality. The sand content of a Pannonian formation may grade continuously from 80 percent near the depositional axis to 20 percent at the margins, and no single hydraulic conductivity value adequately represents this variation.

**Voxels** represent the subsurface as a continuous field of property values, with no explicit boundaries between units. This representation is natural for numerical modeling -- simulation codes like MODFLOW assign properties to individual cells, not to discrete geological objects. It captures spatial variability within formations (each cell can have a different hydraulic conductivity) and naturally supports the kind of cell-by-cell calculations that flow simulation requires. Its limitation is storage: a voxel model that covers 100 km x 100 km x 2 km at 100 m x 100 m x 10 m resolution contains 200 million cells, each requiring multiple property values. At eight bytes per property per cell, a model with five properties occupies eight gigabytes -- manageable, but large enough to strain desktop GIS software.

In practice, both representations are used. The geological modeling phase (GSIS workflow, sections 18.5--18.6) works with geo-objects and multipatches to capture the geological structure. The numerical modeling phase (Chapter 21) converts this structure into a voxel grid by assigning property values to each cell based on which geo-object it falls within. The two representations are complementary, not competing.

ArcGIS Pro has supported voxel layers natively since version 2.9, allowing users to visualize 3D grids directly in the GIS environment. Voxel layers can be sliced, filtered by value, rendered with transparency, and combined with other 2D and 3D data in the same scene. This capability bridges the gap between geological modeling (multipatch-based) and numerical simulation (voxel-based) by allowing both representations to be viewed and queried in a single environment.

### 18.10.3 Creating Voxel Models from Borehole Data

The most common workflow for creating a voxel model of the subsurface starts with borehole data and proceeds through interpolation, much like the surface-creation workflow described earlier but extended to three dimensions.

**Step 1: Define the voxel grid.** Specify the horizontal extent (matching the study area boundary), the vertical extent (from the ground surface to the deepest formation of interest), and the cell dimensions. For a regional model of the Great Hungarian Plain, typical dimensions might be 250 m x 250 m x 10 m, yielding a manageable number of cells while capturing the major geological features.

**Step 2: Extract borehole property data.** For each borehole interval (each segment between consecutive contacts), extract the property of interest -- for example, the dominant lithology code or the estimated hydraulic conductivity based on grain-size analysis. Assign this value to all voxels that the borehole interval passes through.

**Step 3: Interpolate between boreholes.** Use 3D interpolation to estimate property values at voxels that are not penetrated by any borehole. Common methods include 3D kriging (the extension of the 2D kriging discussed in Chapter 8 to three dimensions), sequential Gaussian simulation (which produces multiple equally probable realizations of the property field, capturing the uncertainty inherent in interpolation from sparse data), and indicator kriging (which estimates the probability that each voxel belongs to a particular lithological category).

**Step 4: Validate.** Compare the interpolated property values at withheld boreholes (boreholes not used in the interpolation) to the observed values. Calculate error statistics (mean error, root mean squared error, correlation coefficient) to assess the interpolation quality. If the errors are unacceptably large, refine the interpolation parameters (variogram model, search radius) or increase the data density by incorporating additional information (geological maps, geophysical surveys).

The resulting voxel model is a complete three-dimensional description of the subsurface property field, ready for use in numerical groundwater simulation or for visualization in the 3D scene viewer.

## 18.11 Connecting 3D GIS to Groundwater Modeling

The 3D data structures described in this chapter -- PointZ, PolylineZ, PolygonZ, multipatch, and voxel -- are not ends in themselves. Their ultimate purpose, in a groundwater context, is to provide the geometric and property framework for numerical simulation models. This connection between 3D GIS and groundwater modeling is the bridge from this chapter (static 3D representation) to Chapter 20 (building the model conceptualization) and Chapter 21 (running the simulation).

### 18.11.1 From Geomodel to Simulation Grid

A numerical groundwater model (such as MODFLOW, FEFLOW, or HydroGeoSphere) requires a discretized grid where each cell has defined dimensions and assigned hydraulic properties. The geomodel provides the geological framework -- the geometry and identity of each hydrogeological unit -- that guides this discretization. The conversion process involves:

1. **Grid generation.** Creating a computational grid (structured or unstructured) that conforms to the boundaries of the hydrogeological units defined in the geomodel. Each unit becomes one or more layers in the grid, with the layer top and bottom elevations taken from the interpolated surfaces in the geomodel.

2. **Property assignment.** Assigning hydraulic conductivity, storage coefficient, porosity, and other parameters to each cell based on the hydrogeological unit it belongs to. Initial values come from the geomodel's attribute data; these are refined during model calibration (Chapter 21).

3. **Boundary condition specification.** Defining where water enters and leaves the model (recharge, rivers, wells, springs) by combining the geomodel geometry with hydrological data. A river that intersects the outcrop zone of an aquifer becomes a recharge boundary; a fault that connects two aquifer units becomes an internal flow pathway.

The Arc Hydro Groundwater toolset in ArcGIS Pro provides tools that automate much of this conversion, exporting geomodel surfaces and property data in formats that MODFLOW and other simulation codes can read directly. In the open-source world, the FloPy Python library provides a scripting interface for building MODFLOW models, and it can read geological surfaces from standard raster formats (GeoTIFF, ASCII grid) to define model layer geometries.

### 18.11.2 The Role of Uncertainty

Every 3D geological model is uncertain. The borehole data are sparse, the interpolation between boreholes is an estimate (not a measurement), and the geological interpretation involves subjective judgment. This uncertainty propagates into the groundwater simulation: different plausible geological models produce different flow predictions, different capture zones, and different sustainability assessments.

Quantifying this uncertainty is an active area of research and an increasingly common requirement in professional practice. The approach is typically stochastic: generate many (hundreds to thousands) of equally plausible geological models, run the groundwater simulation on each, and analyze the resulting ensemble of predictions. If 95 percent of the models predict that the thermal aquifer at Hajduszoboszlo will maintain adequate water levels for the next 50 years under the proposed extraction rate, the assessment is much more robust than if only a single model prediction is available.

GSIS software like Leapfrog supports this approach through its ability to rapidly regenerate models when input data or interpretive choices change. GemPy, being fully scriptable, is particularly well-suited to stochastic geological modeling because the model-generation process can be automated in a loop.

## 18.12 Practical Guidance: Building Your First 3D Subsurface Model

This section provides practical guidance for readers who want to construct a 3D subsurface model using the tools and concepts described in this chapter. The advice is organized as a sequence of decisions and recommendations based on the experience of hydrogeological practitioners.

### 18.12.1 Choosing Your Tools

The choice of software depends on your budget, your data, and your intended application.

**For a student project or academic research with limited budget:** Start with GemPy (free, Python-based, well-documented). Prepare your borehole data in CSV format (x, y, z, formation name), define your stratigraphic sequence, and let GemPy's implicit modeling engine generate the 3D model. Visualize in PyVista or export surfaces to QGIS 3D.

**For a professional project with ArcGIS licenses:** Use the Arc Hydro Groundwater toolset within ArcGIS Pro. It provides a standardized geodatabase schema for groundwater data, tools for creating Z-enabled features from borehole data, surface interpolation, cross-section construction, and export to MODFLOW. The 3D scene view in ArcGIS Pro handles visualization.

**For a project requiring detailed geological interpretation:** Invest in Leapfrog Geo or GMS. These provide the interactive 3D editing and rapid model updating that are essential for complex geology. The cost is significant (several thousand euros per year for a commercial license), but the productivity gain for a professional hydrogeological consultancy is substantial.

### 18.12.2 Preparing Your Data

The single most important factor in the quality of a 3D geological model is the quality and consistency of the input borehole data. Before you begin modeling, invest time in data preparation:

**Standardize formation names.** In the Hungarian national borehole database, the same geological formation may be described differently by different drillers: "Upper Pannonian sand," "felsopannon homok," "Fp homok," and "pannonian sand" may all refer to the same unit. Create a lookup table that maps all variant names to a single standardized name, and apply it to your dataset before importing it into the GSIS.

**Check coordinates.** Verify that all borehole coordinates are in the same coordinate system and that the values are reasonable. For Hungarian data in EOV, check that Northing values fall in the range 0--400,000 and Easting values in the range 400,000--1,000,000. Boreholes that plot in the Adriatic Sea or in Ukraine have coordinate errors.

**Reconcile depths and elevations.** Ensure that all stratigraphic contacts are referenced to the same vertical datum. Convert depth-below-surface values to elevation-above-sea-level using the ground surface elevation from a DEM or from the borehole record.

**Assess data density.** Map your borehole locations and assess whether the density is sufficient for the resolution you need. If you have 20 boreholes spread across 500 square kilometers, you can construct a regional-scale model of major geological units but cannot resolve individual sand bodies. If you need higher resolution, look for additional data sources: geophysical surveys, published cross-sections, geological maps, or data from neighboring projects.

### 18.12.3 Common Pitfalls

**Ignoring vertical exaggeration when interpreting results.** It is remarkably easy to look at a vertically exaggerated cross-section and unconsciously assume that geological layers are steeper, thicker, and more dramatically structured than they actually are. Always check the exaggeration factor and mentally adjust your interpretation.

**Over-interpreting sparse data.** With 10 boreholes spread across 100 square kilometers, the geological model between boreholes is almost entirely interpolation -- it reflects the mathematical algorithm more than the actual geology. Resist the temptation to present a smooth, beautifully rendered model as "truth." It is one possible interpretation, and the uncertainty is high.

**Forgetting the Z datum.** Combining data from different sources with different vertical datums (Baltic, NAVD88, EGM2008, depth-below-surface) without proper conversion is the 3D equivalent of combining data in different map projections without reprojection. The results look plausible but are wrong.

**Neglecting stratigraphic rules.** An interpolation algorithm does not know that the Quaternary must lie above the Pannonian. If your interpolated surfaces cross each other (younger formation surface dipping below older formation surface), the model is geologically impossible. Check for and correct surface intersections before building volumes.

## 18.13 Looking Forward

The 3D subsurface GIS techniques described in this chapter provide the geometric foundation for everything that follows in Part V. Chapter 19 will examine the specific features used to represent wells and boreholes -- the primary data sources for subsurface characterization -- and the aquifer maps that synthesize borehole data into regional-scale descriptions of groundwater systems. Chapter 20 will describe how the 3D geomodel is populated with hydraulic properties and boundary conditions to create a complete conceptual model of a groundwater system. Chapter 21 will show how this conceptual model is translated into a numerical simulation that predicts groundwater flow, contaminant transport, and the response of the system to pumping, recharge, and climate change.

The progression from this chapter to the next three mirrors the progression from geometry to physics: here we have defined the shape of the underground, and in the chapters that follow we will bring it to life with the equations that govern how water moves through it. The 3D data structures -- PointZ for borehole contacts, PolylineZ for borehole paths, PolygonZ for cross-sections, multipatch for volumes, and voxels for property fields -- are the language in which we write our description of the subsurface. Learning this language is the necessary first step toward understanding, predicting, and managing the hidden water that sustains much of human civilization.

The connection between 3D subsurface representation and the broader themes of this book is worth emphasizing. In Part II, we learned to read the landscape from raster data -- elevation, slope, flow direction. In Part III, we followed water downhill across that landscape, delineating watersheds and stream networks. In Part IV, we mapped the consequences when water exceeds the landscape's capacity -- flood inundation, emergency response. Now, in Part V, we follow the water underground, into a domain where the "landscape" is three-dimensional, the "streams" are invisible, and the "maps" must show not just area but depth. The conceptual framework is the same -- spatial data, interpolation, modeling, prediction -- but the dimensionality is fundamentally different, and the tools must rise to meet that challenge.
