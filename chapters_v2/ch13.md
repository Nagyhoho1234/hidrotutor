# Chapter 13: Seeing the Ground in 3D: LiDAR

Every analysis described in this book so far has depended on knowing the shape of the ground. We used digital elevation models to compute slope and aspect (Chapter 7), to determine which way water flows (Chapter 10), to delineate watersheds (Chapter 11), and to calculate the height of any point above the nearest river (Chapter 12). But where do these elevation numbers actually come from? For most of the twentieth century, the answer was surveying and photogrammetry applied to aerial photographs -- painstaking work that produced contour maps at scales of 1:24,000 or 1:25,000, from which digital elevation models were later interpolated. The resulting DEMs, at resolutions of 10 to 90 meters, served hydrology well for regional-scale work but could not resolve the fine-scale features that matter for urban flooding, levee integrity, or channel morphology.

Then came LiDAR -- Light Detection and Ranging -- and everything changed. By firing millions of laser pulses per second from an aircraft and measuring the precise travel time of each returning pulse, LiDAR instruments produce three-dimensional point clouds of extraordinary density and accuracy. A single flight over a river corridor can yield measurements every few centimeters on the ground, with vertical accuracies better than 10 centimeters. For the first time, we could see individual levee crest elevations, road culverts, ditches, and the subtle topographic variations within a floodplain that determine where water goes during an overbank event. For hydrology, this was not merely an incremental improvement in data quality -- it was a paradigm shift.

This chapter tells the LiDAR story from the perspective of water science. We begin with the physics of how LiDAR works and the distinction between conventional linear-mode sensors and the newer Geiger-mode technology that promises to transform national mapping programs. We examine the major programs that coordinate LiDAR collection -- the US 3D Elevation Program, European national LiDAR campaigns, and the growing role of drones. We work through the critical processing concepts that every water professional must understand: the difference between a digital surface model and a bare-earth model, the LAS point cloud format, and the specialized green-wavelength LiDAR that can see through water to map river and coastal bathymetry. We then confront the computational challenge that LiDAR creates -- datasets so massive that they demand parallel computing and supercomputer resources -- and follow that thread to its culmination in the national Height Above Nearest Drainage (HAND) map that is now operationally deployed for flood forecasting across the United States. Throughout, we keep one eye on the international landscape: which countries have nationwide LiDAR, which rely on alternative elevation sources, and what options exist for the many regions of the world where LiDAR remains a future aspiration.

## 13.1 The Physics of LiDAR

### 13.1.1 The Basic Principle

The concept behind LiDAR is straightforward. An instrument emits a short pulse of laser light, that pulse travels to a target (the ground, a tree, a building), reflects, and returns to a detector mounted alongside the emitter. Because the speed of light is known with extraordinary precision -- approximately 299,792,458 meters per second in a vacuum, and only slightly less in the atmosphere -- measuring the round-trip travel time yields the distance from the instrument to the target. If the instrument's own position and orientation are known (via GPS and an inertial measurement unit), the target's three-dimensional coordinates can be computed.

The governing equation is simple:

$$d = \frac{c \cdot t}{2}$$

where $d$ is the one-way distance from instrument to target, $c$ is the speed of light, and $t$ is the measured round-trip travel time. A target 1,000 meters below the aircraft produces a round-trip time of approximately 6.67 microseconds. Modern electronics can resolve timing differences of sub-nanoseconds, which translates to range precision on the order of centimeters.

Because the speed of light is so much greater than the speed of the aircraft (roughly 300,000,000 m/s versus 70 m/s), the aircraft is effectively stationary during the time a pulse travels to the ground and back. This means that the measurement geometry is clean: each pulse samples a single, well-defined point on or above the ground surface.

### 13.1.2 The Scanning Mechanism

A single laser firing straight down would trace a single line along the aircraft's flight path. To build a wide swath of measurements, the instrument includes a scanning mechanism -- typically a rotating mirror or an oscillating mirror -- that deflects successive pulses to the left and right of the flight line. As the aircraft advances and the mirror sweeps, the pulses trace a zigzag pattern on the ground, producing a dense strip of measurements beneath the aircraft. Adjacent flight lines are planned to overlap by 20-30%, ensuring complete ground coverage and enabling quality checks where the strips intersect.

Modern airborne LiDAR systems emit between 100,000 and 500,000 pulses per second (the pulse repetition frequency, or PRF). At a flying altitude of 1,000-2,000 meters and a speed of 60-80 m/s, this produces point densities on the ground of 2 to 25 points per square meter, depending on the system configuration and the quality level specified for the survey. Over the course of a typical survey flight, the instrument generates billions of individual three-dimensional measurements -- a point cloud that collectively describes the geometry of the terrain and everything on it.

### 13.1.3 Positioning: GPS and Inertial Navigation

The accuracy of a LiDAR survey depends not only on the precision of the range measurement but critically on knowing the instrument's position and orientation at the instant each pulse is fired. Two complementary systems provide this information.

A **differential GPS receiver** on the aircraft, referenced to one or more base stations on the ground, determines the aircraft's position to within a few centimeters. The GPS solution provides accurate absolute position but updates at a relatively low rate (typically 1-2 Hz) and can be momentarily disrupted by satellite geometry changes or signal interference.

An **inertial measurement unit (IMU)**, consisting of accelerometers and gyroscopes, tracks the aircraft's orientation (roll, pitch, and heading) and short-term changes in position at rates of 200 Hz or more. The IMU provides precise relative information at high frequency but tends to drift over time.

The two systems are complementary: GPS corrects the IMU's drift, while the IMU fills the gaps between GPS fixes and provides the orientation information that GPS cannot supply. The mathematical integration of GPS and IMU data -- typically through a Kalman filter algorithm -- is what enables each individual LiDAR pulse to be accurately geolocated. Without this integrated positioning solution, a 10-centimeter range measurement would be meaningless because the instrument's own position would be uncertain by meters.

### 13.1.4 Wavelength and Eye Safety

Most topographic LiDAR systems operate in the near-infrared portion of the electromagnetic spectrum, typically at wavelengths around 1,064 nanometers (Nd:YAG laser). This wavelength reflects strongly from solid surfaces (soil, rock, vegetation, buildings) but is absorbed by water, which is why conventional LiDAR measures only the water surface of rivers and lakes, not the bottom. As we will see in Section 13.7, bathymetric LiDAR uses a different wavelength -- green light at approximately 532 nanometers -- that penetrates water.

All airborne LiDAR systems must comply with eye safety regulations. The laser beams, though intense at the source, are designed so that by the time they reach the ground (after traveling 500 to 3,000 meters through the atmosphere and spreading over a footprint of 20-50 centimeters), their energy density is below the threshold for eye damage. This is achieved through careful control of pulse energy, beam divergence, and scan rate. Modern systems are classified as Class 1 or Class 1M -- eye-safe under all normal operating conditions.

## 13.2 Linear Mode LiDAR

### 13.2.1 How Linear Mode Works

The conventional LiDAR technology that has produced most of the world's existing data is termed **linear mode**. In a linear mode system, each laser pulse is emitted individually, travels to the ground, and returns to a single detector element. The detector measures the intensity and timing of each returning signal. "Linear" refers to the fact that the detector's response is proportional to the intensity of the incoming light -- a stronger reflection produces a proportionally stronger electronic signal.

The acquisition sequence is straightforward:

1. The laser fires a pulse.
2. The rotating mirror directs the pulse at a specific angle across-track.
3. The pulse travels to the ground (or to a tree, a building, or another object).
4. Photons reflect back toward the aircraft.
5. The detector records the return signal's timing and intensity.
6. The system fires the next pulse.

This happens hundreds of thousands of times per second. Each return, combined with the aircraft's GPS/IMU-derived position and the mirror angle at the instant of firing, yields a single georeferenced point with x, y, z coordinates and intensity.

### 13.2.2 Multiple Returns

A crucial feature of linear mode LiDAR is its ability to record **multiple returns** from a single pulse. When a laser pulse encounters a partially transparent target -- a tree canopy, for example -- some photons reflect off the upper leaves (producing a first return), others pass through gaps and reflect from lower branches (producing intermediate returns), and the remaining photons reach the ground (producing a last return). Modern systems can record 4-7 discrete returns per pulse.

This multiple-return capability is what makes LiDAR uniquely valuable for hydrology compared to other remote sensing technologies. A radar satellite or a photogrammetric camera sees only the top surface -- the canopy in a forest, the roof in a city. LiDAR penetrates the canopy and reaches the ground beneath. This is why LiDAR can produce accurate bare-earth elevation models in forested areas where other technologies cannot.

The time separation between the first return (canopy top) and the last return (ground surface) encodes the height of the vegetation. For a 20-meter-tall tree, the time difference between the canopy return and the ground return is approximately:

$$\Delta t = \frac{2 \times 20 \text{ m}}{3 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}} \approx 133 \text{ ns}$$

This 133-nanosecond difference is well within the resolving capability of modern detectors, which can discriminate returns separated by as little as 2-5 nanoseconds.

### 13.2.3 Full Waveform LiDAR

Standard discrete-return LiDAR records only the peaks of the return signal -- the individual returns described above. **Full waveform** LiDAR records the complete time history of the returning signal at very high temporal resolution (typically 1-nanosecond sampling intervals). The full waveform contains far more information than the discrete peaks alone: the shape of the waveform encodes details about the vertical distribution of reflecting surfaces within the laser footprint.

In forestry, full waveform analysis can distinguish between dense canopy (producing a broad, smeared return) and open canopy with a well-defined understory (producing distinct peaks for canopy, understory, and ground). In hydrological applications, full waveform data can improve ground detection under dense vegetation, where discrete-return systems sometimes fail to record a clear ground return. The trade-off is data volume: full waveform files are typically 5-10 times larger than discrete-return files for the same survey area.

### 13.2.4 Typical Flight Parameters

The operational parameters of a linear mode LiDAR survey are constrained by physics and economics. Flying higher increases the swath width (and thus the area covered per flight line) but reduces point density and accuracy. Flying faster covers more ground per hour but also reduces point density. Higher pulse repetition frequencies increase point density but require more powerful lasers and faster detectors.

**Table 13.1**: Typical operational parameters for linear mode airborne LiDAR surveys

| Parameter | Low-density survey | Standard survey | High-density survey |
|-----------|-------------------|-----------------|---------------------|
| Flying altitude (AGL) | 2,000-3,000 m | 1,000-2,000 m | 500-1,000 m |
| Aircraft speed | 70-80 m/s | 60-70 m/s | 50-60 m/s |
| Pulse repetition freq. | 100-150 kHz | 200-300 kHz | 400-500 kHz |
| Swath width | 1,500-2,500 m | 800-1,500 m | 400-800 m |
| Point density | 1-2 pts/m^2 | 2-8 pts/m^2 | 8-25 pts/m^2 |
| Vertical accuracy | 15-25 cm | 8-15 cm | 5-10 cm |
| Typical application | Regional mapping | Floodplain/corridor | Urban/infrastructure |

The cost of a LiDAR survey is dominated by aircraft time, which is why point density and accuracy involve direct trade-offs against cost. Covering a large area at high density requires many flight lines and many flying hours. This economic reality drives the design of national LiDAR programs, which must balance quality against the sheer scale of the territory to be covered.

## 13.3 Geiger Mode LiDAR

### 13.3.1 The Next Generation

While linear mode LiDAR has revolutionized topographic mapping, it has a fundamental efficiency limitation: each pulse is individually tracked by a single detector, and higher point densities require proportionally more pulses and more flying time. A newer technology -- **Geiger mode LiDAR** -- breaks through this limitation with a fundamentally different detection approach that promises to transform national mapping programs.

Geiger mode LiDAR was originally developed for military reconnaissance, where the need to map terrain in conflict zones such as Iraq and Afghanistan required high-altitude, high-speed survey capability. Flying at high altitude keeps the aircraft out of range of ground-based weapons; flying fast minimizes exposure time. These military requirements drove the development of a sensor that could acquire dense point clouds from altitudes and speeds far beyond the capabilities of linear mode systems.

### 13.3.2 How Geiger Mode Differs

In a Geiger mode system, instead of a single detector, the sensor contains a **two-dimensional array of detector elements** -- analogous to the pixel array in a digital camera, but sensitive to individual photons. Each detector element is so sensitive that it triggers on the arrival of a single photon (hence "Geiger mode," by analogy with the single-particle detection of a Geiger counter for radiation).

The laser illuminates a wide area on the ground, and the detector array captures a two-dimensional image of the returning photons. Because each element operates independently, the system effectively acquires thousands of range measurements simultaneously rather than one at a time. The aircraft can fly higher (typically 7,000-9,000 meters versus 1,000-2,000 meters for linear mode) and faster (100+ m/s versus 60-80 m/s), yet still achieve point densities comparable to or exceeding those of linear mode systems.

**Table 13.2**: Comparison of linear mode and Geiger mode LiDAR characteristics

| Characteristic | Linear Mode | Geiger Mode |
|----------------|-------------|-------------|
| Detection approach | Individual tracked pulses | 2D photon-counting array |
| Sensitivity | Analog (proportional) | Single-photon trigger |
| Aircraft altitude | 1,000-3,000 m | 7,000-9,000 m |
| Aircraft speed | 60-80 m/s | 100+ m/s |
| Swath width | 800-2,500 m | 2,000-4,000 m |
| Coverage rate per hour | 50-200 km^2 | 500-1,500 km^2 |
| Observations per point | Typically 1 | Multiple overlapping |
| Signal processing | Relatively simple | Computationally intensive |

### 13.3.3 The Overlapping Observation Advantage

A distinctive feature of Geiger mode acquisition is that the wide illumination area and the aircraft's forward motion mean that any given point on the ground is observed from many different angles -- from above, from the left, from the right, from ahead, and from behind. This multi-angle observation provides significant advantages for classification (distinguishing ground from vegetation, for instance) and for penetrating canopy gaps to reach the ground surface. It also provides built-in redundancy: if a noise photon triggers a detector element on one pass, subsequent observations of the same point from different angles will yield correct measurements, allowing the noise to be filtered statistically.

The trade-off is computational complexity. Instead of a simple list of range measurements, the Geiger mode sensor produces a massive volume of overlapping photon-by-photon data that must be computationally registered, filtered for noise, and fused into a coherent three-dimensional point cloud. The signal processing chain for Geiger mode data is substantially more complex than for linear mode data, requiring sophisticated algorithms to separate true ground returns from noise triggers and solar background photons.

### 13.3.4 Implications for National Mapping

The efficiency gains of Geiger mode are transformative at the national scale. It has been estimated that with three Geiger mode-equipped aircraft operating simultaneously, the entire contiguous United States could be remapped in a single year. Under the current 3DEP program using linear mode technology, complete national coverage requires eight years or more. Geiger mode could eventually make annual remapping of the nation practical, enabling change detection, infrastructure monitoring, and post-disaster damage assessment on timescales that were previously impossible.

As of 2026, Geiger mode technology is transitioning from military and experimental use into operational civilian mapping. The technology has been used in several 3DEP contracts, and the USGS is actively evaluating how Geiger mode data can be integrated into the national elevation dataset alongside linear mode data. The Harris Corporation (now L3Harris) Geiger mode sensor has been the principal system in civilian trials, and Leica Geosystems has developed the SPL100 (single photon LiDAR), which uses a related photon-counting approach.

## 13.4 National LiDAR Programs

### 13.4.1 The US 3D Elevation Program (3DEP)

The foundation of national LiDAR data collection in the United States is the **3D Elevation Program (3DEP)**, operated by the US Geological Survey as part of its topographic mapping mission. The program builds upon the legacy of the **National Elevation Dataset (NED)**, which established the principle of blending elevation measurements from diverse sources into a continuous surface with consistent specifications for coordinate systems, elevation units, and horizontal and vertical datums. The NED was assembled primarily from digitized contour maps, and while it represented a landmark achievement in its day, its 10-meter resolution and meter-level vertical accuracy were increasingly inadequate for modern flood mapping and infrastructure management.

The 3DEP program was authorized with the goal of acquiring LiDAR-derived elevation data for the entire United States, replacing the contour-derived NED with data that is an order of magnitude more accurate and an order of magnitude finer in resolution. The program defines several quality levels that specify minimum performance requirements for contracted LiDAR surveys:

**Table 13.3**: 3DEP quality levels for LiDAR data acquisition

| Quality Level | Vertical Accuracy (RMSE) | Minimum Point Density | Typical Application |
|---------------|--------------------------|----------------------|---------------------|
| QL3 (legacy) | ~20 cm | 1 point/m^2 | Regional mapping, legacy data |
| QL2 (current standard) | ~10 cm | 2 points/m^2 | Floodplain mapping, watershed analysis |
| QL1 (high quality) | ~5 cm | 8 points/m^2 | Urban modeling, infrastructure |
| QL0 (emerging) | ~3 cm | 25+ points/m^2 | Detailed engineering, change detection |

Accuracy in practice varies with terrain and land cover. Flat, open surfaces such as roads and parking lots routinely achieve 5-8 cm vertical accuracy. Forested areas, where the laser must penetrate canopy to reach the ground, may yield only 20-25 cm accuracy, even with the same sensor system, because fewer pulses reach the ground and the ground returns are noisier.

The current 3DEP target is QL2 coverage for the entire conterminous United States, with higher quality levels collected where specific applications (urban flood mapping, coastal hazard assessment, critical infrastructure) justify the additional cost. As of 2025, QL2 coverage exceeds 80% of the conterminous states, with states such as Kansas, Florida, and Iowa essentially complete. The program is funded through a partnership model: USGS contributes federal funding, and state and local agencies contribute matching funds, with the data becoming freely available to all users upon completion.

Alaska presents a unique challenge. Persistent cloud cover over much of the state prevents reliable optical LiDAR collection. Instead, **Interferometric Synthetic Aperture Radar (IfSAR)** is employed: radar's longer wavelengths penetrate clouds, and the phase difference between two radar antennas mounted on the aircraft yields elevation information. IfSAR achieves approximately 1-2 meter vertical accuracy -- far less than LiDAR -- but it is the only practical technology for systematic mapping of the vast, remote, and persistently cloudy terrain of Alaska.

### 13.4.2 European National LiDAR Programs

Europe has no single coordinated LiDAR program equivalent to the US 3DEP. Instead, individual nations have pursued their own campaigns at different times, to different specifications, and with varying degrees of completeness and public accessibility. The result is a patchwork of excellent national datasets in some countries and no coverage at all in others.

**The Netherlands: Actueel Hoogtebestand Nederland (AHN).** The Netherlands operates one of the world's most comprehensive and frequently updated national LiDAR programs. The AHN has been collected in multiple cycles: AHN1 (1997-2003) at approximately 1 point/m^2, AHN2 (2007-2012) at 6-10 points/m^2, AHN3 (2014-2019) at 6-10 points/m^2, and AHN4 (2020-2022) at 10-14 points/m^2. The data are freely downloadable as both point clouds and gridded DEMs. For a country where over a quarter of the land surface lies below sea level, high-accuracy elevation data is not a luxury but a survival necessity -- the entire system of dikes, polders, and drainage channels depends on knowing ground elevations to within centimeters. AHN data are freely available through the PDOK (Publieke Dienstverlening Op de Kaart) portal.

**United Kingdom: Environment Agency LiDAR.** The UK Environment Agency has collected LiDAR across England at resolutions ranging from 25 cm to 2 m, with coverage focused initially on flood-risk areas and progressively expanding toward national coverage. The data are freely available through the DEFRA Data Services Platform. Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland have separate but coordinated programs. The UK data have been instrumental in flood risk mapping under the EU Floods Directive and its post-Brexit successor framework.

**Finland: National Laser Scanning (NLS).** Finland's National Land Survey has collected nationwide LiDAR at a minimum density of 0.5 points/m^2, making Finland one of the first countries to achieve complete national LiDAR coverage. The data are freely available through the NLS open data service. Finland's flat, heavily forested terrain makes LiDAR particularly valuable, as conventional photogrammetry cannot see through the boreal forest canopy to the ground.

**Denmark.** Denmark completed a nationwide LiDAR survey as part of its grundkort (base map) program, achieving approximately 4-5 points/m^2 density. The data are freely available through the Danish Agency for Data Supply and Infrastructure (Dataforsyningen).

**Switzerland: swissALTI3D.** Switzerland maintains a 0.5-meter resolution DTM derived from LiDAR in lower elevations and photogrammetry in high-alpine areas, updated on a six-year cycle. Given Switzerland's extreme topographic complexity, this dataset is essential for avalanche modeling, hydropower planning, and natural hazard assessment.

### 13.4.3 Hungary: The LiDAR Gap

Hungary does not have a nationwide LiDAR program. This stands in contrast to several of its EU neighbors and represents a significant gap in the country's spatial data infrastructure. The best nationally available elevation dataset is the **DDM-5 (Digitalis Domborzatmodell)**, a 5-meter resolution DEM produced by the Lechner Knowledge Centre (formerly FOMI) from digitized 1:10,000 topographic contour lines and stereophotogrammetry. The DDM-5 has been updated periodically (most recently for major terrain changes from highway construction, mining, and similar activities), but it is fundamentally a contour-derived product with vertical accuracy in the 1-2 meter range -- an order of magnitude less accurate than LiDAR.

Fragmentary LiDAR data exist for specific areas of hydrological or environmental interest. National park authorities have commissioned LiDAR surveys over ecologically sensitive areas such as the Aggtelek Karst (approximately 2 points/m^2), and water management directorates have obtained LiDAR coverage along certain flood-prone river valleys, particularly along the Tisza and its tributaries. These project-specific datasets are available through the Lechner Knowledge Centre or the relevant water directorate, but they do not form a contiguous national dataset.

For Hungarian hydrological work that requires better elevation data than the DDM-5 provides, the practical alternatives are the Copernicus DEM products: the **EEA-10** (10-meter resolution, available to EU/EEA countries with restricted access) and the **GLO-30** (30-meter resolution, freely available globally). These are radar-derived digital surface models from the TanDEM-X mission, not LiDAR-derived bare-earth models, so they include vegetation and building heights. For applications where bare-earth accuracy is critical -- such as floodplain hydraulic modeling or levee assessment -- Hungary remains dependent on the DDM-5 or on commissioning project-specific LiDAR surveys.

This situation has practical consequences. When the Continental HAND map was computed for the United States (Section 13.9), it relied on 10-meter LiDAR-derived DEMs covering the entire country. Computing an equivalent HAND map for Hungary at comparable resolution and accuracy would require either a national LiDAR campaign or algorithmic methods to remove vegetation and building heights from the Copernicus DSM -- a process that introduces uncertainties, particularly in forested areas.

### 13.4.4 Global Perspective

Outside the US and Western Europe, nationwide LiDAR coverage remains rare. Japan has extensive LiDAR coverage, particularly along its densely populated coastal plains and in areas of volcanic and seismic hazard. New Zealand completed national LiDAR coverage through its LiDAR programme coordinated by Toitu Te Whenua (Land Information New Zealand). Australia has progressively expanding coverage, with the state of Victoria and coastal flood-risk areas as priorities under the Elevation Foundation Spatial Data program.

For the majority of the world's land surface -- most of Africa, large parts of Asia and South America -- LiDAR data do not exist at national scale and are unlikely to be collected in the near term. These regions rely on satellite-derived DEMs: SRTM (30 m, from year 2000), ALOS AW3D30 (30 m, optical stereo), MERIT DEM (90 m, error-corrected), and the Copernicus DEM GLO-30 (30 m, radar-derived). While these products have transformed hydrological analysis in data-sparse regions (see the HydroSHEDS global river network derived from SRTM, referenced in Chapter 3), they cannot substitute for LiDAR where centimeter-level accuracy is needed for flood defense, urban drainage, or infrastructure engineering.

## 13.5 Drone-Based LiDAR and Photogrammetry

### 13.5.1 Miniaturized LiDAR Sensors

The past decade has seen the development of LiDAR sensors small enough and light enough to be carried on unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs or drones). Companies such as Velodyne, RIEGL, Livox, and DJI now produce LiDAR units weighing 500 grams to 3 kilograms, compact enough to mount on a quadcopter or hexacopter. These miniaturized sensors achieve point densities of 50 to 300 points per square meter -- far higher than manned aircraft -- because the drone flies at altitudes of 30 to 100 meters rather than 1,000 to 3,000 meters.

The trade-off is coverage area. A typical drone LiDAR mission covers 0.1 to 2 km^2 per flight, compared to 50-200 km^2 per flight for a manned aircraft. Drone LiDAR is therefore not a replacement for airborne LiDAR programs but a complement: it excels for detailed surveys of specific sites where extremely high resolution is needed.

For water professionals, the most common drone LiDAR applications include:

- **Levee and embankment surveys.** Drone LiDAR can resolve the crest elevation, side slopes, and structural details of a levee with centimeter-level accuracy, enabling engineers to identify low spots, settlement, and erosion that threaten levee integrity. A 20-km levee reach can be surveyed in a single day with a drone, compared to weeks of conventional terrestrial survey.

- **Channel cross-sections.** For hydraulic modeling with HEC-RAS or similar tools, detailed channel cross-sections are essential. Drone LiDAR can produce cross-sections at any interval -- every meter if desired -- rather than the widely spaced cross-sections that are typically available from conventional surveys.

- **Post-flood damage assessment.** After a major flood event, drone LiDAR surveys can rapidly map changes in terrain: new erosion channels, sediment deposits, levee breaches, and infrastructure damage. By comparing pre-event and post-event point clouds, volumetric changes can be quantified.

- **Riparian vegetation mapping.** Multi-return drone LiDAR can characterize the vertical structure of riparian vegetation -- tree heights, canopy density, understory presence -- which affects hydraulic roughness, habitat quality, and flood conveyance.

### 13.5.2 Structure from Motion Photogrammetry

An alternative to drone-based LiDAR that has become remarkably accessible is **Structure from Motion (SfM) photogrammetry**. In this approach, a standard camera (often the RGB camera already built into a consumer drone) captures hundreds or thousands of overlapping photographs of a site from different positions and angles. Software algorithms -- available in commercial packages such as Agisoft Metashape, Pix4D, and the open-source OpenDroneMap -- automatically identify matching features across photographs, reconstruct the camera positions, and generate a dense three-dimensional point cloud.

SfM photogrammetry has two major advantages over drone LiDAR: cost (the sensor is a standard camera, not a specialized LiDAR unit) and color (each point in the cloud carries full RGB color information, enabling visual interpretation). The main disadvantage is that SfM cannot penetrate vegetation canopy. It produces a digital surface model (DSM), not a bare-earth model. In open terrain -- agricultural fields, bare soil, roads, low grassland -- SfM can achieve centimeter-level accuracy comparable to LiDAR. In forested or densely vegetated areas, SfM sees only the canopy top, and a separate ground elevation source is needed.

For many hydrological applications, the choice between drone LiDAR and SfM photogrammetry is driven by vegetation cover. If the site is predominantly open (a floodplain after harvest, a construction site, a desert wadi), SfM is often sufficient and far cheaper. If the site has significant tree cover or dense vegetation that must be seen through to the ground, drone LiDAR is the only option.

## 13.6 Digital Surface Models and Bare-Earth Models

### 13.6.1 The Critical Distinction

Perhaps the most important processing concept that any user of LiDAR data must understand is the distinction between a **Digital Surface Model (DSM)** and a **bare-earth Digital Elevation Model (DEM)**. Confusing the two is a surprisingly common error with serious consequences for hydrological analysis.

A **DSM** represents the top of everything: the ground in open areas, the roof of a building in a city, the treetop in a forest. If you placed a sheet of shrink-wrap over the landscape -- stretching it tightly over every rooftop, every tree crown, every car in a parking lot -- the resulting surface would be the DSM. A DSM is what you get from a single-return sensor, from satellite radar (such as SRTM or TanDEM-X), or from SfM photogrammetry. It includes all above-ground features.

A **bare-earth DEM** (sometimes called a Digital Terrain Model, or DTM) represents the ground surface only -- the surface you would see if all buildings were demolished, all vegetation were removed, and all vehicles and other objects were cleared away. It is the surface that water actually flows over, the surface that determines channel geometry, and the surface against which flood depths are measured.

For hydrological analysis, the bare-earth DEM is almost always what you need. Routing water flow across a DSM that includes building footprints and tree canopy produces nonsensical results: water would pond behind every building, flow over rooftops, and be deflected by every tree. The fundamental algorithms of hydrological terrain analysis -- flow direction (Chapter 10), flow accumulation (Chapter 10), watershed delineation (Chapter 11), and HAND (Chapter 14) -- all require bare-earth elevation data. The DEM conditioning techniques of Chapter 9 (pit filling, stream burning, the AGREE method) assume a bare-earth surface as their starting point.

### 13.6.2 How Bare-Earth Models Are Derived

The process of extracting a bare-earth model from a LiDAR point cloud exploits the multiple-return capability described in Section 13.2.2. The key steps are:

**Point classification.** Each point in the raw point cloud is classified as ground, vegetation, building, water, noise, or other categories using algorithms that analyze the geometric relationships between neighboring points. Ground classification algorithms (such as the widely used progressive morphological filter and cloth simulation filter) work by identifying the lowest local points and progressively building a surface upward, classifying points as "ground" if they fall near this surface and "above-ground" if they are significantly higher.

In simple terms, the algorithm asks: "Is this point consistent with being on a smooth ground surface, or does it stick up above its neighbors in a way that suggests it is on a tree or a building?" Points on a flat road are clearly ground. Points 20 meters above their neighbors are clearly vegetation or building. The challenge lies in the intermediate cases: a point on a steep hillside may be much higher than its downslope neighbor even though both are on the ground.

**Ground model interpolation.** Once the ground points have been identified, they are interpolated onto a regular grid to produce the bare-earth DEM. Various interpolation methods are used -- inverse distance weighting, kriging, natural neighbor -- with the choice depending on point density and terrain complexity. In areas of high point density (10+ ground points per square meter), virtually any interpolation method produces excellent results. In areas of sparse ground returns (dense forest canopy with few gaps), the interpolation must bridge larger gaps, and accuracy decreases.

**Quality control.** Automated classification algorithms are imperfect. Common errors include misclassifying low vegetation as ground (producing a DEM that is too high), misclassifying steep terrain features as above-ground objects (producing a DEM that is too smooth), and failing to remove bridge decks over rivers (producing artificial dams in the DEM that block flow). Manual review and editing are typically required for hydrologically critical features such as bridge crossings, culverts, and levees.

### 13.6.3 Applications of the DSM-DEM Difference

The difference between the DSM and the bare-earth DEM is not merely a nuisance to be removed -- it is itself a valuable dataset. This difference, often called a **normalized Digital Surface Model (nDSM)** or a **Canopy Height Model (CHM)** in forestry contexts, represents the height of above-ground features at every point in the landscape.

In **forestry**, the CHM derived from LiDAR is the standard method for mapping tree heights, estimating timber volumes, and characterizing forest canopy structure at landscape scales. The intermediate returns between the canopy top and the ground provide information about canopy density and vertical layering. LiDAR-derived forest inventories have replaced or supplemented traditional field-based forest inventories in many countries.

In **urban hydrology**, the nDSM identifies building footprints and heights, enabling three-dimensional flood modeling that accounts for flow around and between buildings. This is critical for urban flood risk assessment, where the height and arrangement of buildings determines how floodwater moves through a city.

In **ecological applications**, the vertical structure revealed by multi-return LiDAR is used to map habitat quality for wildlife species that depend on specific vegetation structures. For example, certain bird species require understory vegetation below a closed canopy, and LiDAR can map the three-dimensional vegetation structure across entire landscapes in ways that field surveys cannot.

## 13.7 Coastal and Bathymetric LiDAR

### 13.7.1 The Challenge of the Land-Water Interface

Standard topographic LiDAR, operating at near-infrared wavelengths, is absorbed by water. This means that conventional LiDAR surveys produce excellent elevation data on dry land but can only measure the water surface of rivers, lakes, and coastal waters. The actual channel bottom or seabed -- the **bathymetry** -- remains invisible. This creates a gap in the elevation model that is particularly problematic at the land-water interface: the very zone where flooding, erosion, storm surge, and habitat dynamics are most critical.

Mapping the coastal zone also involves complex vertical datum considerations. Various tidal datums -- Mean Higher High Water (MHHW), Mean High Water (MHW), Mean Sea Level (MSL), Mean Low Water (MLW), Mean Lower Low Water (MLLW) -- define legally and physically significant boundaries. The boundary between state-owned tidelands and privately owned uplands, for example, is typically defined by the MHW line, and its position depends on knowing both the ground elevation and the tidal datum to high accuracy. Converting between tidal datums, the ellipsoid (used by GPS), the geoid (the equipotential surface that approximates mean sea level), and orthometric heights (elevations above the geoid) requires careful geodetic work.

### 13.7.2 Green-Wavelength Bathymetric LiDAR

Bathymetric LiDAR solves the water penetration problem by using **green-wavelength laser light** at approximately 532 nanometers (the second harmonic of the standard Nd:YAG laser). Green light penetrates clear water far more effectively than the near-infrared wavelength used for topographic LiDAR. Depending on water clarity, green LiDAR can measure bathymetry to depths of 10 to 50 meters -- sufficient for coastal mapping and many river applications.

The physics of bathymetric measurement involves an additional complexity: **Snell's Law of refraction**. When the laser pulse crosses the air-water interface, it bends toward the vertical because water has a higher refractive index than air (approximately 1.33 versus 1.00). The refracted pulse path is different from the path it would follow in air, and this bending must be accounted for when computing the depth. The governing relationship is:

$$n_1 \sin\theta_1 = n_2 \sin\theta_2$$

where $n_1$ and $n_2$ are the refractive indices of air and water, and $\theta_1$ and $\theta_2$ are the angles of incidence and refraction relative to the surface normal. For a pulse hitting the water at 20 degrees from vertical in air, the refracted angle in water is:

$$\sin\theta_2 = \frac{n_1}{n_2} \sin\theta_1 = \frac{1.00}{1.33} \sin(20°) = 0.257$$
$$\theta_2 \approx 14.9°$$

The pulse is bent closer to the vertical, traveling along a steeper path through the water than through the air. Failure to account for this refraction would introduce systematic depth errors.

The bathymetric LiDAR instrument detects two distinct returns from the water: a **surface return** (backscatter from the water surface, which acts as a partial reflector) and a **bottom return** (reflection from the seabed or riverbed). The time difference between these two returns, corrected for the speed of light in water ($c_{water} = c / n \approx 2.25 \times 10^8$ m/s), yields the water depth.

### 13.7.3 CZMIL and JALBTCX

The **Coastal Zone Mapping and Imaging LiDAR (CZMIL)** instrument represents a specialized implementation of bathymetric LiDAR technology. Originally developed at the Naval Oceanographic Office in Bay Saint Louis, Mississippi, under the leadership of Grady Tuell at Georgia Tech, CZMIL was designed for systematic mapping of the US coastline. The project survived the devastation of Hurricane Katrina in 2005, which destroyed the development team's facilities and homes, forcing dispersal and eventual reconstruction -- a testament to the resilience of the researchers and the importance attached to the mission.

CZMIL is operated through the **Joint Airborne LiDAR Bathymetry Technical Center of Excellence (JALBTCX)**, a program of the US Army Corps of Engineers. Aircraft fly at approximately 400 meters altitude -- much lower and slower than terrestrial LiDAR aircraft -- collecting simultaneous data on land topography, water-surface elevation, and underwater bathymetry along the coastline. The program goal is to map a strip extending 1,000 meters seaward and 500 meters landward from the shoreline, creating a seamless surface that bridges the land-water boundary.

The JALBTCX program has repeatedly surveyed hurricane-prone coastlines, with some areas mapped multiple times to track erosion, sediment transport, and barrier island migration. The data are invaluable for hurricane storm-surge modeling, where the underwater bathymetry near shore strongly influences how storm surge builds and propagates inland.

### 13.7.4 Topobathymetric LiDAR for Rivers

A long-term goal of the LiDAR community is to extend bathymetric technology inland to create a **single continuous topographic-bathymetric surface** that includes river channel bathymetry. Current terrestrial LiDAR surveys produce DEMs with a gap wherever there is water -- the DEM shows the water surface, not the channel bottom. For hydraulic modeling, this means that channel cross-sections must be obtained separately (typically from boat-mounted sonar or wading rod surveys) and manually integrated with the LiDAR-derived floodplain surface.

Green-wavelength topobathymetric LiDAR systems such as the Leica Chiroptera and the CZMIL can map shallow rivers, provided the water is reasonably clear. The USGS and other agencies have used these systems for **ecological habitat studies** in rivers, where water depth and flow velocity determine habitat suitability for fish (such as trout and salmon), macroinvertebrates (mussels, crayfish), and aquatic vegetation. Mapping the full three-dimensional geometry of a river -- bed topography, water depth, banks, and floodplain -- from a single airborne platform represents a significant advance for both flood management and aquatic ecology.

In Europe, bathymetric LiDAR has been deployed along several major rivers. The Rhine, Danube (in its Austrian and German reaches), and Elbe have been partially surveyed with topobathymetric systems for navigation channel maintenance, flood modeling, and ecological assessment. The Danube River is particularly important for Hungary -- as the country's primary flood threat -- and topobathymetric LiDAR surveys of the Danube's Hungarian reach would significantly improve the hydraulic models used for flood forecasting along the Budapest and lower Danube segments.

## 13.8 The LAS Point Cloud Format

### 13.8.1 Why a Standard Format Matters

LiDAR instruments produce point clouds: unstructured collections of billions of individual three-dimensional measurements. Unlike a raster grid, which has a regular structure of rows and columns, a point cloud is simply a list of points, each with its own coordinates and attributes. Storing, exchanging, and processing these enormous datasets requires a standardized file format that is compact, self-describing, and supported by all major software platforms.

The **LAS format**, developed and maintained by the **American Society for Photogrammetry and Remote Sensing (ASPRS)**, fills this role. LAS is a binary file format (not human-readable text) designed specifically for LiDAR point cloud data. Its binary structure is essential for efficiency: a typical LiDAR survey of a single county might contain 50 to 500 billion points, and storing these in a text format would multiply the file size by a factor of 3-5 and make I/O prohibitively slow.

### 13.8.2 Point Record Contents

Each point in a LAS file carries attributes beyond simple x, y, z coordinates:

**Table 13.6**: Key attributes stored per point in a LAS file

| Attribute | Description | Hydrological Relevance |
|-----------|-------------|----------------------|
| X, Y, Z | Three-dimensional coordinates | Position and elevation |
| Intensity | Strength of the return signal | Surface material identification |
| Return number | Which return (1st, 2nd, ..., last) | Canopy vs. ground separation |
| Number of returns | Total returns from this pulse | Vegetation density indicator |
| Classification | ASPRS class code (ground, veg, building, water, ...) | Filtering for DEM extraction |
| GPS timestamp | Time of acquisition | Multi-temporal analysis |
| RGB color | Red, green, blue values (when co-acquired) | Visual interpretation |
| Scan angle | Angle of the laser beam from nadir | Quality indicator (edge-of-swath) |
| Point source ID | Identifies the flight line | Strip adjustment, quality control |

The **classification** attribute deserves special attention because it is central to all LiDAR-derived hydrological products. The ASPRS classification scheme assigns numeric codes to different point types:

**Table 13.7**: Selected ASPRS LAS classification codes relevant to hydrology

| Code | Classification | Hydrological Use |
|------|---------------|-----------------|
| 1 | Unclassified | Default; awaiting processing |
| 2 | Ground | Bare-earth DEM extraction |
| 3 | Low vegetation | Surface roughness, land cover |
| 4 | Medium vegetation | Surface roughness, land cover |
| 5 | High vegetation | Canopy height model |
| 6 | Building | Urban flood modeling |
| 7 | Low point (noise) | Remove before analysis |
| 9 | Water | Water surface mapping |
| 10 | Rail | Infrastructure |
| 11 | Road surface | Impervious surface mapping |
| 17 | Bridge deck | Must be removed for hydrologic DEM |
| 18 | High noise | Remove before analysis |

Filtering the point cloud by classification -- extracting only class 2 (ground) points -- is the fundamental operation for creating a bare-earth DEM from LiDAR data. Extracting class 9 (water) points maps the extent of water bodies at the time of acquisition.

### 13.8.3 LAZ Compression and LAS Version Evolution

Raw LAS files are large. At approximately 26-36 bytes per point (depending on the LAS version and point record format), a dataset of 10 billion points occupies 260-360 gigabytes. The **LAZ** compressed format, developed by Martin Isenburg as part of the LAStools software suite, achieves lossless compression ratios of 5:1 to 15:1, reducing that same dataset to 20-70 gigabytes. LAZ has become the de facto distribution format for LiDAR data: nearly all public data portals deliver data in LAZ rather than uncompressed LAS.

The LAS format has evolved through several versions. LAS 1.0 through 1.3 progressively added support for GPS timestamps, waveform data, and additional classification codes. **LAS 1.4**, the current version, introduced 256 classification codes (up from 32), support for larger files, and additional point record formats that accommodate full waveform data and near-infrared intensity. The ASPRS also developed **COPC (Cloud Optimized Point Cloud)**, a variant of LAS 1.4 that organizes points into an octree structure for efficient streaming and cloud-based access -- a development driven by the growing trend toward processing LiDAR data in cloud computing environments rather than downloading massive files to local machines.

### 13.8.4 From Point Cloud to Grid: The Data Reduction Step

Converting a LiDAR point cloud to a gridded DEM is a data reduction step that involves significant compression of information. A point cloud with 10 points per square meter, when gridded to a 1-meter DEM, reduces the data volume by roughly a factor of 10. The elevation assigned to each 1-meter grid cell must somehow represent the 10 (or more) ground-classified points that fall within it -- typically through averaging, minimum selection, or interpolation.

This reduction step is necessary for practical hydrological analysis: the flow direction, flow accumulation, and watershed delineation algorithms described in Chapters 9-11 all operate on regular grids, not on irregular point clouds. However, it involves real information loss. The point cloud preserves the full three-dimensional complexity of the terrain -- every rock, every rut, every microtopographic feature -- while the grid smooths this into a single value per cell. For most hydrological applications at 1-meter resolution, the smoothing is acceptable. For ultra-high-resolution studies (e.g., overland flow paths on parking lots or construction sites), working directly with the point cloud or with sub-meter grids may be necessary.

## 13.9 High-Performance Computing for LiDAR Processing

### 13.9.1 The Computational Challenge

The progression from 90-meter SRTM DEMs to 10-meter NED DEMs to 1-meter LiDAR DEMs represents an extraordinary increase in data volume. Going from 90 meters to 1 meter multiplies the number of cells by a factor of 8,100 -- and the computational cost of most raster algorithms scales linearly with the number of cells. A flow accumulation calculation that takes one second on a 90-meter DEM takes over two hours on a 1-meter DEM of the same area, and the memory requirements increase proportionally.

For a national-scale analysis, the numbers become staggering. The conterminous United States, at 1-meter resolution, comprises approximately 8 trillion grid cells. A single floating-point value (4 bytes) per cell requires 32 terabytes of storage for one grid layer. The hydrological processing chain -- pit removal, flow direction, flow accumulation, stream delineation, HAND computation -- requires multiple grid layers in memory simultaneously. No desktop computer, and no single server, can handle this.

The gap between data availability and processing capability defines the central computational challenge of the LiDAR era. The data exist -- 3DEP is collecting 1-meter LiDAR across the nation -- but extracting the hydrological information from this data requires computing infrastructure and algorithms that did not exist when the standard hydrological processing tools were developed for 10-meter or 30-meter DEMs.

### 13.9.2 Parallel Processing with TauDEM

The **TauDEM (Terrain Analysis Using Digital Elevation Models)** software, developed by David Tarboton at Utah State University, was one of the first hydrological terrain analysis tools to implement parallel processing capability using the Message Passing Interface (MPI) standard. The parallelization strategy is based on spatial domain decomposition: the raster is divided into horizontal stripes, each stripe is assigned to a separate processor core, and the processors communicate boundary information with their neighbors as the algorithm iterates.

Consider the pit removal step (Planchon-Darboux algorithm) as an example of how this parallelization works:

1. Each processor initializes all cells in its stripe to a very high elevation value, except for cells on the grid boundary (which retain their original elevation).
2. Processors iteratively sweep across their stripes, lowering each cell to the maximum of its original elevation and the minimum of its neighbors' current elevations.
3. After each sweep, processors exchange boundary rows with their neighbors: the bottom row of the upper stripe is sent to the adjacent lower stripe, and vice versa.
4. Iteration continues until no further modifications are made -- convergence has been reached.

The boundary exchange step is the key to correctness: without it, pits that span the boundary between two stripes would not be resolved correctly. The iterative nature of the algorithm, with boundary exchanges at each iteration, ensures that information propagates across stripe boundaries until the global solution is reached.

The evolution of TauDEM's capability illustrates the progress in computational hydrology:

**Table 13.8**: Evolution of TauDEM processing capability

| Version / Era | Maximum Grid Size | Processors | Key Advance |
|---------------|------------------|------------|-------------|
| TauDEM v4 (~2008) | ~0.22 GB | 1 (serial) | Desktop only |
| Early parallel (~2010) | ~4 GB | 8 | MPI parallelization |
| 64-bit parallel (~2013) | No theoretical limit | Hundreds | 64-bit addressing, HPC systems |
| Current (2026) | Multi-TB | Thousands | Cloud/HPC, I/O optimization |

### 13.9.3 Domain Decomposition for National-Scale Analysis

Processing the entire United States at even 10-meter resolution requires dividing the problem into manageable pieces. The natural decomposition unit for hydrological analysis is the watershed -- specifically, the **Hydrologic Unit Code (HUC)** system used by the USGS to organize the nation's drainage network into nested catchments. Six-digit HUCs (HUC6) partition the country into approximately 350 regions, each small enough to be processed on a single computing node.

However, hydrological algorithms do not respect arbitrary boundaries. A pit that straddles the boundary between two HUC6 units must be resolved consistently across both units. Flow accumulation from one unit into the next must be handled correctly. The solution is **buffering**: each processing unit is expanded by a buffer zone (typically 1-5 km) beyond its nominal boundary, processed independently, and then trimmed back to the nominal boundary. The buffer ensures that boundary effects do not propagate into the interior of the processed area.

This divide-process-merge workflow is an instance of a general parallel computing strategy, but the specific details -- how to partition the domain along hydrologically meaningful boundaries, how wide the buffer needs to be, how to merge results across boundaries -- require domain-specific knowledge that standard parallel computing frameworks do not provide. This is why hydrological parallel processing has been developed within the water science community rather than simply adopted from computer science.

### 13.9.4 OpenTopography

**OpenTopography**, hosted at the San Diego Supercomputer Center and funded by the US National Science Foundation, provides web-based access to LiDAR data and processing capabilities. The platform addresses a significant barrier to LiDAR utilization: even when data are freely available, many water professionals lack the computing resources or software expertise to process raw point clouds into the derived products they need.

Through OpenTopography's web interface, a user can:

1. Select an area of interest on a map.
2. Choose from available LiDAR datasets covering that area.
3. Request derived products to be computed on-the-fly, including:
   - Rasterized DEMs at user-specified resolution
   - Hydrologically corrected (pit-filled) DEMs
   - D-infinity flow directions (Tarboton's algorithm)
   - D-infinity specific catchment area
   - Topographic wetness index

Processing is performed on high-performance computing clusters at the San Diego Supercomputer Center. Results are typically delivered within minutes to hours (depending on the area size and products requested) via email link. The user downloads finished products without having to install software, manage data storage, or configure computing environments.

OpenTopography has expanded from its originally US-focused collection to include international datasets. The Copernicus GLO-30 DEM is available through the platform for global on-demand processing, and several national LiDAR datasets from outside the US have been incorporated. The platform's processing tools -- particularly the TauDEM-based hydrological derivatives -- can be applied to any elevation data, making it a valuable resource for researchers worldwide, including those working in regions without local high-performance computing infrastructure.

### 13.9.5 Supercomputing for Continental-Scale Analysis

The most demanding LiDAR processing tasks require supercomputing resources. Collaboration between the University of Texas at Austin and the **National Center for Supercomputing Applications (NCSA)** at the University of Illinois enabled processing of the entire conterminous United States at 10-meter resolution for the first time. Key technical developments in this collaboration included:

**Algorithm optimization.** Yan Liu and colleagues at NCSA optimized the D-infinity flow direction algorithm, achieving a **250-fold speedup** over the original TauDEM implementation. The optimization involved restructuring memory access patterns to exploit processor cache hierarchies, reducing redundant calculations, and improving the convergence behavior of iterative algorithms. This is a general lesson in high-performance computing: the choice of algorithm and its implementation details often matter more than raw hardware speed.

**I/O management.** At the scale of thousands of processor cores working simultaneously, the bottleneck shifted from computation to **input/output (I/O)** -- the time required to read elevation data from storage and write results back. When 2,000 cores are all trying to read data from the same file system simultaneously, the storage system becomes the limiting factor, not the processors. Solutions included parallel file systems, data staging (pre-loading data into memory before computation begins), and careful scheduling to avoid I/O conflicts.

**Quality control at scale.** Processing tens of thousands of watersheds automatically, without human review of each one, requires robust algorithms that handle edge cases gracefully. Flat areas, coastal boundaries, internally draining basins, and data voids all require special handling that may not have been important when processing a single study watershed but becomes critical when processing the entire nation.

### 13.9.6 The National HAND Map

The culmination of the high-performance computing work described above was the computation of the **Height Above Nearest Drainage (HAND)** map for the entire conterminous United States. HAND, introduced conceptually in Chapter 10 and developed fully in Chapter 14, measures the vertical distance from each point in the landscape to the nearest stream channel, following the flow path downhill. It is the key variable linking terrain to flood inundation: locations with small HAND values (close in elevation to the nearest stream) are flood-prone; locations with large HAND values (elevated well above the nearest stream) are not.

The continental HAND map was computed on May 29, 2016, using supercomputing resources at NCSA. The computation:

- Covered **3.2 million miles** (5.1 million kilometers) of streams and rivers, using the NHDPlus stream network as the target drainage.
- Processed the 10-meter NED (later replaced by 3DEP data) across the entire conterminous US.
- Was completed in a **single day** of wall-clock time.
- Equivalent processing on a conventional desktop computer would have taken an estimated **seven years**.
- The alternative approach -- computing flood inundation using a full hydraulic model (HEC-RAS) for every reach of the national stream network -- would cost approximately **$1.6 billion** at $500 per mile, compared to effectively zero marginal cost for the HAND computation once the elevation data exist.

The national HAND map reveals geomorphological patterns at continental scale. Broad, low-lying areas such as the Mississippi embayment, the Florida coast, and the Texas Gulf Coast appear as deep blue (small HAND values, extensive low-relief terrain near rivers), while mountainous regions such as the Appalachians and the Rocky Mountains appear white (large HAND values, indicating steep terrain where rivers are deeply incised into narrow valleys). The map provides an immediate visual impression of where flood risk is concentrated and where it is not.

This national HAND dataset is operationally deployed at the **National Water Center** in Tuscaloosa, Alabama, for real-time flood inundation mapping during major river flooding events. When the National Water Model predicts elevated streamflow on a given river reach, the HAND map enables rapid translation of that flow prediction into a spatial inundation footprint -- the same analysis described in Chapters 14 and 15, but executed automatically for 2.7 million stream reaches simultaneously.

### 13.9.7 Toward LiDAR-Resolution National Processing

The continental HAND map described above was computed at 10-meter resolution using the NED. The next frontier is computing the same analysis at 1-meter resolution using LiDAR-derived DEMs from 3DEP. This represents approximately a 100-fold increase in data volume -- from approximately 8 billion cells at 10 meters to approximately 800 billion cells at 1 meter for the conterminous US.

At this scale, the six-digit HUC processing units that worked at 10-meter resolution become unwieldy. Some HUC6 units contain tens of billions of cells at 1-meter resolution, exceeding the memory capacity of individual computing nodes. New strategies for domain decomposition are required, potentially using smaller HUC units (HUC8 or HUC10), adaptive tiling, or hierarchical approaches that process local areas at full resolution and aggregate upward.

The cloud computing paradigm offers a path forward. Rather than relying on a single supercomputing center, the computation can be distributed across cloud infrastructure (Amazon Web Services, Microsoft Azure, Google Cloud) where thousands of virtual machines can be provisioned on demand, each processing its assigned tile of the national mosaic. This approach has been explored in several academic and governmental projects and is likely to be the operational model for national-scale LiDAR processing in the coming years.

## 13.10 European and International Context

### 13.10.1 Computing Continental HAND for Europe

The HAND methodology is not inherently US-specific -- it can be applied anywhere elevation data and a stream network definition exist. Computing HAND for Europe would require:

1. **Elevation data**: The Copernicus DEM EEA-10 (10-meter resolution) covers all EU/EEA countries and would serve as the elevation input. Alternatively, national LiDAR datasets (where available) could be used for higher-resolution results in specific countries.

2. **Stream network definition**: The **EU-Hydro** dataset, produced under the Copernicus Land Monitoring Service, provides a pan-European river network that could serve the same role that NHDPlus serves in the US computation.

3. **Computational infrastructure**: The European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts (ECMWF) operates substantial supercomputing resources, as do national meteorological and hydrological services. The PRACE (Partnership for Advanced Computing in Europe) network provides access to tier-0 and tier-1 supercomputers for scientific research.

The Joint Research Centre (JRC) of the European Commission has already computed flood hazard maps for Europe using a different methodology (LISFLOOD hydrodynamic modeling) at approximately 25-meter resolution. These maps cover return periods from 10 to 500 years and are freely available. HAND-based mapping could complement this by providing a terrain-based flood susceptibility index at higher resolution, at a fraction of the computational cost of hydrodynamic modeling.

### 13.10.2 Hungary's Position

For Hungary specifically, a HAND computation using the Copernicus GLO-30 DEM and the EU-Hydro river network is feasible today with modest computing resources. The country's relatively flat terrain (the Great Hungarian Plain, or Alfold, dominates the eastern half of the country) means that even a 30-meter DEM captures the broad-scale flood susceptibility patterns along the Tisza and Danube. However, the critical details -- the exact height of levee crests, the microtopography within the protected floodplain that determines where retained water pools, the subtle elevation differences between agricultural fields and drainage channels -- require resolution better than what any currently available free dataset provides.

The Hungarian water management system, operated by the OVF (Orszagos Vizugyi Foigazgatosag) and its twelve regional directorates, relies on an extensive network of levees along the Tisza and its tributaries. These levees protect over 20,000 km^2 of floodplain that would otherwise be regularly inundated. The integrity of this levee system -- and the ability to identify weak points before a flood event -- depends fundamentally on high-accuracy elevation data. A national LiDAR program, even if limited initially to the levee corridors and major floodplains, would represent a transformative investment in Hungary's flood defense capability.

### 13.10.3 OpenTopography as a Global Resource

OpenTopography's growing international collection and its ability to process the freely available Copernicus DEM for any location on Earth make it an important resource for water professionals outside the United States. A researcher in Sub-Saharan Africa who lacks local computing infrastructure can use OpenTopography to obtain hydrologically conditioned DEMs, flow direction grids, and topographic wetness indices for any study area, computed on US supercomputing resources and delivered via download link.

This democratization of LiDAR processing capability -- separating the data and algorithms from the local computing infrastructure -- is one of the most important trends in computational hydrology. It means that the benefits of high-performance computing for terrain analysis are available to anyone with an internet connection, regardless of their institutional computing resources. For the educational context of this book, it means that students in Debrecen, Nairobi, or Dhaka can perform the same terrain analyses as students in Austin or Boulder, using the same tools and the same data.

## 13.11 Practical Considerations for Water Professionals

### 13.11.1 Choosing the Right Data Source

A water professional approaching a new project must make a fundamental decision about elevation data early in the workflow. The choice depends on the project's spatial extent, the required accuracy, the budget, and the available data:

**Table 13.9**: Decision guide for elevation data selection

| Project Type | Recommended Data | Typical Resolution | Expected Accuracy |
|-------------|-----------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Continental/global water balance | Copernicus GLO-30 or MERIT DEM | 30-90 m | 2-5 m |
| National/regional watershed analysis | National DEM (3DEP, DDM-5, etc.) | 5-10 m | 0.5-2 m |
| Floodplain mapping | LiDAR (3DEP QL2, AHN, EA) | 1-2 m | 0.1-0.15 m |
| Urban flood modeling | LiDAR (QL1 or better) | 0.5-1 m | 0.05-0.10 m |
| Levee/infrastructure assessment | Drone LiDAR or RTK survey | 0.1-0.5 m | 0.02-0.05 m |
| Channel bathymetry | Topo-bathymetric LiDAR or sonar | Variable | 0.15-0.30 m |

The guiding principle is to match the data resolution and accuracy to the scale of the features that matter for the analysis. Using 1-meter LiDAR for a continental water balance study wastes computational resources without improving results. Using 30-meter SRTM for levee assessment misses the features that determine flood outcomes.

### 13.11.2 Common Pitfalls

Several errors recur frequently in LiDAR-based hydrological analysis:

**Using a DSM when a bare-earth DEM is needed.** This is the most common and consequential error. Many freely available datasets -- including the Copernicus DEM and SRTM -- are digital surface models that include vegetation and building heights. Using a DSM for flow direction analysis produces incorrect results wherever vegetation or buildings alter the apparent surface gradient. Always verify whether your elevation data is a DSM or a bare-earth DEM before beginning hydrological processing.

**Ignoring bridge decks.** LiDAR sees bridge decks as solid surfaces. In a DEM, an unremoved bridge appears as a dam across the river valley, blocking computed flow paths and creating artificial ponds upstream. Most LiDAR-derived hydrological DEMs include bridge deck removal as a processing step, but not all do. Check your DEM at major road crossings by viewing it in 3D or examining elevation profiles along the channel.

**Resolution mismatch.** Combining high-resolution LiDAR terrain data with low-resolution soil, land cover, or precipitation data can create a false sense of precision. The hydrological response is only as accurate as the weakest input. A 1-meter DEM combined with 10-km precipitation data does not produce 1-meter-resolution flood predictions.

**Edge effects.** Processing a DEM that clips a watershed boundary introduces errors at the edges. Always extend your DEM beyond the expected watershed boundary by a generous buffer. The cost of processing a slightly larger area is trivial; the cost of incorrect watershed delineation is not.

### 13.11.3 The Evolving Resolution Question

LiDAR has created an interesting scientific question that did not exist in the era of 30-meter and 10-meter DEMs: at what resolution do the standard hydrological algorithms break down?

The D8 flow direction algorithm (Chapter 10), which assigns flow from each cell to one of its eight neighbors, was designed for DEMs where each cell represents a meaningful landscape element -- a hillslope segment, a portion of a channel, a section of floodplain. At 10-meter resolution, this is generally the case: a 10-meter cell on a hillslope captures the local slope direction meaningfully. At 1-meter resolution, individual cells may represent rocks, ruts, tire tracks, tree root mounds, or construction debris -- features that deflect water locally but are not meaningful elements of the landscape-scale hydrological system.

The consequence is that 1-meter flow direction grids often appear noisy and chaotic compared to 10-meter grids. Flow paths zigzag around individual features rather than following the smooth, coherent patterns that match our physical intuition about how water moves across a hillslope. Various strategies have been proposed to address this: smoothing the DEM before computing flow directions, using multi-directional flow algorithms (D-infinity, MFD) that distribute flow to multiple downslope neighbors, or computing at 1-meter resolution but interpreting results at a coarser aggregation scale.

This is an active area of research in 2026, and no consensus approach has emerged. The practical advice for water professionals is to be thoughtful about resolution: the highest available resolution is not always the best choice for a given analysis. Match the resolution to the phenomenon you are studying, and validate your results against field observations whenever possible.

## 13.12 The Future of LiDAR in Water Science

### 13.12.1 Repeat LiDAR for Change Detection

As national LiDAR programs complete their first full cycles and begin repeat acquisitions, a new capability emerges: detecting and quantifying terrain changes over time. By comparing point clouds or DEMs from two different acquisition dates, changes in ground elevation can be identified:

- **Coastal erosion and accretion**: measuring shoreline retreat, dune migration, and sediment redistribution after storms.
- **Channel migration**: quantifying how river channels move laterally over years to decades, and how floodplain surfaces are reworked by erosion and deposition.
- **Levee subsidence**: detecting centimeter-scale settlement of earthen levees that may indicate internal erosion or foundation weakness.
- **Urban development**: tracking the growth of impervious surfaces (roofs, roads, parking lots) that increase runoff and flood risk.
- **Post-disaster assessment**: comparing pre-event and post-event terrain to quantify landslide volumes, debris flow deposits, channel avulsions, and structural damage.

In the Netherlands, where AHN has been collected four times over 25 years, multi-temporal analysis has revealed patterns of land subsidence in peat areas, coastal dune dynamics, and changes in drainage ditch geometry. These change products are arguably as valuable as the elevation data themselves for long-term water management.

### 13.12.2 Integration with Real-Time Flood Forecasting

The operational deployment of the national HAND map at the US National Water Center (Section 13.9.6) represents the first generation of LiDAR-terrain integration into real-time flood forecasting. The next generation will likely integrate LiDAR-derived terrain more deeply into forecast models:

- **Pre-computed inundation libraries** (Chapter 15): for every stream reach, a set of flood inundation maps corresponding to different flow levels, computed in advance using the LiDAR-derived DEM and stored as a lookup table. When the forecast model predicts a specific flow, the corresponding pre-computed map is retrieved instantly.
- **Dynamic flood mapping**: using real-time stream gauge data and LiDAR terrain to produce updated flood inundation maps every few hours during an event.
- **Impact assessment**: combining flood inundation maps with LiDAR-derived building footprints and heights to estimate the number of structures affected and the depth of flooding at each structure.

In Europe, the **European Flood Awareness System (EFAS)** and its global counterpart **GloFAS** provide the flood forecast inputs. Coupling these forecasts with high-resolution terrain data -- whether from national LiDAR programs or from the Copernicus DEM -- to produce detailed local inundation maps is a key development goal for the Copernicus Emergency Management Service.

### 13.12.3 Satellite-Based LiDAR

The ICESat-2 satellite, launched by NASA in 2018, carries a photon-counting LiDAR instrument (ATLAS) that measures surface elevation along its orbital ground track. While ICESat-2 does not provide the dense areal coverage of airborne LiDAR (it measures along lines, not over areas), its global coverage and repeat measurements make it valuable for tracking changes in ice sheet elevation, lake levels, forest canopy height, and even river surface elevation at global scale.

The ESA BIOMASS mission, scheduled for launch in the late 2020s, will use P-band radar to map forest biomass globally -- complementing LiDAR-derived canopy height measurements with information about wood volume. For hydrological applications, this combination will improve estimates of canopy interception and transpiration, which are significant components of the water balance in forested watersheds.

These satellite missions do not replace airborne LiDAR for detailed local mapping, but they extend the reach of three-dimensional terrain measurement to the entire planet, including the vast areas that will never be covered by airborne surveys.

## 13.13 Worked Example: HAND Computation Workflow

To ground the concepts of this chapter in a practical workflow, consider a worked example of computing the Height Above Nearest Drainage (HAND) from LiDAR data for a small watershed. This example follows the same conceptual steps as the continental HAND computation described in Section 13.9.6, but at a scale accessible on a standard desktop computer.

**Study area**: A 50 km^2 watershed in central Hungary, covering a tributary of the Zagyva River in the Northern Hungarian Mountains (Matra foothills). Elevation ranges from approximately 120 m to 450 m.

**Step 1: Obtain elevation data.** In the absence of LiDAR for this area, we use the Copernicus DEM GLO-30, downloaded from OpenTopography or the Copernicus Data Space. The 30-meter resolution is adequate for demonstrating the workflow, though a national LiDAR survey would provide 1-meter data.

**Step 2: Pit removal.** The raw DEM contains local depressions (pits) that would trap water in the flow direction algorithm. We apply the Planchon-Darboux pit filling algorithm (either through TauDEM, QGIS/GRASS, or the OpenTopography web service). For this 50 km^2 area at 30-meter resolution, the grid contains approximately:

$$N_{cells} = \frac{50 \times 10^6 \text{ m}^2}{30^2 \text{ m}^2} \approx 55,600 \text{ cells}$$

This is trivially small for modern hardware -- processing takes under one second.

**Step 3: Flow direction.** Compute D8 flow directions from the pit-filled DEM. Each cell is assigned a flow direction toward the lowest of its eight neighbors.

**Step 4: Flow accumulation.** Accumulate upslope contributing area for each cell. Cells with accumulation values exceeding a threshold (e.g., 100 cells, corresponding to a contributing area of 0.09 km^2 at 30-meter resolution) are identified as stream cells.

**Step 5: Define the drainage network.** Rather than using a flow accumulation threshold, a more robust approach is to use a known stream network (e.g., from EU-Hydro or from the Hungarian water management maps available through data.vizugy.hu) and burn it into the DEM before computing flow directions. This ensures that the computed stream network matches the known hydrography.

**Step 6: Compute HAND.** For each non-stream cell, trace the flow path downstream until a stream cell is reached. The HAND value is the difference between the cell's elevation and the stream cell's elevation:

$$\text{HAND}(i) = z(i) - z(s_i)$$

where $z(i)$ is the elevation of cell $i$ and $z(s_i)$ is the elevation of the stream cell to which cell $i$ drains.

**Step 7: Interpret results.** In this Matra foothills example, the HAND values would show:
- Values of 0-2 m along the narrow valley floor of the Zagyva tributary -- the area most susceptible to flooding.
- Values of 5-15 m on the lower valley slopes -- occasionally reached by extreme floods.
- Values exceeding 50 m on the upper hillslopes and ridgelines -- never at risk from river flooding (though surface runoff and flash flooding may still be relevant).

**Step 8: Estimate flood inundation.** If the stream stage rises by 3 meters above its normal level, we can estimate the flood extent by selecting all cells with HAND values less than or equal to 3 meters. This produces an approximate inundation map that, while less accurate than a full hydraulic model, is computed in seconds rather than hours and provides an immediately useful first estimate for emergency response.
