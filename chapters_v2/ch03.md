# Chapter 3: Where the Data Lives

Every water resources project begins with the same question: what data can I get? Not what question would I like to answer, not what model should I run, but what information actually exists, where is it stored, and how do I access it? The answer to this question determines everything that follows. A beautifully conceived hydrological model is worthless without the elevation data to define the terrain, the precipitation records to drive it, and the discharge measurements to validate it. The data in hand dictate the questions that can be credibly answered.

This chapter is a guided tour of the world's water data infrastructure, organized not by country but by data type. For each category of information -- elevation, rivers, land cover, soil, weather, discharge, floods, demographics, and satellite imagery -- we trace a consistent path: first the United States, which has invested more than any other nation in systematic, publicly accessible hydrological data; then the European Union and Hungary, where the Copernicus programme and national agencies provide increasingly powerful alternatives; and finally the global datasets that serve the rest of the world, from the data-rich nations of East Asia to the gauge-sparse regions of sub-Saharan Africa. The goal is not an exhaustive catalogue -- that would fill a book by itself -- but a practical guide to the datasets you will actually use in the chapters that follow.

A reader in Texas and a reader in Debrecen face fundamentally different data landscapes, yet the underlying logic is the same: find the terrain, find the rivers, find the rain, find the flow. The case studies in Sections 1.8--1.10 demonstrated how these data streams come together in practice; this chapter shows you where each stream originates. Chapter 4 then introduces the GIS tools for assembling them into a working project. By the end of this chapter, both readers should know exactly where to look.

## 3.1 The Logic of Water Data

### 3.1.1 Data Types and Their Roles

Water does not respect the boundaries that humans draw on maps, but the data we use to study water is organized by the institutions that collect it. Understanding the institutional landscape is essential for finding data efficiently. Broadly, the datasets needed for any watershed-scale hydrological study fall into nine categories, each answering a specific question about the physical system:

**Elevation** answers the question: what does the land surface look like? Digital elevation models (DEMs) are the foundation of nearly all spatial hydrology. From elevation data, we derive slope, aspect, flow direction, flow accumulation, stream networks, and watershed boundaries. Without a DEM, there is no computational hydrology.

**River networks** answer the question: where does the water flow? Pre-delineated stream networks save enormous effort compared to deriving everything from raw DEMs, and they carry attributes -- names, reach codes, connectivity, estimated flows -- that raw terrain data cannot provide.

**Land cover** answers the question: what covers the ground? The distinction between forest, cropland, urban pavement, and bare soil determines how much rainfall infiltrates, how much runs off, and how fast it moves across the surface.

**Soil** answers the question: what lies beneath the surface? Soil texture, hydraulic conductivity, and depth to bedrock govern infiltration rates and subsurface flow -- the invisible half of the water cycle.

**Weather and climate** answer the question: what falls from the sky? Precipitation drives the entire hydrological system. Temperature governs evapotranspiration, snowmelt, and the phase of precipitation. Without weather data, models have nothing to run on.

**Discharge and water levels** answer the question: how much water is actually moving through the rivers? Stream gauge records are the ground truth against which all models are tested. They are the single most valuable dataset in hydrology, and in many parts of the world, the scarcest.

**Flood data** answers the question: where has water gone, and where might it go? Flood hazard maps, inundation models, and real-time forecasting systems translate hydrological knowledge into actionable warnings.

**Demographics** answer the question: who is at risk? Combining flood maps with population data, building footprints, and infrastructure locations transforms a physical science problem into a human one.

**Satellite imagery** answers the question: what can we see from above? Remote sensing fills the gaps where ground-based networks are sparse, providing land surface temperature, vegetation indices, soil moisture, water extent, and even gravity-derived estimates of total water storage.

### 3.1.2 The Three-Tier Data Landscape

The world's water data infrastructure has evolved along three parallel tracks that reflect different institutional histories and funding models.

The **United States** built the most comprehensive national water data system in the world, driven by federal agencies -- the USGS, NOAA, FEMA, the EPA, and NASA -- with decades of sustained investment. The NHDPlus framework, the USGS gauge network, the National Water Model, and the Landsat programme are all products of this tradition. US datasets are generally free, well-documented, and accessible through standardized web services. They are also, inevitably, the datasets that dominate English-language textbooks and tutorials.

The **European Union** followed a different path, building continent-wide data infrastructure through the Copernicus programme (for Earth observation), the Water Framework Directive (for water quality and river basin management), and a network of EU-level agencies -- the European Environment Agency (EEA), the Joint Research Centre (JRC), and the European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts (ECMWF). Hungary, as an EU member state, benefits from this infrastructure while also maintaining its own national systems through the General Directorate of Water Management (OVF), the Hungarian Meteorological Service (OMSZ), and the Lechner Knowledge Centre.

**Globally**, the past two decades have seen an explosion of free, high-quality datasets that serve regions without comprehensive national infrastructure. HydroSHEDS provides river networks and watershed boundaries for the entire planet. CHIRPS and GPM provide precipitation estimates where rain gauges are scarce. SoilGrids maps soil properties at 250-metre resolution worldwide. Google Earth Engine puts petabytes of satellite data at anyone's fingertips. For a researcher in Lagos, Dhaka, or Amman, these global datasets are not supplements -- they are the primary source.

## 3.2 Elevation and Digital Elevation Models

### 3.2.1 The Foundation of Spatial Hydrology

Elevation data is the single most important input to any watershed-scale hydrological analysis. From a digital elevation model, we can derive the direction water flows across every cell of the landscape, accumulate those flows to identify stream channels, and trace boundaries to delineate watersheds. The entire analytical chain described in Chapters 9 through 12 -- DEM conditioning, flow direction, flow accumulation, watershed delineation -- begins with an elevation grid. The quality of the DEM -- its spatial resolution, vertical accuracy, and freedom from artifacts -- propagates through every subsequent step.

The resolution question matters enormously. A 90-metre DEM might be adequate for delineating a major river basin, but it cannot resolve the small tributary that floods a village. A 1-metre LiDAR-derived DEM can map individual ditches and levees, but processing it over a large area requires serious computational resources. Choosing the right DEM for the task is one of the first decisions in any project.

### 3.2.2 United States: NED and 3DEP

The United States has invested more than any other country in systematic elevation mapping. The **National Elevation Dataset (NED)**, now being superseded by the **3D Elevation Program (3DEP)**, provides seamless DEM coverage of the entire country at multiple resolutions. The standard product is the 1/3 arc-second grid, which corresponds to approximately 10 metres on the ground. In areas covered by airborne LiDAR surveys -- and 3DEP aims to cover the entire country -- the resolution improves to 1/9 arc-second (approximately 3 metres) or better. The dataset contains approximately 10 billion elevation values and is distributed through the National Map viewer at:

```
https://nationalmap.gov
```

Because the NED is stored in geographic coordinates (latitude and longitude), the ground dimensions of a single cell vary with latitude. At 30 degrees north (Austin, Texas), a 1 arc-second cell measures approximately 26.8 metres east-west by 30.9 metres north-south. At 42 degrees north (Logan, Utah), the east-west dimension shrinks to approximately 22.9 metres while the north-south dimension remains essentially the same. This is not a defect -- it is an inherent property of geographic coordinate systems -- but it means that area and distance calculations require a projected coordinate system for accuracy, a topic covered in detail in Chapter 2.

The 3DEP programme represents a paradigm shift from photogrammetric DEMs to LiDAR-derived surfaces. LiDAR (Light Detection and Ranging) produces point clouds with vertical accuracies of 10-20 centimetres, enabling applications that were impossible with the older NED -- mapping individual levees, detecting subtle changes in channel geometry, and computing the Height Above Nearest Drainage (HAND) index that drives modern flood mapping (Chapter 14). The 3DEP data are accessible through the same National Map portal and increasingly through cloud-optimized formats on commercial platforms.

### 3.2.3 Europe and Hungary: Copernicus DEM and DDM-5

Europe's primary elevation dataset is the **Copernicus DEM**, derived from the German-led TanDEM-X radar satellite mission that mapped the Earth's surface between 2011 and 2015. It comes in three variants:

- **GLO-30**: 30-metre resolution, global coverage, freely available to all users
- **GLO-90**: 90-metre resolution, global coverage, freely available
- **EEA-10**: 10-metre resolution, covering 39 European countries, available to qualified users

The Copernicus DEM represents a Digital Surface Model (DSM), meaning it captures the tops of buildings and trees rather than the bare ground beneath them. For hydrological applications in forested or urban areas, this distinction matters: a DSM will show a forested hillside as higher than the actual ground, potentially affecting flow routing. In open terrain, the distinction is negligible. The vertical accuracy specification is better than 4 metres at 90% confidence globally, with performance typically better than 1 metre in flat European terrain.

Access is through the Copernicus Data Space Ecosystem:

```
https://dataspace.copernicus.eu/explore-data/data-collections/copernicus-contributing-missions/collections-description/COP-DEM
```

Bulk downloads are available via S3 and OData APIs, and the data are also hosted on OpenTopography, AWS, Google Earth Engine, and Microsoft Planetary Computer. The format is GeoTIFF with the EGM2008 geoid as the vertical datum.

Hungary has its own national DEM: the **DDM-5** (Digitalis Domborzatmodell), produced by the Lechner Knowledge Centre (formerly FOMI). At 5-metre resolution in the national EOV projection (EPSG:23700), it is derived from 1:10,000 topographic contour lines and stereophotogrammetry, with updates in 2000, 2005, 2015, and 2022 for areas of significant terrain change (highway construction, mining operations). The DDM-5 is the highest-resolution national DEM available for Hungary, but it is not freely downloadable -- it must be ordered through the Lechner data services portal or the geoshop:

```
https://lechnerkozpont.hu/oldal/domborzatmodell
```

Hungary does not have a comprehensive nationwide LiDAR programme comparable to the US 3DEP. LiDAR surveys exist for specific areas of scientific or engineering interest -- the Aggtelek Karst (a UNESCO World Heritage Site), various flood-prone valleys, and corridor surveys along major infrastructure -- but these are fragmented and typically available only through the surveying authority or the relevant water directorate. For most purposes, the Copernicus DEM EEA-10 provides the best freely available elevation data for Hungary at 10-metre resolution, while the DDM-5 remains the authoritative national product for projects that justify the procurement effort.

### 3.2.4 Global: SRTM, ALOS, and MERIT

For the rest of the world, three global DEM products dominate hydrological practice.

The **Shuttle Radar Topography Mission (SRTM)** remains the most widely used global DEM, despite being based on data collected during a single 11-day Space Shuttle flight in February 2000. SRTM provides coverage from 60 degrees north to 56 degrees south at 1 arc-second (~30 metres) resolution. Its longevity reflects both its quality and its early availability -- it was the first global DEM good enough for serious watershed-scale hydrology. SRTM data are available through multiple portals:

```
https://portal.opentopography.org/raster?opentopoID=OTSRTM.082015.4326.1
https://dwtkns.com/srtm30m/
https://earthexplorer.usgs.gov/
```

The **ALOS World 3D (AW3D30)** dataset from the Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency (JAXA) provides an alternative 30-metre global DEM derived from optical stereo imagery collected by the ALOS satellite's PRISM sensor. Current version 4.1 (March 2025) often outperforms SRTM in mountainous and tropical regions, where SRTM's radar signal is affected by canopy penetration and layover effects. It is available free of charge from:

```
https://www.eorc.jaxa.jp/ALOS/en/dataset/aw3d30/aw3d30_e.htm
```

The **MERIT DEM** (Multi-Error-Removed Improved-Terrain) takes a different approach: rather than collecting new data, it applies sophisticated error correction to existing DEMs (primarily SRTM and AW3D30), removing stripe noise, absolute bias, speckle noise, and tree height bias. Developed by Dai Yamazaki at the University of Tokyo, MERIT is available at 3 arc-second (~90 metres) resolution and is widely regarded as the best global DEM for large-scale hydrological modeling:

```
https://global-hydrodynamics.github.io/MERIT_DEM/
```

The **Copernicus DEM GLO-30** is increasingly preferred over SRTM for new projects, owing to its more recent acquisition dates (2011-2015 versus 2000) and higher vertical accuracy. For any project starting today, GLO-30 should be the default choice unless a specific reason favours an alternative.

## 3.3 River Networks

### 3.3.1 Why Pre-Built River Networks Matter

It is entirely possible to derive a stream network from a DEM using the flow direction and flow accumulation algorithms described in Chapters 10 and 11. But for practical project work, starting from a pre-built, authoritative river network dataset saves enormous effort and avoids common pitfalls. Pre-built networks carry attributes -- stream names, reach identifiers, connectivity information, estimated flows -- that cannot be derived from elevation alone. They have been checked against mapped rivers, adjusted for known discrepancies, and integrated with gauge data. They also provide a common reference framework: when you say "COMID 5781369" or "EU-Hydro segment 12345," everyone working with the same dataset knows exactly which river segment you mean.

The three major systems -- NHDPlus for the United States, EU-Hydro and national databases for Europe, and HydroSHEDS for the globe -- represent different philosophies of river network representation, but all serve the same fundamental purpose: providing a digital skeleton of the drainage system that can be linked to other datasets.

### 3.3.2 United States: NHDPlus

The **National Hydrography Dataset Plus (NHDPlus)** is the foundational geospatial hydrologic framework for the United States. It integrates four major national datasets -- the National Hydrography Dataset (stream geometry), the National Elevation Dataset (terrain), the National Land Cover Dataset (land use), and the Watershed Boundary Dataset (drainage boundaries) -- into a single, seamless product containing approximately **2.7 million reach catchments** across the country. Each reach has an average length of about 2 kilometres, and each catchment has an average area of about 3 square kilometres.

What makes NHDPlus more than just a line dataset is its value-added attributes. Every reach carries:

- A unique identifier (COMID in NHDPlus V2, NHDPlusID in NHDPlus HR) that serves as a universal address for any point on any stream in the country
- Gauge-adjusted mean annual flow estimates from the Enhanced Runoff Method (EROM)
- Drainage area (local and cumulative upstream)
- Channel slope derived from the DEM
- Strahler stream order
- Percentage of upstream drainage in different land cover classes (from NLCD)
- Downstream connectivity via the ToHUC field, enabling network tracing

The same COMID identifiers are used by the **National Water Model** (Chapter 16), providing a direct link between the static river network and the operational flood forecasting system. This integration -- a single identifier connecting the map, the model, and the forecast -- is one of the most powerful aspects of the US water data infrastructure.

NHDPlus data are downloadable by USGS hydrologic region from:

```
http://www.horizon-systems.com/nhdplus/NHDPlusV2_data.php
```

or through the National Map viewer. A high-resolution version (NHDPlus HR), based on 1:24,000-scale mapping, is under development and provides finer-scale delineation.

The United States also employs a hierarchical watershed classification system based on **Hydrologic Unit Codes (HUCs)**. The system proceeds from 2-digit regions (21 nationwide) down to 12-digit sub-watersheds (approximately 100,000 nationwide). The Watershed Boundary Dataset (WBD) provides polygon boundaries at all six levels and is distributed as part of the NHDPlus download.

### 3.3.3 Europe and Hungary: EU-Hydro and National Networks

Europe's equivalent to NHDPlus is **EU-Hydro**, a pan-European river network and catchment dataset produced by the Copernicus Land Monitoring Service. EU-Hydro provides:

- River network lines derived from high-resolution satellite imagery (Sentinel-2, aerial orthophotos) and the Copernicus DEM
- Catchment boundaries at multiple scales
- River/lake connectivity attributes
- Strahler stream order

EU-Hydro covers all EU and EEA member states plus cooperating countries and is available free of charge through the Copernicus Land Monitoring Service:

```
https://land.copernicus.eu/en
```

While EU-Hydro provides the continental framework, Hungary's national water data is managed by the **General Directorate of Water Management (OVF)**, which maintains the Central Hydrographic Database (VRA). This database, accessible through the data.vizugy.hu portal, contains the national river network topology along with associated hydrographic observations -- water level, discharge, water temperature, and groundwater levels from monitoring wells. Since July 2024, all data have been freely and openly available:

```
https://data.vizugy.hu
```

The OVF system serves a dual role that is split between two different institutions in the United States: it provides both the river network geometry (comparable to NHDPlus) and the gauge observation data (comparable to USGS NWIS). For real-time water levels and flood forecasts, the companion portal **HYDROINFO** provides operational data for 98 forecast sections across the Danube, Tisza, and Drava-Mura catchments:

```
https://www.hydroinfo.hu
```

At the EU level, the **Water Information System for Europe (WISE)** provides a pan-European portal linking to Water Framework Directive reporting data, including surface water body status, groundwater body status, pressures, and impacts across all member states:

```
https://water.europa.eu/
```

One important conceptual difference between the US and EU systems relates to watershed hierarchy. The United States uses the HUC system described above -- a rigid, nationally consistent hierarchy from region to sub-watershed. The EU uses a different framework built around the **Water Framework Directive (WFD)**, which organizes European waters into River Basin Districts, sub-basins, and water bodies. Hungary belongs to the Danube River Basin District, the largest in Europe, and its internal water management is organized by regional water directorates rather than by a HUC-like numerical coding scheme. The EU also uses the **NUTS hierarchy** (Nomenclature of Territorial Units for Statistics) for administrative boundaries -- NUTS0 (countries) through NUTS3 (counties/districts) and LAU (municipalities) -- which serves a similar spatial organizing function for socioeconomic data as the HUC system does for hydrological data.

### 3.3.4 Global: HydroSHEDS

For regions outside the US and Europe, **HydroSHEDS** (Hydrological data and maps based on SHuttle Elevation Derivatives at multiple Scales) provides the definitive global hydrographic framework. Developed by the World Wildlife Fund's Conservation Science Program, HydroSHEDS is derived from the SRTM DEM and provides:

- **HydroRIVERS**: a global river network with reach-level attributes including Strahler stream order, upstream drainage area, and estimated long-term discharge
- **HydroBASINS**: a global dataset of watershed boundaries at 12 hierarchical levels, enabling nested watershed analysis anywhere on Earth
- **HydroLAKES**: a global database of 1.4 million lakes with surface area greater than 10 hectares, with attributes including volume estimates and residence times

All HydroSHEDS products are freely available from:

```
https://www.hydrosheds.org/
```

HydroSHEDS is the global equivalent of NHDPlus, and for many international applications -- particularly in data-sparse regions of Africa, Asia, and South America -- it is the only systematic river network and watershed boundary dataset available. HydroSHEDS version 2 is under development, incorporating the improved Copernicus DEM and MERIT DEM rather than the original SRTM.

## 3.4 Land Cover

### 3.4.1 Why Land Cover Matters for Hydrology

Land cover is the interface between the atmosphere and the land surface. It determines how much solar energy is absorbed versus reflected (albedo), how much water is intercepted by vegetation and returned to the atmosphere (evapotranspiration), how much rainfall infiltrates versus runs off (curve numbers, Manning's n), and how fast overland flow moves across the surface (roughness). In practical hydrological modeling, land cover classes are translated into model parameters: a forest pixel gets one set of infiltration rates and roughness coefficients; an urban pixel gets another. Getting the land cover right -- its spatial distribution, its classification accuracy, and its temporal currency -- directly affects the quality of model predictions.

Land cover is also one of the most dynamic inputs. Urban expansion converts permeable farmland into impervious surfaces, increasing peak runoff. Deforestation exposes soil to erosion and reduces evapotranspiration. Agricultural drainage networks alter the timing and routing of subsurface flow. For any study that spans more than a few years, the question is not just "what is the land cover?" but "how has it changed?"

### 3.4.2 United States: NLCD

The **National Land Cover Database (NLCD)** is produced by the Multi-Resolution Land Characteristics Consortium and provides 30-metre resolution land cover classification for the entire United States, derived from Landsat satellite imagery. The NLCD uses a 16-class system that includes:

- Open water
- Developed land at four intensity levels (open space, low, medium, high)
- Barren land
- Forest at three types (deciduous, evergreen, mixed)
- Shrubland
- Grassland/herbaceous
- Agricultural land (pasture/hay, cultivated crops)
- Wetlands (woody, emergent herbaceous)

The NLCD is available for multiple epochs (2001, 2006, 2011, 2016, 2019, 2021), and one of its most valuable products is the **land cover change dataset** that quantifies transitions between categories across intervals. These change products are essential for assessing how urbanization and land use shifts affect hydrological response over time. In NHDPlus, NLCD-derived land cover statistics are pre-computed for each catchment, providing the percentage of upstream drainage area in each class -- a ready-made input for runoff estimation.

Data are accessible through the MRLC portal:

```
http://www.mrlc.gov
```

### 3.4.3 Europe and Hungary: CORINE Land Cover

Europe's standard land cover product is the **CORINE Land Cover (CLC)** inventory, produced by the Copernicus Land Monitoring Service. CORINE uses a **44-class hierarchical system** organized into three levels -- far more detailed than the NLCD's 16 classes. For example, while the NLCD has a single "Cultivated Crops" class, CORINE distinguishes between non-irrigated arable land, permanently irrigated land, rice fields, vineyards, fruit trees and berry plantations, olive groves, pastures, annual crops associated with permanent crops, and complex cultivation patterns.

CORINE is available as both vector polygons and 100-metre raster grids for the years 1990, 2000, 2006, 2012, and 2018, with change layers between consecutive epochs. The next update, referencing the 2020-2024 period, is expected by late 2025. Data are freely accessible from:

```
https://land.copernicus.eu/en/products/corine-land-cover
```

Beyond CORINE, the **Copernicus Land Monitoring Service (CLMS)** provides a broader portfolio of land monitoring products including High Resolution Layers (imperviousness, tree cover, grassland, wetness, water bodies) and bio-geophysical variables (Leaf Area Index, Fraction of Absorbed Photosynthetically Active Radiation, soil moisture). These products are complementary to CORINE and offer higher spatial resolution for specific themes:

```
https://land.copernicus.eu/en
```

For Hungary specifically, CORINE provides the standard land cover classification used in national and EU-funded hydrological studies. The 44-class system is particularly well-suited to the Hungarian landscape, which features extensive agricultural land, wetlands along the Tisza, forests in the northern hills, and the rapidly urbanizing agglomeration around Budapest.

### 3.4.4 Global: ESA WorldCover and Dynamic World

Two global land cover products stand out for their combination of high resolution and global coverage.

**ESA WorldCover** provides the first global land cover maps at **10-metre resolution**, derived from Sentinel-1 (radar) and Sentinel-2 (optical) satellite data. Available for 2020 and 2021, it uses an 11-class system (tree cover, shrubland, grassland, cropland, built-up, bare/sparse vegetation, snow and ice, permanent water bodies, herbaceous wetland, mangroves, moss and lichen). At 10 metres, WorldCover can resolve individual fields, small settlements, and narrow riparian corridors that are invisible in the 30-metre NLCD or the 100-metre CORINE:

```
https://esa-worldcover.org/
```

**Dynamic World**, developed jointly by Google and the World Resources Institute, takes a fundamentally different approach. Rather than producing static maps at discrete time steps, Dynamic World provides **near-real-time land cover classification** updated continuously with each new Sentinel-2 image (every 5 days at the equator). It classifies 9 land use/cover types and runs entirely on Google Earth Engine:

```
https://dynamicworld.app/
```

For historical analysis, the **MODIS Land Cover product (MCD12Q1)** provides annual global land cover at 500-metre resolution since 2001, offering the longest consistent time series for change analysis:

```
https://modis.gsfc.nasa.gov/data/dataprod/mod12.php
```

## 3.5 Soil Data

### 3.5.1 The Invisible Driver

Soil is the invisible regulator of the hydrological cycle. When rain reaches the ground, soil determines how much infiltrates downward (governed by saturated hydraulic conductivity and porosity), how much is stored temporarily in the root zone (governed by field capacity and wilting point), and how much runs off the surface (governed by antecedent moisture conditions and soil texture). In rainfall-runoff modeling, the soil parameters often matter more than the DEM for predicting peak flows in small catchments. Yet soil data receives far less attention than elevation or precipitation, partly because it is harder to measure, harder to visualize, and historically harder to access.

The soil properties most critical for hydrological modeling are:

- **Texture** (sand, silt, clay fractions) -- controls pore size distribution and hence hydraulic conductivity
- **Saturated hydraulic conductivity** ($K_{sat}$) -- the rate at which water moves through saturated soil, typically ranging from less than 0.01 cm/hr for dense clay to more than 10 cm/hr for clean sand
- **Porosity and bulk density** -- total pore volume per unit soil volume
- **Field capacity and wilting point** -- the range of soil moisture available to plants
- **Depth to bedrock or impermeable layer** -- determines total storage capacity
- **Organic carbon content** -- affects water retention and aggregate stability

### 3.5.2 United States: SSURGO and POLARIS

The United States has the world's most detailed national soil survey, maintained by the **Natural Resources Conservation Service (NRCS)** of the US Department of Agriculture. Two primary products are available:

**SSURGO** (Soil Survey Geographic Database) provides detailed soil mapping at 1:24,000 scale (county level). A typical county contains hundreds of soil map units described by thousands of polygons. For Travis County, Texas, for example, 103 soil map units are represented by 7,530 polygons with an average area of about 35 hectares. SSURGO includes detailed tabular data on physical and chemical soil properties, hydrologic soil group, and engineering characteristics. Data are accessible through the Web Soil Survey:

```
https://websoilsurvey.nrcs.usda.gov
```

**STATSGO** (State Soil Geographic Database) provides broader-scale coverage at 1:250,000, suitable for regional and national analyses where SSURGO's detail is not needed.

**POLARIS** (Probabilistic Remapping of SSURGO) provides a 30-metre gridded version of SSURGO soil properties for the contiguous United States, produced using machine-learning techniques at Princeton University. POLARIS eliminates the polygon-based representation of SSURGO and provides continuous spatial predictions of soil properties -- a more natural input for gridded hydrological models.

### 3.5.3 Europe: ESDAC

The **European Soil Data Centre (ESDAC)**, operated by the Joint Research Centre (JRC) of the European Commission, is the primary source of soil information for European hydrological modeling. ESDAC provides:

- The **European Soil Database** at 1-km resolution, with soil type maps and associated property tables
- **LUCAS soil point data** -- physical and chemical properties measured at thousands of sample points across Europe under the Land Use/Cover Area frame statistical Survey
- Thematic layers on soil erosion (by water and wind), organic carbon, compaction, contamination, sealing, and landslide susceptibility
- **Soil hydraulic properties** derived from pedotransfer functions -- the parameters needed directly by hydrological models

ESDAC is accessible at:

```
https://esdac.jrc.ec.europa.eu
```

Some datasets are freely downloadable; others require registration and a data request form. For Hungarian applications, ESDAC provides the best available systematic soil data at the European scale, complemented by the Hungarian soil survey data that Hungary has contributed to the European Soil Database.

### 3.5.4 Global: SoilGrids and HWSD

Two global soil datasets serve regions without national-scale soil surveys.

**SoilGrids** (ISRIC -- World Soil Information) provides global soil property predictions at **250-metre resolution** using machine learning trained on soil profile observations from around the world. It predicts 14 properties at six standard depth intervals (0-5 cm, 5-15 cm, 15-30 cm, 30-60 cm, 60-100 cm, 100-200 cm): pH, organic carbon, bulk density, sand/silt/clay fractions, cation exchange capacity, and nitrogen. SoilGrids is freely available under a CC-BY 4.0 licence:

```
https://soilgrids.org/
https://isric.org/explore/soilgrids
```

Access is available through a web map viewer, WCS/WMS services, WebDAV for bulk download, and Google Earth Engine. Note that the REST API has been intermittently unavailable; the WebDAV and GEE access paths remain the most reliable.

The **Harmonized World Soil Database (HWSD)** version 2.0, developed jointly by IIASA and FAO, provides a comprehensive global soil inventory at 1-km resolution (30 arc-seconds) with nearly 30,000 soil mapping units and seven depth layers from 0 to 200 cm:

```
https://www.fao.org/soils-portal/data-hub/soil-maps-and-databases/harmonized-world-soil-database-v20/en/
```

For hydrological modeling in data-sparse regions, the practical recommendation is straightforward: use SoilGrids at 250 m for the spatial pattern and property predictions, and consult HWSD for broader context and the soil classification framework.

## 3.6 Weather and Climate Data

### 3.6.1 The Engine of the Water Cycle

Precipitation is the primary driver of the terrestrial water cycle, and temperature governs how much of that precipitation returns to the atmosphere through evapotranspiration. Together, they define the water balance of a watershed: the difference between what falls from the sky and what evaporates back determines what is left for streamflow, groundwater recharge, and human use. Getting precipitation and temperature right -- in space, in time, and in magnitude -- is arguably the single most important data challenge in hydrology.

The difficulty is that precipitation is highly variable in both space and time. A thunderstorm can deliver 50 mm of rain to one side of a hill while the other side stays dry. A rain gauge measures precipitation at a single point; interpolating that measurement to a watershed of hundreds of square kilometres introduces uncertainty that propagates through every subsequent calculation. The past two decades have brought revolutionary improvements through radar, satellite, and reanalysis products that provide spatially continuous precipitation fields, but each approach carries its own biases and limitations. Understanding the strengths and weaknesses of different precipitation products is essential for choosing the right data for a given application.

### 3.6.2 United States: PRISM and NCEI

The **Parameter-elevation Regressions on Independent Slopes Model (PRISM)**, developed at Oregon State University, is the standard gridded climate dataset for the United States. PRISM produces monthly and daily maps of precipitation and temperature by interpolating station observations using a sophisticated algorithm that accounts for the effects of elevation, coastal proximity, orographic enhancement, and rain shadow effects. The result is a set of gridded products at approximately 4-km resolution that capture the spatial patterns of climate far better than simple inverse-distance interpolation:

```
https://prism.oregonstate.edu
```

PRISM data are also available through ArcGIS Online's Living Atlas, making them directly accessible within Esri's software ecosystem.

The **National Centers for Environmental Information (NCEI)**, part of NOAA, serves as the primary archive for US weather and climate data. NCEI maintains records from thousands of weather stations across the country, including the Global Historical Climatology Network (GHCN), the Cooperative Observer Program (COOP), and the Automated Surface Observing System (ASOS):

```
https://www.ncei.noaa.gov
```

For model-consistent gridded data, the **National Land Data Assimilation System (NLDAS)** provides hourly precipitation, temperature, and other meteorological variables at 1/8-degree (~12.5 km) resolution for the continental United States. Its global counterpart, **GLDAS**, provides similar data at 1/4-degree resolution worldwide. These products are internally consistent -- they satisfy water balance constraints so that runoff never exceeds precipitation -- making them particularly valuable for driving land surface models.

### 3.6.3 Europe and Hungary: ERA5, E-OBS, CARPATCLIM, and OMSZ

Europe benefits from what is arguably the world's most powerful weather and climate data infrastructure, anchored by the **European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts (ECMWF)** and distributed through the Copernicus Climate Data Store.

**ERA5** is the fifth-generation ECMWF atmospheric reanalysis, covering the period from 1940 to present at approximately 31-km horizontal resolution with hourly time steps. It provides hundreds of atmospheric, land, and oceanic variables, making it the most comprehensive global reanalysis dataset available. For European hydrological studies, **ERA5-Land** provides a higher-resolution (~9 km) version of the land surface component, which is excellent for catchment-scale work in Hungary:

```
https://cds.climate.copernicus.eu/
```

Data are accessible through a web portal and a Python API (cdsapi) that enables automated download of large datasets. ERA5 is a model-based product: it assimilates observations into a physical atmospheric model, producing a physically consistent reconstruction of the atmosphere. This makes it excellent for variables like wind, humidity, and radiation that are poorly observed at the surface, but it means that precipitation can differ from station observations, especially for convective events.

**E-OBS** provides an observation-based complement to ERA5. Produced by the KNMI (Royal Netherlands Meteorological Institute) and the European Climate Assessment & Dataset (ECA&D) project, E-OBS is a daily gridded dataset of temperature and precipitation for Europe from 1950 to present, at 0.1 and 0.25 degree resolution. Current version is v32.0e (November 2025). E-OBS provides a 20-member ensemble with uncertainty estimates, making it possible to quantify the interpolation uncertainty in gridded precipitation -- a feature rare in climate datasets:

```
https://surfobs.climate.copernicus.eu/dataaccess/access_eobs.php
```

For Hungary and the broader Carpathian region specifically, **CARPATCLIM** provides a high-resolution (0.1 degree, approximately 10 km) daily gridded climate dataset covering the period 1961-2010. Produced by a consortium of meteorological services from nine countries led by OMSZ (the Hungarian Meteorological Service), CARPATCLIM is homogenized and harmonized across national boundaries -- particularly important in the Carpathian Basin, where the political borders bisect a single climatic region. Variables include temperature, precipitation, and more than 30 derived climate indices:

```
https://surfobs.climate.copernicus.eu/dataaccess/access_carpatclim.php
```

The **Hungarian Meteorological Service (OMSZ)** operates 136 stations providing 10-minute resolution data on precipitation, temperature, humidity, solar radiation, and wind. Climate summaries are freely available on the OMSZ website; detailed station data may require a formal data request:

```
https://www.met.hu/en/eghajlat/
```

The practical recommendation for Hungarian studies is to use ERA5-Land as the primary driving dataset for continuous simulation, E-OBS for observation-based validation, and CARPATCLIM for historical climate analysis in the 1961-2010 period. For site-specific studies, OMSZ station data provide ground truth but require spatial interpolation.

### 3.6.4 Global: CHIRPS, GPM, and CRU

For regions outside the US and Europe, three global products fill different niches.

**CHIRPS** (Climate Hazards Group InfraRed Precipitation with Station) is a quasi-global rainfall dataset spanning 1981 to present at 0.05-degree (~5 km) resolution, combining satellite infrared imagery with ground station data. CHIRPS was specifically designed for regions with sparse rain gauge networks -- sub-Saharan Africa, Central Asia, parts of South America -- and is the standard precipitation input for drought monitoring and food security applications in these regions. Version 3 is now available, with version 2 production ending in December 2026:

```
https://www.chc.ucsb.edu/data/chirps
```

**GPM/IMERG** (Global Precipitation Measurement / Integrated Multi-satellitE Retrievals) provides near-global precipitation estimates at 0.1-degree (~10 km) resolution and half-hourly temporal resolution, covering 67S to 67N latitude. GPM is a joint NASA/JAXA mission launched in 2014 as the successor to TRMM, and IMERG is the merged precipitation product derived from the constellation of satellites:

```
https://gpm.nasa.gov/
```

GPM/IMERG is particularly valuable for extreme event analysis because of its high temporal resolution -- it can capture the diurnal cycle of convective precipitation and the passage of individual storm systems.

**CRU TS** (Climate Research Unit Time Series) from the University of East Anglia provides monthly gridded climate data for global land areas at 0.5-degree resolution from 1901 to 2024. It is the longest-running global gridded climate dataset, making it essential for trend analysis and climate change studies:

```
https://crudata.uea.ac.uk/cru/data/hrg/
```

For Asia specifically, **APHRODITE** (Asian Precipitation Highly-Resolved Observational Data Integration Towards Evaluation of Extremes) provides the best gauge-based daily gridded precipitation at 0.25 and 0.5 degree resolution for monsoon Asia, the Middle East, and Russia:

```
https://www.chikyu.ac.jp/precip/english/
```

## 3.7 Discharge and Water Level Data

### 3.7.1 The Ground Truth of Hydrology

Stream gauge records are the ground truth of hydrology. Every rainfall-runoff model, every flood frequency analysis, every water balance study ultimately depends on measured discharge data for calibration and validation. A model that matches observed streamflow earns credibility; one that does not, regardless of its theoretical elegance, remains a hypothesis.

Yet stream gauges are declining worldwide. Operating a gauging station requires infrastructure (stilling wells, pressure transducers, data loggers, telemetry), regular maintenance (clearing debris, servicing equipment), and periodic discharge measurements (wading or boat-based velocity profiling) to maintain an accurate stage-discharge relationship. In many developing countries, the costs are prohibitive, and even wealthy nations have seen gauge networks contract under budget pressure. The global peak in the number of operating gauges occurred in the 1980s; since then, networks have shrunk in Africa, South America, and parts of Asia. This "data gap" is one of the most serious challenges in global hydrology.

### 3.7.2 United States: USGS NWIS

The **National Water Information System (NWIS)**, operated by the U.S. Geological Survey, provides the most comprehensive publicly accessible streamflow database in the world. NWIS serves real-time and historical data for approximately 7,000 active stream gauges across the country, with many stations having continuous records extending back decades or even more than a century. Data include:

- Streamflow and stage (real-time and historical)
- Groundwater levels
- Water quality parameters
- Site information (location, drainage area, datum)

Data are accessible through a web interface and web services:

```
https://waterdata.usgs.gov
```

The companion tool **USGS WaterWatch** provides real-time visualization of streamflow conditions, showing which rivers are running above or below normal. For data integration, the **CUAHSI HydroClient** provides unified search across approximately 100 different water observation networks through a single interface:

```
https://data.cuahsi.org
```

The standardized data exchange format underlying these systems is **WaterML**, an XML-based language for water time series data developed by Maidment, Tarboton, and collaborators and subsequently adopted by the USGS and the World Meteorological Organization. WaterML enables machine-to-machine data exchange and is a key component of the emerging "Internet of Water."

### 3.7.3 Europe and Hungary: OVF, HYDROINFO, and EU Systems

Hungary's stream gauge network is operated by the **OVF (General Directorate of Water Management)** and its 12 regional water directorates. The Central Hydrographic Database provides water level, discharge, water temperature, and groundwater level data from stations across the country. Systematic records extend from the early twentieth century for major rivers, with some Danube gauge records reaching back to the nineteenth century. Since July 2024, all data are freely available:

```
https://data.vizugy.hu
https://www.ovf.hu/en/hydrography-water-quality/hydrographic-data
```

For operational monitoring and flood forecasting, **HYDROINFO** provides real-time water levels and forecasts for the Danube, Tisza, and Drava-Mura systems:

```
https://www.hydroinfo.hu
```

The Hungarian gauge network is denser than what might be expected for a country of its size, reflecting the critical importance of flood management in the low-lying Tisza Plain, where the river has been extensively regulated since the nineteenth century and catastrophic floods remain a persistent threat. The 2001, 2006, and 2010 Tisza floods and the record-breaking 2013 Danube flood all highlighted the essential role of the gauging network in flood warning and emergency response.

At the EU level, the **WISE Water Framework Directive Database** provides water body status data reported by all member states:

```
https://www.eea.europa.eu/data-and-maps/data/wise-wfd
```

### 3.7.4 Global: GRDC

The **Global Runoff Data Centre (GRDC)**, operated by the German Federal Institute of Hydrology (BfG) under the auspices of the World Meteorological Organization, maintains the world's largest collection of quality-assured river discharge data. The GRDC holds records from more than 9,800 stations globally, representing approximately 435,000 station-years of data. Records include daily and monthly discharge in WaterML2 and CSV formats:

```
https://grdc.bafg.de/
```

The GRDC is particularly valuable for transboundary river basins where no single country holds the complete hydrological picture. For the Danube, which crosses or borders 10 countries before reaching the Black Sea, the GRDC provides the only integrated dataset spanning the entire river system. Similarly, for the Nile, Mekong, and Niger basins, the GRDC aggregates national contributions into consistent, quality-controlled records.

For water quality data at the global scale, **GEMStat** (Global Water Quality Database) provides the operational database of the UNEP GEMS/Water Programme:

```
https://gemstat.org/
```

Regional water data initiatives complement these global systems. The **Mekong River Commission** provides monitoring data for the Lower Mekong Basin (Cambodia, Laos, Thailand, Vietnam). The **Nile Basin Initiative** coordinates data sharing among 10 Nile riparian countries. India's **Water Resources Information System (India WRIS)** provides dashboards for rainfall, water levels, and discharge across the subcontinent, though international access can be inconsistent. For the Middle East, where water scarcity is the defining challenge, **FAO AQUASTAT** provides country-level water resource statistics and withdrawal data for all MENA countries:

```
https://www.fao.org/aquastat/en/
```

## 3.8 Flood Data and Forecasting Systems

### 3.8.1 From Hazard Maps to Real-Time Forecasts

Flood data falls into two fundamentally different categories: **static hazard maps** that show where flooding has occurred or might occur under specified conditions, and **dynamic forecasting systems** that predict flooding in real time based on current weather conditions and hydrological models. Both are essential, but they serve different purposes. Hazard maps inform land use planning, insurance rates, and emergency preparedness. Forecasting systems trigger evacuations, deploy resources, and save lives during events. The interplay between the two -- using static mapping to pre-compute flood scenarios that are then activated by real-time forecasts -- is one of the most important developments in modern flood management, as discussed in detail in Chapters 15 and 16.

### 3.8.2 United States: FEMA and the National Water Model

The United States has two principal systems for flood information.

The **FEMA National Flood Hazard Layer** (NFHL) provides mapped flood zones -- primarily the 100-year (1% annual exceedance probability) and 500-year (0.2% AEP) floodplains -- organized by county. These maps determine flood insurance requirements, building codes, and development restrictions. However, events like Hurricane Harvey (2017) demonstrated the limitations of static mapping: nearly 60% of the 218,000 damaged buildings in Houston were located outside the mapped 100-year floodplain. Flood risk is not a line on a map; it is a probability distribution that extends well beyond any mapped boundary.

The **National Water Model** (NWM), introduced in Section 1.7.1 and compared with its European and global counterparts in Table 1.1, provides continental-scale hydrologic forecasts for every stream reach in the NHDPlus network. It generates four forecast types:

- **Analysis**: current conditions, updated in near real-time
- **Short-range**: hourly time steps for 18 hours ahead
- **Medium-range**: extends several days ahead
- **Long-range**: extends weeks ahead using ensemble weather forecasts

The NWM uses the same COMID identifiers as NHDPlus, providing a seamless connection between the static river network and the dynamic forecast. Forecast data are accessible through the NOAA Water portal:

```
https://water.noaa.gov/map
```

### 3.8.3 Europe and Hungary: EFAS, JRC, and National Systems

Europe's flood forecasting architecture operates at two scales: continental (EU-wide) and national.

The **European Flood Awareness System** (EFAS), introduced in Section 1.7.2, is operated by ECMWF under the Copernicus Emergency Management Service and provides pan-European probabilistic flood forecasts. EFAS uses ECMWF ensemble weather forecasts to drive the LISFLOOD hydrological model over the EU-Hydro river network, producing medium-range flood forecasts, flash flood indicators, seasonal outlooks, and monitoring of soil moisture, runoff, and snow water equivalent. Archived data are publicly available; real-time forecasts are restricted to EFAS partner organizations (national hydrological services):

```
https://european-flood.emergency.copernicus.eu/efas_frontend/
```

The **Joint Research Centre (JRC)** provides static flood hazard maps for Europe, showing modeled flood extent for return periods from 10 to 500 years. These are the European equivalent of FEMA flood maps, though produced through continental-scale hydrological and hydraulic modeling rather than the piecemeal county-by-county approach used in the US:

```
https://data.jrc.ec.europa.eu/collection/id-0054
```

The **Copernicus Emergency Management Service (EMS)** provides on-demand rapid mapping for active disaster events (floods, fires, earthquakes), activated by authorized users such as national civil protection agencies:

```
https://emergency.copernicus.eu/
```

Hungary's national flood risk maps are produced by OVF in compliance with the EU Floods Directive 2007/60/EC. These maps show flood extent for various return periods and were most recently revised in 2022. Flood risk management plans are accessible through:

```
https://vizeink.hu
```

Hungary's flood management infrastructure is one of the most sophisticated in Central Europe, shaped by the country's geography: the Tisza Plain is one of the largest continuous floodplains in Europe, protected by an extensive levee system built following the catastrophic floods of the nineteenth century. The 2001 and 2006 Tisza floods and the 2010 "red sludge" disaster at Kolontar tested this infrastructure to its limits, while the 2013 Danube flood -- the largest on the upper Danube since 1501 -- demonstrated the need for transboundary coordination through systems like EFAS.

### 3.8.4 Global: GloFAS and Disaster Databases

The **Global Flood Awareness System (GloFAS)**, also operated under Copernicus, extends EFAS to global coverage. GloFAS version 4.0 provides flood forecasts at 0.05-degree (~5 km) resolution up to 30 days ahead and seasonal outlooks up to 4 months ahead:

```
https://global-flood.emergency.copernicus.eu/
```

For regions without their own forecasting systems -- which includes much of sub-Saharan Africa, Central Asia, and parts of Southeast Asia -- GloFAS provides the only available medium-range flood forecast. The **African Flood and Drought Monitor (AFDM)** provides experimental early warning for flood and drought conditions across Africa:

```
https://hydrology.soton.ac.uk/apps/afdm/
```

The **Dartmouth Flood Observatory (DFO)** at the University of Colorado maintains a global archive of large flood events since 1985, using satellite observations to monitor surface water extent and estimate discharge at ungauged locations:

```
https://floodobservatory.colorado.edu/
```

For disaster loss accounting, **DesInventar Sendai** provides standardized disaster records for 82 countries under the UN Sendai Framework for Disaster Risk Reduction:

```
https://www.desinventar.net/
```

## 3.9 Demographics and Population Data

### 3.9.1 Connecting Water Hazards to Human Exposure

A flood that inundates empty farmland is a natural event. A flood that inundates a city is a disaster. The difference is people -- and to quantify flood risk, we need to know where people live, how many there are, and what infrastructure surrounds them. Chapter 17 explores this connection in detail; here we focus on the data sources that make it possible.

Demographic data serves multiple purposes in water resources analysis: estimating the population exposed to flood hazard, calculating per-capita water demand for supply planning, assessing the economic value of assets at risk, and identifying vulnerable communities (the elderly, low-income households, populations without transportation) for emergency planning. The spatial resolution of demographic data matters: knowing that a country has 10 million people is useful for national policy, but it tells you nothing about whether a specific floodplain neighbourhood has 500 or 50,000 residents.

### 3.9.2 United States: Census Bureau

The **U.S. Census Bureau** provides the most detailed publicly available demographic dataset in the world, organized in a nested hierarchy from states down to individual census blocks:

**Table 3.6**: US Census geographic hierarchy

| Level | Approximate Count | Population Data | Socioeconomic Data |
|-------|------------------|----------------|-------------------|
| Census blocks | 11,000,000 | Yes (counts only) | No |
| Block groups | 220,000 | Yes | Yes (ACS) |
| Census tracts | 74,000 | Yes | Yes (ACS) |
| Counties | ~3,100 | Yes | Yes |
| States | 50 + territories | Yes | Yes |

The **American Community Survey (ACS)** supplements the decennial census with continuously updated socioeconomic data, including income, education, housing characteristics, and commuting patterns. ACS data are available as 1-year estimates (for larger areas) and 5-year estimates (for all areas down to block groups). Geographic boundary files are provided through the **TIGER/Line** shapefiles programme, which provides machine-readable boundaries for all census geographies.

Census block groups are the most useful unit for spatial analysis in water resources because they combine reasonable spatial resolution (~600 households) with the availability of socioeconomic attributes needed for vulnerability assessment.

### 3.9.3 Europe and Hungary: Eurostat, GISCO, and KSH

The **Hungarian Central Statistical Office (KSH)** provides the national equivalent of the Census Bureau. The 2022 Census (nepszamlalas) provides population and housing data at multiple geographic levels:

- **Megye** (county) -- 19 counties plus Budapest
- **Jaras** (district) -- approximately 175 districts
- **Telepules** (settlement/municipality) -- approximately 3,155 municipalities
- **Szamlalokorzt** (census tract) -- finest available spatial unit

Census data are accessible through the KSH web portal and API:

```
https://www.ksh.hu/?lang=en
https://nepszamlalas2022.ksh.hu/en/database/
https://data.ksh.hu/
```

At the EU level, **Eurostat** provides harmonized demographic and socioeconomic data across all member states, organized by the **NUTS hierarchy** (Nomenclature of Territorial Units for Statistics):

- NUTS0: Countries
- NUTS1: Major socioeconomic regions
- NUTS2: Basic regions for the application of regional policies
- NUTS3: Small regions (counties in Hungary)
- LAU: Local Administrative Units (municipalities)

The **GISCO** (Geographic Information System of the Commission) programme provides the geographic boundary files for all NUTS and LAU regions in shapefile, GeoJSON, and TopoJSON formats:

```
https://ec.europa.eu/eurostat/web/gisco/geodata
```

### 3.9.4 Global: WorldPop and LandScan

For regions without detailed census geography, two gridded population products provide spatially continuous population estimates.

**WorldPop** provides gridded population estimates at **100-metre and 1-km resolution** for the entire globe, with particular emphasis on low- and middle-income countries in Africa, Asia, and Latin America. WorldPop uses a combination of census data, satellite imagery, and machine-learning models to disaggregate national and subnational population totals into fine-resolution grids:

```
https://www.worldpop.org/
```

**LandScan**, produced by Oak Ridge National Laboratory (ORNL), provides a global population distribution dataset at approximately **1-km resolution**, updated annually. LandScan estimates the "ambient" (24-hour average) population, accounting for the fact that people move between home, work, and other locations during the day:

```
https://landscan.ornl.gov/
```

**GADM** (Global Administrative Areas) provides the boundary files needed to map demographic data, with high-resolution boundaries for all countries and their administrative subdivisions:

```
https://gadm.org/
```

For a flood exposure study in, say, Bangladesh -- where census data may be available at the district level but not the village level -- the combination of WorldPop (for spatially detailed population) and GADM (for administrative boundaries) provides a workable approximation that would be impossible from census tables alone.

## 3.10 Satellite Remote Sensing Platforms

### 3.10.1 Seeing the Earth from Above

Satellite remote sensing has transformed hydrology from a data-limited science into a data-rich one. The Landsat programme alone has produced more than 50 years of continuous Earth observation. The Sentinel constellation provides 10-metre optical imagery every 5 days. GRACE measures changes in Earth's gravity field to track water storage from space. MODIS captures the entire globe daily at moderate resolution. And cloud computing platforms like Google Earth Engine make it possible to analyze these petabytes of data without downloading a single file.

For water resources applications, the key satellite-derived products include:

- **Land surface reflectance and vegetation indices** (NDVI, EVI) -- for monitoring crop health, evapotranspiration estimation, and land cover mapping
- **Land surface temperature** -- for energy balance and evapotranspiration models
- **Soil moisture** -- from microwave radiometry (SMAP, SMOS) and radar backscatter (Sentinel-1)
- **Water extent** -- mapping flood inundation, lake area changes, and wetland dynamics from optical and radar imagery
- **Snow cover and snow water equivalent** -- from visible/infrared (MODIS) and passive microwave sensors
- **Total water storage changes** -- from GRACE/GRACE-FO gravity measurements (the only way to monitor groundwater depletion from space)

### 3.10.2 United States: Landsat and MODIS

**Landsat**, the longest-running Earth observation programme, has provided continuous multispectral imagery since 1972. Landsat 8 and 9 currently provide 30-metre resolution imagery with a 16-day revisit (effectively 8 days with both satellites). Landsat data are freely available through the USGS EarthExplorer and NASA Earthdata:

```
https://earthexplorer.usgs.gov/
https://earthdata.nasa.gov/
```

**MODIS** (Moderate Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer), aboard the Terra and Aqua satellites, provides daily global coverage at 250 m to 1 km resolution. MODIS products include land cover, vegetation indices, land surface temperature, snow cover, and fire detection. The **NASA Worldview** tool provides daily MODIS imagery for quick visualization:

```
https://worldview.earthdata.nasa.gov
```

**NASA Earthdata** serves as the unified gateway to all NASA Earth observation data, providing access to more than 178 petabytes through multiple Distributed Active Archive Centers (DAACs):

```
https://earthdata.nasa.gov/
```

### 3.10.3 Europe: Copernicus Data Space and Sentinel

The **Copernicus Data Space Ecosystem** provides free access to all Sentinel mission data:

- **Sentinel-1**: C-band Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR), all-weather day-and-night imaging. Critical for flood mapping (water appears dark in radar images), soil moisture estimation, and land deformation monitoring.
- **Sentinel-2**: Multispectral optical imagery at **10-metre resolution** with a 5-day revisit at the equator. The workhorse for land cover mapping, vegetation monitoring, and water quality assessment. At 10 metres, Sentinel-2 provides substantially higher resolution than Landsat (30 m) while maintaining free and open data access.
- **Sentinel-3**: Ocean and land monitoring at coarser resolution, including sea surface temperature, ocean colour, and land surface temperature.
- **Sentinel-5P**: Atmospheric composition (NO$_2$, SO$_2$, O$_3$, CH$_4$, CO, aerosol).

The Copernicus Data Space replaced the older Open Access Hub (scihub.copernicus.eu) in October 2023 and provides multiple access mechanisms:

```
https://dataspace.copernicus.eu/
https://browser.dataspace.copernicus.eu/
```

Access methods include the Sentinel Hub APIs, openEO (a standardized API for Earth observation processing), STAC (SpatioTemporal Asset Catalogs), OData, and direct S3 bucket access. For processing Sentinel data without downloading, the platform provides JupyterLab, Data Workspace, and on-demand processing capabilities.

**GRACE/GRACE-FO** (Gravity Recovery and Climate Experiment) deserves special mention as it measures something no other satellite can: changes in Earth's gravity field caused by the redistribution of water mass. By measuring monthly variations in gravity, GRACE tracks changes in total terrestrial water storage -- groundwater, soil moisture, snow, ice, and surface water combined. For groundwater depletion studies, particularly in regions like the Middle East, North India, and North Africa where aquifers are being overexploited, GRACE provides the only observational constraint on rates of groundwater loss. The European G3P (Global Gravity-based Groundwater Product) further separates the groundwater component from total water storage:

```
https://grace.jpl.nasa.gov/data/get-data/
https://gravis.gfz.de/gws
```

### 3.10.4 The Cloud Computing Revolution: Google Earth Engine

**Google Earth Engine (GEE)** has fundamentally changed how researchers interact with satellite data. Rather than downloading terabytes of imagery to a local computer, GEE brings the computation to the data. Its multi-petabyte catalog includes Landsat (the full 50+ year archive), all Sentinel missions, MODIS, ERA5, CHIRPS, SoilGrids, WorldPop, and hundreds of other datasets. Analyses that would take days on a local machine -- computing annual land cover change across an entire country, for example -- can be completed in minutes on GEE's cloud infrastructure:

```
https://earthengine.google.com/
```

GEE is free for academic and research use and provides both a JavaScript-based Code Editor (in the browser) and a Python API for programmatic access. For students and researchers in data-sparse regions, GEE is often the single most important tool: it provides free access to both the data and the computing power needed to analyze it.

## 3.12 Building a Watershed Base Map

### 3.12.1 Purpose and Overview

Every water resources GIS project, regardless of location, begins with the same fundamental task: assembling a **watershed base map** -- the foundational collection of geographic layers needed for hydrological analysis. The base map typically includes a watershed boundary, sub-watershed boundaries, a stream network with flow attributes, gauge locations, and optionally elevation, land cover, and soil data. This section demonstrates the workflow twice: first using US data sources, then using Hungarian/EU data sources, showing that the analytical logic is identical even though the specific datasets and portals differ.

### 3.12.2 US Workflow: Little Bear Logan Watershed (NHDPlus)

The US workflow uses the NHDPlus framework, which integrates multiple national datasets into a single download. The target area is the Little Bear Logan watershed in northern Utah (HUC-8 16010203), a mountainous catchment draining approximately 2,289 km$^2$.

**Step 1: Download the data.** Navigate to the National Map viewer and locate the NHDPlus High Resolution dataset for USGS Hydrologic Region 16 (Great Basin). Download the NHDPlus geodatabase, which contains the Watershed Boundary Dataset, NHD flow lines, EROM flow tables, and catchment boundaries.

```
https://viewer.nationalmap.gov
```

**Step 2: Set up the project.** Create a new GIS project with a feature dataset in the UTM coordinate system appropriate for the study area. Logan, Utah (longitude ~111 degrees west) falls in UTM Zone 12N (NAD 1983). Using a projected coordinate system ensures that areas and distances are computed in meaningful metric units.

**Step 3: Extract the watershed boundary.** Add the WBD HUC-8 layer from the downloaded geodatabase. Select the polygon for HUC-8 16010203 and export it to the project feature dataset. After projection to UTM, the shape area is reported as approximately 2.289 x 10$^9$ m$^2$ (2,289 km$^2$).

**Step 4: Extract sub-watersheds.** Add the WBD HUC-12 layer. Select all sub-watersheds whose HUC-12 code begins with "16010203" -- these are the HUC-12 units nested within the target HUC-8. Export to the feature dataset. The attribute table includes HUC-12 identifier, area, name, and downstream connectivity (ToHUC).

To visualize intermediate-level HUC-10 watersheds, add a new integer field and calculate:

```python
int(!HUC12!) / 100
```

This integer division truncates the last two digits of each HUC-12 code, producing the corresponding HUC-10 identifier. Symbolizing by unique HUC-10 values reveals the intermediate watershed hierarchy.

**Step 5: Add the stream network.** Drag NHD flow lines from the geodatabase. Join the EROM mean annual flow table using the NHDPlus identifier as the join key, adding gauge-adjusted mean annual flow estimates (in cubic feet per second) to each reach. Select flow lines whose centres fall within the watershed boundary and export them. Symbolize using graduated line widths proportional to flow, producing a map where the main stem appears thicker than tributaries.

**Step 6: Add stream gauges.** Import gauge locations from a table of latitude/longitude coordinates (from NWIS) using the XY Table to Point tool. Convert DMS coordinates to decimal degrees:

$$\text{Latitude (DD)} = \text{Degrees} + \frac{\text{Minutes}}{60} + \frac{\text{Seconds}}{3600}$$

Maintain at least 5 significant figures to avoid positional imprecision. For western hemisphere longitudes, negate the result. Specify the original datum (often NAD 1927 for older USGS stations) before projecting to the project coordinate system.

**Step 7: Validate flows.** Compare measured mean annual flow at each gauge (from NWIS records) with the NHDPlus EROM estimate at the adjacent reach. Differences of 30-40 CFS are typical and reflect the inherent uncertainty in regression-based flow estimation as well as differences in periods of record.

**Step 8: Connect to the National Water Model.** For any reach in the network, retrieve real-time and forecast flow data from the National Water Model using the COMID identifier:

```
https://water.noaa.gov/map
```

This connects the static base map to the dynamic forecasting system that uses the same underlying geospatial framework.

### 3.12.3 Hungarian Workflow: Zala River Watershed (EU-Hydro + Copernicus DEM)

The Hungarian workflow demonstrates the same analytical process using European data sources. The target is the **Zala River watershed**, which drains approximately 2,622 km$^2$ before entering Lake Balaton -- the largest lake in Central Europe. The Zala is Hungary's most important tributary to Balaton and is the subject of ongoing water quality monitoring and eutrophication management.

**Step 1: Obtain elevation data.** Download the Copernicus DEM GLO-30 for the area of interest. The tiles covering the Zala watershed (approximately 46.5-47.2N, 16.5-17.5E) can be obtained from the Copernicus Data Space:

```
https://dataspace.copernicus.eu/
```

Alternatively, the same tiles are available through OpenTopography, which provides a convenient mosaic and clipping interface. The data arrive in GeoTIFF format with WGS84 geographic coordinates and the EGM2008 geoid as the vertical datum. For higher resolution, request the EEA-10 (10-metre) product if your institution qualifies for access.

**Step 2: Set up the project.** Create a new GIS project with a feature dataset. For the Zala watershed, which lies approximately between 16.5 and 17.5 degrees east longitude, the appropriate projected coordinate system is **UTM Zone 34N (ETRS89, EPSG:25834)**. Alternatively, for integration with national Hungarian data, use the **EOV (EPSG:23700)** system. The choice depends on whether the project primarily uses European (Copernicus, EU-Hydro) or national (OVF, Lechner) data sources.

**A note on Hungarian coordinates**: when working with Hungarian datasets, be aware that EOV coordinate labels are frequently swapped in data files. Identify the correct assignment by **value range**: Northing values fall between 0 and 400,000; Easting values fall between 400,000 and 1,000,000. Do not trust the column headers -- trust the numbers.

**Step 3: Obtain the river network.** Download EU-Hydro river network data for Hungary from the Copernicus Land Monitoring Service. The data include stream lines with Strahler stream order, upstream drainage area, and connectivity attributes.

```
https://land.copernicus.eu/en
```

For national gauge data, access the OVF Central Hydrographic Database:

```
https://data.vizugy.hu
```

**Step 4: Delineate the watershed.** Unlike the US workflow, where the Watershed Boundary Dataset provides pre-delineated HUC polygons ready for download, the European workflow may require watershed delineation from the DEM. Using the Copernicus DEM:

1. Fill sinks in the DEM (Chapter 9)
2. Compute flow direction (Chapter 10)
3. Compute flow accumulation (Chapter 10)
4. Define the watershed outlet at the Zala River's entry to Lake Balaton
5. Delineate the watershed boundary upstream of the outlet (Chapter 11)

Alternatively, HydroBASINS provides pre-computed watershed boundaries at multiple scales that can be used directly:

```
https://www.hydrosheds.org/
```

The EU also defines river basin boundaries under the Water Framework Directive; Hungary's sub-basin delineations for the Zala are available through the national water management plans.

**Step 5: Obtain land cover.** Download CORINE Land Cover for the Zala watershed from the Copernicus Land Monitoring Service. The 44-class system distinguishes between the vineyard-covered slopes along the northern shore of Balaton, the arable farmland of the Zala valley, the forested uplands of the Bakony Mountains, and the wetlands at the Kis-Balaton reservoir (an artificial wetland constructed for water quality management).

**Step 6: Obtain soil data.** Download soil property data from ESDAC for the Hungarian portion of the Carpathian Basin, or use SoilGrids for consistent global coverage at 250-metre resolution.

**Step 7: Obtain discharge data.** Access historical discharge records for OVF gauge stations on the Zala from data.vizugy.hu. For real-time monitoring, check HYDROINFO for current water levels and forecasts.

**Step 8: Validate and integrate.** Overlay the EU-Hydro stream network on the DEM-derived flow accumulation grid to check consistency. Compare OVF gauge records with any available GRDC data for the same stations. Overlay CORINE land cover on the watershed to compute land use statistics for each sub-catchment.

The result is a watershed base map functionally equivalent to the US NHDPlus-based version, assembled from European data sources that are equally free and equally well-documented. The main difference is that the US workflow benefits from the pre-integrated NHDPlus package (elevation + network + land cover + boundaries in a single download), while the European workflow requires assembling equivalent layers from separate sources. This difference reflects institutional history, not data quality.

## 3.13 Practical Considerations

### 3.13.1 Data Access Registrations

Most of the datasets described in this chapter require free registration before data can be downloaded. It is worth creating accounts early -- before the pressure of a project deadline -- at the following platforms:

- **NASA Earthdata** (https://earthdata.nasa.gov/) -- required for SRTM, MODIS, GRACE, GPM, and all NASA data
- **Copernicus Climate Data Store** (https://cds.climate.copernicus.eu/) -- required for ERA5, E-OBS, CARPATCLIM
- **Copernicus Data Space** (https://dataspace.copernicus.eu/) -- required for Sentinel-1/2/3, Copernicus DEM
- **Google Earth Engine** (https://earthengine.google.com/) -- required for cloud-based analysis of most datasets
- **GRDC** (https://grdc.bafg.de/) -- required for global discharge data

For Hungarian national data, no registration is needed for data.vizugy.hu (OVF) or HYDROINFO, though the former runs as an Angular web application that may require a modern browser. KSH data (https://data.ksh.hu/) are freely accessible, and the OMSZ climate portal (https://www.met.hu/en/eghajlat/) provides summaries without registration.

### 3.13.2 Data Formats and Interoperability

Water data arrives in a bewildering variety of formats. Elevation data typically comes as GeoTIFF or NetCDF. River networks come as shapefiles, geodatabases, or GeoPackage. Climate data comes as GRIB (ECMWF's format) or NetCDF. Gauge data comes as CSV, WaterML, or through web service APIs. Satellite data comes as SAFE (Sentinel) or HDF5 (MODIS) containers.

For practical work, the critical skill is not memorizing formats but understanding the general pattern: raster data (elevation, precipitation, land cover, soil) comes as regular grids in GeoTIFF or NetCDF; vector data (river networks, watershed boundaries, gauge locations) comes as shapefiles or geodatabases; time series data (discharge, water levels, weather) comes as tabular files or through APIs. Any modern GIS software (ArcGIS Pro, QGIS) or scientific Python environment (with rasterio, geopandas, xarray) can read all of these formats. Chapter 4 discusses the software tools in detail.

### 3.13.3 Coordinate System Considerations

When assembling data from multiple sources, the most common technical problem is mismatched coordinate systems. US data arrives in NAD83 geographic coordinates or State Plane/UTM projections. Hungarian data arrives in HD72/EOV (EPSG:23700). EU data arrives in ETRS89 (EPSG:4258) or ETRS89/LAEA (EPSG:3035). Global data arrives in WGS84 (EPSG:4326). All of these must be transformed to a common system before overlay and analysis.

The transformation from HD72/EOV to ETRS89 (EPSG:1449) is a 7-parameter Helmert transformation available in all major GIS software and the PROJ library. For sub-metre accuracy, a national grid shift file from Lechner is needed, but for most hydrological applications the standard transformation is adequate. When combining Hungarian national data (EOV) with Copernicus data (typically ETRS89 or WGS84), this transformation is a routine first step. See Chapter 2 for a detailed treatment of coordinate systems and transformations.

### 3.13.4 When Data Sources Conflict

It is common for two datasets covering the same area to disagree. The CORINE land cover map may show a pixel as forest while ESA WorldCover shows it as cropland. The SRTM elevation may differ from the Copernicus DEM by several metres at the same location. An ERA5 precipitation estimate for a given day may be twice what E-OBS reports. These discrepancies are not errors in the usual sense -- they reflect different data sources, different algorithms, different time periods, and different definitions of the same variable.

The practical response is to understand the provenance of each dataset and choose the one best suited to the application. For land cover in Europe, CORINE's 44-class system provides more thematic detail than WorldCover's 11 classes, but WorldCover's 10-metre resolution captures spatial detail that CORINE's 100-metre raster cannot. For precipitation, ERA5 provides physical consistency (water balance closure) while E-OBS preserves fidelity to station observations. For elevation, a national product like DDM-5 is always more accurate than a global product for the same area, but a global product is more convenient and more interoperable.

## 3.14 Looking Forward: The Evolving Data Landscape

The water data landscape described in this chapter is a snapshot of 2026, and it is changing rapidly. Several trends will reshape data availability in the coming years.

**Cloud-native data access** is replacing traditional file downloads. Instead of downloading a 50-gigabyte DEM to a local hard drive, analysts increasingly access data through cloud-optimized formats (Cloud Optimized GeoTIFF, Zarr, STAC catalogs) that allow reading only the tiles and bands needed for a specific analysis. The Copernicus Data Space, Microsoft Planetary Computer, and Google Earth Engine all represent this shift.

**Open data mandates** continue to expand. The EU's Open Data Directive and the US OPEN Government Data Act are pushing agencies to make data freely available by default. Hungary's OVF made its hydrographic database freely available in July 2024, following this trend. As more countries adopt open data policies, the global gap between data-rich and data-poor regions will narrow.

**AI-derived datasets** are beginning to complement traditional observations. Machine learning is used to produce SoilGrids from sparse observations, to create Dynamic World from Sentinel-2 imagery in near-real-time, and to generate MERIT DEM by correcting systematic errors in existing DEMs. Future datasets will increasingly be AI products -- trained on observations but generating predictions where observations are absent.

**The Internet of Water** -- a vision of interconnected, machine-readable water data systems -- is slowly becoming reality through standards like WaterML, STAC, and openEO. The ultimate goal is a world where a researcher in Debrecen can write a single query that retrieves precipitation data from OMSZ, discharge data from OVF, and satellite imagery from Copernicus, all formatted consistently and ready for analysis.

Until that future arrives, this chapter provides the map. The data is out there. Knowing where to find it is the first step toward doing something useful with it.
