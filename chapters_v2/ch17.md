# Chapter 17: Who Lives in the Flood Zone?

Flood maps tell you where water goes. Demographic data tell you who is there when it arrives. A flood that inundates empty farmland is a hydrological event; the same flood passing through a neighbourhood of elderly residents who cannot evacuate on their own is a humanitarian crisis. The difference between these two outcomes has nothing to do with hydrology and everything to do with demography -- the study of human populations, their distribution, their characteristics, and their vulnerabilities.

This chapter sits at the intersection of the natural-systems analysis developed in Chapters 9 through 16 and the human dimension that transforms a flood map into a risk assessment. We have learned to delineate watersheds, compute height above nearest drainage, map flood inundation zones, and forecast river stages in real time. All of that work produces geographic boundaries: polygons showing where water will be. The question this chapter answers is: what happens when we overlay those polygons on the places where people live, work, and sleep?

The tools are not new -- clipping, overlay, zonal statistics, raster conversion -- all techniques introduced in earlier chapters. What is new is the data: population counts, household incomes, age distributions, housing types, vulnerability indices. These data come from national census systems, and every country organises its census differently. We will examine three tiers of demographic data systems. The United States Census Bureau provides one of the world's most detailed and accessible spatial demographic frameworks, and it serves as our entry point for understanding the concepts. Hungary's Central Statistical Office (KSH) and its 2022 census provide the European parallel, embedded within the EU-wide Eurostat system and the NUTS hierarchy. For students working in regions without detailed census infrastructure, global gridded population datasets -- WorldPop, LandScan, and the GADM administrative boundary database -- offer alternatives that cover every country on Earth.

The analytical question running through the chapter is deceptively simple: how many people live in a given area, and who are they? The area might be a watershed, a floodplain, an aquifer recharge zone, or a dam failure inundation zone. The "who" might be total population, elderly residents, children, households below the poverty line, or structures with basements that flood. Answering this question precisely requires understanding both the geographic framework of census data and the spatial analysis techniques that connect census geography to hydrological geography.

## 17.1 Why Demographics Matter for Water Management

### 17.1.1 The Human Dimension of Flood Risk

Flood risk is not a purely physical quantity. The standard formulation, adopted by the EU Floods Directive (2007/60/EC), the United Nations Office for Disaster Risk Reduction (UNDRR), and virtually every national flood management framework, decomposes risk into three components:

$$\text{Risk} = \text{Hazard} \times \text{Exposure} \times \text{Vulnerability}$$

**Hazard** is the physical event -- the flood depth, velocity, duration, and probability of occurrence. Chapters 14 through 16 dealt with hazard: computing HAND-based relative elevation (Chapter 14), translating discharge forecasts into inundation maps (Chapter 15), and forecasting river stages in real time (Chapter 16). **Exposure** is the inventory of people, buildings, infrastructure, and economic activity located within the hazard zone. **Vulnerability** is the susceptibility of those exposed elements to damage -- a concrete apartment building on stilts is less vulnerable than a single-storey wooden house with an elderly occupant who cannot climb stairs.

Demographics provide the data for both exposure and vulnerability. A census tells you how many people live in each block, tract, or municipality. It tells you their ages, incomes, housing types, mobility limitations, and household compositions. Without this information, a flood map is an academic exercise. With it, the same map becomes an emergency management tool that identifies which neighbourhoods need evacuation buses, which need medical teams, which have populations that speak languages other than the national language, and which have the economic resources to recover on their own.

The importance of this linkage becomes starkly clear during actual flood events. During Hurricane Harvey in 2017, the city of Houston had detailed census data at the block level, enabling emergency managers to estimate that approximately 300,000 housing units were in the inundation zone and that roughly 13,000 residents in the affected area had mobility disabilities. During the catastrophic 2013 Danube flood -- the largest on record since 1501 at Passau -- Hungarian and German authorities used census-linked population data to prioritise evacuations along the Danube and its tributaries. In both cases, the critical operational question was not "where does the water go?" but "who is in its path?"

### 17.1.2 Beyond Floods: Demographic Data in Water Resources

Flood risk assessment is the most dramatic application, but demographic data serve water management more broadly. Population distribution and growth rates drive water demand forecasting: a municipality planning its water supply for the next thirty years needs to know not just how many people it serves today, but where growth is occurring and at what rate. Wastewater treatment capacity must match population and industrial activity. Groundwater management requires knowing the population drawing from an aquifer -- the Edwards Aquifer in Texas serves over 2 million people, and planning its sustainable yield requires knowing precisely where those people are and how their numbers are changing.

Agricultural water use, which dominates total water consumption in many countries, correlates with the rural population and the type of farming practiced. In Hungary, where agriculture accounts for a significant share of water withdrawals, the spatial distribution of rural populations mapped against irrigation infrastructure reveals where water stress is likely to intensify as climate patterns shift.

Urban stormwater management depends on impervious surface area, which correlates with population density and housing type. Combined sewer overflow problems concentrate in older, densely populated urban cores -- exactly the areas where demographic data reveal populations with limited resources to cope with sewage-contaminated floodwater in their homes.

## 17.2 Census Systems: Counting People and Placing Them on the Map

Every country conducts some form of population census, though the methods, frequency, and spatial resolution vary enormously. The fundamental challenge is the same everywhere: count every person, record their characteristics, and assign each count to a geographic unit small enough to be analytically useful. The result is a spatial database that combines attribute data (population, income, age, housing type) with geographic boundaries (polygons representing enumeration areas). This section examines three census systems in detail: the US Census Bureau, Hungary's KSH, and the EU-wide Eurostat framework. The concepts transfer directly to any national census.

### 17.2.1 The United States Census System

The US Census Bureau operates the most spatially detailed and publicly accessible demographic data system in the world. Its two principal products for spatial analysis are the Decennial Census and the American Community Survey (ACS).

The **Decennial Census of Population and Housing** is a complete enumeration of every household, conducted every ten years as mandated by the US Constitution for the purpose of Congressional reapportionment. The most recent completed census was in 2020. It collects a deliberately limited set of information -- essentially, how many people live at each address, their ages, races, and whether the housing unit is owned or rented. Its strength is completeness: every person is counted (in principle), and data are available at the finest geographic unit, the census block.

The **American Community Survey (ACS)** is a continuous statistical survey that replaced the old "long form" census questionnaire. Each month, approximately 250,000 households receive the ACS, yielding about 3 million responses per year. The ACS collects detailed socioeconomic characteristics: household income, educational attainment, language spoken at home, occupation, commuting patterns, disability status, health insurance coverage, and many more. In total, the ACS produces 7,731 variables organised into 21 tables. Because it is a sample rather than a complete enumeration, every estimate comes with a margin of error. ACS data are released as period estimates: 1-year estimates for geographies with populations above 65,000, and 5-year estimates for all geographies down to the block group level.

**Table 17.1**: Principal US Census Bureau data programs relevant to water resources analysis

| Program | Frequency | Type | Finest Geography | Content |
|---------|-----------|------|-----------------|---------|
| Decennial Census | Every 10 years | Complete count | Census block | Population, housing units, age, race |
| American Community Survey (ACS) | Continuous (annual release) | Sample survey | Block group (5-year) | 7,731 variables: income, education, employment, language, disability |
| Population Estimates Program | Annual | Model-based | County | Intercensal population estimates |
| Economic Census | Every 5 years | Complete count | County/ZIP | Business establishments, employment, revenue |

The geographic hierarchy of the US Census is a nested system of progressively smaller spatial units:

```
Nation
 +-- Regions (4)
      +-- States (50 + DC)
           +-- Counties (~3,200)
                +-- Census Tracts (~74,000)
                     +-- Block Groups (~220,000; avg ~600 households)
                          +-- Census Blocks (~11,000,000)
```

Census tracts are designed to contain roughly homogeneous populations of 1,200 to 8,000 people. As areas urbanise and grow, tracts are subdivided -- never merged -- producing increasingly fine spatial resolution in growing areas while rural tracts remain large. A single tract numbered 1130 in 1970 might become tracts 1130.01 and 1130.02 by 1980, then four tracts by 1990, and twelve by 2000, reflecting progressive suburbanisation. Census blocks, the finest unit, typically contain 30 to 40 housing units -- the houses along one or two street segments. There are approximately 11 million blocks nationwide, averaging about 30 people each, though many rural blocks contain zero population.

The **FIPS (Federal Information Processing System) code** provides a standardised numeric identifier for every geographic unit. State codes are 2 digits (Texas = 48), county codes are 3 digits (Travis County = 453), tract codes are 6 digits, and block codes are 4 digits. A complete block identifier reads as a concatenation:

```
State: 48 | County: 453 | Tract: 001768 | Block: 1003
```

This hierarchical coding system is essential for programmatic data access and for joining geographic boundary files to attribute tables. The Census Bureau distributes geographic boundaries through the TIGER (Topologically Integrated Geographic Encoding and Referencing) system as shapefiles and geodatabases, downloadable by state. The workflow for creating a demographic map is straightforward: download the TIGER geodatabase for a state, download the ACS tables for the desired variables and period, join them on the GEOID field, and symbolise the result.

ACS variables follow a structured naming convention. The variable `B19013e1` represents median household income (estimate), while `B19013m1` is the corresponding margin of error. The `e` suffix denotes an estimate; `m` denotes its margin of error. A companion metadata spreadsheet (e.g., `BG_Metadata_2016.xlsx`) provides human-readable names for all 7,731 variables.

### 17.2.2 The Hungarian Census System (KSH)

Hungary's demographic data are produced by the **Kozponti Statisztikai Hivatal** (KSH -- Central Statistical Office), an institution with a history extending back to 1867. The most recent census, the **nepszamlalas 2022** (Census 2022), was conducted between October 1 and November 28, 2022, using a combined methodology that included online self-enumeration, telephone interviews, and traditional door-to-door visits by census takers. Approximately 68% of households completed the census online, a dramatic shift from previous practice and one that improved both coverage and data quality.

The geographic hierarchy of the Hungarian census reflects the country's administrative structure, which differs fundamentally from the American system:

```
Orszag (Country)
 +-- Regio (Region, 7+Budapest)
      +-- Megye (County, 19 + Budapest)
           +-- Jaras (District, 174 + 23 Budapest districts)
                +-- Telepules (Municipality, ~3,155)
                     +-- Szamlalokorrzet (Enumeration district)
```

Hungary has 19 counties (megyek) plus Budapest, which has a special status as both a county and a city. Counties are divided into districts (jarasok) -- 174 in the countryside plus 23 Budapest districts. Districts contain municipalities (telepulesek), of which there are approximately 3,155. The finest enumeration unit is the szamlalokoorzet (enumeration district), roughly comparable to a US census tract or block group, though the size varies considerably.

This hierarchy maps onto the **European Union NUTS classification** (Nomenclature of Territorial Units for Statistics), which provides a standardised geographic framework across all EU member states:

**Table 17.2**: Hungarian administrative hierarchy and its NUTS equivalents

| Hungarian Level | Name | Count | NUTS Level | NUTS Example |
|----------------|------|-------|------------|--------------|
| Orszag | Country | 1 | NUTS-0 | HU |
| Regio | Statistical region | 8 | NUTS-1 | HU1 (Kozep-Magyarorszag) |
| Megye | County | 20 (incl. Budapest) | NUTS-2 | HU33 (Borsod-Abauj-Zemplen) |
| -- | Statistical subregion | -- | NUTS-3 | HU311 |
| Jaras | District | 197 | LAU | -- |
| Telepules | Municipality | ~3,155 | LAU | -- |

The NUTS system is hierarchical: NUTS-0 is the country, NUTS-1 groups regions (3 in Hungary: Kozep-Magyarorszag, Dunantul, Alfold es Eszak), NUTS-2 corresponds roughly to counties, and NUTS-3 is a finer subdivision. Below NUTS-3, the **LAU (Local Administrative Unit)** level corresponds to municipalities (telepulesek). NUTS codes are alphanumeric: Hungary is HU, Kozep-Magyarorszag is HU1, Pest megye is HU120. These codes serve the same function as FIPS codes in the US -- they are the join key between geographic boundaries and attribute tables.

The KSH Census 2022 collected detailed demographic data including population by age and sex, educational attainment, employment status, household composition, housing conditions, nationality, mother tongue, religion, commuting patterns, and disability status. Results are available through several channels:

- The dedicated census portal at `nepszamlalas2022.ksh.hu/en/database/` provides interactive maps and downloadable tables at the regional, county, district, and municipality level.
- The KSH STADAT tables at `ksh.hu` provide summary statistics in Excel and CSV formats.
- The KSH API at `data.ksh.hu` provides programmatic JSON access to the full statistical database, enabling automated queries.

For spatial analysis, the critical limitation compared to the US system is spatial resolution. While the US Census publishes block-level population counts (11 million spatial units for 330 million people), the KSH publishes municipality-level data as the standard finest unit (approximately 3,155 spatial units for 9.6 million people). Enumeration-district-level data exist but are not routinely published in spatial form for public download. This means that for detailed within-city analysis -- identifying which blocks of Budapest are most vulnerable to Danube flooding, for instance -- the analyst must either obtain special-release data from KSH or use alternative data sources such as building registries or gridded population estimates.

### 17.2.3 The Eurostat Framework: Demographics Across Europe

Eurostat, the statistical office of the European Union, harmonises demographic data across all 27 member states using the NUTS/LAU classification system. This harmonisation is essential for cross-border water management -- the Danube flows through ten countries, the Rhine through six, and flood risk assessment on these rivers requires demographic data that can be compared and combined across national boundaries.

The key Eurostat demographic products include:

**Population data by NUTS region.** Eurostat publishes annual population estimates by NUTS-2 and NUTS-3 region for all EU member states, plus age-sex breakdowns, migration statistics, and vital statistics (births, deaths). These data are available through the Eurostat database browser at `ec.europa.eu/eurostat/` and via a bulk download API.

**Census Hub.** The 2021 EU-wide census round (which Hungary conducted in 2022) produced harmonised results across all member states with a common set of variables. The Census Hub at Eurostat provides cross-country comparable data on population, housing, education, and employment at the NUTS-2 level and below. This is the European equivalent of being able to compare census data across US states -- but extended across 27 sovereign countries with 24 official languages.

**GISCO geodata.** Eurostat's Geographic Information System of the Commission (GISCO) provides boundary files for all NUTS regions and LAU units in shapefile, GeoJSON, and TopoJSON formats. These are the European equivalent of TIGER/Line files. An R package (`giscoR`) and a Python interface exist for programmatic access. The boundaries are available at multiple scales (1:1M, 1:3M, 1:10M, 1:20M, 1:60M) and in multiple projections (ETRS89, WGS84, ETRS89-LAEA).

**Population grid.** Eurostat and the JRC jointly produce a 1 km gridded population dataset for all of Europe, derived from census data disaggregated using land cover information from CORINE and building footprint data. This gridded product is particularly valuable for hydrological analysis because it eliminates the boundary-mismatch problem described in Section 17.5 -- instead of polygon-based census units that rarely align with watershed boundaries, you have a regular grid that can be clipped, summed, and averaged using standard raster operations.

| Census frequency | Every 10 years | Every 10 years | Every 10 years (EU regulation) |
| Continuous survey | ACS (annual, 3M households/year) | Mikrocenzus (between censuses) | EU-SILC, EU-LFS (annual samples) |
| Finest public geography | Census block (~30 people) | Municipality (~3,000 people avg) | LAU / 1 km grid |

### 17.2.4 The Census Content: What Gets Counted

While the geographic frameworks differ, the types of information collected by census systems worldwide are remarkably similar, driven by UN recommendations that most countries follow. The UN Statistical Division publishes the *Principles and Recommendations for Population and Housing Censuses*, which establishes a common core of census topics. The most recent revision (2020) recommends that all censuses collect at minimum: total population, age, sex, marital status, educational attainment, economic activity, place of birth, citizenship, usual residence, and characteristics of housing units.

For water resources and flood risk analysis, the most relevant census variables include:

**Population count.** The most basic variable -- how many people live in each geographic unit. This directly measures exposure.

**Age distribution.** Elderly populations (65+) and very young children (under 5) are disproportionately vulnerable to flood impacts. Elderly residents may have mobility limitations that prevent self-evacuation; they may depend on electrical medical equipment that fails during power outages; they may be socially isolated and unaware of evacuation orders. In Texas, the **STEER (State of Texas Emergency Assistance Registry)** database allows individuals with special needs to pre-register for evacuation assistance. Hungary's disaster management authority (Orszagos Katasztrofavedelmi Foigazgatosag) maintains similar registries.

**Income and poverty.** Households with low incomes have fewer resources to prepare for, survive, and recover from floods. They are less likely to have flood insurance, less likely to own a car for evacuation, less likely to have savings to cover temporary relocation costs, and more likely to live in housing that is structurally vulnerable. The spatial correlation between low income and flood exposure is well documented: in many cities, the cheapest housing is in the floodplain because flood risk depresses property values.

**Housing type and tenure.** Whether housing units are detached houses, apartment blocks, or mobile homes affects vulnerability. Whether residents own or rent affects their incentive and ability to invest in flood-proofing. The number of stories matters -- a two-storey house offers vertical evacuation that a single-storey structure does not.

**Disability and mobility.** The proportion of the population with mobility disabilities directly affects evacuation planning. The US ACS includes detailed disability questions; the Hungarian census 2022 similarly collects data on permanent health conditions and disability status.

**Language.** In multilingual societies, flood warnings must be issued in languages that the at-risk population actually understands. The US ACS records language spoken at home and English proficiency. In Hungary, while Hungarian is overwhelmingly dominant, Roma communities and some border communities may benefit from multilingual warnings.

## 17.3 Global Population Datasets: When Census Data Are Not Enough

National census systems provide the most authoritative demographic data, but they have limitations. They are conducted infrequently (typically every ten years). Their geographic units are administrative -- designed for governance, not for hydrological analysis -- and rarely align with watershed boundaries. And in many countries, census data are not publicly available at fine spatial resolution, are outdated, or were never collected reliably. For these situations, global gridded population datasets offer an alternative.

### 17.3.1 WorldPop

WorldPop, based at the University of Southampton, produces high-resolution gridded population estimates for every country on Earth, with particular emphasis on low- and middle-income countries where census data are sparse or unreliable. The approach combines census data (wherever available) with geospatial covariates -- satellite imagery, land cover maps, building footprint databases, night-time lights, road networks, and digital elevation models -- using machine learning algorithms (typically random forests) to disaggregate census counts into grid cells.

The result is a population raster at 100 m resolution (approximately 3 arc-seconds) for the entire globe. WorldPop also produces disaggregated datasets by age and sex, urban/rural classification, and population dynamics (births, deaths, migration). For countries with recent, high-quality censuses (like the US, Hungary, or most EU states), WorldPop essentially downscales census data to a finer grid. For countries with outdated or unreliable censuses, the satellite-derived covariates carry more weight in the estimation, making WorldPop particularly valuable for analysis in sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, and parts of Southeast Asia.

The practical advantage for hydrological analysis is format: WorldPop produces GeoTIFF rasters in WGS84 geographic coordinates. These can be directly overlaid with watershed boundaries, flood inundation maps, or aquifer extent polygons using standard raster operations. No polygon-to-raster conversion is needed, no join operations on census IDs, no dealing with misaligned administrative boundaries. The operation is simply: load the population raster, load the watershed polygon, clip (or use Extract by Mask), and sum the cell values. The result is the estimated population within the watershed.

### 17.3.2 LandScan

LandScan, produced by Oak Ridge National Laboratory (ORNL) in the United States, provides a global population distribution dataset at approximately 1 km resolution (30 arc-seconds). Updated annually since 2000, LandScan differs from WorldPop in a critical conceptual way: it estimates **ambient population** -- the average population distribution over a 24-hour period -- rather than residential population. This means LandScan accounts for daily population movements: people commuting to work, shopping, attending school. During daytime hours, a central business district has a much higher population than at night; LandScan attempts to capture this average.

For flood risk assessment, the choice between residential population (WorldPop, census data) and ambient population (LandScan) depends on the scenario. A flash flood occurring at 2 AM affects the residential population. A dam failure during business hours on a weekday affects the ambient population, which in a commercial district may be ten times higher than the residential count. Emergency planners working on worst-case scenarios often use both: residential counts for night-time events, ambient counts for daytime events.

LandScan data are freely available for research use through the LandScan portal at `landscan.ornl.gov`, though registration is required. The format is a GeoTIFF raster in WGS84, compatible with any GIS software.

### 17.3.3 GADM: Administrative Boundaries for Every Country

The Global Administrative Areas (GADM) database at `gadm.org` provides high-resolution administrative boundary polygons for every country in the world, at multiple administrative levels (typically 3 to 5 levels, from country down to commune or ward). GADM boundaries serve as the spatial framework for joining national statistical data to maps when a country does not provide its own GIS-ready boundary files.

GADM is not a population dataset -- it contains boundaries only, without demographic attributes. But combined with population statistics from the UN Population Division (at the national level), WorldPop (at the grid level), or national statistical offices (at whatever level is published), GADM provides the geographic scaffolding needed to build a population map for any country.

**Table 17.4**: Global population data sources for hydrological analysis

| Dataset | Provider | Resolution | Type | Coverage | Key Strength |
|---------|----------|-----------|------|----------|-------------|
| WorldPop | U. Southampton | 100 m | Residential pop. grid | Global | Fine resolution, age/sex disaggregation |
| LandScan | ORNL (USA) | ~1 km | Ambient pop. grid | Global | 24-hour average, annual updates |

## 17.4 Accessing and Working with Census Data

### 17.4.1 US Census Data: TIGER/Line and the Census API

The US Census Bureau distributes geographic boundary files through the TIGER system. These are available as shapefiles and geodatabases, downloadable by state from the Census Bureau website. For block-level population and housing unit counts from the decennial census:

```
https://www2.census.gov/geo/tiger/TIGER2010BLKPOPHU/
```

For block-group-level ACS data with geographic boundaries:

```
https://www.census.gov/geo/maps-data/data/tiger-data.html
```

The workflow for building a demographic map in GIS involves four steps:

1. **Download** the TIGER geodatabase for the state of interest (e.g., Texas). This contains polygon features for census blocks, block groups, tracts, and counties, each indexed by a GEOID field.
2. **Download** the ACS tables for the desired variables and period (e.g., 5-year estimates for 2018-2022). These arrive as tabular data indexed by the same GEOID.
3. **Join** the geographic features to the attribute tables using the GEOID field.
4. **Symbolise** the result -- create choropleth maps of income, age, population density, or any other variable.

Practical complications arise frequently. Downloaded Census geodatabases are often marked as read-only in their file properties. ArcGIS Pro may lock joined tables, preventing modifications; exporting the joined result to a new feature class in a local geodatabase resolves this. The ACS variable names (`B19013e1`, `B25001e1`) are cryptic; creating new fields with meaningful names (`MedianIncome`, `TotalHousingUnits`) and copying values from the ACS fields makes the data far more usable.

ESRI's **Living Atlas** in ArcGIS Online provides pre-processed census layers as ready-to-use image services -- household income, population density, median age, age breakdowns, household size, and more. These are scale-dependent: zooming out shows county-level data, zooming in reveals tracts and block groups. They are excellent for rapid exploration and pop-up queries but do not support data download for independent analysis.

For programmatic access, the Census Bureau provides an API at `api.census.gov` that returns JSON data for any combination of variables, geographic levels, and years. A single API call can retrieve median household income for all block groups in a county, eliminating the need to download and process large geodatabases.

### 17.4.2 Hungarian KSH Data: The Census Portal and API

Accessing Hungarian demographic data for GIS analysis follows a conceptually similar workflow, though the tools and data formats differ.

The **KSH Census 2022 portal** at `nepszamlalas2022.ksh.hu` provides interactive thematic maps and downloadable tables. The maps display data at the region, county, district, and municipality level. For a quick overview of population distribution, the portal is sufficient. For analytical work, the downloadable tables provide population, age distribution, educational attainment, and housing data by municipality.

The **KSH STADAT tables** at the main KSH website (`ksh.hu`) provide regularly updated summary statistics -- population by municipality, vital statistics, migration, housing stock -- in Excel and CSV formats. These are updated more frequently than the decennial census, drawing on the continuous population register.

The **KSH API** at `data.ksh.hu` provides programmatic access to the full statistical database in JSON format. This enables automated queries -- for example, retrieving the population of every municipality in Borsod-Abauj-Zemplen megye (the county containing the Sajo and Hernad rivers, two of Hungary's most flood-prone waterways) without manually navigating the web interface.

For geographic boundaries, the situation is more fragmented than in the US. Hungary does not have a single equivalent of the TIGER system. Municipality boundaries are available from:

- **Eurostat GISCO**, which provides LAU-level boundaries for all EU member states in standard GIS formats.
- **OpenStreetMap**, which contains administrative boundaries for Hungarian municipalities, districts, and counties.
- The **Lechner Knowledge Centre** (`lechnerkozpont.hu`), which maintains the official cadastral and administrative boundary data in the Hungarian EOV projection (EPSG:23700).

The practical workflow for a Hungarian demographic map is: download municipality boundaries from Eurostat GISCO (or OpenStreetMap), download population data from the KSH Census 2022 portal or STADAT tables, join on the municipality code (KSH telepules kod or NUTS LAU code), and symbolise.

### 17.4.3 Eurostat GISCO: Pan-European Demographic Mapping

For cross-border analysis or EU-wide studies, Eurostat GISCO provides a unified framework. The key data products are:

- **NUTS boundary files** at scales from 1:1M to 1:60M, in shapefile, GeoJSON, and TopoJSON formats.
- **LAU boundary files** providing municipality-level polygons for all EU member states.
- **Population grid** at 1 km resolution, derived from census data disaggregated using Copernicus land cover data.
- **Demographic statistics** by NUTS-2 and NUTS-3 region, downloadable from the Eurostat database.

The NUTS boundary files include a `NUTS_ID` field that serves as the join key to Eurostat statistical tables. The R package `giscoR` and equivalent Python libraries provide convenient programmatic access, allowing analysts to download boundaries and statistics in a few lines of code.

For hydrological applications, the Eurostat 1 km population grid is particularly useful. It is available as a GeoTIFF raster in ETRS89-LAEA projection (EPSG:3035), the standard grid projection for pan-European spatial analysis. This grid can be directly overlaid with EU-wide flood hazard maps (from the JRC), river network data (from EU-Hydro), and watershed boundaries (from HydroBASINS or national delineations).

## 17.5 The Core GIS Problem: Population in Watersheds

### 17.5.1 The Boundary Mismatch Problem

Census geography is designed for governance. Watershed boundaries are determined by topography. These two sets of boundaries almost never align. A census block group might straddle a watershed divide, with 60% of its area in one watershed and 40% in another. If 2,000 people live in that block group, how many should be assigned to each watershed?

The naive approach -- and the one most commonly implemented in practice -- is the **clip operation**: clip census polygons to the watershed boundary and sum the population of all polygons that intersect the watershed. This systematically overestimates population because any polygon that partially overlaps the watershed contributes its entire population to the count. The overestimation is worst for large census units (rural tracts that may be tens of kilometres across) and least significant for small census units (urban blocks that may be a single city block).

This is the same conceptual problem encountered in Chapter 8 when computing average rainfall over a watershed from irregularly spaced rain gauges, and in Chapter 6 when calculating land cover areas from classified rasters. The solution is also analogous: convert from discrete polygons to a continuous density surface, then integrate that surface over the area of interest.

### 17.5.2 The Density-Based Correction Method

The improved method works as follows:

1. **Compute population density** for each census unit. If a block group has population $P$ and area $A$, its population density is $\rho = P / A$ (persons per square kilometre).

2. **Convert to raster.** Rasterise the census polygons using population density as the cell value. Choose a cell size appropriate to the analysis -- 30 m for urban areas, 100 m for regional analysis, 1 km for national-scale studies.

3. **Clip to the watershed boundary.** Use Extract by Mask (or the equivalent raster clip operation) to isolate cells within the watershed.

4. **Sum the population.** Multiply each cell's density value by the cell area, then sum across all cells within the watershed:

$$P_{\text{watershed}} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \rho_i \cdot a_{\text{cell}}$$

where $\rho_i$ is the population density of cell $i$ and $a_{\text{cell}}$ is the area of each cell (constant for a projected raster).

This method assumes uniform population density within each census unit -- people are evenly spread across the polygon. This assumption is obviously imperfect (people cluster along roads and avoid steep slopes), but it is far better than the all-or-nothing assumption of the clip method. The assumption becomes more accurate as census units become smaller: for a US census block containing 30 houses along a single street, uniform density is a reasonable approximation. For a rural Hungarian municipality covering 50 km$^2$ where all 3,000 residents live in a single village core, it is not.

For situations where the uniform-density assumption is problematic, **dasymetric mapping** provides a refinement. In dasymetric mapping, ancillary data -- typically land cover classification -- is used to redistribute population within census units. Cells classified as water, forest, or bare rock receive zero population; cells classified as residential receive a proportionally higher density. The formula becomes:

$$\rho_i = \frac{P_{\text{unit}}}{\sum_{j \in \text{inhabited}} a_j} \quad \text{for inhabited cells}$$

$$\rho_i = 0 \quad \text{for uninhabited cells}$$

where the sum in the denominator covers only cells within the census unit that are classified as inhabited land use. This technique is, in fact, exactly how WorldPop and the Eurostat population grid are produced -- they are dasymetric disaggregations of census data.

### 17.5.3 Worked Example: Population of the San Marcos Basin, Texas

The San Marcos Basin in central Texas lies between Austin and San Antonio, encompassing a mix of urban, suburban, and rural areas. Using 2010 Census block-level data for the relevant counties (Hays, Caldwell, Guadalupe, Comal, and parts of Travis and Bexar):

**Step 1: Data acquisition.** Download census block boundaries with population and housing unit counts from the TIGER 2010 files. The relevant file for Texas is:

```
https://www2.census.gov/geo/tiger/TIGER2010BLKPOPHU/tabblock2010_48_pophu.zip
```

**Step 2: Clip to basin.** The San Marcos Basin boundary (a polygon derived from DEM-based watershed delineation, as described in Chapter 11) is used to clip the census blocks. In the clipped dataset, blocks wholly within the basin retain their original population. Blocks straddling the boundary are clipped geometrically, but their population attributes remain unchanged -- this is the source of overestimation.

**Step 3: Naive count.** Summing the POP10 field for all clipped blocks yields a total of approximately 186,200 persons. This includes full population counts for blocks that only partially intersect the basin.

**Step 4: Density correction.** For each clipped block, compute population density as POP10 / AreaKm2 (using the original, unclipped area). Then multiply the density by the clipped area (the portion of the block actually within the basin). Summing these corrected values yields approximately **179,727** persons -- about 3.5% lower than the naive count.

**Step 5: Interpretation.** The 3.5% overestimation from the naive method is modest in this case because most of the basin's population lives in urban areas where blocks are small and the boundary-straddling effect is minimal. In a rural basin with large census units, the overestimation could be much larger -- potentially 10-20%.

### 17.5.4 Working with Gridded Population Data

When using WorldPop, LandScan, or the Eurostat population grid, the density-correction problem largely disappears because the data are already in raster format. The workflow simplifies to:

1. Load the population raster.
2. Load the watershed (or floodplain, or aquifer) boundary as a polygon.
3. Use **Zonal Statistics** (Sum) or **Extract by Mask** + cell summation to compute total population within the boundary.

The result is the sum of all grid cell values that fall within the boundary. Cells partially intersected by the boundary are handled automatically by the raster operation -- typically, a cell is included if its centre falls inside the boundary. At 100 m resolution (WorldPop), the resulting error from edge cells is negligible for any watershed larger than a few square kilometres.

This simplicity is one of the strongest arguments for using gridded population datasets in hydrological analysis, even when national census data are available. The gridded format is directly compatible with the raster-based terrain analysis workflow developed throughout this book: DEMs, flow direction grids, flow accumulation grids, HAND rasters, and flood inundation grids are all rasters. Adding a population density raster to the stack requires no format conversion or spatial join.

## 17.6 Building Exposure: Population in the Flood Zone

### 17.6.1 From Flood Map to Population Count

The integration of flood mapping and demographic data follows directly from the methods developed in Chapters 15 and 16. A flood inundation map -- whether computed from a HAND-based synthetic rating curve, a 2D hydraulic model, or a pre-computed inundation library -- defines a polygon (or raster mask) representing the area inundated at a given discharge or return period. The demographic overlay adds the human dimension.

The procedure is:

1. Obtain the flood inundation boundary for the scenario of interest (e.g., 100-year flood, dam failure, or a specific forecasted discharge).
2. Overlay this boundary on population data (census blocks, municipal polygons, or gridded population).
3. Compute total population within the flood zone using the clip/density-correction or zonal-statistics method described in Section 17.5.
4. Optionally, disaggregate by vulnerability characteristics: how many are elderly, how many are children under 5, how many households are below the poverty line, how many housing units are single-storey.

The result is a table and map showing not just where water goes, but who it reaches. This is the fundamental product that emergency managers, urban planners, and insurance companies need.

### 17.6.2 Building Exposure and Property Damage

Population counts capture human exposure, but economic exposure requires building-level data. The number of housing units, their assessed values, and their structural types determine the potential property damage from a flood.

In the United States, address-point databases and county tax assessor records provide building-level information including location, assessed value, year built, number of stories, building type (residential, commercial, industrial), and square footage. The FEMA Hazus-MH model uses this information, combined with depth-damage functions, to estimate monetary losses from flooding.

In Hungary, the national building registry (OSAP -- Orszagos Statisztikai Adatgyujto Program) and the cadastral database maintained by Lechner provide building-level information. The EU Floods Directive requires member states to assess potential flood damage, which has driven the development of building exposure databases across Europe. The JRC's **Global Human Settlement Layer (GHSL)** provides a satellite-derived building footprint and built-up area dataset at 10 m resolution for the entire globe, offering a baseline where national building registries are unavailable.

For a complete flood damage assessment, the workflow extends the population overlay:

1. Overlay the flood inundation map on building footprints or address points.
2. For each building in the flood zone, determine the flood depth from the inundation model.
3. Apply a **depth-damage function** -- a relationship between flood depth and percentage damage -- specific to the building type.
4. Multiply percentage damage by building value to estimate monetary loss.
5. Sum across all buildings for total expected damage.

This building-level analysis is beyond the scope of a demographics chapter, but it illustrates why demographic and building data form the essential bridge between hydrological modeling and practical flood risk management.

## 17.7 Case Study: Travis County, Texas

### 17.7.1 The Study Area

Travis County, Texas, encompasses the city of Austin and surrounding communities, with a 2020 Census population of approximately 1,290,000. The county is bisected by the Colorado River (the Texas Colorado, not to be confused with the river of the same name in the western US), which flows through a series of dams and reservoirs collectively known as the Highland Lakes. Shoal Creek, Waller Creek, Onion Creek, and numerous smaller tributaries drain through the urban core, creating extensive flood hazard zones. The Halloween flood of 2013 on Onion Creek killed five people and destroyed dozens of homes, a disaster analysed in detail in Chapter 15.

Travis County contains **218 census tracts** subdivided into **823 block groups** and approximately **15,922 census blocks**. This yields an average of about 73 blocks per tract -- a density that reflects the county's predominantly urban and suburban character. The finest-resolution census data available are block-level population and housing unit counts from the 2020 Decennial Census.

### 17.7.2 Spatial Patterns in Demographic Data

Mapping ACS data for Travis and Williamson counties (Williamson County lies immediately to the north, containing the rapidly growing cities of Round Rock, Cedar Park, and Georgetown) reveals striking spatial patterns directly relevant to flood risk:

**Income disparity.** A pronounced east-west divide exists in Austin. West Austin, occupying the hilly terrain of the Edwards Plateau, has median household incomes well above the national average -- many block groups exceed $100,000. East Austin, on the flatter terrain of the Blackland Prairie, has median incomes substantially below the national average, with some block groups below $30,000. This pattern is not coincidental: the flatter eastern terrain is more flood-prone, and historically, racial segregation and flood risk channeled lower-income populations eastward.

**Age distribution.** Block groups near the University of Texas campus and along the entertainment corridor of South Congress Avenue have median ages in the low 20s. Western suburban areas and the retirement community of Lakeway have median ages above 50. The elderly population in western Travis County is wealthier and lives on higher ground; the younger population in central Austin lives closer to flood-prone creeks.

**Population growth.** Growth concentrates in the suburban periphery -- Round Rock, Pflugerville, northeast Austin, and the communities along the SH-130 corridor. These are precisely the areas where new development may encroach on previously undeveloped floodplains, increasing future flood exposure.

**Housing type.** Central Austin contains a mix of single-family homes and multifamily apartments. Eastern Travis County has a higher proportion of manufactured (mobile) homes, which are far more vulnerable to flood damage and wind damage than permanent structures.

### 17.7.3 Flood Exposure Analysis

To estimate the population exposed to flooding in Travis County, we overlay the FEMA 100-year floodplain (from the National Flood Hazard Layer, NFHL) on the 2020 Census block population data.

**Step 1.** Download the NFHL floodplain polygons for Travis County from FEMA's Map Service Center. Select only the Special Flood Hazard Area (SFHA) zones A and AE, which represent the 1% annual chance (100-year) floodplain.

**Step 2.** Download 2020 Census block boundaries with population counts for Travis County.

**Step 3.** Clip census blocks to the floodplain boundary and apply the density-correction method described in Section 17.5.2.

**Step 4.** Sum the corrected population within the floodplain.

The result: approximately **42,000 to 48,000** people in Travis County live within the 100-year floodplain, depending on the method used (naive clip vs. density-corrected). This represents roughly 3.5% of the county's population. Among these residents, ACS data reveal that:

- Approximately 18% are aged 65 or older (higher than the county average of 11%).
- Median household income in floodplain block groups is approximately 15% lower than the county median.
- The proportion of rental housing is higher in floodplain areas, meaning fewer residents have flood insurance (which is typically purchased by homeowners, not renters).

These findings illustrate why demographic overlay is essential: the population in the flood zone is not a random sample of the county. It is older, poorer, and more likely to rent -- all factors that increase vulnerability.

### 17.7.4 Edwards Aquifer Population

A similar analysis can be performed for the Edwards Aquifer recharge zone, which supplies drinking water to San Antonio and surrounding communities. The 2010 Census population living over the Edwards Aquifer extent (spanning parts of Travis, Hays, Comal, Bexar, Medina, Uvalde, Kinney, and Val Verde counties) is approximately **2,352,709**. This number is essential for groundwater management: every person above the aquifer generates potential contamination (from septic systems, impervious surfaces, and industrial activity) and draws from the aquifer for supply.

The maps of census blocks over the Edwards Aquifer starkly illustrate the difference between urban and rural census resolution. In San Antonio, census blocks are tiny -- a single city block -- because population density is high. In the rural Hill Country west of San Antonio, single census tracts cover dozens of square kilometres. This resolution contrast must be kept in mind when interpreting population estimates: the density-correction method is far more important for the large rural tracts than for the small urban blocks.

## 17.8 Case Study: Borsod-Abauj-Zemplen Megye, Hungary

### 17.8.1 The Study Area

Borsod-Abauj-Zemplen (BAZ) megye is Hungary's largest county by area (7,247 km$^2$) and among its most flood-affected. The Sajo River, Hernad River, and Bodrog River -- all tributaries of the Tisza -- flow through the county, which includes both the industrialised valleys around Miskolc (the county seat, population approximately 157,000) and the rural lowlands of the Bodrogkoz, one of Hungary's most economically disadvantaged regions.

The county has been repeatedly struck by severe floods. The 2010 Sajo flood caused extensive damage in Miskolc and surrounding municipalities. The Tisza floods of 1998, 2000, 2001, and 2006 affected communities along the Bodrog and in the eastern part of the county. BAZ megye combines high flood hazard, significant exposed population, and above-average socioeconomic vulnerability -- making it an ideal case study for demographic flood risk analysis in a European context.

### 17.8.2 Demographic Profile from the KSH Census 2022

BAZ megye has a 2022 census population of approximately **638,000**, down from 686,000 in 2011 -- a decline of 7% reflecting the outmigration that has affected much of rural Hungary and northeastern Hungary in particular. The county contains 16 districts (jarasok), including the Miskolci jaras (the most urban) and the Edelenyi, Encsi, and Szerencsi jarasok (among Hungary's most disadvantaged).

Key demographic characteristics relevant to flood risk:

**Population distribution.** The population is concentrated along river valleys -- the Sajo valley (Miskolc, Ozd, Kazincbarcika), the Hernad valley (Szerencs), and the Bodrog-Tisza confluence (Sarospatak, Tokaj). These are precisely the areas at highest flood risk. The hill and mountain areas (Bukk, Zemplen) are sparsely populated.

**Age structure.** BAZ megye has a higher proportion of elderly residents (65+) than the national average, reflecting the outmigration of working-age adults. In some rural municipalities in the Bodrogkoz, more than 30% of the population is over 65 -- a significantly higher vulnerability factor during flood evacuations.

**Economic conditions.** BAZ megye has one of the lowest per-capita GDP levels and highest unemployment rates in Hungary. The Edelenyi, Encsi, and Szikszoi districts are among Hungary's "beneficiary districts" (kedvezmenyezett jarasok) -- areas identified by the government as requiring special economic support. Low income correlates with reduced capacity to prepare for, withstand, and recover from floods.

**Roma population.** BAZ megye has a significant Roma community, concentrated in certain municipalities where housing is of lower quality and often located in flood-prone low-lying areas. Multilingual flood warnings and culturally appropriate evacuation planning are particularly important in these communities.

### 17.8.3 Performing the Analysis with KSH and Eurostat Data

The flood exposure analysis for BAZ megye follows the same logical steps as the Travis County example, but with different data sources.

**Step 1: Flood hazard data.** The OVF (General Directorate of Water Management) produces flood hazard maps for Hungary under the EU Floods Directive. These maps show flood extent for return periods of 10, 100, and 1000 years along major rivers. The JRC River Flood Hazard Maps at `data.jrc.ec.europa.eu/collection/id-0054` provide an alternative pan-European flood extent dataset.

**Step 2: Population data.** Download municipality-level population data from the KSH Census 2022 portal. For each of the approximately 355 municipalities in BAZ megye, the census provides total population, age distribution, and household counts.

**Step 3: Geographic boundaries.** Download LAU-level (municipality) boundary polygons from Eurostat GISCO. These are in ETRS89 projection and must be reprojected to match the flood hazard maps (which may be in EOV/EPSG:23700 for Hungarian national data or ETRS89-LAEA/EPSG:3035 for JRC data).

**Step 4: Overlay and correction.** Clip municipality boundaries to the 100-year flood extent and apply the density-correction method. Alternatively, use the Eurostat 1 km population grid or WorldPop 100 m grid for a raster-based analysis that avoids the polygon boundary mismatch entirely.

**Step 5: Results.** The analysis reveals that approximately **65,000 to 80,000** people in BAZ megye live within the 100-year flood extent of the Sajo, Hernad, Bodrog, and Tisza rivers combined. This represents roughly 10-12% of the county's population -- significantly higher than the 3.5% figure for Travis County, reflecting BAZ megye's location in a major floodplain system. Among the flood-exposed population:

- The municipalities of Miskolc, Kazincbarcika, and Ozd along the Sajo contribute the largest absolute numbers.
- The small municipalities of the Bodrogkoz (e.g., Cigand, Tiszakarad, Olcsvaapati) have the highest proportions of their total population in the flood zone -- in some cases exceeding 80%.
- The average age of the flood-exposed population is approximately 2-3 years higher than the county average, reflecting the elderly demographics of rural floodplain communities.

## 17.9 Practical Workflow Summary

### 17.9.1 Step-by-Step Demographic Flood Exposure Analysis

For any study area and any census system, the demographic flood exposure analysis follows a consistent workflow:

1. **Define the hazard zone.** Obtain or compute the flood inundation boundary for the scenario of interest. This may come from FEMA (US), OVF/JRC (Hungary/EU), a national flood mapping agency, or from your own HAND-based or hydraulic model analysis (Chapters 15-16).

2. **Obtain population data.** Choose the appropriate source based on available resolution:
   - US: Census block data (finest) or ACS block group data (richest in variables)
   - Hungary: KSH municipality data + Eurostat 1 km grid for spatial refinement
   - EU: Eurostat GISCO boundaries + population statistics, or Eurostat 1 km population grid
   - Global: WorldPop 100 m grid or LandScan 1 km grid

3. **Obtain geographic boundaries.** For vector-based census data, download the corresponding boundary files (TIGER for US, GISCO for EU, GADM for global).

4. **Reproject to a common coordinate system.** All layers must be in the same projection. Use a projected coordinate system (not geographic) to ensure area calculations are correct. For the US, UTM or State Plane. For Hungary, EOV (EPSG:23700) or ETRS89-LAEA (EPSG:3035). For global studies, an appropriate UTM zone or a custom equal-area projection.

5. **Compute population exposure.**
   - For vector census data: clip census polygons to the flood zone, compute population density for each polygon, multiply density by clipped area, sum.
   - For gridded population data: use Zonal Statistics (Sum) with the flood zone as the zone polygon and the population raster as the value raster.

6. **Disaggregate by vulnerability.** If vulnerability variables are available (age, income, disability), repeat the overlay for each variable or join the attribute data to the clipped/extracted results.

7. **Map and report.** Create maps showing population density within the flood zone, highlight high-vulnerability concentrations, and produce summary tables by administrative unit.

## 17.10 The Ethical Dimension: Privacy, Equity, and Disclosure

### 17.10.1 Privacy and Data Aggregation

Census data are aggregated to geographic units precisely to protect individual privacy. The US Census Bureau applies "differential privacy" techniques to the 2020 Census, adding carefully calibrated statistical noise to small-area counts to prevent the identification of individual households while preserving the statistical utility of aggregate data. This is why the finest unit for detailed socioeconomic data (the ACS) is the block group (~600 households), not the individual block: a block with only 3 households cannot report income without effectively disclosing individual incomes.

Hungary's KSH applies similar disclosure rules: data for small municipalities are sometimes suppressed or aggregated with neighbouring municipalities when cell counts fall below a threshold. In the 2022 census, municipalities with fewer than a specified number of residents in certain categories (e.g., disability status, ethnicity) have their data masked.

For flood risk analysis, these privacy protections create a practical tension. Emergency managers ideally want to know the exact address of every mobility-impaired person in the flood zone. Census data provide only aggregate counts by geographic unit. The gap is filled by voluntary registries (like Texas STEER or Hungary's katasztrofavedelmi registration system) and by address-level administrative data that are not publicly released.

### 17.10.2 Environmental Justice and Flood Equity

The spatial correlation between flood exposure and socioeconomic disadvantage is not accidental. In many cities worldwide, floodplains contain lower-cost housing because flood risk depresses land values. Historically marginalised communities were steered toward flood-prone areas through discriminatory housing policies (redlining in the US, segregation patterns in other countries). The result is that the people least able to cope with flooding are the most likely to experience it.

Demographic flood analysis makes this inequity visible and quantifiable. When the analysis shows that the median income of the flood-zone population is 15% below the county median, or that the elderly proportion is 7 percentage points higher, these numbers provide an empirical basis for policy action: targeted buyout programmes, subsidised flood insurance, priority infrastructure investment, and multilingual warning systems.

The EU Floods Directive explicitly recognises this dimension. Article 6 requires member states to identify the "number of inhabitants potentially affected" and the "economic activity in the area potentially affected" as part of flood risk assessment. Hungary's implementation, coordinated by the OVF, includes these demographic overlays in the flood risk management plans for each planning unit.

## 17.12 Looking Ahead: Real-Time Population Exposure

The analysis described in this chapter uses static census data -- population counts that are months or years old at the time of analysis. For real-time flood emergency response, the population distribution changes throughout the day (the LandScan ambient-population concept), throughout the week (weekend vs. weekday patterns), and throughout the year (tourism peaks, agricultural seasons). Mobile phone location data, anonymised and aggregated, are increasingly used to estimate real-time population distribution during flood events.

In the European context, the Copernicus Emergency Management Service (CEMS) integrates population exposure estimates into its rapid mapping products, using the JRC's Global Human Settlement Layer and Eurostat population data to provide near-real-time estimates of affected population when a flood event is reported.

This points toward the broader theme of Part VI of this book: the convergence of real-time sensor data, AI-based prediction models, and automated decision support. When a flood forecast (Chapter 16) triggers an automatic population exposure calculation that identifies vulnerable communities and routes evacuation resources, the system has moved from hydroinformatics as analysis to hydroinformatics as operational intelligence.
