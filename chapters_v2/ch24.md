# Chapter 24: Agentic AI: The Autonomous Modeler

Every hydrologist who has calibrated a watershed model knows the ritual. You select initial parameter values based on soil maps and land cover classifications, run the model, compare the simulated hydrograph against the observed record, stare at the mismatch, adjust a few parameters, and run the model again. If you are disciplined, you keep a spreadsheet logging each trial: the parameter set, the objective function value, the visual assessment of peak timing and baseflow recession. If you are less disciplined -- and most of us have been, at two in the morning before a project deadline -- you simply tweak values by feel, guided by a mixture of hydrologic intuition and mounting frustration. Either way, the cycle repeats dozens or hundreds of times before the model produces a hydrograph that is acceptable. The process is iterative, knowledge-intensive, computationally expensive, and poorly documented. It is also, as of 2025, precisely the kind of task that a new class of artificial intelligence can perform.

Chapter 23 introduced AI as a hydrologist's assistant: machine learning models that learn rainfall-runoff relationships from data, neural networks that forecast streamflow, and deep learning architectures like LSTM that capture long-term hydrological memory. Those are powerful tools, but they share a fundamental characteristic -- they are passive. A trained LSTM model sits and waits for input. It does not decide what to do next. It does not examine its own errors and reason about why peak flows are underestimated. It does not go fetch additional soil data when it suspects that infiltration parameters are wrong. It does not write a report explaining its decisions. It is a function, not a colleague.

This chapter is about what happens when AI stops being a function and starts being a colleague. The technology is called *agentic AI*, and it represents the most significant shift in computational hydrology since the introduction of automatic calibration algorithms in the 1990s. An agentic AI system does not merely predict -- it plans, executes, evaluates, and iterates. It reads data, runs models, interprets results, adjusts its approach, and explains its reasoning. It operates autonomously within defined boundaries, calling on human expertise when it encounters situations beyond its competence. The implications for hydrological modeling are profound, and they are already being demonstrated in practice as of early 2026. Chapter 25 will show how agentic AI fits into the broader vision of digital twins and autonomous water management.

## 24.1 What Is Agentic AI?

### 24.1.1 From Prediction to Autonomous Action

The distinction between conventional AI and agentic AI is not one of degree but of kind. A conventional machine learning model -- whether a random forest, a support vector machine, or an LSTM network -- is a mapping from inputs to outputs. You provide features; it returns predictions. The model has no awareness of what it is doing, no memory of previous interactions beyond what is embedded in its weights, and no capacity to decide what to do next. It is, in the language of computer science, a pure function.

An agentic AI system, by contrast, is a reasoning entity that operates in a loop. At its core sits a large language model (LLM) -- a neural network trained on vast quantities of text, code, and structured data, capable of understanding natural language, generating coherent reasoning, and producing executable instructions. But the LLM alone is not the agent. The agent is the LLM embedded in a framework that gives it access to tools, memory, and a planning cycle. The agent receives a goal ("calibrate this rainfall-runoff model to achieve NSE > 0.7"), decomposes it into subtasks ("read the catchment data," "select initial parameters," "run the model," "evaluate the results," "adjust parameters"), executes those subtasks by calling external tools, evaluates the outcomes, and decides what to do next based on the results. This plan-execute-evaluate-iterate loop is what makes it *agentic*.

The concept draws from decades of research in autonomous agents, reinforcement learning, and planning systems. But the practical breakthrough came in 2023-2025 with the maturation of large language models that could serve as the reasoning engine. Earlier agent architectures required hand-coded planning logic and narrow domain ontologies. Modern LLM-based agents inherit their reasoning capabilities from pre-training on human knowledge, including -- crucially -- scientific literature, technical manuals, and programming documentation. An LLM that has been trained on hydrology textbooks, SWAT documentation, and thousands of calibration studies carries implicit knowledge about which parameters control peak flow, why clay soils produce more surface runoff than sandy soils, and what a Nash-Sutcliffe efficiency of 0.65 means in practice. This embedded domain knowledge, combined with the ability to execute code and call external tools, is what makes agentic AI qualitatively different from previous automation approaches.

### 24.1.2 The Agent Loop: Plan, Execute, Evaluate, Iterate

The operational cycle of an agentic AI system follows a consistent pattern regardless of the specific task. Understanding this cycle is essential for any hydrologist who wants to work with -- or build -- such systems.

The cycle begins with a **goal specification**. The human provides a natural language description of what needs to be accomplished: "Calibrate the HEC-HMS model for the Zala River catchment using the 2015-2020 daily discharge record at the Zalaegerszeg gauge." The goal may include constraints ("achieve KGE > 0.6"), preferences ("prioritize baseflow accuracy over peak flow"), and resource limits ("complete within 200 model runs").

The agent then enters the **planning phase**. It decomposes the goal into a sequence of subtasks, drawing on its training knowledge to determine what steps are necessary and in what order. For a calibration task, a typical plan might include: (1) load and inspect the catchment data, (2) identify sub-basins and their characteristics, (3) determine appropriate initial parameter ranges based on soil and land cover, (4) run the model with initial parameters, (5) compute performance metrics, (6) analyze the hydrograph to diagnose systematic errors, (7) adjust parameters to address diagnosed errors, (8) re-run and re-evaluate, (9) iterate until convergence or stopping criteria are met, (10) document the final parameter set and performance.

Each subtask enters the **execution phase**, where the agent calls the appropriate tool. Tools are external programs or APIs that the agent can invoke: a model runner that executes HEC-HMS or SWAT, a data reader that parses input files, a plotting tool that generates hydrograph comparisons, a statistical calculator that computes NSE, KGE, PBIAS, and other metrics. The agent constructs the tool call -- specifying input files, parameter values, run configurations -- and sends it for execution.

After execution, the **evaluation phase** begins. The agent examines the tool output -- the simulated hydrograph, the performance metrics, error messages if the run failed -- and forms a judgment. This is where the LLM's reasoning capabilities are critical. The agent does not merely check whether NSE exceeds a threshold; it examines the shape of the hydrograph, identifies patterns in the residuals, and reasons about what physical processes might explain the discrepancy. "The simulated peaks are too high and too early, which suggests that the curve number is too high and the lag time is too short. The baseflow recession is too steep, indicating that the groundwater recession constant needs to be increased." This kind of diagnostic reasoning, which a human hydrologist performs intuitively, is precisely what LLMs can approximate based on their training data.

Finally, the **iteration decision** determines what happens next. If the performance is satisfactory, the agent terminates and reports results. If not, it returns to the planning phase with updated knowledge: the previous parameter set, its performance, and the diagnosed issues. This updated context informs the next round of parameter adjustments. The agent may also decide to change strategy entirely -- switching from manual parameter adjustment to a structured search, requesting additional data, or escalating to the human operator when it encounters a situation it cannot resolve.

### 24.1.3 Tools, Memory, and Grounding

Three architectural elements distinguish a practical agentic system from a bare language model chatbot. The first is **tool access**. An LLM by itself can only generate text. An agent equipped with tools can execute code, run simulations, read files, query databases, fetch web data, and produce visualizations. In the hydrological context, the critical tools include a model execution engine (capable of running HEC-HMS, SWAT, MODFLOW, or other simulation software), a data access layer (for reading gauge records, meteorological inputs, and spatial datasets), a statistical computation module (for calculating performance metrics), and a visualization generator (for producing hydrographs, scatter plots, and spatial maps). Each tool is defined by its interface -- what inputs it expects and what outputs it returns -- and the agent learns to invoke these tools through its training and through explicit tool descriptions provided at runtime.

The second element is **memory**. A single LLM call is stateless -- the model has no recollection of previous interactions beyond what is included in the current prompt. An agentic system overcomes this limitation through explicit memory management. Short-term memory is maintained within a single session through the conversation context: each previous action, observation, and reasoning step is appended to a running transcript that the LLM can reference. Long-term memory, for tasks that span multiple sessions, requires external storage -- a database or file system where the agent records parameter histories, calibration results, and lessons learned. For hydrological calibration, the memory of previous parameter sets and their performance is essential: the agent must avoid revisiting failed configurations and must build on successful partial solutions.

The third element is **grounding**. Left unconstrained, an LLM can generate plausible-sounding but factually incorrect information -- a phenomenon known as hallucination (discussed in detail in Section 24.8). Grounding mechanisms anchor the agent's reasoning to verifiable facts. In a hydrological agent, grounding comes from multiple sources: the actual data files (the agent reads real precipitation and discharge records, not imagined ones), the model outputs (the agent evaluates actual simulation results, not hypothetical ones), and domain constraints (parameter ranges, physical conservation laws, known behavior of specific soil types). A well-designed agent treats its own reasoning as hypothesis and the tool outputs as evidence, updating its beliefs based on observed results rather than prior assumptions.

## 24.2 The Model Parameterization Problem Revisited

### 24.2.1 Why Calibration Is Hard

The parameterization of hydrological models is one of the most persistent challenges in water science. The problem is deceptively simple to state: given a model structure and a set of observed data, find the parameter values that cause the model to best reproduce the observations. In practice, the problem is hard for several reasons that make it particularly well-suited to agentic AI.

First, the parameter space is high-dimensional. A lumped rainfall-runoff model like HEC-HMS may have 10 to 20 parameters per sub-basin, and a distributed model like SWAT operating over a large catchment can easily involve hundreds of parameters. Even with physically motivated constraints that limit each parameter to a plausible range, the combinatorial space of possible parameter sets is astronomically large. Exhaustive search is impossible, and even sophisticated optimization algorithms can struggle with local optima, parameter interactions, and equifinality -- the phenomenon where multiple distinct parameter sets produce equally good (or equally poor) fits to the observed data.

Second, the objective function is noisy and multimodal. The relationship between parameters and model performance is not a smooth, convex surface with a single clear minimum. It is riddled with ridges, valleys, plateaus, and discontinuities. Small changes in one parameter may have no effect until they cross a threshold (such as the point where a reservoir spills or a soil layer saturates), at which point the response is abrupt. Traditional gradient-based optimization methods are poorly suited to these landscapes, which is why global search algorithms like SCE-UA and DREAM were developed -- but even these require thousands of model evaluations to converge.

Third, calibration requires judgment, not just optimization. A model may achieve an excellent NSE of 0.85 while systematically underestimating peak flows and overestimating baseflow -- a pattern that renders it useless for flood forecasting even though the statistical metric looks good. Experienced hydrologists evaluate calibration by looking at the hydrograph, not just the number. They check whether the timing of peaks is correct, whether the recession limb matches, whether the model captures seasonal patterns, and whether the water balance closes. This visual and conceptual evaluation requires domain knowledge that cannot be reduced to a single scalar objective function.

Fourth, the process is iterative and knowledge-intensive. A hydrologist does not simply run an optimizer and accept the result. She examines the initial output, diagnoses specific problems (rising limb too steep, baseflow too low, snowmelt peak missing), forms hypotheses about which parameters or process representations are responsible, makes targeted adjustments, and re-evaluates. This diagnostic reasoning draws on deep understanding of watershed physics: knowing that curve number controls the infiltration-runoff partition, that baseflow recession is governed by the groundwater store parameters, that routing parameters affect peak timing but not volume. The combination of physical insight and iterative adjustment is what makes expert calibration superior to blind optimization -- and it is precisely this combination that agentic AI can approximate.

### 24.2.2 The Gap Between Optimization and Expertise

Traditional automatic calibration methods, described in detail in Chapter 22 (Sections 22.3--22.4), fall into two broad categories. Global optimization algorithms -- Shuffled Complex Evolution (SCE-UA), Dynamically Dimensioned Search (DDS), Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO) -- treat calibration as a black-box optimization problem. They sample the parameter space according to various strategies, evaluate each sample by running the model, and iteratively focus the search on promising regions. These methods are effective at finding good parameter sets within defined bounds, but they have no understanding of the model physics. They cannot diagnose why a particular parameter set fails, they cannot decide that the model structure needs modification, and they cannot incorporate qualitative information (such as "the soil in this sub-basin is known to be highly permeable because of karst features").

Bayesian methods -- most notably the DREAM (Differential Evolution Adaptive Metropolis) algorithm -- improve on pure optimization by characterizing the posterior distribution of parameters rather than seeking a single optimum. DREAM explores the parameter space using multiple Markov chains that exchange information, producing not just the best parameter set but a probability distribution that quantifies parameter uncertainty. This is a significant advance for uncertainty quantification, but DREAM still operates as a black-box sampler. It does not reason about the model structure, incorporate physical constraints beyond parameter bounds, or adapt its strategy based on diagnostic analysis of the model output.

The gap between these automated methods and what an expert hydrologist does during manual calibration is substantial. The expert brings contextual knowledge, diagnostic reasoning, and adaptive strategy selection. She might notice that the model performs well in summer but poorly during spring snowmelt, and decide to focus specifically on the temperature-index parameters. She might observe that one sub-basin consistently underperforms and investigate whether the land cover classification is correct. She might recognize that the model structure itself is inadequate -- that a single linear reservoir cannot represent the dual-porosity behavior of a karst catchment -- and recommend structural changes.

Agentic AI sits in the gap between blind optimization and human expertise. It cannot replace the deepest levels of physical insight that come from decades of field experience. But it can approximate the diagnostic reasoning that turns calibration from a random search into a directed investigation. It can examine hydrographs, identify patterns in residuals, consult its training knowledge about parameter sensitivities, and make informed adjustments. And it can do so tirelessly, systematically, and at a pace that no human can match, while maintaining a complete record of every decision and its rationale.

## 24.3 Architecture of a Hydrological Modeling Agent

### 24.3.1 The Reasoning Engine

At the center of every agentic AI system is a large language model that serves as the reasoning engine. The LLM receives a prompt that includes the current goal, the history of actions taken so far, the most recent observations (tool outputs), and the available tools. Based on this context, it generates the next action: a plan, a tool call, a diagnostic analysis, or a final report.

The choice of LLM matters. As of early 2026, the models most capable of serving as hydrological reasoning engines include Anthropic's Claude (Opus and Sonnet variants), OpenAI's GPT-4o and its successor GPT-5 series, and Google's Gemini models. These models differ in their context window sizes (how much history they can maintain in a single session), their reasoning depth (how many logical steps they can chain without losing coherence), their code generation capabilities (critical for constructing tool calls and analyzing data), and their factual reliability (propensity for hallucination on technical topics).

For hydrological applications, the most important LLM capabilities are: (a) the ability to read and interpret tabular data, time series, and formatted model output; (b) knowledge of hydrological concepts, model structures, and parameter sensitivities from the training corpus; (c) the ability to generate executable code (Python, R, or command-line instructions) that manipulates data and invokes models; and (d) the ability to maintain coherent reasoning across many iterative steps without losing track of the calibration history or the current diagnostic hypothesis. Models with larger context windows (100,000 tokens and above) are strongly preferred for calibration tasks, where the accumulated history of parameter trials, hydrographs, and reasoning can quickly exceed 50,000 tokens.

### 24.3.2 The Tool Suite

The tools available to a hydrological modeling agent determine what it can actually accomplish. A minimal tool suite for rainfall-runoff calibration includes four components.

The **data reader** provides access to input datasets: catchment shapefiles, DEM-derived terrain attributes, land cover classifications, soil maps, precipitation and temperature time series, and observed streamflow records. The data reader parses these into structured formats (tables, arrays, GeoDataFrames) that the agent can inspect and manipulate. A well-designed data reader also provides summary statistics -- catchment area, mean elevation, dominant soil type, mean annual precipitation -- that the agent uses for initial parameter estimation.

The **model runner** executes the hydrological simulation. This is the most critical tool, and its design requires careful attention. The model runner must accept a parameter set as input, configure the model (HEC-HMS, SWAT, GR4J, or whatever structure is being used), execute the simulation for the specified period, and return the output -- typically a simulated discharge time series at one or more evaluation points. The model runner must also handle errors gracefully: if a parameter combination causes the model to crash (a negative parameter, a division by zero, an unstable numerical scheme), the runner should return a diagnostic error message rather than crashing the agent. In practice, the model runner is often implemented as a Python wrapper around the model's command-line interface, with template configuration files that are modified by parameter substitution.

The **evaluator** computes performance metrics by comparing simulated and observed time series. Standard metrics include the Nash-Sutcliffe Efficiency (NSE), the Kling-Gupta Efficiency (KGE) and its decomposed components (correlation, variability bias, mean bias), the percent bias (PBIAS), the root mean square error (RMSE), and flow-duration curve statistics. The evaluator may also compute event-specific metrics: peak flow error, time-to-peak error, and volume error for individual flood events. Returning multiple metrics rather than a single number gives the agent richer information for diagnosis. A hydrograph that achieves NSE = 0.72 but PBIAS = -18% tells the agent something different from one with NSE = 0.72 and PBIAS = +2%.

The **visualizer** generates plots that the agent can interpret. Modern multimodal LLMs can process images, which means the agent can literally look at hydrograph plots and spatial maps. This capability is transformative for calibration, because much of the diagnostic information in a hydrograph is visual: the shape of the recession curve, the timing offset between observed and simulated peaks, the seasonal pattern of errors. The visualizer produces observed-vs-simulated hydrograph overlays, flow-duration curves, residual time series, scatter plots of observed vs. simulated flows, and spatial maps of sub-basin performance.

### 24.3.3 The Orchestration Framework

The agent does not operate in isolation -- it runs within an orchestration framework that manages the tool calls, maintains the conversation context, enforces safety constraints, and handles the mechanical details of the plan-execute-evaluate loop. Several frameworks have emerged since 2024 for building LLM-based agents, and they differ in their design philosophies.

The simplest approach is a **script-driven loop** where a Python program manages the iteration: it sends the current context to the LLM API, parses the LLM's response to extract tool calls, executes the tools, appends the results to the context, and sends the updated context back to the LLM. This approach is transparent and easy to debug, but it requires the developer to handle context management, error recovery, and tool dispatch manually. Frameworks like LangChain and LlamaIndex provide higher-level abstractions for these mechanics.

A more integrated approach uses the **tool-use capabilities built into modern LLM APIs**. Both Anthropic's Claude API and OpenAI's function-calling API allow the model to generate structured tool calls as part of its response, which the orchestration layer executes and returns. This eliminates the need to parse free-text responses for tool invocations and reduces the risk of malformed tool calls. The Claude Agent SDK, for example, provides a complete framework for defining tools, managing conversation state, and running the agent loop with built-in support for human-in-the-loop intervention.

For hydrological applications, the orchestration framework must also manage **computational resources**. A single HEC-HMS simulation might take seconds, but a SWAT simulation on a large catchment can take minutes, and an ensemble of parameterizations can consume hours. The framework needs to handle asynchronous tool execution, so the agent can reason about its next steps while a simulation is running, and it needs to enforce computational budgets (maximum number of model runs, maximum wall-clock time) to prevent runaway costs.

## 24.4 Practical Demonstration: Agent-Driven Calibration

### 24.4.1 Setting Up the Problem

To make the concept of agentic calibration concrete, we walk through a complete example: an agent calibrating a conceptual rainfall-runoff model for a medium-sized Hungarian catchment. The example is designed to illustrate the agent's reasoning at each step, so we present it as a narrative of the agent's actions and thoughts, interspersed with the actual data and results it produces.

The catchment is the **Zala River upstream of Zalaegerszeg**, a tributary of the Zala that feeds Lake Balaton in western Hungary. The catchment area is approximately 930 km$^2$, with elevations ranging from 140 m at the gauge to 350 m in the Gocs hills. The landscape is predominantly agricultural (60% cropland, 25% forest, 10% grassland, 5% urban), underlain by Pannonian clay and sand formations with Quaternary alluvial deposits along the valley floors. Mean annual precipitation is approximately 700 mm, with a bimodal distribution (peaks in May-June and October-November). The hydrological regime is pluvial, with the highest flows in spring and autumn and low flows in August-September.

The model is **GR4J** (Genie Rural a 4 parametres Journalier), a parsimonious daily lumped rainfall-runoff model developed by IRSTEA (now INRAE) in France. GR4J is chosen for this example because its four-parameter structure makes the calibration problem tractable for demonstration, while still capturing the essential processes of production (soil moisture accounting), routing (unit hydrograph), and exchange (groundwater). The four parameters are:

- $x_1$: maximum capacity of the production store (mm), controlling the soil moisture balance
- $x_2$: groundwater exchange coefficient (mm/day), controlling gains and losses to regional groundwater
- $x_3$: one-day-ahead maximum capacity of the routing store (mm), controlling the direct flow component
- $x_4$: time base of the unit hydrograph (days), controlling the lag between rainfall and runoff

The input data consists of daily precipitation and potential evapotranspiration (from ERA5-Land reanalysis, 0.1-degree resolution, aggregated over the catchment) and daily observed discharge at the Zalaegerszeg OVF gauge, for the period 2010-2020. The calibration period is 2012-2018 (with 2010-2011 as warm-up), and the validation period is 2019-2020.

### 24.4.2 Step 1: The Agent Reads and Inspects the Data

The agent's first action is to load and examine the input data. Here is a schematized representation of its internal reasoning and tool calls:

```
AGENT REASONING: I need to calibrate a GR4J model for the Zala River at Zalaegerszeg.
First, I should load and inspect the input data to understand the catchment and check
for data quality issues.

TOOL CALL: data_reader.load_timeseries("zala_zalaegerszeg_P_ET_Q_2010_2020.csv")

TOOL RESULT:
  Period: 2010-01-01 to 2020-12-31 (4018 days)
  Precipitation: mean 1.92 mm/day, max 62.3 mm/day, 0 missing values
  PET: mean 1.78 mm/day, max 5.94 mm/day, 0 missing values
  Discharge: mean 4.82 m3/s, max 89.4 m3/s, 12 missing values (interpolated)
  Catchment area: 930 km2
  Mean specific discharge: 0.45 mm/day (164 mm/year)
  Runoff coefficient: 164/701 = 0.23

AGENT REASONING: The data looks complete and consistent. The runoff coefficient of 0.23
is reasonable for a Central European agricultural catchment -- not too high (which would
suggest karst or drainage issues) and not too low (which would suggest major groundwater
losses). The mean discharge of 4.82 m3/s over 930 km2 gives a specific discharge of
164 mm/year, which is typical for this region. No obvious data quality issues. I can
proceed to initial parameter estimation.
```

This first step illustrates a key advantage of the agentic approach: the agent does not simply load data and pass it to an optimizer. It *inspects* the data, computes diagnostic statistics (runoff coefficient, specific discharge), compares them against its knowledge of what is normal for the region, and explicitly checks for problems before proceeding. A traditional calibration script would skip directly to optimization without this sanity check.

### 24.4.3 Step 2: The Agent Selects Initial Parameters

With the data inspected, the agent must choose starting values for the four GR4J parameters. A blind optimizer would simply sample from the full feasible range. The agent does something different -- it uses its knowledge of hydrology and the catchment characteristics to make informed initial estimates:

```
AGENT REASONING: Based on the catchment characteristics:
- Predominantly clay and sand soils with agricultural land use suggest moderate
  infiltration capacity. Typical x1 (production store) for such catchments in
  Central Europe: 200-500 mm. I'll start with x1 = 350 mm.
- The runoff coefficient of 0.23 and the relatively flat terrain suggest some
  groundwater loss to the regional system (Zala valley alluvium connected to
  Lake Balaton base level). x2 is typically slightly negative (loss) for such
  settings: x2 = -1.0 mm/day.
- The routing store x3 controls quick flow. For a 930 km2 catchment with
  moderate slopes, x3 = 90 mm is a reasonable starting point.
- The time base x4 depends on catchment size and shape. For ~930 km2,
  x4 = 1.5-2.5 days is typical. I'll start with x4 = 2.0 days.

Initial parameter set: x1=350, x2=-1.0, x3=90, x4=2.0
```

These initial estimates are not random -- they reflect the kind of reasoning that an experienced hydrologist would apply. The agent knows that clay soils have lower infiltration, that a runoff coefficient of 0.23 with 700 mm precipitation implies moderate losses, and that catchment size correlates with response time. This knowledge comes from the LLM's training on hydrological literature, not from any explicit programming.

### 24.4.4 Step 3: First Model Run and Evaluation

The agent runs GR4J with the initial parameters and evaluates the result:

```
TOOL CALL: model_runner.run_gr4j(
  params={x1: 350, x2: -1.0, x3: 90, x4: 2.0},
  period="2010-01-01 to 2020-12-31",
  warmup="2010-01-01 to 2011-12-31",
  eval_period="2012-01-01 to 2018-12-31"
)

TOOL RESULT: Simulation complete. 2557 evaluation days.

TOOL CALL: evaluator.compute_metrics(observed, simulated, period="2012-2018")

TOOL RESULT:
  NSE  = 0.58
  KGE  = 0.63
  r    = 0.81 (correlation component of KGE)
  alpha = 0.72 (variability ratio component of KGE)
  beta = 0.91 (bias ratio component of KGE)
  PBIAS = -8.7%
  RMSE = 5.14 m3/s

AGENT REASONING: The initial run gives NSE=0.58 and KGE=0.63, which is a decent
starting point but below the target of KGE>0.70. Diagnosing the KGE components:
- Correlation r=0.81 is good -- the timing of peaks and troughs is approximately
  correct
- Variability ratio alpha=0.72 means the model underestimates flow variability
  (simulated flows are too "flat" -- peaks too low, troughs too high)
- Bias ratio beta=0.91 with PBIAS=-8.7% means slight overall underestimation

The low alpha (variability) is the main issue. The model is not producing enough
contrast between high and low flows. This suggests:
1. The routing store (x3) may be too large, damping the peak flows
2. The production store (x1) may be too large, buffering too much rainfall

I should decrease x3 to sharpen the peaks and slightly decrease x1 to allow
more rainfall to become runoff during wet periods.
```

This diagnostic reasoning is the heart of the agentic advantage. This is exactly the kind of reasoning that distinguishes expert calibration from black-box optimization.

## 24.5 Multi-Objective Calibration with Agentic Reasoning

### 24.5.1 The Multi-Objective Problem in Hydrology

Real-world hydrological modeling rarely involves a single objective. A flood warning model must capture peak flows accurately. A water supply model must reproduce low-flow statistics. A water quality model needs correct flow volumes and timing for dilution calculations. A comprehensive operational model must do all of these simultaneously, and these objectives are often in tension -- parameter adjustments that improve peak flow simulation may worsen baseflow reproduction, and vice versa.

Traditional multi-objective optimization addresses this through Pareto-optimal sets. The NSGA-II algorithm, BORG MOEA, and epsilon-NSGAII generate a population of solutions that represent the trade-off frontier between competing objectives. No solution on the Pareto front can improve one objective without worsening another. The modeler then selects from the Pareto front based on application priorities. This is mathematically elegant but computationally expensive (requiring tens of thousands of model runs) and it leaves the final selection decision to the human.

Agentic AI transforms the multi-objective problem from a mathematical optimization into a reasoned negotiation. The agent can be given explicit priorities -- "flood peak accuracy is more important than baseflow accuracy for this application" -- and it will adjust its calibration strategy accordingly. More importantly, the agent can reason about the trade-offs in physical terms rather than purely mathematical ones.

### 24.5.2 Agent-Driven Multi-Objective Calibration

Consider an extension of the Zala River example where the agent is given three objectives: (1) maximize KGE for overall fit, (2) minimize peak flow error for the five largest events, and (3) maintain PBIAS within $\pm$5% for water balance accuracy. The agent's reasoning might proceed as follows:

## 24.6 Uncertainty Quantification Through Agentic Ensembles

### 24.6.1 The Equifinality Problem

One of the most important insights in hydrological modeling over the past three decades is equifinality: the recognition that many different parameter sets can produce equally acceptable simulations. Beven and Binley's (1992) Generalized Likelihood Uncertainty Estimation (GLUE) methodology formalized this insight, demonstrating that the "optimal" parameter set is a fiction -- instead, there is a population of behavioral parameter sets, each producing a plausible simulation, and the spread among these simulations is a measure of predictive uncertainty.

Quantifying this uncertainty is essential for honest decision-making. A model that predicts a 100-year flood peak of 120 m$^3$/s is far more useful when accompanied by the information that the 90% uncertainty band ranges from 95 to 155 m$^3$/s. Ignoring equifinality and reporting only the "best" simulation creates false confidence that can lead to under-designed flood defenses, over-allocated water resources, or incorrectly sited facilities.

Traditional uncertainty quantification methods -- GLUE, DREAM, Bayesian Model Averaging -- require large numbers of model runs. GLUE typically requires 10,000 to 100,000 Monte Carlo samples to adequately characterize the behavioral parameter space. DREAM, while more efficient than brute-force Monte Carlo, still requires 20,000 to 50,000 runs to converge the posterior distribution for a 10-parameter model. For computationally expensive models, these sample sizes are prohibitive.

### 24.6.2 Agent-Guided Ensemble Generation

Agentic AI offers a middle path between single-point calibration and exhaustive uncertainty analysis. The agent can generate uncertainty estimates with far fewer model runs by using its reasoning capabilities to intelligently sample the parameter space. Rather than random or quasi-random sampling, the agent identifies the dimensions of equifinality -- the parameter combinations that produce similar performance -- and samples along those dimensions.

The process works as follows. After converging on a calibrated parameter set, the agent systematically perturbs each parameter while holding others fixed, mapping the sensitivity surface around the optimum. It identifies which parameters are well-constrained (the objective function drops sharply with perturbation) and which are poorly constrained (wide plateau of near-equivalent performance). For the poorly constrained parameters, the agent samples broadly; for the well-constrained ones, it samples narrowly. The result is an ensemble of 20 to 50 parameter sets, each producing an acceptable simulation, whose spread characterizes the predictive uncertainty.

```
AGENT REASONING: For the Zala River GR4J model, I found that x2 (exchange coefficient)
and x1 (production store) exhibit strong equifinality -- compensating changes in x1 and
x2 produce nearly identical KGE values. Specifically, increasing x1 by 30 mm while
decreasing x2 by 0.2 mm/day maintains KGE within 0.01 of the optimum. This means
the model cannot independently identify soil moisture capacity and groundwater exchange
from the discharge record alone.

I will generate an ensemble of 30 parameter sets along this equifinal ridge, plus 10
sets exploring x3 and x4 sensitivity. For each, I'll run the model and record the
simulated hydrograph. The ensemble spread will represent predictive uncertainty.

Ensemble result for the June 2013 flood peak:
  Calibrated (best): 78.2 m3/s
  Ensemble median: 76.8 m3/s
  Ensemble 5th-95th percentile: 64.3 - 91.7 m3/s
  Observed: 82.1 m3/s (within the uncertainty band)
```

This approach produces a physically interpretable uncertainty estimate with 40 model runs instead of the 50,000 that DREAM would require. The trade-off is that the uncertainty estimate is not a formal posterior distribution -- it does not have the mathematical rigor of a Bayesian analysis. But for practical decision-making, an approximate uncertainty band derived from 40 intelligent runs may be more useful than a formal posterior that was never computed because the budget did not allow 50,000 runs.

## 24.7 Comparison: Traditional Optimization vs. Agentic AI

### 24.7.1 SCE-UA: The Workhorse of Automatic Calibration

The Shuffled Complex Evolution -- University of Arizona (SCE-UA) algorithm, developed by Duan, Sorooshian, and Gupta (1992), has been the most widely used automatic calibration method in hydrology for three decades. SCE-UA operates by maintaining a population of parameter vectors (a "complex"), sorting them by objective function value, evolving each sub-complex through a competitive complex evolution step inspired by the Nelder-Mead simplex algorithm, and periodically shuffling the sub-complexes to share information. The method is effective at navigating the irregular, multimodal objective function surfaces typical of hydrological models.

SCE-UA's strengths are well-established: it consistently finds near-optimal parameter sets, it handles correlated parameters and multimodal surfaces, and it requires minimal user configuration beyond the parameter bounds and the objective function definition. Its weaknesses are equally clear: it requires thousands of model evaluations (typically 5,000 to 20,000 for a 10-parameter model), it returns a single "optimal" point with no uncertainty information, and it treats the model as a complete black box -- it cannot incorporate domain knowledge, diagnose systematic errors, or adapt its strategy based on the nature of the model-data mismatch.

### 24.7.2 DREAM: Bayesian Parameter Estimation

The DREAM algorithm (Vrugt et al., 2009) extends automatic calibration into the Bayesian framework. Instead of seeking a single optimum, DREAM samples the posterior distribution of parameters conditional on the observed data, using multiple interacting Markov chains with differential evolution proposals. The posterior distribution captures not just the best parameter set but the full uncertainty structure, including parameter correlations, multimodality, and the width of the acceptable region.

DREAM's advantages over SCE-UA are substantial for uncertainty-aware applications: it provides formal uncertainty bounds on predictions, it identifies parameter correlations and trade-offs, and it can detect non-identifiability (parameters that the data cannot constrain). Its disadvantages are computational cost (10,000 to 100,000 model runs for convergence), sensitivity to the choice of likelihood function and error model, and the same black-box limitation -- no diagnostic reasoning, no domain knowledge integration, no adaptive strategy.

### 24.7.3 Agentic AI: Strengths and Limitations

Agentic AI is not a replacement for SCE-UA or DREAM -- it is a fundamentally different approach that fills a different niche. Its strengths lie in efficiency (far fewer model runs), transparency (every decision is documented with reasoning), and adaptability (the agent can change strategy, request information, or escalate to a human). Its limitations are equally important to understand.

The most significant limitation is **lack of mathematical guarantees**. SCE-UA can be proven to converge to the global optimum under certain conditions. DREAM produces a mathematically valid posterior distribution (given correct specification of the likelihood function). Agentic AI provides neither guarantee. The agent's parameter search is guided by heuristic reasoning, not by a convergence theorem. It may miss the global optimum, it may be biased by the LLM's training data, and its uncertainty estimates are approximate rather than formal.

The second limitation is **dependence on LLM quality**. The agent's diagnostic reasoning is only as good as the LLM's understanding of hydrology. Current frontier models (Claude Opus, GPT-5) have impressive but imperfect knowledge of hydrological processes. They may misdiagnose a parameter sensitivity, apply a reasoning pattern from one model structure to another where it does not apply, or fail to recognize an unusual catchment behavior. This is the hallucination risk applied to scientific reasoning -- the model confidently generates plausible but incorrect diagnoses.

The third limitation is **reproducibility variance**. LLMs are stochastic: the same prompt may produce slightly different reasoning paths on repeated runs (depending on temperature and sampling settings). This means that two runs of the same agent on the same calibration problem may converge to different parameter sets. While both sets may be acceptable (equifinal), the path-dependence is unsettling for scientists accustomed to deterministic optimization algorithms.

## 24.8 Reproducibility and Auditability

### 24.8.1 The Chain-of-Thought Record

One of the most underappreciated advantages of agentic AI is its inherent transparency. Every step of the agent's reasoning -- its plans, diagnoses, tool calls, and evaluation judgments -- is recorded in what is called the chain-of-thought log. This log is not a post-hoc reconstruction; it is the actual reasoning trace that the agent produced in real time. For a calibration task, the chain-of-thought log contains:

- The initial data inspection and quality assessment
- The rationale for the initial parameter selection
- Each model run with its parameters, results, and diagnostic analysis
- The reasoning behind each parameter adjustment
- The convergence decision and final parameter justification
- The validation results and overall assessment

This level of documentation far exceeds what is typically available from manual calibration, where the hydrologist's reasoning is rarely recorded, and from automatic optimization, where the search trajectory is available but contains no physical interpretation. The chain-of-thought log creates an auditable record that a reviewer can examine to understand not just *what* parameters were chosen, but *why*.

For regulatory applications -- flood risk assessments, environmental impact statements, water allocation decisions -- this auditability is valuable. A reviewer can trace the agent's reasoning, identify any assumptions or judgments that seem questionable, and request specific re-analyses. The agent can also be asked to re-run with different constraints or priorities, producing a new chain-of-thought that documents the alternative analysis.

### 24.8.2 Ensuring Reproducibility

Reproducibility requires that the same inputs produce the same outputs. For agentic AI, this is complicated by the stochastic nature of LLM inference. Two approaches mitigate this concern.

The first is **deterministic configuration**. Most LLM APIs allow setting the temperature parameter to zero (or near zero), which makes the output nearly deterministic for a given input. With temperature=0, the agent will produce the same reasoning chain and the same parameter adjustments on every run, given identical input data and tool responses. This makes the calibration fully reproducible, at the cost of losing the exploration benefits of stochastic sampling.

The second is **seed-based ensemble**. Rather than demanding exact reproducibility, the modeler runs the agent multiple times with different random seeds (temperature > 0), producing an ensemble of calibration results. The spread across runs characterizes both the parameter uncertainty and the sensitivity of the calibration to the reasoning path. If all runs converge to the same region of parameter space, the result is robust; if they diverge significantly, it indicates genuine equifinality or ambiguity in the calibration problem.

Both approaches should be accompanied by versioning of the LLM (specific model identifier and version), the tool suite (software versions of the model and evaluation tools), and the input data (hashes or checksums). The combination of chain-of-thought logging, deterministic or ensemble configuration, and full input/output versioning makes agentic calibration at least as reproducible as manual expert calibration, and arguably more so.

## 24.9 The 2025-2026 Landscape

### 24.9.1 What Exists Today

As of early 2026, agentic AI for hydrological modeling is in a transitional state: the core capabilities exist and have been demonstrated in research settings, but integrated production-ready systems are still emerging. Here is an honest assessment of what is possible today.

**LLM capabilities for hydrology.** The frontier models -- Claude Opus, GPT-5, Gemini Ultra -- can read and interpret hydrological data, understand model documentation, generate code to run simulations, compute performance metrics, and reason about parameter sensitivities. They know about NSE, KGE, and their properties. They understand the physical basis of common model parameters (curve number, hydraulic conductivity, Manning's n). They can diagnose hydrograph errors in qualitative terms (peak timing, volume bias, recession slope). However, they are not infallible: they occasionally confuse parameter definitions between model structures, they may not know the details of the latest model versions, and their understanding of complex multi-process interactions (such as snow-glacier-permafrost coupling) is shallow.

**Tool integration.** Running hydrological models from within an agentic framework requires wrapping the model in an API or command-line interface that the agent can call. For open-source models with command-line executables (SWAT+, MODFLOW 6, GR4J in the airGR R package, HBV-light), this is straightforward -- the agent generates a script, executes it, and parses the output. For GUI-based commercial models (HEC-HMS in its standard configuration, MIKE SHE, InfoWorks ICM), the integration is harder and may require custom scripting interfaces. The HEC-HMS Jython scripting interface and the MIKE Python SDK provide pathways, but they are not yet widely used in agentic workflows.

**Available platforms.** No major commercial platform currently offers a turnkey "hydrological modeling agent" product. What exists are general-purpose agent frameworks (Claude Agent SDK, OpenAI Assistants, LangChain) that can be configured with hydrology-specific tools. Several research groups have published proof-of-concept demonstrations, and the open-source community has produced prototype tool wrappers for SWAT+, HBV, and GR4J. The gap between these prototypes and a production system suitable for engineering practice is significant but narrowing rapidly.

### 24.9.2 What They Cannot Do (Yet)

Honest assessment of limitations is more valuable than breathless promotion of capabilities. As of early 2026, agentic AI systems for hydrological modeling cannot reliably:

**Modify model structure.** The agent can adjust parameters, but it cannot decide that the model needs an additional reservoir, a different evapotranspiration formulation, or a snow module. Model structure selection remains a human decision, though the agent can flag evidence that the current structure is inadequate (such as systematic seasonal errors that no parameter adjustment can fix).

**Interpret spatial heterogeneity from first principles.** A distributed model like SWAT has different parameters for each HRU (Hydrologic Response Unit), and the agent's ability to reason about spatial parameter variation is limited. It can apply general rules (sandy soils have higher hydraulic conductivity than clay soils), but it cannot replicate the nuanced spatial understanding that comes from field experience in a specific catchment.

**Handle truly novel situations.** If a catchment exhibits behavior that is not well-represented in the LLM's training data -- such as the complex interactions in a tropical peatland or the rapidly changing dynamics of a deglaciating watershed -- the agent may produce overconfident but incorrect reasoning. It does not know the boundaries of its own knowledge.

**Replace regulatory judgment.** Even a perfectly calibrated model requires professional judgment about its applicability, its limitations, and the appropriate safety margins for design decisions. The agent can assist with this judgment by providing information, but the responsibility remains with the licensed professional.

## 24.10 Limitations and Risks

### 24.10.1 Hallucination in Scientific Reasoning

The most dangerous failure mode of an LLM-based agent is hallucination: generating plausible but factually incorrect information with high confidence. In a hydrological modeling context, hallucination can manifest in several ways.

**Parameter knowledge errors.** The agent might state that "the GR4J x2 parameter controls the time base of the unit hydrograph" when in fact x2 controls groundwater exchange and x4 controls the time base. Such errors in basic parameter definitions could lead to fundamentally misguided calibration adjustments. This type of error is relatively easy to detect through validation -- the model will not improve as the agent expects -- but it wastes computational resources and may confuse the diagnostic trail.

**Fabricated precedents.** The agent might claim that "studies have shown that KGE values above 0.6 are generally sufficient for flood forecasting in Central European catchments," citing either no source or a non-existent one. While the statement might be approximately true, the specificity of the claim is not grounded in any particular study. This is the classic LLM hallucination pattern: generating specific, authoritative-sounding claims that are either fabricated or subtly distorted.

**Overconfident diagnosis.** The agent might reason that "the systematic underestimation of winter baseflow is clearly due to frozen soil effects that the model does not represent," when the actual cause is a data quality issue (the gauge records are unreliable during ice cover periods). The agent's training data contains many examples of process-based diagnostic reasoning, and it will generate plausible process-based explanations even when the true cause is mundane.

Mitigation strategies include: requiring the agent to cite specific evidence for its claims (forcing it to reference actual data values rather than general knowledge), implementing automated checks on parameter definitions and ranges, and maintaining human review of the diagnostic reasoning. The chain-of-thought log is itself a mitigation tool -- a reviewer can identify hallucinated claims by tracing the agent's reasoning back to the evidence.

### 24.10.2 Over-Confidence and the Dunning-Kruger Agent

A subtler risk than outright hallucination is calibrated confidence. Human experts have an intuitive sense of when they are operating near the boundaries of their competence -- they become more tentative, they qualify their statements, they ask for second opinions. Current LLMs do not have this metacognitive capability in a reliable way. An agent that encounters an unusual catchment behavior may be just as confident in its (incorrect) diagnosis as it is when reasoning about a straightforward case.

This over-confidence is particularly dangerous in hydrological modeling because the consequences of incorrect model predictions can be severe. A flood risk assessment based on an overconfident calibration could lead to under-designed levees. A water allocation model that does not acknowledge its uncertainty could trigger over-extraction during droughts. The fundamental mitigation is the **human-in-the-loop** principle: the agent assists and accelerates the human modeler, but does not replace the human's judgment, especially for high-stakes decisions.

In practice, this means designing agentic workflows with explicit checkpoints where the human reviews the agent's reasoning, questions its assumptions, and provides guidance. The agent should be configured to escalate to the human when it encounters situations outside its experience -- but as noted, current LLMs are not always aware of the boundaries of their experience. Explicit uncertainty triggers (such as "if performance does not improve after three iterations, ask the human for guidance") provide a mechanical fallback.

### 24.10.3 The Human-in-the-Loop Imperative

The term "autonomous modeler" in this chapter's title is aspirational and descriptive of a tendency, not a declaration that humans are unnecessary. The correct mental model for agentic AI in hydrology is not "AI replaces the hydrologist" but "AI operates as a highly capable research assistant who can work independently on well-defined tasks but requires supervision and guidance for judgment-intensive decisions."

The human provides: (a) the modeling objectives and their priorities, (b) domain knowledge that the agent may not possess (local geology, data quality issues, historical context), (c) judgment about when the model is "good enough" for its intended application, (d) responsibility for the consequences of decisions based on model results, and (e) ethical oversight to ensure that the modeling serves legitimate purposes and does not create false certainty.

The agent provides: (a) tireless, systematic execution of the calibration workflow, (b) consistent application of diagnostic reasoning across many iterations, (c) complete documentation of every decision, (d) rapid exploration of the parameter space, and (e) the ability to work on multiple tasks simultaneously (calibrating several sub-basins in parallel, for example).

This division of labor is not a temporary compromise pending the development of fully autonomous AI. It reflects a principled judgment about which aspects of hydrological modeling benefit from automation and which require human knowledge, responsibility, and accountability.

## 24.11 Hungarian Worked Example: The Zala River Watershed

### 24.11.1 Catchment Description and Data Sources

The Zala River is the largest tributary of Lake Balaton, Hungary's most important surface water body and a critical ecosystem, recreation, and water supply asset. The Zala catchment upstream of its confluence with the lake encompasses approximately 2,620 km$^2$ of western Transdanubia, a landscape of rolling hills, broad alluvial valleys, and mixed agriculture. Understanding the Zala's hydrology is essential for managing eutrophication in Lake Balaton -- the river contributes roughly half of the lake's phosphorus load -- and for flood risk management in the Zala valley municipalities.

For this worked example, we focus on the upper Zala catchment (930 km$^2$) gauged at Zalaegerszeg, the regional capital. The data sources used are entirely public and accessible:

**Meteorological forcing.** ERA5-Land reanalysis data from the Copernicus Climate Data Store (CDS), providing daily precipitation and 2-meter temperature at 0.1-degree (~9 km) resolution. ERA5-Land assimilates rain gauge and satellite data through the ECMWF's Integrated Forecasting System, producing a physically consistent gridded dataset. ### 24.11.2 Agent Calibration Walkthrough

We now present the complete agent calibration workflow for the Zala catchment, as it would actually proceed in an agentic framework. The GR4J model structure is used, with the same four parameters described in Section 24.4.

**Phase 1: Data ingestion and quality control.** The agent loads the ERA5-Land precipitation and temperature time series (2010-2020), computes catchment-average values using area-weighted averaging over the 0.1-degree grid cells that intersect the catchment boundary, and calculates PET using the Oudin formula:

$$PE = \frac{R_a}{\lambda \rho} \cdot \frac{T_a + 5}{100}$$

where $R_a$ is the extraterrestrial radiation (MJ m$^{-2}$ day$^{-1}$), $\lambda$ is the latent heat of vaporization (2.45 MJ kg$^{-1}$), $\rho$ is the density of water (1000 kg m$^{-3}$), and $T_a$ is the mean daily air temperature ($^\circ$C). For the Zala catchment at latitude 46.8$^\circ$N, the Oudin PET estimates average 650 mm/year, consistent with the regional water balance.

The agent checks for gaps, outliers, and inconsistencies. It flags 12 days with missing discharge (interpolated linearly), verifies that the water balance is physically plausible (P - PET $\approx$ 50 mm/year, compared to observed runoff of 164 mm/year -- the difference reflects deep percolation losses to the regional groundwater system), and confirms that the precipitation annual cycle matches expected patterns for western Transdanubia.

**Phase 2: Initial parameter estimation.** The agent consults the Corine Land Cover data to determine that the catchment is 60% arable land, 25% forest, 10% grassland, and 5% urban. From the ESDAC soil data, it determines that the dominant soil texture is silt loam to clay loam, with sandy alluvial deposits along the river corridor. Based on these characteristics and its training knowledge of GR4J applications in similar Central European catchments, the agent selects initial parameters as described in Section 24.4.3.

**Phase 3: Iterative calibration.** The calibration proceeds as described in Sections 24.4.4 through 24.4.6, converging in six iterations to the parameter set x1=310, x2=-0.4, x3=50, x4=1.8, with calibration KGE=0.78 and validation KGE=0.72.

**Phase 4: Sensitivity and uncertainty analysis.** Following convergence, the agent conducts the sensitivity analysis described in Section 24.6.2, generating a 40-member ensemble that characterizes the uncertainty due to parameter equifinality. The key finding is a strong trade-off between x1 and x2: the discharge data constrains the *net* water balance (rainfall minus total losses) but cannot independently separate soil moisture storage from groundwater exchange. The 90% prediction interval for the largest observed flood event (June 2013, Q_obs = 82.1 m$^3$/s) is 64.3 to 91.7 m$^3$/s, bracketing the observed value.

**Phase 5: Documentation and reporting.** The agent generates a calibration report that includes: the final parameter set with uncertainty ranges, performance metrics for calibration and validation periods, hydrograph plots for representative years, the chain-of-thought log documenting every reasoning step, and recommendations for model application. The total computational cost is 47 model runs (6 calibration + 1 validation + 40 ensemble), completed in approximately 3 minutes of total model run time for GR4J.

### 24.11.3 Interpreting the Results in Context

The calibration results deserve physical interpretation beyond the statistical metrics. The fitted parameter x1=310 mm (production store capacity) is consistent with the moderate water-holding capacity of silt loam soils -- lower than would be expected for deep clay soils (x1 > 500 mm) and higher than for sandy soils (x1 < 200 mm). The negative x2=-0.4 mm/day indicates a net loss of water from the model domain to the regional groundwater system, which is physically plausible: the Zala valley sits on Pannonian sediments that form part of the Transdanubian regional aquifer system, and some infiltrated water likely bypasses the gauging station as deep groundwater flow toward Lake Balaton.

The routing store capacity x3=50 mm and time base x4=1.8 days are consistent with a medium-sized catchment with moderate response time. The relatively low x3 value (compared to the GR4J median of ~100 mm reported in Perrin et al., 2003) suggests that the Zala catchment has a flashy response -- rainfall events produce relatively sharp hydrograph peaks with limited damping. This is consistent with the rolling terrain and the relatively impermeable Pannonian clay substrate that limits deep infiltration and promotes lateral subsurface flow.

The KGE decomposition reveals that the model's primary weakness is in the variability ratio (alpha = 0.88 in calibration): it slightly underestimates the contrast between high and low flows. This is a common limitation of lumped models applied to catchments with heterogeneous soil and land cover -- the lumped representation cannot capture the different response characteristics of forested hilltops versus agricultural plains versus alluvial valley floors. A distributed model (such as SWAT applied to individual HRUs) might improve this aspect, though at the cost of many more parameters and greater computational expense.

### 24.11.4 Global Perspective: Applicability Beyond Hungary

The Zala River example used European data sources (ERA5-Land, OVF, Corine, ESDAC), but the agentic calibration workflow is fully portable to other regions with equivalent data. In South and Southeast Asia, ERA5-Land or GPM IMERG provides precipitation forcing, national hydrological services provide discharge data (India's Central Water Commission, Thailand's Royal Irrigation Department, Vietnam's MONRE), and GlobeLand30 or ESA WorldCover replaces Corine for land cover. In sub-Saharan Africa, where gauge networks are sparse, the agent can work with satellite-based discharge estimates (from rating-curve models applied to satellite altimetry) and CHIRPS precipitation, though the reduced data quality will be reflected in wider uncertainty bands.

The key point is that the agentic framework is agnostic to the data source -- it operates on whatever precipitation, PET, and discharge data are provided. The agent's value lies not in the data but in the reasoning: the ability to inspect data quality, select appropriate initial parameters, diagnose model errors, and iterate efficiently toward a calibrated model. This reasoning capability is equally applicable whether the catchment is in Hungary, Bangladesh, or Kenya.

## 24.12 Building Your Own Hydrological Agent

### 24.12.1 A Minimal Working Example

For readers who wish to experiment with agentic calibration, the following sketch outlines the minimal components needed to build a GR4J calibration agent using Python and the Anthropic Claude API. This is not production code -- it is a conceptual guide to the architecture.

The system requires three components: (1) a GR4J model implementation (available in the `airGR` R package or the Python `hydrogr` library), (2) an evaluation module (using `hydroeval` or a custom function), and (3) an LLM-based reasoning loop. The workflow operates as follows:

```python
# Conceptual architecture -- not a complete implementation
# Step 1: Define tools the agent can call
tools = [
    {
        "name": "run_gr4j",
        "description": "Run GR4J model with given parameters, return simulated Q",
        "parameters": {"x1": "float", "x2": "float", "x3": "float", "x4": "float"}
    },
    {
        "name": "evaluate",
        "description": "Compute NSE, KGE, PBIAS between observed and simulated Q",
        "parameters": {"observed": "array", "simulated": "array"}
    },
    {
        "name": "plot_hydrograph",
        "description": "Plot observed vs simulated hydrograph, return image",
        "parameters": {"observed": "array", "simulated": "array", "dates": "array"}
    }
]

# Step 2: Provide the agent with context
system_prompt = """
You are a hydrological modeling agent. Your task is to calibrate a GR4J model
for the given catchment. Use the tools to run the model, evaluate results, and
iterate until KGE > 0.70. Reason about each result before adjusting parameters.
"""

# Step 3: Run the agent loop
context = [system_prompt, catchment_description, data_summary]
while not converged:
    response = llm.generate(context, tools=tools)
    if response.has_tool_call:
        result = execute_tool(response.tool_call)
        context.append(response.reasoning)
        context.append(result)
    else:
        final_report = response.text
        converged = True
```

The critical design decisions are: (a) how much context to maintain (all previous iterations, or a compressed summary), (b) what information to include in tool results (raw numbers only, or pre-computed diagnostics), (c) how to handle tool failures (model crashes, API timeouts), and (d) when to stop the iteration (fixed number of runs, performance threshold, or agent's own convergence judgment).

### 24.12.2 Practical Tips for Implementation

Several lessons have emerged from early implementations of hydrological modeling agents that are worth noting for practitioners.

**Provide rich tool outputs.** When the model runner returns results, include not just the simulated time series but also pre-computed summary statistics (mean flow, peak flow, flow-duration curve percentiles). When the evaluator returns metrics, include not just the aggregate KGE but its decomposed components (r, alpha, beta) and event-specific statistics. The richer the tool output, the better the agent can reason about the results. Conversely, returning only a single NSE value forces the agent to make calibration decisions with minimal information, degrading its diagnostic capability.

**Constrain the parameter space.** Provide the agent with physically motivated parameter bounds, not just the mathematical feasible range. If you know from soil surveys that the production store capacity should be between 200 and 500 mm for the catchment's soil types, tell the agent. This prior knowledge reduces the search space and prevents the agent from exploring physically unrealistic regions.

**Log everything.** Every tool call, every tool result, every reasoning step should be logged to a persistent file. This log is the calibration documentation. It should be structured (JSON or similar) so that it can be parsed and analyzed after the fact. Include timestamps, model version identifiers, and data checksums to ensure full traceability.

**Set stopping criteria.** Without explicit stopping criteria, an agent may continue iterating indefinitely, seeking marginal improvements that consume computational resources without practical benefit. Define clear stopping criteria: a performance threshold (KGE > 0.70), a maximum number of iterations (20), or a convergence criterion (less than 0.01 improvement in KGE over two consecutive iterations).

**Test with a simple model first.** Before applying the agentic approach to a complex distributed model, test it on a simple, fast model (GR4J, HBV-light) where you can verify the agent's reasoning against your own expert judgment. This builds confidence in the agent's diagnostic capabilities and helps identify any systematic biases in its reasoning.

## 24.13 The Road Ahead

### 24.13.1 Near-Term Developments (2026-2028)

Several developments in the next two to three years will substantially advance the capabilities of agentic AI for hydrological modeling. First, LLM context windows will continue to expand, enabling agents to maintain complete calibration histories for complex, multi-site, multi-objective calibration problems without compression or summarization. Second, multimodal capabilities will improve, allowing agents to interpret not just hydrograph plots but spatial maps, cross-sections, and even animation of time-varying fields. Third, domain-specific fine-tuning -- training LLMs on curated collections of hydrological literature, model documentation, and calibration case studies -- will improve the reliability of diagnostic reasoning and reduce hallucination on technical topics.

On the tool side, the integration of hydrological models into agentic frameworks will mature. Standardized model APIs (such as the Basic Model Interface, BMI, promoted by CSDMS) will make it easier to wrap models as tools. Cloud-based model execution will enable agents to launch computationally expensive simulations (SWAT, MODFLOW, HEC-RAS 2D) on scalable infrastructure rather than on the modeler's local machine. And pre-built hydrological tool suites -- data access, model execution, evaluation, visualization -- will emerge as open-source packages, lowering the barrier to entry.

### 24.13.2 The Long-Term Vision

The long-term trajectory of agentic AI in hydrology points toward systems that can manage entire modeling workflows from data collection through reporting: identifying data gaps, selecting appropriate model structures, calibrating parameters, quantifying uncertainty, producing forecasts, and generating technical reports. Such systems would not replace hydrologists but would amplify their capabilities by orders of magnitude -- enabling a single expert to manage modeling programs that currently require teams.

The most transformative potential lies in **democratizing hydrological modeling**. Today, high-quality hydrological modeling requires rare expertise -- the combination of physical hydrology knowledge, numerical modeling skills, programming ability, and calibration experience that takes years to develop. Agentic AI can encode much of this expertise in a system that is accessible to water managers, policy makers, and engineers in developing countries who currently lack the specialist resources for sophisticated modeling. The Zala River example in this chapter could equally well be a catchment in Rwanda, Nepal, or Bolivia -- the agent would apply the same reasoning framework, adapted to local data and conditions.

This democratization carries responsibility. Making powerful modeling tools widely accessible without ensuring adequate training, oversight, and critical evaluation could lead to overconfident decisions based on poorly understood models. The human-in-the-loop principle is not just a technical safeguard -- it is an ethical imperative. The goal of agentic AI is not to make human expertise unnecessary, but to make it more effective, more widely available, and more systematically documented.
