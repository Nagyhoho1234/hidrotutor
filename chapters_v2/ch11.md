# Chapter 11: Drawing Watersheds from Data

Every drop of rain that falls on the land surface begins a journey. Some evaporates, some infiltrates into the soil, and the rest moves downhill across the surface -- first as a thin sheet of water spreading across the hillslope, then concentrating into rills, then merging into channels that carry it to a river and eventually to the sea. The boundary between "water spreading across hillslopes" and "water concentrated in channels" is not arbitrary. It is a physical transition governed by the shape of the terrain, the intensity of rainfall, the resistance of the soil, and the slope of the land. Finding that boundary -- determining where channels begin and where their contributing areas end -- is the central challenge of this chapter.

The previous two chapters built the foundation. Chapter 9 prepared the digital elevation model by filling sinks and resolving flat areas. Chapter 10 computed flow directions and flow accumulation, creating a grid where every cell knows which way water flows and how much upstream area drains through it. Now we use those products to answer the questions that matter most for hydrology: Where are the streams? How dense is the channel network? Where does each sub-basin begin and end? And how do we convert these raster products into the vector features -- stream lines, catchment polygons, drainage points -- that form the backbone of hydrologic models?

These are not academic questions. The stream network and watershed boundaries derived from a DEM form the spatial skeleton of every distributed hydrologic model, every flood forecasting system, and every water resource management plan. The US National Water Model routes flow through 2.7 million stream reaches defined this way. The European Flood Awareness System (EFAS) does the same across the continent. Hungary's own flood forecasting system at the Orszagos Vizugyi Foigazgatosag (OVF) depends on watershed delineations for the Danube and Tisza basins. Getting the delineation right -- choosing the correct level of detail, ensuring physical consistency, maintaining topological integrity -- determines whether the resulting model is a useful representation of reality or a numerical artifact.

## 11.1 Stream Definition by Threshold

### 11.1.1 The Constant Support Area Method

The simplest and most widely used method for defining a stream network from a DEM is conceptually straightforward: classify every cell whose flow accumulation exceeds a specified threshold as a "stream cell," and leave everything else as "hillslope." Formally:

$$\text{Stream}(c) = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } A(c) \geq T \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

where $A(c)$ is the flow accumulation at cell $c$ (the number of upstream cells draining through $c$, or equivalently the upstream drainage area) and $T$ is the threshold, often called the **support area** or **channelization threshold**. The threshold can be expressed in number of cells or in area units (by multiplying cell count by cell area). In practice, cell counts are more commonly used during the computation, with area-based interpretation applied afterward.

This operation is a simple reclassification of the flow accumulation grid -- a single map algebra statement that produces a binary raster of stream cells and non-stream cells. In ArcGIS Pro, the Raster Calculator or Con tool performs this operation; in TauDEM, the Stream Definition by Threshold tool does the same. The computational cost is trivial. The intellectual cost -- choosing the right value of $T$ -- is not.

The choice of $T$ directly controls the detail and density of the resulting network. Consider a watershed covered by a 10-meter DEM. Each cell represents 100 square meters. A threshold of $T = 100$ cells means that any point with at least 10,000 square meters (1 hectare) of upstream drainage area is classified as a channel. This produces an extremely dense network that includes tiny ephemeral rills visible only during heavy rain. A threshold of $T = 5{,}000$ cells (50 hectares) produces a much sparser network showing only the perennial streams that would appear on a topographic map. A threshold of $T = 50{,}000$ cells (500 hectares, or 5 square kilometers) might show only the major rivers in the basin, omitting all tributaries.

The difference is dramatic. The same DEM, processed with the same flow direction algorithm, can yield a network with 500 stream segments or one with 50 -- simply by changing a single number. This is not a software limitation; it reflects a genuine physical reality. The landscape contains a hierarchy of drainage features spanning many orders of magnitude in scale, and the threshold determines which part of that hierarchy we choose to represent.

### 11.1.2 Why the Threshold Matters for Hydrologic Modeling

The threshold does far more than control the visual appearance of the network. It determines the fundamental spatial discretization of any hydrologic model built upon it. Hydrologic models typically represent two distinct domains with different process equations:

**Hillslope processes** describe how water moves from where it falls (as precipitation) to the nearest stream channel. This includes infiltration, soil moisture redistribution, overland flow, and shallow subsurface flow. The governing equations -- kinematic wave, Green-Ampt infiltration, Richards equation -- operate on the hillslope length scale: the distance water must travel as dispersed flow before reaching a concentrated channel.

**Channel processes** describe how water moves within the stream network from headwaters to outlet. This includes open-channel flow, flood wave routing, backwater effects, and channel storage. The governing equations -- Saint-Venant, Muskingum-Cunge, diffusion wave -- operate on the channel length scale: the distances between junctions, gauge locations, or model nodes along the stream.

The threshold $T$ determines where the model switches from hillslope equations to channel equations. If $T$ is too high (too few streams), the model forces water to travel unrealistically long distances as overland flow before reaching a channel. The hillslope travel times become too long, peak flows arrive too late, and the hydrograph is overly attenuated. If $T$ is too low (too many streams), the model introduces channels where dispersed hillslope flow actually occurs. Water reaches the channel too quickly, peak flows are overestimated, and the model may become computationally expensive because of the large number of stream segments that must be routed.

In short, choosing the threshold is not a cosmetic decision -- it is a modeling decision with consequences for predicted flow magnitudes, timing, and spatial distribution. The question is whether we can make this choice objectively, rather than arbitrarily. As we shall see in Section 11.3, the answer is yes.

### 11.1.3 Drainage Density: The Quantitative Measure

To reason quantitatively about stream network detail, we need a metric. **Drainage density** ($D_d$) is the total length of all channels within a watershed divided by the watershed area:

$$D_d = \frac{\sum L}{A}$$

where $\sum L$ is the total stream length and $A$ is the total drainage area. The units are inverse length -- typically $\text{km}^{-1}$ -- meaning drainage density expresses the number of kilometers of channel per square kilometer of watershed.

Drainage density is intimately related to hillslope length. Consider a simplified rectangular catchment: a single stream of length $L$ running down the center, with hillslopes of width $B$ on each side. The total watershed area is $A = 2B \cdot L$. The drainage density is:

$$D_d = \frac{L}{2B \cdot L} = \frac{1}{2B}$$

Rearranging:

$$B = \frac{1}{2 D_d}$$

This result is general (not just for rectangles): the mean hillslope length in a watershed is approximately $1/(2D_d)$. A drainage density of 2.0 km$^{-1}$ implies a mean hillslope length of 250 meters. A drainage density of 0.5 km$^{-1}$ implies a mean hillslope length of 1,000 meters -- four times longer. This directly affects the time water takes to reach the nearest channel and therefore the shape of the storm hydrograph.

## 11.2 Topographic Variability and the Problem of a Single Threshold

### 11.2.1 Landscapes Have Different Textures

One of the most important lessons from geomorphology is that topographic texture -- the fineness or coarseness of landscape dissection -- varies enormously from place to place. The Badlands of South Dakota exhibit an extraordinarily fine drainage texture, with channels incised every few meters. The gently rolling Great Plains a few hundred kilometers to the east may have channels spaced kilometers apart. The dissected Appalachian Plateau looks utterly different from the smooth coastal plain of the Gulf of Mexico.

This variability is not random. It reflects differences in climate, geology, vegetation, and erosional history. Arid badlands erode rapidly in unconsolidated sediments, creating fine-scale channel networks. Humid, vegetated landscapes resist channelization, allowing smooth convex hillslopes to persist over larger areas. The channelization threshold -- the point at which dispersed hillslope flow concentrates into a channel -- varies with the local balance of erosive and resistive forces.

Montgomery and Dietrich (1992) articulated this principle clearly in a landmark paper in *Science*: "Landscape dissection into distinct valleys is limited by a threshold of channelization that sets a finite scale to the landscape." Their key insight was that this scale varies from place to place, implying that **a single constant support area threshold cannot correctly represent channel initiation across diverse terrain**.

Consider two very different landscapes within Hungary. The Bukk Mountains in the northeast are deeply dissected karst terrain with steep valleys and dense drainage networks. The Great Hungarian Plain (Alfold) east of the Tisza is flat alluvial terrain where channels are widely spaced and often artificial. Applying the same support area threshold to both landscapes would either overestimate channels on the plain or underestimate them in the mountains.

### 11.2.2 Alternative Stream Definition Methods

Several approaches have been proposed to account for spatial variability in drainage density:

**Grid network ordering (Peckham).** Apply Strahler ordering to the complete grid network (before thresholding), then keep only streams above a specified order. Because stream order reflects local network complexity, this approach captures some spatial variability. However, it requires processing the enormous number of links in the full grid network and introduces its own arbitrary threshold (minimum order instead of minimum area).

**Area-slope threshold (Montgomery and Dietrich, 1992).** Define channels where the product $A \cdot S^n$ exceeds a threshold, where $S$ is the local slope and $n$ is an exponent (typically around 2). The physical motivation is that channel initiation depends on both the volume of flow (proportional to area) and its erosive power (related to slope). In theory, this should produce more channels on steep terrain and fewer on gentle terrain. In practice, the method tends to produce "feathering" -- spurious parallel channels on steep but unchanneled hillslopes -- and may miss channels in flat, crenulated areas where slopes are low but channels clearly exist.

**Peuker-Douglas curvature approach.** A filtering algorithm that identifies valley cells based on local surface curvature, then uses flow accumulation of only those valley cells to define connected streams. This method, described in detail in Section 11.4, produces spatially variable drainage density that naturally adapts to landscape texture. It is the preferred approach in the TauDEM software.

Of these, the Peuker-Douglas method combined with the objective stream drop test (Section 11.3) provides the most principled and practically successful approach. But first, we need to understand the stream drop test itself.

## 11.3 Objective Stream Definition: The Stream Drop T-Test

### 11.3.1 The Geomorphologic Foundation

Is there a way to choose the channelization threshold objectively -- not by visual inspection or arbitrary convention, but by a criterion rooted in the physics and geometry of real drainage networks? Tarboton, Bras, and Rodriguez-Iturbe (1991, 1992) developed such a method, based on established geomorphologic laws that describe the statistical regularity of natural channel networks.

The method rests on a remarkable empirical observation made by Broscoe (1959): the **constant stream drop property**. A "stream drop" is the elevation difference between the upstream and downstream ends of a **Strahler stream** -- that is, the entire contiguous sequence of channel segments having the same Strahler order, from where that order first appears (at a source for first-order streams, or at the junction of two lower-order streams for higher orders) to where the stream meets another stream of equal or higher order (or reaches the basin outlet).

Formally, the stream drop for a Strahler stream of order $\omega$ is:

$$\Delta z_\omega = z_{\text{source}} - z_{\text{mouth}}$$

where $z_{\text{source}}$ is the elevation at the upstream end and $z_{\text{mouth}}$ is the elevation at the downstream end of the Strahler stream.

The constant stream drop property states that the mean value of $\Delta z$ is approximately the same for all stream orders in a given watershed. First-order streams (small headwater channels) have approximately the same mean elevation drop as second-order streams (formed by the junction of two first-order streams), which have approximately the same mean drop as third-order streams, and so on. This is a statistical statement -- individual stream drops vary widely -- but the means are remarkably consistent.

This property has a physical basis in the form instability theory of Smith and Bretherton (1972), who showed that the channelization of a uniform surface leads to a network whose geometric properties, including the constant drop property, emerge naturally from the interaction of erosion and diffusion processes.

### 11.3.2 The Logic of the Test

The stream drop test exploits the constant drop property as a diagnostic tool. The reasoning is as follows:

If we set the channelization threshold correctly -- defining streams where channels truly begin in the landscape -- then the resulting first-order streams should be real channels, and their mean stream drop should be consistent with the mean drops of higher-order streams. The constant drop property should hold.

If we set the threshold too low -- defining streams too far up the hillslope, in areas where concentrated flow does not actually occur -- then the resulting "first-order streams" include hillslope elements. These hillslope segments have different geometric properties from real channels. In particular, hillslope profiles tend to be convex (decreasing slope in the downhill direction), while channel profiles tend to be concave (increasing slope in the downstream direction). The elevation drops of these spurious hillslope "streams" will be systematically different from the drops of real higher-order streams. The constant drop property will be violated.

The test therefore compares the mean stream drop of first-order streams against the mean stream drop of all higher-order streams (orders 2 and above) using a standard two-sample t-test.

### 11.3.3 The T-Test Procedure

The procedure is applied across a range of candidate thresholds. For each threshold $T$:

1. Delineate the stream network by classifying all cells with flow accumulation $\geq T$ as stream cells.
2. Assign Strahler stream orders and identify individual Strahler streams.
3. Compute the stream drop $\Delta z$ for every Strahler stream.
4. Separate the drops into two groups: first-order streams (group 1) and all higher-order streams (group $h$).
5. Compute the t-statistic:

$$t = \frac{\bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_h}{\sqrt{\frac{s_1^2}{n_1} + \frac{s_h^2}{n_h}}}$$

where $\bar{x}$, $s$, and $n$ denote the sample mean, standard deviation, and sample size for each group.

6. Apply the decision rule: if $|t| > 2$, the means are significantly different at approximately the 95% confidence level (for large sample sizes, $t_{\text{critical}} \approx 1.96$). A significant difference indicates that first-order streams do not conform to the constant drop property, meaning the threshold is too fine.

The objective threshold is the **smallest $T$** (finest resolution, densest network) for which $|t| < 2$. This is the most detailed stream network that remains consistent with the constant drop property -- the densest network that still looks geomorphologically like a real channel network.

At the finest threshold ($T = 50$), the t-statistic is $-4.2$, far exceeding the critical value of 2 in absolute terms. The negative sign indicates that first-order stream drops (8.4 m on average) are systematically smaller than higher-order drops (18.2 m). This makes physical sense: many of the "first-order streams" at this fine threshold are actually hillslope segments with small elevation differences, not real channels with the characteristic concave profile that produces larger drops.

At $T = 100$, the t-statistic is still significant ($-2.8$), though the first-order mean drop has increased to 12.1 m as the shortest, shallowest pseudo-channels are eliminated.

At $T = 200$, the t-statistic drops below 2 for the first time ($-1.8$). The first-order mean drop (16.3 m) is now close to the higher-order mean (17.8 m). This is the objective threshold: $T = 200$ cells, yielding a drainage density of 1.7 km$^{-1}$ and a mean hillslope length of about 294 meters.

The method pins down the drainage density to the range between successive tested thresholds -- here, between 1.7 and 2.3 km$^{-1}$. This precision is typically sufficient, given that DEM-derived stream networks are themselves approximate representations of the true channel system.

### 11.3.5 Connection to Slope-Area Scaling

The stream drop test is conceptually related to a well-known geomorphologic observation: the scaling relationship between channel slope and contributing area. In established channel networks, slope $S$ decreases as a power function of drainage area $A$:

$$S \propto A^{-\theta}$$

where $\theta$ is the concavity index, typically around 0.5 for fluvial channels. This relationship reflects the downstream hydraulic geometry of rivers: as drainage area increases, discharge increases, channel width and depth increase, and the slope required to convey the flow decreases.

This power-law relationship breaks down at small contributing areas, where the terrain transitions from concave channels to convex hillslopes. On a log-log plot of slope versus area, there is a visible inflection point: above the inflection area, slope decreases with area (channel behavior); below it, slope may increase or remain constant with area (hillslope behavior). The inflection point corresponds to the channelization threshold -- the contributing area at which dispersed hillslope flow transitions to concentrated channel flow.

The stream drop test identifies essentially the same transition point, but through a statistical test rather than visual identification of a log-log inflection. The two approaches are complementary, and the stream drop test has the advantage of providing an objective, automated criterion.

## 11.4 The Peuker-Douglas Method for Spatially Variable Drainage Density

### 11.4.1 Curvature-Based Stream Source Identification

The constant support area threshold assumes that channels begin everywhere at the same upstream drainage area. As we discussed in Section 11.2, this is physically unrealistic: channels begin at smaller areas in steep, dissected terrain and at larger areas in gentle, smooth terrain. What we need is a method that adapts the drainage density to the local topographic texture.

Peuker and Douglas (1975) proposed an elegantly simple approach based on local surface curvature. Their algorithm examines every 2$\times$2 window (four adjacent cells) in the DEM:

1. Within each window, identify the highest cell.
2. "Unflag" (mark as non-valley) the highest cell.
3. After processing all windows across the entire DEM, the cells that remain flagged (never identified as the highest in any window) are the **upward-curved (valley)** cells.

The logic is intuitive. In a valley bottom, the surrounding cells are higher, so the valley-bottom cell is never the highest in its 2$\times$2 windows. On a ridge, the cell is consistently the highest and gets unflagged. On a planar slope, some cells are highest in some windows but not others, producing a mixed pattern.

After processing all windows, the remaining flagged cells (never the highest in any window) are identified as upward-curved valley cells. These cells tend to lie along valley bottoms and stream channels.

The resulting pattern of valley cells is **disconnected** -- many valley cells are isolated, separated by unflagged cells on the valley sides. To create a connected stream network from these scattered valley cells, a modified flow accumulation is performed: instead of accumulating all upstream cells equally, only the valley cells contribute to the accumulation. Each cell's "valley-cell contributing area" represents how many upstream valley cells drain through it, rather than how many total upstream cells drain through it.

This weighted accumulation connects the valley cells along the drainage network. A threshold applied to this weighted accumulation then defines the stream network. Crucially, because valley cells are concentrated in rugged terrain (where curvature is high) and sparse in gentle terrain (where curvature is low), the resulting stream network has **spatially variable drainage density**: more streams in dissected mountains, fewer streams on smooth plains.

### 11.4.2 Applying the Stream Drop Test to the Peuker-Douglas Method

The stream drop t-test (Section 11.3) can be applied to stream networks derived from the Peuker-Douglas weighted accumulation, in exactly the same way as for the constant support area threshold. The only difference is that the threshold now specifies the minimum number of upstream valley cells (not total upstream cells) required to define a stream.

Because the Peuker-Douglas valley cells are not uniformly distributed -- they cluster in rugged terrain and are sparse in smooth terrain -- the same numerical threshold produces different physical drainage densities in different parts of the watershed. A threshold of 20 valley cells might correspond to 2 hectares of contributing area in a steep mountain valley (where nearly every cell is a valley cell) but 20 hectares on a gentle plain (where valley cells are rare). This automatic adaptation is the method's key advantage.

In a dissected sub-basin of the watershed, the local drainage density might be 2.5 km$^{-1}$; on the adjacent gentle slope, it might be 0.8 km$^{-1}$. Both values emerge from the same global threshold, adapted to the local terrain through the Peuker-Douglas curvature filter.

This combined approach -- Peuker-Douglas curvature filtering plus stream drop t-test -- is the preferred method for stream definition in TauDEM and represents the current state of practice for objective, physically motivated stream network delineation. It is implemented as the **Peuker-Douglas Stream Definition** tool in TauDEM, which automates the entire sequence: curvature filtering, weighted accumulation, threshold testing, and stream raster output.

## 11.5 Stream Segmentation and Strahler Ordering

### 11.5.1 Breaking the Network into Links

Once the stream cells have been identified (whether by constant threshold or Peuker-Douglas), the continuous raster of stream cells must be segmented into individual **stream links** (also called reaches or segments). A link is a contiguous sequence of stream cells between:

- A **source** (a headwater cell with no upstream stream cell) and the first downstream junction,
- A **junction** (where two or more streams meet) and the next downstream junction, or
- The final junction and the **watershed outlet**.

Each link receives a unique integer identifier, establishing the fundamental unit of the stream network. In ArcGIS Pro, the **Stream Link** tool performs this segmentation; in TauDEM, the **Stream Reach and Watershed** tool does the same as part of a broader workflow.

The number of links in the network depends directly on the stream definition threshold. A fine threshold produces many short links; a coarse threshold produces fewer, longer links. For a typical third-order basin, there might be 20--50 stream links; for a fifth-order basin, there might be several hundred. The National Hydrography Dataset (NHDPlus) for the entire continental United States contains 2.7 million stream links (called "reaches" and identified by COMID).

### 11.5.2 Strahler Stream Ordering

Stream ordering is a method for classifying streams by their position in the drainage hierarchy. The most widely used system, developed by Arthur Strahler (1957), assigns orders according to three rules:

1. **First-order streams** are headwater channels with no tributaries -- they originate at sources.
2. When two streams of the **same order** $n$ merge at a junction, the downstream stream becomes order $n + 1$.
3. When two streams of **different orders** merge, the downstream stream retains the **higher** of the two orders.

A first-order stream joining another first-order stream creates a second-order stream. A second-order stream joining a second-order stream creates a third-order stream. But a first-order stream joining a second-order stream leaves the downstream reach as second-order -- the larger stream simply absorbs the smaller tributary without gaining rank.

A **Strahler stream** is the entire contiguous sequence of links having the same order. For example, a third-order Strahler stream extends from the junction where it first becomes third order (where two second-order streams meet) to either the watershed outlet or the next junction where it meets another third-order stream (creating a fourth-order stream). The stream drop discussed in Section 11.3 is the elevation change along the full length of such a Strahler stream.

Stream order is assigned as an attribute to each link in the output network. It provides a simple but powerful summary of network complexity: the order of the outlet stream (the basin order) indicates the overall size and branching complexity of the network. The Amazon is approximately 12th order; the Mississippi is approximately 10th order; Hungary's Tisza is approximately 8th order at its confluence with the Danube; a small headwater catchment might be 3rd or 4th order.

### 11.5.3 Horton's Laws: The Statistical Regularity of Drainage Networks

The stream ordering framework reveals remarkable statistical regularities in natural drainage networks, first described by Robert Horton (1945) and subsequently refined by Strahler and others. These regularities, known as **Horton's Laws**, describe how network properties scale across stream orders:

**The Law of Stream Numbers (Bifurcation Ratio).** The ratio of the number of streams of order $\omega$ to the number of streams of order $\omega + 1$ is approximately constant:

$$R_b = \frac{N_\omega}{N_{\omega+1}} \approx \text{constant}$$

The bifurcation ratio $R_b$ typically falls between 3 and 5, with most natural watersheds clustered around 3.5--4.5. A bifurcation ratio of 4 means that there are roughly four times as many first-order streams as second-order streams, four times as many second-order streams as third-order streams, and so on.

**The Law of Stream Lengths (Length Ratio).** The ratio of mean stream lengths of successive orders is approximately constant:

$$R_L = \frac{\bar{L}_{\omega+1}}{\bar{L}_\omega} \approx \text{constant}$$

The length ratio $R_L$ typically falls between 1.5 and 3.5. A ratio of 2.5 means that the average second-order stream is 2.5 times longer than the average first-order stream, the average third-order stream is 2.5 times longer than the average second-order stream, and so on.

**The Law of Stream Areas (Area Ratio).** The ratio of mean drainage areas of successive orders is approximately constant:

$$R_A = \frac{\bar{A}_{\omega+1}}{\bar{A}_\omega} \approx \text{constant}$$

The area ratio $R_A$ typically falls between 3 and 6, often close to $R_L^2$ (since area scales as the square of a length).

**The Law of Stream Slopes (Slope Ratio).** Mean stream slope decreases geometrically with increasing order:

$$R_S = \frac{\bar{S}_\omega}{\bar{S}_{\omega+1}} \approx \text{constant}$$

The slope ratio $R_S$ is typically between 1.5 and 3.

These laws are not exact -- they are statistical tendencies observed across thousands of watersheds worldwide, in climates ranging from arctic to tropical and in geologies from granite to limestone. They reflect fundamental organizing principles of erosional landscapes: the fractal-like self-similarity of drainage networks across scales. When a DEM-derived stream network obeys these laws, it is a strong indication that the delineation is geomorphologically reasonable. Violations -- particularly a bifurcation ratio that deviates strongly from the expected range -- can signal problems with the DEM, the threshold, or the flow direction algorithm.

## 11.6 Catchment and Watershed Delineation

### 11.6.1 From Streams to Catchments

With the stream network segmented into individually identified links, the next step is to delineate the **catchment** draining to each link. A catchment is defined as the area that drains directly to a single stream link without passing through any other stream link. For every stream link, there is exactly one corresponding catchment. This one-to-one correspondence between streams and catchments is a foundational principle of the hydrologic information model.

The computation is straightforward, using the flow direction raster and the stream link raster:

1. Every stream link has a unique identifier (from the segmentation step).
2. For every non-stream cell in the DEM, trace the flow path downstream (following D8 flow directions) until a stream cell is reached.
3. Assign that non-stream cell to the catchment of the stream link it drains to.
4. Stream cells themselves belong to the catchment of their own link.

The result is a raster where every cell carries the identifier of its associated stream link -- a complete tessellation of the landscape into non-overlapping catchments. In ArcGIS Pro, the **Watershed** tool performs this operation (using the stream link raster as pour points); in TauDEM, the **Stream Reach and Watershed** tool produces both the stream network and the watershed grid simultaneously.

This tessellation is not arbitrary. Unlike political boundaries, census tracts, or regular grids, catchment boundaries are defined by physical rules -- topographic ridgelines that separate flow going to one stream from flow going to the adjacent stream. They are nature's own spatial units for organizing hydrological processes. This is why catchment-based spatial discretization is preferred for distributed hydrologic modeling: it respects the physical topology of water movement in ways that regular grids or administrative boundaries cannot.

### 11.6.2 Sub-watersheds and Watersheds: A Hierarchy of Spatial Units

The terminology around drainage areas can be confusing, so precision matters:

**Catchment** (also called **subcatchment** or **elementary catchment**): The area draining directly to a single stream link. Catchments form a non-overlapping, complete tessellation of the landscape. They are the finest spatial unit in the delineation hierarchy.

**Sub-watershed**: The area draining to a specific designated point (such as a stream gauge, water intake, or dam) without passing through any other designated point. A sub-watershed comprises one or more complete catchments aggregated together. Sub-watersheds are also non-overlapping -- they tessellate the watershed.

**Watershed** (or **basin**): The total area draining to a designated outlet. A watershed for the main outlet encompasses all sub-watersheds within the basin. Watersheds for different outlets may be **nested** (one contains the other) but are not overlapping in the sense of double-counting area.

The flexibility of this hierarchy is its power. A basin with 200 catchments can be organized into 10 sub-watersheds (one per gauge), or into 5 planning units (one per water intake), or used at the full catchment resolution for detailed modeling -- all from the same underlying delineation. The catchments are the atoms; sub-watersheds and watersheds are molecules assembled from them.

In the context of the EU Water Framework Directive, the hierarchy is formalized: **River Basin Districts** are the largest units, subdivided into **water bodies** (roughly equivalent to NHDPlus reaches with their catchments). Hungary's entire territory falls within the Danube River Basin District, subdivided into major sub-basins (Tisza, Drava, Balaton, etc.), each further divided into water bodies for WFD reporting.

### 11.6.3 The Watershed as a Tessellation

The set of all catchments in a basin constitutes a **tessellation** of the landscape -- a subdivision into non-overlapping polygons that together cover the entire basin area, like tiles on a floor. Unlike arbitrary tessellations (regular grids, Thiessen polygons around random points), this tessellation is defined by physical rules: topographic flow directions determine which cells belong to which catchment.

This physically based tessellation has remarkable properties (and it is the same tessellation used in Chapter 14 to compute HAND values on a reach-by-reach basis, and in Chapter 15 to assign flood depths from individual stream reaches to their contributing catchments):

- **Conservation**: The total area of all catchments equals the total basin area. No area is double-counted or omitted.
- **Connectivity**: Every catchment connects to exactly one downstream catchment (or to the basin outlet), creating a directed tree structure that mirrors the stream network.
- **Scalability**: Catchments can be aggregated to any desired level (sub-watershed, watershed, region) by following the tree structure upstream from any point.
- **Uniqueness**: For a given stream network and DEM, the tessellation is deterministic -- the same input always produces the same catchments.

These properties make the catchment tessellation the natural spatial framework for distributed hydrologic modeling. Each catchment receives precipitation, generates runoff according to its land cover and soil properties, and delivers that runoff to its associated stream link. The stream network then routes flow downstream from catchment to catchment, through the connected sequence of links, to the basin outlet. This is the computational architecture of every major operational flood forecasting system, from the US National Water Model to EFAS to Australia's Bureau of Meteorology flood forecasting system.

### 11.6.4 Outlet Snapping: Getting the Pour Point Right

In many practical applications, the goal is not to delineate the entire basin's catchment system but to find the watershed boundary for a specific outlet -- typically a stream gauge, a water intake, a dam, or a proposed monitoring site. The user provides the coordinates of the desired outlet, and the GIS delineates all upstream area.

There is a practical problem: the user-specified outlet coordinates almost never fall exactly on a DEM-derived stream cell. The reasons include:

- **DEM resolution**: A 10-meter or 30-meter DEM cannot represent the exact position of a gauge attached to a bridge abutment. The nearest stream cell might be 20 meters away.
- **Coordinate system differences**: The gauge coordinates may have been measured in a different datum or projection than the DEM. In Hungary, gauge coordinates might be in HD72/EOV (EPSG:23700) while the DEM is in ETRS89 (EPSG:4258) or WGS84 -- transformation errors of a few meters are common.
- **Positional uncertainty**: GPS coordinates of field-surveyed locations have inherent accuracy limits, typically 3--5 meters for consumer-grade GPS and sub-meter for survey-grade equipment.

If the outlet point falls on a non-stream cell, the watershed delineation will be incorrect -- potentially capturing only a tiny local drainage area rather than the entire upstream basin.

The solution is **outlet snapping** (also called **pour point snapping**): automatically moving the user-specified outlet to the nearest DEM-derived stream cell. In TauDEM, the **Move Outlets to Streams** tool traces from the specified outlet along D8 flow directions until a stream cell is reached, within a user-specified maximum search distance. In ArcGIS Pro, the **Snap Pour Point** tool performs a similar operation, snapping the point to the cell with the highest flow accumulation within a specified search radius.

The maximum search distance (or snap radius) should be large enough to accommodate positional uncertainties but small enough to avoid snapping to the wrong stream. A search distance of 100--500 meters is typical for gauge locations on medium-resolution DEMs. For very precise outlet locations (e.g., dam outlets from engineering surveys), a smaller distance of 50--100 meters may suffice.

After snapping, it is essential to verify the result by comparing the DEM-derived drainage area at the snapped outlet to the known drainage area. For example, if the USGS reports that gauge 10109000 on the Logan River has a drainage area of 554 km$^2$, and the DEM-derived area at the snapped outlet is 548 km$^2$ -- a 1% difference -- the snapping was successful. If the derived area is 12 km$^2$, the point snapped to the wrong stream, and the search distance needs adjustment.

## 11.7 Conversion to Vector Features

### 11.7.1 From Raster Streams to Vector Polylines

The stream network and catchments have been computed as raster grids -- integer-valued grids where each cell carries the identifier of its stream link or catchment. For most hydrologic analyses, these rasters must be converted to vector features: polylines for streams and polygons for catchments. The vector representation enables attribute storage, topology construction, network traversal, and integration with other vector datasets (gauge locations, water intakes, administrative boundaries).

The **Stream to Feature** conversion (ArcGIS Pro: **Stream to Feature** or **Raster to Polyline**; TauDEM: output of **Stream Reach and Watershed**) traces each stream link as a polyline through the centers of its constituent cells. The resulting polyline has a staircase pattern (because cells are square), but the positions are exact -- each vertex sits at a cell center.

An important option during conversion is whether to **simplify** (smooth) the resulting polylines. Simplification removes intermediate vertices to create smoother lines, which look better on maps but may disconnect the vector streams from the underlying raster grid. For hydrologic analysis where the vector streams must correspond exactly to the raster streams (for example, when linking vector stream attributes to raster-based computations), simplification should be **disabled**. For cartographic purposes, it can be enabled.

Each drainage line in the output feature class carries rich attributes:

- **Grid code**: The unique identifier inherited from the stream link raster, serving as the relational key to the corresponding catchment polygon.
- **Shape length**: The total length of the polyline, in the coordinate system's units.
- **Upstream/downstream link IDs**: Identifiers of the connecting links, enabling network traversal -- given any link, you can trace upstream to all contributing links or downstream to the outlet.
- **Contributing area**: The total drainage area at the downstream end of the link.
- **Strahler order**: The stream order of the link.
- **Stream drop**: The elevation difference from the upstream to the downstream end.
- **Stream slope**: The ratio of stream drop to link length.
- **Distance to outlet**: The cumulative channel distance from the link's downstream end to the basin outlet.

These attributes, computed automatically during the delineation, provide a comprehensive geomorphometric characterization of every stream segment in the basin. They are the starting point for hydrologic model parameterization: channel routing requires reach length, slope, and drainage area; water quality modeling requires distance to outlet and travel time; flood forecasting requires connectivity and contributing area.

### 11.7.2 From Raster Catchments to Vector Polygons

The catchment raster is converted to a vector polygon feature class using a standard **Raster to Polygon** conversion. Each contiguous group of cells with the same identifier becomes a polygon. The resulting feature class has one polygon per catchment, each carrying:

- **Grid code**: Matching the stream link identifier for that catchment.
- **Shape area**: The area of the polygon in the coordinate system's units.

An important detail: at some junctions, a catchment may consist of **disjoint pieces**. This occurs when a small sliver of cells on the "wrong" side of a junction drains to the same downstream link as the main body of the catchment. Enabling the **create multipart features** option during conversion keeps these disjoint pieces as a single polygon feature with the correct identifier. Without this option, they would become separate features with the same grid code, complicating subsequent joins and analyses.

### 11.7.3 The Relational Structure: Linking Streams, Catchments, and Points

The grid code is the linchpin of the entire relational structure. It links three feature classes:

- **DrainageLine** (polyline): The stream segments.
- **Catchment** (polygon): The contributing areas.
- **DrainagePoint** (point): Located at the center of the outlet cell of each catchment (the cell where the catchment's flow exits to the downstream link).

A simple table join on grid code connects any stream to its catchment, any catchment to its stream, and both to their drainage point. This relational structure, combined with the upstream/downstream connectivity encoded in the drainage line attributes, constitutes a complete **topological model** of the drainage network -- a model that captures not just the geometry (where things are) but the connectivity (how things are connected) and the hierarchy (what drains to what).

This topological model is the core of what David Maidment and colleagues formalized as the **Arc Hydro** data model (Maidment, 2002). Arc Hydro extends the basic stream-and-catchment framework with a universal identifier system (**HydroID**) that assigns unique identifiers across all feature classes, enabling cross-layer relationships. A stream gauge point can be linked to the stream it monitors, the catchment it represents, and all upstream catchments that contribute to it -- all through the relational framework.

The Arc Hydro data model is not just an academic exercise. It is the spatial data architecture underlying the US National Hydrography Dataset (NHDPlus) and, by extension, the National Water Model. Its concepts are echoed in the EU-Hydro dataset and in HydroSHEDS at the global scale. Every operational hydrologic forecasting system that routes flow through a stream network relies on some version of this relational structure.

## 11.8 Comparison with Established Hydrographic Datasets

### 11.8.1 NHDPlus: The US National Standard

The National Hydrography Dataset Plus (NHDPlus) is a pre-computed, nationally consistent dataset of stream networks and catchments covering the entire conterminous United States. It represents the operational realization of the DEM-based delineation methods described in this chapter, applied at continental scale. NHDPlus contains:

- **2.7 million stream reaches** covering 3.2 million miles (5.1 million km) of mapped streams and rivers, each identified by a unique COMID (Common Identifier).
- **2.7 million associated catchments**, one per reach, also identified by COMID.
- **Flow attributes** including estimated mean annual flow and velocity from the Enhanced Runoff Method (EROM).
- **Connectivity information** encoding the upstream-downstream relationships between all reaches, enabling network routing from any point to the coast.

NHDPlus was not built in a day. Its construction spanned approximately 20 years, beginning with the component datasets -- the National Elevation Dataset (NED) for elevation, the National Hydrography Dataset (NHD) for cartographically mapped stream positions, the Watershed Boundary Dataset (WBD) for basin boundaries, and the National Land Cover Dataset (NLCD) for land cover. Integrating these four datasets into a consistent, routable network required reconciling different resolutions (30 m DEM, variable-scale hydrography), different vintages (maps compiled over decades), and different production methods (automated DEM extraction vs. manual cartography).

The result is the spatial backbone of water resources management in the United States. The National Water Model routes flow through every NHDPlus reach. The EPA uses NHDPlus for water quality assessment and TMDL (Total Maximum Daily Load) calculations. FEMA uses the associated catchments for flood risk mapping. State agencies use it for permitting, monitoring, and planning. The COMID identifier system enables these agencies to share data using a common spatial reference.

### 11.8.2 EU-Hydro: The European Equivalent

In Europe, the Copernicus Land Monitoring Service produces **EU-Hydro**, a pan-European river network and catchment dataset that serves a role analogous to NHDPlus. EU-Hydro provides:

- A river network derived from a combination of satellite imagery (Sentinel-2), the EU-DEM (now superseded by the Copernicus DEM), and national mapping agency data.
- Catchment boundaries for the approximately 1.6 million km$^2$ EEA-39 area (the 27 EU member states plus Iceland, Liechtenstein, Norway, Switzerland, and candidate countries).
- Linked dam and lake databases.
- River network topology enabling upstream-downstream routing.

EU-Hydro is freely available from the Copernicus Land Monitoring Service portal. For Hungary, it covers the entire territory, including the transboundary Danube and Tisza basins. However, EU-Hydro's resolution and detail are generally lower than what can be achieved by processing high-resolution national DEMs (such as Hungary's DDM-5 at 5-meter resolution or the Copernicus EEA-10 DEM at 10-meter resolution). For detailed watershed studies, it is often preferable to derive the network from the best available DEM, using EU-Hydro as a validation reference.

In Hungary specifically, the **OVF hydrographic database** (accessible at data.vizugy.hu since July 2024 as open data) provides the most authoritative river network and gauge information. OVF's data is based on field surveys and long-term monitoring rather than DEM extraction, making it an excellent validation source for DEM-derived networks. The OVF network covers all significant watercourses, including the many artificial channels (csatorna) that crisscross the Great Hungarian Plain -- channels that DEM-based methods often miss because they were excavated rather than formed by natural erosion.

### 11.8.3 HydroSHEDS: The Global Dataset

For global-scale analyses, or for any country lacking a national hydrographic dataset, **HydroSHEDS** (Hydrological Data and Maps Based on SHuttle Elevation Derivatives at multiple Scales) provides a seamless, worldwide drainage network and catchment dataset. Developed by Bernhard Lehner and colleagues at McGill University using SRTM data (and now being updated to Version 2 using the MERIT DEM), HydroSHEDS includes:

- **HydroRIVERS**: A vectorized global river network containing over 8 million river reaches, each with attributes for upstream drainage area, discharge estimates, stream order, and connectivity.
- **HydroBASINS**: A hierarchical set of watershed boundaries at 12 levels of subdivision, from continental basins (level 1) down to small headwater catchments (level 12). At the finest level, HydroBASINS contains approximately 1 million basins globally.
- **HydroLAKES**: A global database of 1.4 million lakes and reservoirs.

HydroSHEDS is freely available and has become the standard global reference for hydrological applications in data-sparse regions. For students working on watersheds in Asia, Africa, or the Middle East, where national hydrographic datasets may be incomplete or inaccessible, HydroSHEDS provides a consistent starting point.

### 11.8.4 Comparing DEM-Derived and Established Networks

When a watershed is delineated independently from a high-resolution DEM and compared to an established dataset like NHDPlus, EU-Hydro, or the OVF network, discrepancies are inevitable. Understanding these discrepancies is important for quality assurance and for judging the fitness of different datasets for different purposes.

**Boundary differences.** Watershed boundaries delineated from different DEM resolutions differ, particularly in flat terrain near drainage divides. On a ridgeline, the elevation difference between adjacent cells determines which way water flows; at 30-meter resolution, this difference may be within the DEM's noise level (the RMSE of NED is 2.34 meters; the Copernicus DEM's LE90 is less than 4 meters globally). Shifting one cell on a ridgeline can redirect thousands of downstream cells, producing boundary differences of hundreds of meters even though the DEMs are both "correct" within their error bounds.

**Drainage density differences.** A DEM-derived network with a fine threshold will show more streams than NHDPlus or EU-Hydro, which are based on coarser DEMs or cartographic editing. Conversely, established datasets may include streams (especially low-order streams in flat terrain) that DEM extraction misses because the channels are too subtle to appear in the DEM's elevation surface.

**Stream alignment differences.** DEM-derived streams follow the computed flow paths through the DEM grid. Cartographically mapped streams (as in NHD or OVF) follow the actual channel positions visible on aerial photographs or in field surveys. In mountainous terrain with well-defined valleys, the two align closely. In flat terrain, the DEM-derived path may deviate significantly from the actual channel -- especially for rivers that flow through broad floodplains where the topographic gradient is minimal.

These comparisons are not grounds for dismissing either approach. DEM-derived networks have the advantage of physical consistency -- every cell's flow direction connects to the stream network, and every catchment boundary follows the topographic divide. Cartographic networks have the advantage of positional accuracy -- the mapped stream is where the actual water flows. The ideal approach, implemented in NHDPlus's construction, combines both: using cartographic stream positions to "burn in" the DEM (Chapter 9), then deriving flow directions and catchments from the conditioned surface.

## 11.9 Practical Workflow: Logan River, Utah

### 11.9.1 Study Area and Data

The Logan River is a fourth-order stream in northern Utah that drains approximately 554 km$^2$ of the Bear River Range in the Wasatch Mountains before joining the Bear River near Logan, Utah. The USGS operates a stream gauge (Station 10109000, "Logan River near Logan, UT") at the canyon mouth, providing a well-documented outlet point with a known drainage area.

This watershed has been used extensively in terrain analysis research, particularly by David Tarboton and colleagues at Utah State University, who developed TauDEM on this landscape. It is an excellent teaching example because it combines steep mountain terrain (where channels are well-defined) with gentler foothill areas (where the channelization threshold matters), and because high-resolution DEM data (10-meter from 3DEP) is freely available.

### 11.9.2 Step-by-Step Workflow

The complete workflow proceeds as follows, using TauDEM tools (with ArcGIS Pro equivalents noted):

**Step 1: Obtain and prepare the DEM.** Download 1/3 arc-second (~10 m) DEM tiles from the USGS 3DEP (The National Map). For the Logan River, two or three tiles cover the watershed. Mosaic the tiles into a single raster and project to UTM Zone 12N at 10-meter cell size.

To determine the approximate study area extent before downloading DEM data, use the ArcGIS Portal ready-to-use Watershed tool (Section 11.10): click near the gauge location and obtain a preliminary watershed boundary. Buffer this boundary by 1 km to ensure the DEM fully covers the watershed.

**Step 2: Fill sinks.** Run TauDEM's **Pit Remove** tool (ArcGIS equivalent: **Fill**) to create a hydrologically conditioned DEM with no closed depressions. Examine the fill depth (filled DEM minus original DEM) to understand what was filled. Most fills are small (a few centimeters to a few meters) and represent DEM artifacts. Occasionally, a large fill reveals a real depression -- in the Logan River watershed, Peter Sinks is a karst depression 62 meters deep where the US record low temperature of $-56.3\,^\circ\text{C}$ ($-69.3\,^\circ\text{F}$) was recorded.

**Step 3: Compute flow directions and slopes.** Run TauDEM's **D8 Flow Directions** tool, producing the flow direction raster $p$ and the D8 slope raster $s_{d8}$. Optionally, run **D-infinity Flow Directions** for hillslope analysis (not needed for stream/watershed delineation but useful for slope-area analysis).

**Step 4: Compute contributing area.** Run TauDEM's **D8 Contributing Area** tool, producing the contributing area raster $a_{d8}$. At this stage, a quick sanity check: multiply the contributing area at the outlet cell (in number of cells) by the cell area ($100\,\text{m}^2$ for 10-meter cells) and compare to the known drainage area of 554 km$^2$. This verifies that the DEM extent is sufficient and that no major flow routing errors have occurred.

**Step 5: Snap the outlet.** The USGS gauge coordinates for Station 10109000 may not fall exactly on a DEM stream cell. Run TauDEM's **Move Outlets to Streams** tool (ArcGIS: **Snap Pour Point**) with a search distance of 500 meters. Verify by recomputing contributing area upstream of the snapped outlet: the result should be close to 554 km$^2$.

**Step 6: Define streams objectively.** Run TauDEM's **Peuker-Douglas Stream Definition** tool, which automates:
- The Peuker-Douglas curvature filter (identifying valley cells).
- Weighted flow accumulation of valley cells.
- Testing a range of thresholds (e.g., 15 logarithmically spaced values from 1,000 to 50,000 cells).
- Stream drop t-test at each threshold.
- Selection of the finest threshold with $|t| < 2$.

For the Logan River, the selected threshold is typically around 500--700 valley cells (the exact value depends on the DEM version), yielding a drainage density consistent with the observed channel network.

**Step 7: Delineate reaches and catchments.** Run TauDEM's **Stream Reach and Watershed** tool, which produces:
- A watershed raster (each cell assigned to its catchment).
- A stream network shapefile with attributes: link ID, upstream/downstream links, Strahler order, length, contributing area, elevation drop, slope, and distance to outlet.
- A watershed boundary polygon for the entire basin above the outlet.

**Step 8: Convert and analyze.** Convert the watershed raster to polygon shapefile. Join stream and catchment attributes through the common grid code. Compute summary statistics: total stream length, drainage density, mainstream length, bifurcation ratio, and other Horton parameters. Compare the derived network to NHDPlus for validation.

The entire workflow, from raw DEM tiles to complete vector stream network and catchment polygons, can be executed in approximately 15--30 minutes on a modern workstation for a watershed of this size. Chapter 12 describes how to automate this workflow using Python scripting, eliminating the need for manual tool execution.

## 11.10 Practical Workflow: The Sajo River, Hungary

### 11.10.1 Study Area and Motivation

To demonstrate that the same methods work in a completely different geographic and data context, we walk through the workflow for the **Sajo** (Sajó) **River**, a major left-bank tributary of the Tisza in northeastern Hungary. The Sajó rises in Slovakia (where it is called the Slaná), crosses into Hungary near Tornanádaska, and joins the Tisza at Tiszaújváros. Its Hungarian catchment covers approximately 12,700 km$^2$ (including the transboundary portion), making it a substantial fifth-order watershed that has experienced severe floods, most recently in 2010.

The Sajó is an instructive contrast to the Logan River. While the Logan drains steep, well-defined mountain terrain where DEM-based delineation works almost perfectly, the Sajó's watershed includes both the dissected Northern Hungarian Mountains (Bükk, Aggtelek Karst, Cserehát) and the flat alluvial lowlands along the Tisza. The flat areas challenge DEM-based flow routing because elevation gradients are small relative to DEM noise, and artificial channels are common.

Stream burning (Chapter 9) is more important for the Sajó than for the Logan, because the flat lowland areas require an imposed drainage network to prevent routing errors. The EU-Hydro river network or the OVF hydrographic database provides the vector stream lines for burning.

### 11.10.3 Key Differences in the Workflow

The processing steps are identical to those described for the Logan River (fill sinks, compute flow directions, compute contributing area, snap outlet, define streams, delineate catchments). The differences are practical rather than algorithmic:

**Coordinate system.** Hungarian data natively uses HD72/EOV (EPSG:23700). The Copernicus DEM arrives in WGS84 geographic coordinates. Before processing, all data must be projected to a common system. For consistency with Hungarian practice, EOV is convenient for the final products; for compatibility with EU/Copernicus data, ETRS89/UTM Zone 34N (EPSG:32634) is a practical choice. The transformation between HD72 and ETRS89 uses the 7-parameter Helmert transformation (EPSG:1449); for sub-meter accuracy, the Lechner grid shift file should be used.

**Flat terrain handling.** The Sajó's lower reaches cross the Tisza floodplain, where the terrain is almost perfectly flat over kilometers. DEM-based flow routing in such areas is unreliable without stream burning. The standard approach is to burn the OVF or EU-Hydro stream network into the DEM before filling sinks (Chapter 9), forcing flow to follow the known channel positions.

**Artificial channels.** The Great Hungarian Plain is crisscrossed with artificial drainage channels (öntöző- és belvízcsatornák) that were constructed over centuries to drain marshy lowlands. These channels carry water but do not follow natural topographic gradients -- they were excavated into the flat terrain. DEM-based methods typically cannot detect these channels. Including them in the analysis requires either burning them from the OVF network or accepting that the DEM-derived network will omit them in favor of the natural (pre-modification) drainage pattern.

**Transboundary issues.** The Sajó's headwaters are in Slovakia. For a complete watershed delineation, the DEM must extend into Slovak territory. The Copernicus DEM seamlessly covers both countries; the Hungarian DDM-5 does not. For transboundary studies, the Copernicus DEM is the natural choice. The OVF gauge at Felsőzsolca (near Miskolc) provides an outlet point for the Hungarian portion of the watershed; for the full international basin, gauge data can be obtained from the Slovak Hydrometeorological Institute (SHMU) or from GRDC.

### 11.10.4 Results and Validation

Applying the workflow to the Sajó watershed above Felsőzsolca using the Copernicus EEA-10 DEM at 10-meter resolution, with OVF stream burning and the Peuker-Douglas stream drop test, typically yields:

- A delineated drainage area within 2--3% of the OVF official value.
- A stream network with drainage density of approximately 0.4--0.6 km$^{-1}$ in the lowlands and 1.5--2.5 km$^{-1}$ in the Bükk Mountains, reflecting the Peuker-Douglas method's spatial adaptivity.
- Approximately 500--800 stream links (depending on threshold) organized as a 5th-order network with Horton ratios within expected ranges.

Comparing the DEM-derived network to the OVF cartographic network reveals good agreement in the mountainous areas and systematic differences in the lowlands, exactly as expected from the flat-terrain issues discussed above. The DEM-derived boundaries along the Tisza floodplain may differ by several hundred meters from the OVF boundaries, because the topographic gradient is too subtle for the DEM to resolve reliably. In such areas, the OVF network -- based on field surveys and historical drainage management -- is more authoritative.

This comparison reinforces an important practical lesson: DEM-derived delineation is powerful and efficient, but it is not infallible. In well-defined terrain, it produces results that rival or exceed cartographic accuracy. In flat terrain with engineered drainage, it must be supplemented with prior knowledge from field surveys and existing hydrographic datasets.

## 11.11 ArcGIS Ready-to-Use Watershed Tools

### 11.11.1 Cloud-Based Instant Delineation

For rapid, approximate watershed delineation anywhere in the world, ArcGIS Online and ArcGIS Pro provide **ready-to-use** geoprocessing tools that operate on pre-processed global DEMs stored in ESRI's cloud infrastructure:

- **Watershed**: Click a point on a map (or provide coordinates), and the service returns the watershed boundary polygon within seconds. No local DEM processing required.
- **Trace Downstream**: Click a point, and the service traces the flow path from that point downstream to the ocean or a terminal sink, showing the route water would follow.

These tools use globally pre-processed SRTM data -- 90-meter resolution worldwide, 30-meter in the United States -- combined with pre-computed flow direction and flow accumulation grids stored in ESRI's cloud. The heavy computation (DEM conditioning, flow direction, flow accumulation) was done once for the entire globe; the on-demand services simply query the pre-computed results.

The advantages are obvious: no data download, no local processing, instant results, global coverage. The limitations are equally clear: fixed resolution (you cannot use a higher-resolution national DEM), no control over processing parameters (threshold, burn-in, sink filling), and dependence on ESRI's cloud infrastructure and licensing.

For practical work, these tools serve two valuable purposes:

1. **Preliminary study area identification**: Before downloading DEM data and running local processing, use the ready-to-use Watershed tool to approximate the study area extent. This tells you which DEM tiles to download and how large a buffer to apply.

2. **Rapid reconnaissance**: When a question is "approximately how large is the watershed draining to this point?" rather than "exactly where is the watershed boundary cell by cell?", the cloud tools provide a useful answer in seconds rather than the hours that local processing might require.

ESRI demonstrated these tools on watersheds worldwide, including the Yangtze River basin in China, the Brahmaputra basin in India, and the Logan River basin in Utah. The results are approximate but informative -- good enough for planning, not precise enough for engineering design or regulatory compliance.

### 11.11.2 The Software-as-a-Service Trend

The ready-to-use tools exemplify a broader trend in geospatial computing: the shift from **desktop processing** (download data, process locally) to **cloud processing** (send request, receive result). This trend is driven by the growing size of datasets (a global 30-meter DEM occupies terabytes) and the decreasing cost of cloud computation relative to local hardware.

For hydroinformatics, this trend has profound implications. The terrain analysis workflow described in this chapter -- from DEM to catchments -- is computationally well-understood and algorithmically mature. Pre-computing it for the entire globe and serving results on demand converts a week-long local project into a five-second API call. Google Earth Engine offers similar capabilities for DEM processing through its cloud platform, as does Microsoft Planetary Computer.

The risk is the loss of methodological understanding. When the watershed appears at the click of a button, it is tempting to accept it uncritically. But the methods described in this chapter -- the stream drop test, the Peuker-Douglas filter, the drainage density analysis -- provide the knowledge needed to evaluate whether a pre-computed result is appropriate for a given application. The cloud delivers speed; understanding delivers quality.

## 11.12 Operational Forecasting Systems: From Catchments to Real-Time Predictions

### 11.12.1 The US National Water Model

The terrain analysis methods described in this chapter and the preceding chapters form the geographic backbone of the **National Water Model (NWM)**, operated by the National Water Center in Tuscaloosa, Alabama, under the auspices of NOAA's Office of Water Prediction. The NWM produces flow forecasts for every NHDPlus reach in the conterminous United States -- 2.7 million forecast points, updated every hour.

The NWM's computational architecture connects meteorological forcing to hydrological response through the catchment framework:

1. **Atmospheric forcing**: The High-Resolution Rapid Refresh (HRRR) weather model provides precipitation, temperature, and other climate inputs on a 3-km grid, updated hourly.

2. **Land surface hydrology**: The Noah-MP land surface model computes runoff generation per unit area, accounting for infiltration, soil moisture, evapotranspiration, snowmelt, and groundwater interactions. Noah-MP operates on the HRRR grid.

3. **Geographic transformation**: Runoff from each weather-model grid cell must be distributed to the NHDPlus catchments that the grid cell overlaps. This is an intersection-and-weighting operation -- conceptually the same as the Thiessen polygon averaging discussed in Chapter 8, but applied nationally in real time. Each catchment receives a weighted sum of runoff from the grid cells that overlap it, with weights proportional to the overlap area.

4. **Channel routing**: The accumulated runoff in each catchment is delivered to its stream link, and the RAPID (Routing Application for Parallel computation of Discharge) model propagates flow downstream through the NHDPlus network, computing discharge at every reach.

The result is a continuous, real-time picture of streamflow across the entire nation -- from small headwater creeks to major rivers. Before the NWM, flow forecasts were available only at approximately 4,000 USGS gauge locations. The NWM extends this to 2.7 million locations, providing flood forecasts even for ungauged streams where no monitoring station exists.

### 11.12.2 The European Flood Awareness System (EFAS)

Europe's counterpart to the NWM is the **European Flood Awareness System (EFAS)**, operated by the Copernicus Emergency Management Service at the European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts (ECMWF) in Reading, UK. EFAS provides flood forecasts across the entire European continent:

1. **Atmospheric forcing**: ECMWF's deterministic (HRES) and ensemble (ENS) weather forecasts provide precipitation and temperature inputs. EFAS also ingests weather model outputs from national meteorological services, including Hungary's ALADIN model operated by OMSZ (HungaroMet).

2. **Hydrological model**: The LISFLOOD distributed hydrological model computes runoff and routes flow through the European river network at 5-km resolution. LISFLOOD incorporates snowmelt, soil processes, and groundwater components.

3. **Stream network**: EFAS uses a river network derived from a pan-European DEM, with catchment delineation and routing topology functionally equivalent to NHDPlus. The network covers all major European river basins, including the Danube (of which Hungary's rivers are tributaries).

4. **Probabilistic forecasting**: Unlike the NWM's primarily deterministic approach, EFAS emphasizes **ensemble forecasting**. The 51-member ECMWF ensemble provides 51 different weather scenarios, each propagated through LISFLOOD to produce 51 possible river flow outcomes. The spread of the ensemble indicates forecast uncertainty: if 45 of 51 members predict flooding, confidence is high; if only 10 members predict flooding, the forecast is uncertain.

EFAS provides medium-range flood forecasts (up to 15 days ahead), flash flood indicators, seasonal outlooks, and soil moisture information. Real-time forecast data is available to EFAS partner organizations -- the national hydrological services of EU member states, including Hungary's OVF. Historical and archived data is publicly available.

For Hungary, EFAS complements the national forecasting system operated by OVF through HYDROINFO (hydroinfo.hu), which provides real-time water levels and forecasts for 98 sections of the Danube, Tisza, and Drava-Mura river systems. The Hungarian system benefits from dense national monitoring (OVF maintains a network of water level and discharge stations reporting in real time), while EFAS provides the broader European context and longer-range ensemble forecasts.

### 11.12.3 Global Forecasting: GloFAS

For regions beyond the US and Europe -- particularly relevant for students from Asia, the Middle East, and Africa -- the **Global Flood Awareness System (GloFAS)** extends EFAS's principles worldwide. GloFAS, also operated by Copernicus/ECMWF, provides flood forecasts at 0.05-degree resolution (~5 km) globally, using ERA5 atmospheric reanalysis and the LISFLOOD-OS hydrological model. GloFAS is freely accessible and provides flood watches and warnings for river basins worldwide, including those in data-sparse regions where national forecasting infrastructure may be limited.

The progression from NWM to EFAS to GloFAS illustrates how the terrain analysis concepts in this chapter scale from local to global. The methods are the same -- DEM conditioning, flow direction, flow accumulation, stream definition, catchment delineation, network routing -- only the scale and the input datasets change.

## 11.13 Edge Effects, Quality Control, and Practical Pitfalls

### 11.13.1 Boundary Discrepancies Between Resolutions

Every DEM has finite accuracy, and the consequences of that accuracy are most visible at watershed boundaries. The boundary between two adjacent watersheds follows a topographic ridgeline where the elevation gradient changes sign. On a ridgeline, the elevation difference between adjacent cells may be centimeters -- well within the DEM's error margin. At 30-meter resolution (NED RMSE of 2.34 m), or even at 10-meter resolution (3DEP RMSE of approximately 1 m), a single-cell error on a ridge can redirect thousands of downstream cells to the wrong watershed.

The practical consequence is that watershed boundaries delineated from different DEM resolutions will differ. Comparing a 10-meter and a 30-meter delineation of the same watershed typically shows discrepancies of 50--200 meters along the entire boundary perimeter, with occasional larger deviations at saddle points and in flat terrain. The discrepancy in total drainage area is usually less than 5%, which is acceptable for most hydrologic applications -- but it means that watershed boundary maps should always be accompanied by metadata indicating the DEM resolution from which they were derived.

### 11.13.2 Karst, Losing Streams, and Underground Drainage

DEM-based watershed delineation assumes that water flows downhill across the surface, following topographic gradients. This assumption fails in **karst terrain**, where water enters sinkholes, flows through underground conduits, and re-emerges at springs that may be in a completely different surface watershed. Hungary's Aggtelek Karst (a UNESCO World Heritage Site) is a prime example: surface water disappearing into dolines may resurface in springs draining to a different valley.

The DEM-based delineation of a karst watershed will show the **topographic** watershed -- the area that would drain to the outlet if all flow were surface flow. The actual **hydrological** watershed (including underground contributions) may be larger or smaller. For hydrologic modeling in karst terrain, the DEM-based delineation provides a starting point, but tracer studies, spring discharge measurements, and geological mapping are needed to define the true contributing area.

Similarly, **losing streams** (streams that lose water to the subsurface through their bed) violate the assumption that all upstream water reaches the downstream gauge. In such cases, the DEM-derived drainage area overestimates the effective contributing area.

### 11.13.3 Urban and Engineered Drainage

In urban areas, storm sewers and piped drainage networks completely bypass the surface topography. Water falling on a parking lot may enter a storm drain and be conveyed underground for kilometers before discharging to a stream that bears no topographic relationship to the parking lot's surface watershed. DEM-based delineation in urban areas produces the **pre-development** topographic watershed, which may differ substantially from the **engineered** drainage area.

The same issue arises with water transfers, inter-basin diversions, and irrigation canal systems. The Sajó watershed example (Section 11.10) highlighted the artificial channels on the Great Hungarian Plain. In heavily engineered landscapes, DEM-derived watersheds should be considered approximate and validated against engineering records of the drainage infrastructure.

### 11.13.4 Verification Checklist

When completing a watershed delineation, the following checks help ensure quality:

1. **Area check**: Compare the DEM-derived drainage area at the outlet to the known area from gauge records or established databases. Agreement within 5% is good; within 2% is excellent.

2. **Visual check**: Overlay the derived stream network on a satellite image or topographic map. Streams should follow visible valleys. Any stream crossing a ridge or flowing through a hilltop indicates a flow direction error.

3. **Horton analysis**: Compute bifurcation, length, and area ratios from the Strahler ordering. Ratios outside the typical ranges ($R_b$ = 3--5, $R_L$ = 1.5--3.5) may indicate threshold or DEM problems.

4. **Drainage density comparison**: Compare the DEM-derived drainage density to known values for similar terrain. Temperate mountain watersheds typically have $D_d$ = 1--3 km$^{-1}$; flat lowlands have $D_d$ = 0.3--0.8 km$^{-1}$; arid badlands may exceed 5 km$^{-1}$.

5. **Connectivity check**: Verify that every stream link connects to exactly one downstream link (or to the outlet). Disconnected networks indicate flow direction errors or unfilled sinks.

6. **Boundary comparison**: If a reference watershed boundary exists (from NHDPlus, EU-Hydro, OVF, or HydroBASINS), overlay it with the DEM-derived boundary and examine discrepancies. Large discrepancies in well-defined terrain indicate problems; expected discrepancies in flat terrain can be documented and accepted.

## 11.14 From Delineation to Modeling: The Bridge to Chapter 12

The output of the workflow described in this chapter -- a segmented stream network with attributes, a set of catchment polygons with their associated stream links, an outlet point snapped to the network, and a flow direction grid connecting everything -- constitutes the **spatial skeleton** of a distributed hydrologic model. Every catchment becomes a model element that receives rainfall, generates runoff, and delivers it to the stream network. Every stream link becomes a routing element that conveys flow downstream. The connectivity table links these elements into a complete computational network.

This spatial skeleton is the starting point for all subsequent hydrologic analysis: computing rainfall-runoff using unit hydrograph methods or process-based models, routing flow through the stream network using kinematic wave or Muskingum methods, estimating flood peaks from design storms, and producing flood inundation maps by combining channel flow with floodplain topography. The quality of the spatial skeleton -- the accuracy of the stream positions, the appropriateness of the drainage density, the correctness of the catchment boundaries, the completeness of the connectivity -- determines the quality of everything built upon it.

Chapter 12 takes the next step: automating this entire workflow using Python scripting, so that the journey from raw DEM to complete hydrologic model input can be executed repeatably, efficiently, and without manual intervention. The automation is not just a convenience -- it ensures reproducibility (the same input always produces the same output), enables sensitivity analysis (varying the threshold and examining the effect on model results), and makes the workflow scalable from a single small watershed to a national or continental domain.
