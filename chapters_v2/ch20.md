# Chapter 20: Building a Picture of the Underground

The previous two chapters introduced the fundamental building blocks of subsurface GIS: the three-dimensional coordinate systems and data structures that allow us to represent geology in a computer (Chapter 18), and the wells, boreholes, and aquifer maps that provide our primary observational window into the subsurface (Chapter 19). But scattered borehole logs and isolated well records do not, by themselves, tell us how geological layers connect, where aquifers thicken or thin, or how confining units channel groundwater flow across a basin. To answer those questions, we must assemble the individual observations into a coherent spatial framework -- a hydrogeologic model that describes the three-dimensional arrangement of rock units, their boundaries, and their hydraulic properties across an entire region. This chapter is about that assembly process.

The task is fundamentally one of interpolation and interpretation. We have point data -- formation tops recorded at boreholes, geophysical logs, outcrop observations -- and we need to create continuous surfaces, cross-sections, and ultimately three-dimensional volumes that represent the subsurface between and beyond those points. The challenge is not merely geometric. Every interpolation embeds geological judgment: the decision that two formation picks at neighboring wells belong to the same stratigraphic horizon, the assumption that a confining layer is continuous rather than faulted, the choice of which interpolation algorithm best represents the depositional environment. Hydroinformatics provides the tools and data structures for this work, but the geological thinking remains irreducibly human.

We begin with the concept of a hydrogeologic framework and the classification of hydrogeologic units. We then examine the spatial data structures -- GeoArea, GeoSection, GeoVolume, and GeoRasters -- that represent these units at progressively higher dimensions. The creation of cross-sections receives detailed treatment, including the XS2D projection system that translates three-dimensional geology onto a two-dimensional viewing plane. We cover the interpolation workflow from borehole picks to continuous surfaces and volumes, and we close with an extended Hungarian case study: the hydrostratigraphy of the Pannonian Basin and the Great Hungarian Plain thermal aquifer system, one of the most important and complex subsurface frameworks in Central Europe.

## 20.1 The Hydrogeologic Framework Concept

### 20.1.1 From Geology to Hydrogeology

The starting point for any groundwater investigation is a geological framework -- a description of what materials exist underground, how they are arranged, and where they extend. But geology alone is not enough for water management. A geologist might distinguish dozens of named formations based on age, lithology, and fossil content. A hydrogeologist, by contrast, cares about how those formations behave with respect to water: which ones transmit it freely, which ones retard it, and which ones store it. The hydrogeologic framework is therefore a simplification of the geological framework, one that groups and reclassifies rock units according to their hydraulic properties rather than their depositional history.

This reclassification can be dramatic. In the Edwards Aquifer of south-central Texas, for example, ten distinct stratigraphic formations identified by geologists are collapsed into just four hydrogeologic units for groundwater modeling purposes. The Georgetown Formation, the Cyclic and Marine Member, the Leached and Collapsed Member, and the Regional Dense Member -- each with its own lithological character -- are grouped into two broader hydrogeologic units (the Person and Kainer Formations) because their combined hydraulic behavior is what matters for understanding aquifer recharge and flow. Below them, the Upper Glen Rose limestone acts as a confining unit regardless of its internal stratigraphic subdivisions.

The same principle applies everywhere. In the Great Hungarian Plain, Quaternary and Upper Pannonian sediments that a stratigrapher would subdivide into numerous formations based on grain size gradation and fossil assemblages are typically grouped into three or four hydrogeologic units for thermal water management: a shallow unconfined aquifer in Quaternary sands and gravels, an upper confined aquifer in Upper Pannonian deltaic sands, a confining complex of Pannonian clays and marls, and a deep thermal aquifer in Lower Pannonian turbiditic sandstones. The geological detail is not lost -- it informs the classification -- but the hydrogeologic framework is what drives the modeling and management decisions.

### 20.1.2 What a Framework Must Contain

A complete hydrogeologic framework answers several related questions. First, what are the units? Each unit must be named, coded, and described in terms of its hydraulic role: is it an aquifer, a confining unit, or an aquitard? Second, how are the units arranged vertically? The depositional sequence matters because it determines which unit lies above or below another, and this layering controls the vertical movement of groundwater. Third, what is the spatial extent of each unit? Some units are laterally continuous across hundreds of kilometers; others pinch out, are faulted, or are eroded away. Fourth, what are the three-dimensional boundaries? The top and bottom surfaces of each unit define its geometry, and the intersection of those surfaces defines the contacts between units.

In data model terms, these questions translate into a set of linked representations. A table defines the conceptual units (names, codes, hydraulic classifications). Polygon features define the two-dimensional extent of units in map view. Cross-section features show the vertical arrangement along defined transects. Raster surfaces define the continuous top and bottom elevations of each unit. And volume features represent the full three-dimensional geometry. All of these representations describe the same set of conceptual units, and they are linked through a common identifier so that querying a unit in one representation automatically connects to the same unit in every other representation.

### 20.1.3 The HydrogeologicUnit Classification Table

The central organizing element of the hydrogeologic framework is the HydrogeologicUnit table, a tabular representation that defines each unit conceptually before any spatial features are created. This table is not a spatial dataset -- it contains no geometry -- but it is the master reference to which all spatial datasets point.

**Table 20.1**: Attributes of the HydrogeologicUnit table

| Attribute | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| HydroID | Unique identifier within the geodatabase; the key used for all internal relationships |
| HGUCode | Permanent identification code for the unit, used for linking with external information systems |
| HGUName | Human-readable name of the unit, used for labeling and map symbology |
| AquiferID | Groups hydrogeologic units that belong to the same aquifer or aquifer system |
| AqCode | Text descriptor of the parent aquifer, used for labeling and queries |
| Description | Free-text field for detailed descriptions of the unit's lithology, hydraulic properties, and significance |
| HorizonID | Integer index defining the unit's position in the vertical depositional sequence |

The HorizonID attribute deserves particular attention. It is an integer that defines where each unit sits in the vertical stack, numbered from bottom to top. The lowest unit in the depositional sequence receives the smallest HorizonID (typically 0 or 1), and each successively higher unit receives a larger value. This convention is not arbitrary: the algorithms that build cross-sections and volumes from surfaces process the layers from bottom to top, filling the space between successive horizons. If the HorizonIDs are assigned incorrectly, the resulting cross-sections will show layers in the wrong order, and volume calculations will produce nonsensical results.

Consider a practical example. Suppose we are modeling a basin with four hydrogeologic units: a basal confining unit (clay), a lower aquifer (sandstone), an intermediate confining unit (shale), and an upper aquifer (limestone). The HydrogeologicUnit table might look like this:

**Table 20.2**: Example HydrogeologicUnit entries for a four-layer basin

| HydroID | HGUCode | HGUName | AquiferID | AqCode | HorizonID |
|---------|---------|---------|-----------|--------|-----------|
| 101 | CLAY_BASE | Basal Clay | -- | -- | 0 |
| 102 | SAND_LOW | Lower Sand Aquifer | 1001 | AQ_LOWER | 1 |
| 103 | SHALE_MID | Middle Shale Confining | -- | -- | 2 |
| 104 | LIME_UP | Upper Limestone Aquifer | 1002 | AQ_UPPER | 3 |

Note that units 102 and 104 each have an AquiferID linking them to polygon features that define the mapped extent of their respective aquifers. Units 101 and 103 are confining units not associated with a named aquifer, so their AquiferID is null. All four units have HorizonIDs in ascending order from bottom (0) to top (3). This simple table is the foundation upon which all spatial representations will be built.

## 20.2 Spatial Representations of Hydrogeologic Units

### 20.2.1 GeoArea: The Two-Dimensional Footprint

The simplest spatial representation of a hydrogeologic unit is a two-dimensional polygon defining its areal extent. In the Arc Hydro Groundwater data model, these polygons are stored as GeoArea features. Each GeoArea polygon carries a HGUID attribute that links it to its definition in the HydrogeologicUnit table, along with an HGUCode for labeling and a HorizonID that echoes the unit's position in the vertical sequence.

It is important to distinguish GeoArea features from geological outcrop maps. A geological outcrop map (stored as GeologyArea features in the data model, as discussed in Chapter 18) shows where rock units are exposed at the surface. A GeoArea feature is more conceptual: it represents the full horizontal extent of a hydrogeologic unit, including areas where the unit exists in the subsurface beneath other formations. The Edwards Aquifer, for instance, crops out in a narrow band along the Balcones Fault Zone in Texas, but its confined zone extends tens of kilometers to the south beneath younger formations. The GeoArea feature for the Edwards Aquifer would encompass both the outcrop zone and the confined zone, representing the entire area within which the aquifer exists as a distinct hydrogeologic entity.

A single hydrogeologic unit can be represented by multiple GeoArea polygons. This is necessary when a unit is spatially discontinuous -- broken by faults, eroded in some areas, or present as isolated bodies within a larger matrix. Each polygon has its own unique HydroID (its identity as a feature in the geodatabase), but all polygons representing the same unit share the same HGUID and HGUCode. This many-to-one relationship between features and conceptual units is a recurring pattern throughout the hydrostratigraphy data model: the physical complexity of the subsurface means that a single conceptual unit may require many spatial features to represent it accurately.

In practice, GeoArea features are delineated using a combination of sources: geological maps showing outcrop patterns, borehole data showing where formations are encountered in the subsurface, geophysical surveys showing the lateral extent of reflectors, and geological judgment about the likely continuation of units between control points. In the European context, the INSPIRE directive's geology data theme provides a standardized framework for representing geological units as polygons, and national geological surveys -- such as the Mining and Geological Survey of Hungary (MBFSZ, now part of SZTFH -- see Chapter 19, Section 19.1.4) or the British Geological Survey -- publish digital geological maps that can serve as the starting point for GeoArea delineation (see Chapter 19, Section 19.5 for a detailed discussion of geologic map databases).

### 20.2.2 GeoSection and SectionLine: Cross-Sections in Three Dimensions

Cross-sections are the geologist's most powerful tool for visualizing the vertical arrangement of subsurface units. A cross-section slices through the earth along a defined line and shows the layered structure beneath the surface, much as cutting through a layered cake reveals its internal composition. In hydrogeologic frameworks, cross-sections serve both as visualization products and as intermediate steps in building three-dimensional models.

The data model uses two feature classes to represent cross-sections. SectionLine is a polyline feature class that defines the trace of the cross-section on a map -- the line along which the earth is conceptually "cut." Each SectionLine feature carries a HydroID, a human-readable name (SName, typically something like "A-A'" or "Section 1"), and a vertical exaggeration value (VertExag2D) that will be applied when the cross-section is displayed in two dimensions.

GeoSection is a multipatch feature class that stores the three-dimensional panels of the cross-section itself. Each GeoSection feature represents one hydrogeologic unit along one section line. A complete cross-section through four hydrogeologic units, for example, would consist of four GeoSection features, each representing a different unit. The relationship between SectionLine and GeoSection is one-to-many: one section line has many panels. This relationship is established through the SectionID attribute of GeoSection features, which equals the HydroID of the associated SectionLine feature.

**Table 20.3**: Key attributes of GeoSection features

| Attribute | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| HydroID | Unique identifier for this panel feature |
| SectionID | HydroID of the associated SectionLine feature |
| SName | Name of the cross-section (e.g., "A-A'") |
| HGUID | Links this panel to a hydrogeologic unit in the HydrogeologicUnit table |
| HGUCode | Text code for the hydrogeologic unit |
| HorizonID | Depositional sequence index for this unit |
| FType | Distinguishes between panel types (e.g., interpolated vs. observed) |

Creating cross-sections typically follows one of two approaches. The first and more traditional method is to draw section lines connecting a sequence of wells that have borehole log data. The hydrostratigraphic intervals recorded at each well are then connected between adjacent wells to form the panels. This is essentially the digital equivalent of what geologists have done on paper for centuries: drawing lines between known formation contacts at neighboring boreholes, using geological judgment to interpolate the layer boundaries between them. In the geodatabase, BoreLine features (three-dimensional representations of borehole stratigraphy) are connected to form GeoSection multipatch features.

The second approach uses interpolated surfaces rather than direct borehole connections. For each hydrogeologic unit, a raster surface representing the top elevation of the formation is created by interpolating borehole picks across the study area. Cross-sections are then generated by sampling these rasters at regular intervals along the section line and constructing panels from the sampled elevations. This approach produces smoother cross-sections and is more consistent with the continuous surfaces used in subsequent modeling, but it introduces the uncertainties inherent in the interpolation process. In many projects, both approaches are used: direct borehole connections provide validated cross-sections at well-controlled transects, while surface-based cross-sections extend the framework into areas with sparse data.

### 20.2.3 GeoVolume: Three-Dimensional Solids

While cross-sections provide views along specific transects and surfaces define the boundaries of units, GeoVolume features represent the full three-dimensional geometry of hydrogeologic units as solid objects. Each GeoVolume feature is a multipatch that defines a closed volume of space corresponding to a single hydrogeologic unit or a portion thereof. Like all spatial features in the hydrostratigraphy component, GeoVolume features carry HGUID and HGUCode attributes linking them to the HydrogeologicUnit table.

GeoVolume features are the most complete spatial representation of subsurface units, and they are the form most directly usable for volumetric calculations -- estimating the total volume of an aquifer, for instance, or calculating the volume of rock available for storage. They are typically constructed from the bounding surfaces: the top surface of a unit and the top surface of the unit below it (which serves as the bottom boundary). The space between these two surfaces, clipped to the lateral extent defined by GeoArea polygons, defines the volume.

Constructing GeoVolume features is computationally intensive, especially for complex geometries with faults, pinch-outs, and unconformities. The process typically involves converting raster surfaces to triangulated irregular networks (TINs), building the closed solid geometry from the upper and lower TIN surfaces and the lateral boundaries, and then storing the result as a multipatch feature. Modern GIS platforms and specialized geological modeling software (such as Leapfrog, GOCAD/SKUA, or GemPy) provide automated tools for this construction, but the results always require careful quality checking against the original borehole data and geological cross-sections.

The visualization of GeoVolume features in a three-dimensional scene provides perhaps the most intuitive understanding of subsurface structure. Viewers can rotate, slice, and query the model, selecting individual units to see their geometry, examining how they stack and interact, and identifying areas where units thin, thicken, or disappear. For stakeholder communication -- explaining to water managers or municipal planners why a particular well field produces warm water, or why contamination might migrate in a particular direction -- these three-dimensional visualizations are invaluable.

### 20.2.4 GeoRasters: Raster Catalogs for Formation Surfaces

Raster surfaces play a dual role in the hydrogeologic framework. They represent the boundaries of hydrogeologic units (formation tops and bottoms) as continuous grids, and they represent the spatially varying hydraulic properties (conductivity, transmissivity, porosity, specific yield) of those units. The GeoRasters raster catalog provides a structured container for managing these raster datasets within the geodatabase.

A **raster catalog** is not a single raster; it is a collection of raster datasets stored together with attributes that describe each one. Each row in the GeoRasters catalog represents one raster dataset and carries attributes identifying which hydrogeologic unit it describes (HGUID, HGUCode), what it represents (formation top, formation bottom, hydraulic conductivity, etc.), what units it uses (feet above sea level, meters per day, etc.), and where it falls in the depositional sequence (HorizonID).

**Table 20.4**: Attributes of the GeoRasters raster catalog

| Attribute | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| Name | Name of the raster dataset |
| Description | What the raster represents (e.g., "top of formation," "hydraulic conductivity") |
| RasUnits | Units of the raster values (e.g., "meters above sea level," "m/day") |
| HGUID | Links this raster to a hydrogeologic unit in the HydrogeologicUnit table |
| HGUCode | Text code of the associated hydrogeologic unit |
| HorizonID | Depositional sequence index |
| AquiferID | Links this raster to an Aquifer feature |
| AqCode | Text code of the associated aquifer |

The power of the raster catalog becomes apparent when you need to process the data programmatically. Because the rasters are indexed with HorizonIDs, an algorithm can iterate through them in depositional order -- from the deepest unit to the shallowest -- building up a three-dimensional model layer by layer. Cross-sections can be generated by sampling each raster along a section line, with the HorizonID ensuring that the layers are processed in the correct vertical order. Volume features can be constructed by filling the space between successive horizon surfaces. Without the catalog's indexing attributes, each of these operations would require the user to manually identify and sequence the rasters -- a tedious and error-prone process when dealing with frameworks that contain dozens of units.

Consider a typical raster catalog for a framework with four hydrogeologic units. The catalog might contain eight rasters: four representing formation tops and four representing formation bottoms. However, because the bottom of each unit is the top of the unit below it, the catalog can be simplified to five rasters: four formation tops plus one basal surface. The HorizonID values ensure that the software processes these surfaces in the correct order, from the base (HorizonID = 0) upward through each successive formation top.

## 20.3 The XS2D Projection System

### 20.3.1 The Problem of Displaying Vertical Sections

Three-dimensional cross-sections stored as GeoSection multipatch features can be displayed in a 3D scene viewer, where the user can rotate and examine the geology from any angle. But for many purposes -- printed reports, regulatory submissions, detailed stratigraphic correlation -- a traditional two-dimensional cross-section drawing is required. This presents a fundamental display challenge: the cross-section line on the map may curve, bend, or zigzag between boreholes, but on the printed page it must appear as a flat, horizontal axis with depth increasing downward.

The XS2D (Cross-Section 2D) system solves this problem by defining a local coordinate transformation for each section line. The three-dimensional coordinates of features along the cross-section are projected onto a two-dimensional plane where the horizontal axis represents distance along the section line (measured from its starting point) and the vertical axis represents elevation. If the section line curves through space, the curved path is "unfolded" so that the total distance along it maps to a straight horizontal axis. Additionally, a vertical exaggeration factor can be applied to stretch the vertical dimension, making thin layers more visible -- a common need in hydrogeologic cross-sections where units may be only meters thick but extend for kilometers laterally.

### 20.3.2 The Coordinate Transformation

The mathematical transformation underlying XS2D is straightforward. Consider a SectionLine feature as a polyline in map coordinates, with vertices at positions $(x_1, y_1), (x_2, y_2), \ldots, (x_n, y_n)$. The distance along the section line from its starting point to any point $P$ on the line is the cumulative arc length:

$$s(P) = \sum_{i=1}^{k-1} \sqrt{(x_{i+1} - x_i)^2 + (y_{i+1} - y_i)^2} + d(P, V_k)$$

where $V_k$ is the last vertex before $P$ and $d(P, V_k)$ is the distance from that vertex to $P$ along the line segment. For a three-dimensional point near the section line with coordinates $(x, y, z)$, the XS2D coordinates are:

$$s_{2D} = s(P_{nearest})$$
$$z_{2D} = z \times V_e$$

where $P_{nearest}$ is the point on the section line closest to $(x, y)$ and $V_e$ is the vertical exaggeration factor. The transformation thus collapses the two horizontal dimensions into a single distance measure and scales the vertical dimension.

For borehole data projected onto a cross-section, the transformation also records additional information: the offset distance from the borehole to the nearest point on the section line (so that boreholes not exactly on the line can still be projected), the measure (distance along the line) at which the projection occurs, and whether the borehole is to the left or right of the section line (as determined by the direction of digitization). This metadata is stored in the XS2D_BoreLine feature class and is essential for understanding how far each data point has been projected and whether the projection might introduce significant geometric distortion.

### 20.3.3 XS2D Feature Classes

The XS2D system uses a family of feature classes, each representing a different element of the two-dimensional cross-section view. These feature classes are stored in the geodatabase alongside the three-dimensional features, but they are displayed in a separate map data frame with its own coordinate system.

**XS2D_Panel** is a polygon feature class representing hydrogeologic units as two-dimensional panels on the cross-section. Each panel corresponds to one hydrogeologic unit along one section line, analogous to the three-dimensional GeoSection features but projected and flattened for two-dimensional display. The panels are attributed with HGUID and HGUCode for linking to the HydrogeologicUnit table and for symbology.

**XS2D_BoreLine** is a polyline feature class representing borehole data projected onto the cross-section plane. Each feature corresponds to one stratigraphic interval at one borehole, showing its position along the section line (horizontal axis) and its depth range (vertical axis). The attributes include WellID (linking to the Well feature), TopElev and BottomElev (the vertical extent of the interval), Material (the lithological description), and HGUID/HGUCode (the hydrogeologic classification). The Offset attribute records the distance from the borehole to the section line, allowing the user to assess the reliability of the projection -- a borehole very close to the section line provides more reliable information than one projected from a considerable distance.

**XS2D_PanelDivider** is a polyline feature class showing vertical lines at locations where the section line changes direction. These dividers help orient the viewer: when looking at a flattened cross-section, it is useful to know where bends in the original section line occur, since the geology on either side of a bend may be influenced by different structural or depositional processes.

**XS2D_MajorGrid** and **XS2D_MinorGrid** are polyline feature classes representing coordinate grid lines on the cross-section. Because vertical exaggeration distorts the coordinate system, the standard ArcMap coordinate display does not show "true" elevations. The grid lines provide a reference frame with correct elevation values labeled on the vertical axis and distance values on the horizontal axis. Major grid lines typically span the full extent of the cross-section, while minor grid lines appear as tick marks along the borders.

**XS2D_Catalog** is a management table that tracks which XS2D feature classes are associated with which section lines. Because a geodatabase may contain many section lines, each with its own set of XS2D features, the catalog provides a directory of all the components. Each row records the name of the SectionLine feature class, the SectionID (HydroID of the specific section line), the name of the XS2D feature class, and the role that feature class plays (Panel, BoreLine, Grid, etc.).

### 20.3.4 Working with Vertical Exaggeration

Vertical exaggeration is almost always necessary in hydrogeologic cross-sections. A typical aquifer system might span 50 kilometers laterally but only 500 meters vertically. At true scale, the cross-section would appear as an almost imperceptibly thin strip -- the layers would be too thin to see, and the geological structure would be invisible. A vertical exaggeration of 25x or 50x stretches the vertical dimension so that layer thicknesses become visible and structural features like dips, faults, and pinch-outs become apparent.

The vertical exaggeration value is stored as an attribute (VertExag2D) on the SectionLine feature, ensuring that all XS2D features associated with a given section line use the same exaggeration. This is important for consistency: if boreholes and panels were displayed with different exaggerations on the same cross-section, the result would be visually incoherent.

However, vertical exaggeration introduces a well-known distortion: dip angles are exaggerated. A layer that dips at 2 degrees in reality will appear to dip at approximately $\arctan(V_e \times \tan(2°))$ in the cross-section. With a vertical exaggeration of 25x, a true 2-degree dip appears as approximately 41 degrees -- a dramatic overstatement that can mislead viewers unfamiliar with the convention. For this reason, cross-sections with vertical exaggeration must always state the exaggeration factor prominently, and any quantitative measurements of dip or slope must be corrected back to true scale before use. The grid lines provided by the XS2D_MajorGrid and XS2D_MinorGrid features help viewers maintain awareness of the true dimensions.

A worked example clarifies the effect. Suppose a confining clay layer has a true thickness of 15 meters and dips at 1.5 degrees across a 10-kilometer section. At true scale, the layer would be 15 meters thick on a plot 10,000 meters wide -- essentially invisible. With a vertical exaggeration of $V_e = 30$:

- Apparent thickness: $15 \times 30 = 450$ display units (clearly visible on the plot)
- Apparent dip angle: $\arctan(30 \times \tan(1.5°)) = \arctan(30 \times 0.0262) = \arctan(0.786) \approx 38.2°$

The layer that dips gently at 1.5 degrees now appears to plunge at 38 degrees -- a factor that must be communicated to anyone interpreting the cross-section.

## 20.4 Interpolation from Boreholes to Surfaces and Volumes

### 20.4.1 The Interpolation Challenge

The transition from point observations to continuous surfaces is the most technically demanding step in building a hydrogeologic framework. We have formation picks at scattered borehole locations -- the elevation of each formation top at each well -- and we need to create a continuous raster surface that represents that formation top across the entire study area. Between the boreholes, we have no direct observations, and the surface must be inferred.

This is a spatial interpolation problem, and hydrogeology presents it in a particularly challenging form. The data points are unevenly distributed (wells are drilled where water is needed or where geology is interesting, not on a regular grid). The surfaces being interpolated are not smooth -- they may have faults, unconformities, and rapid changes in gradient. The number of data points is often small relative to the area being covered. And the consequences of interpolation errors are significant: an incorrectly placed formation top can lead to a well being drilled to the wrong depth, a confining layer being modeled as absent where it is actually present, or a contamination pathway being missed.

### 20.4.2 Common Interpolation Methods

Several interpolation methods are commonly used for hydrostratigraphic surfaces, each with distinct strengths and limitations. The choice of method depends on the data density, the expected behavior of the surface, and the computational resources available.

**Inverse Distance Weighting (IDW)** is one of the simplest methods. It estimates the value at an unknown point as a weighted average of nearby known values, with the weights decreasing as the distance increases. The decay is controlled by a power parameter $p$:

$$\hat{z}(x_0) = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i \cdot z_i}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i}, \quad w_i = \frac{1}{d_i^p}$$

where $z_i$ is the known value at point $i$, $d_i$ is the distance from the prediction point $x_0$ to point $i$, and $p$ is typically 2. IDW is fast and intuitive, but it tends to create "bull's-eye" patterns around data points and cannot extrapolate beyond the data range. It also ignores any directional trends or anisotropy in the data.

**Kriging** is a geostatistical method that not only interpolates but also provides an estimate of the interpolation uncertainty at each point. It begins by computing a variogram -- a function that describes how the variance between pairs of points changes with distance -- and then uses this variogram to determine optimal weights for interpolation. The kriging predictor is:

$$\hat{z}(x_0) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \lambda_i \cdot z_i$$

where the weights $\lambda_i$ are chosen to minimize the prediction variance. Kriging has several advantages for hydrostratigraphic interpolation: it can incorporate directional anisotropy (if a formation dips preferentially in one direction), it honors the data values exactly (for ordinary kriging), and it provides a kriging variance map that shows where the interpolation is uncertain. The main disadvantage is computational cost and the need to fit an appropriate variogram model, which requires expertise and sufficient data.

**Spline interpolation** fits a smooth mathematical surface through the data points, minimizing the total curvature of the surface. Regularized splines produce smoother surfaces (appropriate for gradually varying formation tops), while tension splines produce surfaces that more closely follow abrupt changes. Splines are effective for moderately smooth geological surfaces but can produce unrealistic oscillations between data points, especially when data are sparse or irregularly distributed.

**Natural Neighbor interpolation** uses Voronoi tessellation to determine influence regions for each data point and assigns weights based on the area of overlap between the Voronoi cell of the prediction point and the cells of the data points. It is well-suited for irregularly distributed data and produces smooth, artifact-free surfaces, but it cannot extrapolate beyond the convex hull of the data points.

### 20.4.3 The Borehole-to-Surface Workflow

The practical workflow for creating formation surfaces from borehole data involves several steps, each requiring both technical skill and geological judgment.

**Step 1: Extract formation picks.** The BoreholeLog table (described in Chapter 19) stores stratigraphic observations at each well. For each formation of interest, the relevant picks are extracted -- typically the elevation of the formation top at each well location. This extraction produces a set of point features with known $(x, y, z)$ coordinates, where $z$ is the formation top elevation.

**Step 2: Quality control.** Not all formation picks are equally reliable. Some wells have detailed geologist's logs based on core samples; others have only driller's logs with imprecise formation identifications. Some picks may be misidentified, miscoded, or affected by faulting. Quality control involves reviewing the picks for consistency -- checking that formation tops are in the correct vertical order at each well, that elevations are reasonable given the regional geological structure, and that no obvious outliers are present. Statistical outlier detection (values more than three standard deviations from the regional trend) can help identify suspect data, but geological judgment is essential because apparent outliers may reflect real structural features such as faults.

**Step 3: Choose the interpolation method.** The choice depends on the data density, the expected surface character, and the project requirements. For dense datasets with relatively smooth surfaces, IDW or natural neighbor interpolation may suffice. For sparse datasets or surfaces with directional trends, kriging is usually preferred because it provides uncertainty estimates and can incorporate anisotropy. For surfaces with known faults, specialized methods that honor fault discontinuities are needed.

**Step 4: Interpolate.** The selected method is applied to produce a raster surface covering the study area. The raster cell size should be chosen based on the data density and the intended use: a 100-meter cell size might be appropriate for a regional framework, while a 10-meter cell size might be needed for a site-scale model. Interpolation beyond the data extent (extrapolation) should be approached cautiously, and the resulting surface should be clipped to the GeoArea polygon defining the unit's lateral extent.

**Step 5: Validate.** The interpolated surface is compared against the original data points and any independent validation data (boreholes not used in the interpolation). Cross-validation -- removing each point in turn, interpolating from the remaining points, and comparing the predicted value to the known value -- provides a quantitative measure of interpolation accuracy. The root mean square error (RMSE) and mean absolute error (MAE) are commonly reported.

**Step 6: Store in the GeoRasters catalog.** The validated raster is loaded into the GeoRasters catalog with appropriate attributes: the HGUID and HGUCode of the associated unit, the HorizonID, a description of what the raster represents, and the units of the values.

### 20.4.4 From Surfaces to Volumes

Once all formation surfaces are in the GeoRasters catalog, the construction of three-dimensional volumes follows logically. Each hydrogeologic unit occupies the space between two bounding surfaces: its top surface and the top surface of the unit below it (which serves as the bottom). The basal unit of the framework requires an additional surface for its bottom boundary, which may be the top of the crystalline basement or the base of the modeled domain.

The volume of a hydrogeologic unit can be estimated by computing the difference between the top and bottom rasters, cell by cell. If the top surface raster has value $z_{top}(i,j)$ at cell $(i,j)$ and the bottom surface raster has value $z_{bot}(i,j)$, then the thickness at that cell is:

$$h(i,j) = z_{top}(i,j) - z_{bot}(i,j)$$

and the total volume of the unit is:

$$V = \sum_{i,j} h(i,j) \times \Delta x \times \Delta y$$

where $\Delta x$ and $\Delta y$ are the cell dimensions. For a raster with 100-meter cells covering an area of 50 km by 30 km, this sum involves 150,000 cells -- trivial for modern computers but representing a calculation that would have been impractical before the era of digital GIS.

A worked example illustrates the process. Suppose we have interpolated the top of a confined sand aquifer and the top of the underlying clay basement across a 20 km by 15 km study area, using 50-meter raster cells. At a randomly selected cell, the aquifer top is at 145.3 meters above sea level and the clay top is at 112.7 meters above sea level, giving a local thickness of 32.6 meters. Across the entire study area (400 by 300 cells = 120,000 cells), the average thickness is 28.4 meters. The estimated aquifer volume is:

$$V = 28.4 \text{ m} \times 50 \text{ m} \times 50 \text{ m} \times 120{,}000 = 8{,}520{,}000{,}000 \text{ m}^3 \approx 8.52 \text{ km}^3$$

If the effective porosity of the sand is 0.25, the total groundwater storage capacity is approximately $8.52 \times 0.25 = 2.13$ km$^3$, or about 2,130 million cubic meters. This kind of calculation -- combining spatial surfaces with hydrogeologic parameters -- is at the heart of groundwater resource assessment and is only possible once the framework surfaces have been properly constructed and validated.

## 20.5 Building a Complete Hydrogeologic Framework: Step by Step

### 20.5.1 The Implementation Workflow

The individual data structures and methods described in the preceding sections come together in a systematic workflow for building a complete hydrogeologic framework. While the details vary with each project, the general sequence is consistent.

**Step 1: Define the conceptual model.** Before creating any spatial data, establish the set of hydrogeologic units that will structure the framework. This requires reviewing the regional geology, examining existing geological maps and borehole data, and deciding how to group stratigraphic units into hydrogeologic categories. The result is a populated HydrogeologicUnit table with HorizonIDs assigned in the correct depositional order.

**Step 2: Create the geodatabase structure.** Set up the feature classes (GeoArea, GeoSection, GeoVolume, SectionLine) and the GeoRasters catalog. Add any project-specific attributes needed beyond the standard data model fields. Document the schema changes for reproducibility.

**Step 3: Delineate GeoArea polygons.** Define the lateral extent of each hydrogeologic unit using geological maps, borehole data, and geological judgment. These polygons will serve as interpolation boundaries and as the lateral limits for volume construction.

**Step 4: Import borehole data.** Populate the BoreholeLog table with stratigraphic observations from wells in the study area. Classify the observations into hydrogeologic units using the HGUCode system defined in Step 1. This is often the most time-consuming step, as it may involve digitizing paper logs, reconciling different stratigraphic nomenclatures, and resolving inconsistencies between data sources.

**Step 5: Create cross-sections.** Define SectionLine features along key transects and create GeoSection features by connecting borehole data along each section line. These cross-sections serve both as framework products and as quality-control tools: viewing the stratigraphy in cross-section often reveals data errors, misclassifications, or areas where the conceptual model needs revision.

**Step 6: Interpolate formation surfaces.** For each hydrogeologic unit, extract formation-top picks from the BoreholeLog table and interpolate them to create raster surfaces. Load the surfaces into the GeoRasters catalog with appropriate attributes. Validate the surfaces against the cross-sections -- the surfaces and cross-sections should be consistent with each other.

**Step 7: Construct volumes.** Use the indexed surfaces in the GeoRasters catalog to build GeoVolume features representing the three-dimensional geometry of each unit. Clip the volumes to the GeoArea extent and validate against borehole data.

**Step 8: Create XS2D views.** For each cross-section needed in reports or presentations, generate the XS2D feature classes by projecting the three-dimensional data onto the two-dimensional cross-section plane with appropriate vertical exaggeration.

**Step 9: Produce deliverables.** Create maps, cross-section plates, three-dimensional scenes, and reports from the completed framework. The geodatabase structure ensures that all products are internally consistent, since they are all derived from the same underlying data.

### 20.5.2 Iterative Refinement

In practice, framework construction is rarely a linear process. Each step reveals issues that require revisiting earlier steps. Cross-sections may reveal that two formation picks at neighboring wells do not correlate, requiring a return to the borehole data to check for errors. Interpolated surfaces may show unrealistic features (such as formation tops that cross each other vertically), requiring adjustments to the conceptual model or the interpolation parameters. New data -- additional boreholes, updated geological maps, geophysical surveys -- may become available during the project, requiring updates to the framework.

The geodatabase approach supports this iterative workflow. Because all spatial representations are linked to the same HydrogeologicUnit table through HGUID, changes to the conceptual model propagate consistently. If a unit is subdivided into two sub-units, new entries in the HydrogeologicUnit table with new HGUIDs are created, and the spatial features are updated to reference the new identifiers. The GeoRasters catalog allows surfaces to be replaced without disrupting the catalog structure -- the new raster simply overwrites the old one, and the catalog attributes remain valid.

## 20.6 International Perspectives on Hydrostratigraphic Frameworks

### 20.6.1 Frameworks and Standards Around the World

The construction of hydrogeologic frameworks is a universal need, but the institutional and technical approaches vary significantly across regions. Understanding these differences is essential for anyone working in an international context.

**Table 20.6**: International comparison of hydrogeologic framework data sources and standards

| Aspect | United States | Hungary / EU | Global |

In the European Union, the INSPIRE Directive (Directive 2007/2/EC) has established harmonized data specifications for geological data, including subsurface features. The INSPIRE Geology theme defines a GeoSciML-based encoding for geological units, boreholes, and mapped features that is, in principle, interoperable across all EU member states. In practice, the level of implementation varies, but the direction is toward pan-European datasets that can be queried and combined across national boundaries. The European Geological Data Infrastructure (EGDI), hosted by EuroGeoSurveys, provides a portal for accessing harmonized geological data from national surveys across Europe.

The GeoSciML (Geoscience Markup Language) standard, developed by the IUGS Commission for the Management and Application of Geoscience Information (CGI), provides an XML-based encoding for geoscience data that is increasingly used for international data exchange. GeoSciML defines schemas for geological units, earth materials, geological structures, and boreholes, providing a vocabulary and structure that parallel many of the concepts in the Arc Hydro Groundwater data model but in a format designed for web services and cross-border interoperability.

### 20.6.2 Open-Source Tools for Framework Construction

While the Arc Hydro Groundwater data model described in this chapter is implemented within the Esri ArcGIS ecosystem, the concepts and workflows are transferable to open-source platforms. Several open-source tools are particularly relevant for hydrostratigraphic framework construction.

**GemPy** is a Python-based open-source library for implicit three-dimensional geological modeling. Rather than interpolating surfaces independently and then stacking them, GemPy uses a potential-field approach where the geological structure is modeled as a scalar field whose iso-surfaces define formation boundaries. This approach naturally handles complex geometries such as faults, unconformities, and intrusions, and it produces geologically consistent models by construction. GemPy integrates with standard Python geoscience libraries and can export results to formats compatible with MODFLOW and other groundwater modeling codes.

**PyVista** and **Subsurface** provide three-dimensional visualization and data management capabilities for subsurface data in Python. The Subsurface library, developed by the Software Underground community, provides data structures specifically designed for wells, cross-sections, surfaces, and volumes -- concepts directly parallel to the Arc Hydro Groundwater feature classes.

**QGIS** with the **Sketsketcher** or **GeoModeller** plugins provides two-dimensional cross-section creation capabilities, and the **QGIS2threejs** plugin enables three-dimensional visualization. While not as integrated as the Arc Hydro Groundwater toolkit, these tools provide accessible alternatives for projects that cannot use commercial software.

The key insight is that the data model -- the conceptual structure of hydrogeologic units, the linked spatial representations, the indexing through HorizonIDs and HGUIDs -- is independent of the software platform. A well-designed hydrogeologic framework built in QGIS with GemPy follows the same logical structure as one built in ArcGIS with Arc Hydro Groundwater. The concepts presented in this chapter apply regardless of the specific tools used.

## 20.7 Hungarian Case Study: Hydrostratigraphy of the Pannonian Basin and the Great Plain Thermal Aquifer System

### 20.7.1 Geological Setting of the Pannonian Basin

The Pannonian Basin of Central Europe offers one of the world's most instructive examples of hydrostratigraphic framework construction, both because of its geological complexity and because of its immense practical importance for water supply, thermal energy, and agriculture. The basin, which underlies most of Hungary and extends into parts of Slovakia, Austria, Romania, Serbia, Croatia, and Ukraine, formed as a back-arc extensional basin during the Miocene, when the retreat of the subducting slab beneath the Carpathian arc caused the overlying crust to thin and subside. The result is a deep sedimentary basin -- reaching depths of 7,000 meters or more in its deepest sub-basins -- filled primarily with sediments deposited in and around the ancient Lake Pannon (Pannonian Lake), which occupied the basin from roughly 11.6 to 4 million years ago.

The fill of the Pannonian Basin has a characteristic architecture that directly controls the hydrogeologic framework. At the base, pre-Neogene rocks (Mesozoic carbonates, Paleozoic metamorphics, and crystalline basement) form an irregular, faulted surface. Above this, Lower and Middle Miocene sediments (marine, then brackish) were deposited during the initial stages of basin formation. The dominant sedimentary package is the Upper Miocene (Pannonian sensu lato) sequence, which records the progressive filling of Lake Pannon from its deepest phase to its eventual infilling and replacement by terrestrial environments. This Pannonian sequence is subdivided into:

- **Deep-water marls and turbidites** (Lower Pannonian, or Endrod Formation equivalent): Fine-grained sediments deposited in the deep lake basin. These typically have low permeability and act as confining units, though turbiditic sandstone layers within them can form deep confined aquifers.

- **Shelf-margin and slope deposits** (Algyo Formation equivalent): Transitional sediments deposited on the prograding shelf slope, with interbedded sands and clays of variable permeability.

- **Delta and shelf deposits** (Upper Pannonian, Ujfalu Formation equivalent): Thick sequences of interbedded sands and clays deposited in delta-front and shelf environments. These are the principal thermal aquifer units of the Great Hungarian Plain, with individual sand bodies ranging from meters to tens of meters thick, separated by clay interbeds.

- **Alluvial plain deposits** (Zagyva Formation equivalent): Continental sediments deposited after the lake phase, consisting of fluvial sands, silts, and clays.

- **Quaternary deposits**: Fluvial, aeolian, and lacustrine sediments forming the uppermost 50-300 meters of the basin fill. These contain the shallow unconfined aquifers that supply most domestic water wells.

This vertical sequence -- from deep-water clays at the base through increasingly sandy deltaic deposits to Quaternary alluvium at the surface -- defines the fundamental hydrostratigraphic architecture of the basin. The challenge for hydrogeologic framework construction is that the boundaries between these units are gradational, laterally variable, and controlled by the local depositional history. A delta lobe that deposited thick sands in one area may have deposited clays in a neighboring area only 10 kilometers away. The resulting hydrogeologic framework is not a simple layer-cake but a complex three-dimensional mosaic of aquifers and aquitards.

### 20.7.2 The Hydrogeologic Framework of the Great Plain

The Great Hungarian Plain (Alfold) occupies the central and eastern part of Hungary and is underlain by the thickest section of Pannonian Basin sediments. The hydrogeologic framework of the Alfold has been the subject of intensive investigation for over a century, driven by the need for water supply, agricultural irrigation, and -- increasingly since the 1950s -- geothermal energy production. Hungary produces more thermal water than almost any other country in Europe, and much of this production comes from the Upper Pannonian aquifer system beneath the Great Plain.

For the purposes of regional groundwater management, the subsurface of the Great Plain is typically divided into four to five hydrogeologic units, which can be represented in the HydrogeologicUnit table as follows:

**Table 20.7**: Hydrogeologic units of the Great Hungarian Plain

| HorizonID | HGUCode | HGUName | Typical Depth (m) | Hydraulic Character | Temperature Range |
|-----------|---------|---------|-------------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| 0 | BASEMENT | Pre-Neogene Basement | > 2000-4000 | Variable; fractured carbonates can be aquifers | > 80-120 C |
| 1 | DEEP_CONF | Lower Pannonian Confining Complex | 1500-3000 | Low permeability marls and clays; local turbidite aquifers | 60-100 C |
| 2 | THERM_AQ | Upper Pannonian Thermal Aquifer System | 500-2000 | Interbedded sands and clays; principal thermal aquifer | 30-80 C |
| 3 | SHALLOW_CONF | Pliocene-Pleistocene Transitional Zone | 100-500 | Variable; interbedded sands, silts, and clays | 15-30 C |
| 4 | SHALLOW_AQ | Quaternary Shallow Aquifer | 0-100 | Unconfined sands and gravels; primary domestic supply | 10-15 C |

The framework construction for the Great Plain draws on an exceptionally rich borehole database. Hungary has been drilling for water, minerals, and hydrocarbons for over 150 years, and the MBFSZ (formerly the Geological Institute of Hungary, MAFI) maintains records of tens of thousands of boreholes. The oil and gas exploration campaigns of the mid-20th century produced deep borehole data that reach the basement across much of the basin, providing the foundation for mapping the deepest units. The water well database maintained by OVF (Orszagos Vizugyi Foigazgatosag, General Directorate of Water Management) adds dense coverage of the shallower units.

The construction of the framework follows the general workflow described in Section 20.5, but with several features specific to the Pannonian Basin context.

**Formation top identification** is complicated by the gradational nature of the Pannonian sequence. Unlike the Edwards Aquifer, where distinct limestone formations create sharp contacts visible in drilling logs, the Pannonian sediments grade from clay to sand and back again repeatedly. Identifying the "top" of the thermal aquifer system requires interpreting a transition from predominantly clay-rich to predominantly sand-rich sequences, a boundary that may be defined differently by different interpreters. Geophysical well logs -- particularly spontaneous potential (SP) and resistivity logs -- provide more objective criteria than lithological descriptions alone, and modern practice increasingly uses quantitative log analysis to define unit boundaries.

**Lateral correlation** is the most challenging aspect of the Great Plain framework. The deltaic depositional environment produced sand bodies that are laterally discontinuous at the scale of individual beds, though the overall sand-rich character of the Upper Pannonian is regionally consistent. For regional framework purposes, the approach is to define hydrogeologic units at a scale that captures the regionally persistent features (the overall thermal aquifer system, the overlying transitional zone, the underlying confining complex) while acknowledging that within each unit, the hydraulic properties are heterogeneous. Site-scale studies for individual well fields or geothermal projects require finer resolution and may define additional sub-units within the regional framework.

### 20.7.3 Interpolation of Framework Surfaces

The interpolation of formation surfaces in the Great Plain benefits from the relatively dense data coverage but is complicated by the geological structure. The basement surface, mapped extensively from seismic reflection surveys and deep boreholes, shows dramatic relief: deep grabens (sub-basins) separated by horst blocks where the basement is much shallower. This surface cannot be interpolated with simple kriging; it requires either fault-bounded interpolation (where the surface is interpolated separately on each side of major faults) or a structural trend surface approach where a regional trend is fitted first and residuals are interpolated separately.

The Upper Pannonian thermal aquifer top, by contrast, is a much smoother surface, reflecting the progradational filling of the basin. Kriging with a linear variogram model typically produces good results for this surface, though anisotropy is often present -- the surface tends to be smoother along the direction of shelf progradation (roughly NW-SE) than perpendicular to it. Incorporating this anisotropy into the kriging model improves the interpolation, particularly in areas with sparse data.

A worked example illustrates the kriging workflow for the thermal aquifer top. Suppose we have extracted formation picks from 200 boreholes in a 40 km by 30 km study area in Hajdu-Bihar County. The formation top elevations range from -450 m a.s.l. (in the deepest part of the area) to -180 m a.s.l. (on the basin margin). The experimental variogram, computed for lag distances from 500 m to 15 km, shows:

- A nugget effect of approximately 100 m$^2$ (reflecting measurement uncertainty and very short-range variability)
- A sill of approximately 4,500 m$^2$ (the total variance of the dataset)
- A range of approximately 12 km (the distance beyond which data points are essentially uncorrelated)
- Anisotropy ratio of 1.5, with the major axis oriented at N315 (NW-SE)

Fitting a spherical variogram model to these parameters and applying ordinary kriging with 50-meter cell size produces a raster surface of the thermal aquifer top. The cross-validation RMSE is 8.3 meters -- reasonable given that the total range of elevations spans 270 meters. The kriging variance map shows lowest uncertainty near the borehole clusters and highest uncertainty in the gaps between boreholes, with a maximum standard deviation of about 25 meters in the most data-sparse areas.

### 20.7.4 The Thermal Water Dimension

What makes the Great Plain hydrostratigraphic framework particularly significant is its connection to thermal water resources. Hungary's geothermal gradient averages about 45 mK/m (millikelvins per meter), roughly 1.5 times the global continental average of 30 mK/m. This elevated gradient, a consequence of the basin's thin crust and high heat flow, means that groundwater at depths of 1,000-2,000 meters reaches temperatures of 50-90 degrees Celsius -- hot enough for district heating, greenhouse agriculture, balneology, and industrial process heat.

The thermal aquifer system defined in the hydrogeologic framework is the principal source of this thermal water. The Upper Pannonian deltaic sands have moderate to good permeability (hydraulic conductivity typically in the range of $10^{-5}$ to $10^{-4}$ m/s), and the confining clays above and below maintain pressure and temperature. Production wells typically penetrate 50-200 meters of net sand within the thermal aquifer system, and artesian conditions are common -- the piezometric surface of the thermal aquifer system often stands above the ground surface, so wells flow naturally without pumping.

The hydrogeologic framework directly informs thermal water management in several ways. First, the geometry of the thermal aquifer system -- its thickness, depth, and lateral extent -- determines where thermal water can be produced and at what temperature. The GeoRasters catalog stores not only the formation surfaces but also interpolated temperature and transmissivity surfaces that guide well siting. Second, the confining units -- their thickness, continuity, and hydraulic properties -- determine the degree of protection of the thermal aquifer from contamination and the degree of hydraulic connection between the thermal system and the shallow aquifers. In areas where the confining layers are thin or absent (due to erosion or non-deposition), the thermal system is vulnerable to over-exploitation and quality degradation. Third, the three-dimensional framework supports flow modeling (to be discussed in Chapter 21) that simulates the movement of thermal water through the system and predicts the long-term effects of production.

Hungary's thermal water management is regulated through the Water Framework Directive (transposed into Hungarian law), which requires quantitative and chemical status assessment for all groundwater bodies, including thermal aquifers. The hydrogeologic framework described here underpins the delineation of groundwater bodies and the assessment of their status. The OVF monitoring network includes dedicated thermal monitoring wells that provide ongoing data for framework refinement and model calibration.

To illustrate the application of the XS2D system in the Hungarian context, consider a cross-section through Hajdu-Bihar County from the Tiszantul region, running approximately NW-SE from the Hortobagy area to the Romanian border near Biharkeresztes. This transect, roughly 80 kilometers long, crosses the deepening axis of the Debrecen Trough, one of the major sub-basins of the Great Plain.

The SectionLine feature is defined connecting 12 deep boreholes with reliable stratigraphic information, with a VertExag2D of 20. The three-dimensional GeoSection features show the five hydrogeologic units from Table 20.7, with the following notable features:

- The basement surface drops from approximately -1,500 m a.s.l. on the NW margin to -4,200 m a.s.l. in the center of the Debrecen Trough before rising to about -2,800 m near the Romanian border. This dramatic relief is controlled by Miocene extensional faults.

- The Lower Pannonian confining complex thickens into the trough center, reaching over 1,500 meters thickness, confirming the deep-water lake environment that persisted longer in the deepest parts of the basin.

## 20.8 Modern Advances in Hydrogeologic Framework Construction

### 20.8.1 Machine Learning for Lithological Classification

One of the most time-consuming steps in framework construction is the classification of borehole data into hydrogeologic units. Historically, this has been done by geologists examining each log and assigning formations based on lithological descriptions, fossil content, and geophysical signatures. For large datasets -- the Hungarian MBFSZ database contains records from tens of thousands of boreholes -- manual classification is prohibitively slow.

Machine learning approaches are increasingly being applied to automate or semi-automate this classification. The training data consist of boreholes where the hydrogeologic classification has been established by expert geologists. Features extracted from the geophysical logs (SP, resistivity, gamma-ray, neutron porosity) at each depth interval serve as input variables, and the hydrogeologic unit assignment serves as the target. Random forest, support vector machine, and neural network classifiers have all shown promising results in published studies, achieving classification accuracies of 85-95% on test datasets.

The critical caveat is that machine learning cannot replace geological judgment entirely. The classifier may correctly identify 90% of intervals but misclassify the remaining 10% -- and these misclassifications may occur precisely at the boundaries between units, which are the most geologically important intervals. A practical workflow uses machine learning for initial classification of the bulk of the data and then routes uncertain or boundary classifications to a geologist for review. This hybrid approach can reduce the manual effort by an order of magnitude while maintaining the quality needed for framework construction.

### 20.8.2 Implicit Modeling and Uncertainty Quantification

Traditional framework construction treats interpolation as a deterministic process: one surface is produced for each formation top, representing the "best estimate" of the boundary position. But given the uncertainties in the borehole data and the vast spaces between data points, a single deterministic surface may give a false impression of certainty.

Implicit modeling approaches, such as those implemented in GemPy and Leapfrog, address this by treating the framework as a probability problem. Rather than interpolating surfaces independently, they model the entire geological structure as a coherent three-dimensional field and can generate multiple equally plausible realizations of the framework. Each realization honors the borehole data but differs in the areas between boreholes, reflecting the range of geological interpretations consistent with the observations.

This probabilistic approach is particularly valuable for risk assessment. If a municipality is considering a new well field in the thermal aquifer, the question is not just "what is the aquifer thickness at this location?" but "what is the probability that the aquifer thickness exceeds 100 meters at this location?" The ensemble of framework realizations provides a direct answer to probability questions, while a single deterministic framework provides only a point estimate.

### 20.8.3 Integration with Remote Sensing

While the subsurface is fundamentally invisible from space, remote sensing contributes to framework construction in several indirect ways. Gravity surveys (including satellite gravity from the GRACE and GRACE-FO missions) can detect large-scale density contrasts between sedimentary basin fill and crystalline basement, helping to constrain the basement topography. Airborne electromagnetic surveys (such as SkyTEM, widely used in Denmark and increasingly in other European countries) can image the subsurface to depths of 200-300 meters, providing continuous coverage of the shallow hydrogeologic units that complements the point-based borehole data. Interferometric synthetic aperture radar (InSAR) can detect land surface subsidence caused by groundwater extraction, providing an indirect constraint on the compressibility and geometry of confining units.

In the Pannonian Basin context, the GRACE-derived terrestrial water storage anomalies, processed through the G3P (Global Gravity-based Groundwater Product) at GFZ Potsdam, provide basin-scale estimates of groundwater storage changes that can be compared with the framework-based storage calculations. This comparison serves as a large-scale validation of the framework: if the framework predicts a certain storage capacity and the GRACE data show storage changes inconsistent with that capacity, the framework may need revision.

## 20.9 Connecting to Groundwater Flow Modeling

### 20.9.1 The Framework as Model Input

The hydrogeologic framework described in this chapter is not an end in itself. Its ultimate purpose is to provide the three-dimensional structural and property description that groundwater flow models require. Chapter 21 will describe how these models simulate the movement of water through the subsurface, but it is important to understand here how the framework feeds into that process.

A groundwater flow model -- whether implemented in MODFLOW, FEFLOW, or another code -- requires a discretized representation of the subsurface. The model domain is divided into layers, each corresponding to one or more hydrogeologic units, and each layer is subdivided into cells with assigned hydraulic properties. The framework provides the inputs for this discretization:

- The **GeoRasters formation surfaces** define the top and bottom of each model layer. These surfaces are resampled to the model grid resolution and used to compute layer thicknesses.

- The **GeoRasters property surfaces** (hydraulic conductivity, storage coefficient, porosity) provide the spatially varying hydraulic properties for each model layer.

- The **GeoArea polygons** define the lateral extent of the model domain and the boundaries of different hydraulic zones within it.

- The **HydrogeologicUnit table** provides the conceptual link between the framework units and the model layers, ensuring that the model structure is consistent with the geological interpretation.

The quality of the framework directly determines the quality of the model. A framework with well-constrained surfaces and realistic hydraulic properties will produce a model that can be calibrated to observed heads and fluxes with reasonable parameter values. A framework with poorly interpolated surfaces or incorrect unit correlations will require unrealistic parameter adjustments to match observations, producing a model that calibrates but does not predict reliably.

### 20.9.2 Feedback from Modeling to Framework

The relationship between framework and model is not one-directional. Calibrated groundwater models often reveal deficiencies in the framework that were not apparent from the geological data alone. If a model cannot reproduce observed water levels without invoking unrealistically high or low hydraulic conductivities in a particular area, this may indicate that the framework structure is incorrect -- perhaps a confining layer is thinner or more permeable than the interpolated surfaces suggest, or perhaps a fault provides a conduit that the framework does not represent.

This feedback loop between framework and model is one of the most productive aspects of hydrogeologic investigation, and it connects directly to the calibration concepts discussed in Chapter 22. The framework provides the geological structure; the model tests whether that structure is hydraulically consistent with observations; discrepancies drive framework revision; and the revised framework improves the model. Over multiple iterations, both the framework and the model converge toward a representation that is geologically plausible and hydraulically validated.

In the Great Plain thermal aquifer system, this iterative process has been particularly important. Early regional models treated the thermal aquifer as a single homogeneous layer, which produced reasonable results for regional flow patterns but could not match the observed temperature and head distributions at individual well fields. Subsequent framework revisions incorporated sub-units within the thermal aquifer -- distinguishing between more permeable delta-front sands and less permeable pro-delta clays -- and the resulting models showed much better agreement with observations. This refinement was only possible because the framework data model (through the HGUID system and the GeoRasters catalog) could accommodate additional units without restructuring the entire database.

## 20.10 Practical Considerations and Pitfalls

### 20.10.1 Data Quality and Documentation

The most common source of errors in hydrogeologic frameworks is not the interpolation algorithm or the data model design but the quality of the input data. Formation picks from different sources may use different stratigraphic nomenclatures, different reference datums (ground level vs. sea level), or different conventions for measuring depth (depth below ground surface vs. elevation above sea level). Before any interpolation is attempted, all data must be reconciled to a common reference system and nomenclature.

In the Hungarian context, a particular challenge arises from the history of borehole data collection. Older boreholes (pre-1970s) may have coordinates in the old Hungarian datum (HD72/EOV) with variable accuracy, depth measurements referenced to ground surface elevation estimates of uncertain precision, and lithological descriptions using nomenclature that has since been revised. Newer boreholes may have GPS coordinates in WGS84, precision depth measurements, and modern stratigraphic terminology. Combining these datasets requires careful attention to coordinate transformations (see Chapter 3 for the relationship between EOV and ETRS89), datum conversions, and nomenclature mapping.

A best practice is to maintain a data quality flag for each formation pick, recording the source of the data, the method of pick identification (core vs. cuttings vs. geophysical log), and the estimated uncertainty. This metadata can be stored as additional attributes in the BoreholeLog table and used to weight the data during interpolation -- giving more influence to high-quality picks and less to uncertain ones.

### 20.10.2 Handling Faults and Unconformities

The standard interpolation workflow assumes that formation surfaces are continuous and smooth. Faults violate this assumption by introducing discontinuities: the same formation may be at very different elevations on opposite sides of a fault. If a fault is not accounted for, the interpolation will produce a smooth surface that averages across the discontinuity, placing the formation at an incorrect elevation on both sides.

The solution is fault-bounded interpolation: the study area is divided into structural blocks separated by known faults, and the interpolation is performed independently within each block. The fault traces must be mapped accurately, and each borehole must be assigned to the correct structural block. This adds complexity to the workflow but is essential for areas with significant faulting, such as the margins of the Pannonian Basin where Miocene extensional faults offset the basement by hundreds or even thousands of meters.

Unconformities present a different challenge. An unconformity is a surface of erosion or non-deposition where younger rocks lie directly on older ones, with an intervening time gap. In the hydrogeologic framework, an unconformity means that one or more units are missing in part of the study area. The interpolation must handle this gracefully: the surface for the missing unit should either not be generated in the area of absence, or it should be flagged as absent so that volume calculations do not include nonexistent rock.

### 20.10.3 Scale and Resolution

Every hydrogeologic framework is constructed at a particular scale, and the choice of scale affects every aspect of the framework from unit definition to interpolation parameters. A regional framework covering an entire basin at 1:500,000 scale will define broad hydrogeologic units and use coarse-resolution surfaces. A site-scale framework for a well field at 1:10,000 scale will define fine-grained units and use high-resolution surfaces.

The critical principle is that the resolution of the framework should be appropriate to the data density. A common mistake is to create high-resolution raster surfaces (small cell sizes) from sparse data, which produces surfaces that are smooth between data points but not actually constrained by observations. The interpolation looks precise but is not accurate. A rough guideline is that the raster cell size should not be smaller than about one-quarter of the average distance between data points. For a study area with boreholes spaced 2 kilometers apart on average, a 500-meter cell size is appropriate; a 50-meter cell size would imply a false precision.

## 20.11 Regional Flow Systems and Gravity-Driven Groundwater Circulation

### 20.11.1 Toth's Theory of Gravity-Driven Flow Systems

A hydrogeologic framework -- however meticulously constructed from boreholes, surfaces, and volumes -- remains a static geometric description until we understand how water moves through it. The single most important conceptual advance in that understanding came from Jozsef Toth (1962, 1963), a Hungarian-born hydrogeologist working at the University of Alberta, who demonstrated mathematically that gravity-driven groundwater flow in a basin organizes itself into hierarchically nested systems: **local**, **intermediate**, and **regional**.

Toth's theoretical model begins with a simple two-dimensional cross-section through a drainage basin. The water table is the upper boundary, shaped by the topography; the base is an impermeable horizontal surface; and the sides are vertical, impermeable divides. In a homogeneous, isotropic medium with a sinusoidal water-table surface superimposed on a regional slope, the potential distribution satisfies the Laplace equation. The resulting flow field -- obtained as a Fourier-series solution -- reveals a remarkable structure:

- **Local flow systems** circulate between adjacent topographic highs and lows. Water infiltrates on a local hilltop and discharges in the nearest valley. Residence times are short -- years to decades.

- **Intermediate flow systems** have their recharge areas and discharge areas separated by at least one local system. The flow paths are longer and deeper, with residence times of decades to centuries.

- **Regional flow systems** span the entire basin, from the highest recharge area at the regional divide to the lowest discharge area at the basin outlet. These flow paths penetrate to the greatest depths and have residence times of thousands to tens of thousands of years.

Toth showed further that the relative importance of these three system types depends on two controls: the amplitude of the local relief (the height of topographic undulations) and the magnitude of the regional slope. Where local relief is large relative to the regional slope, local systems dominate. Where the regional slope is large but local relief is small, a single regional system occupies the entire basin. Where both are present, all three system types coexist in a nested hierarchy.

This theory carries immediate implications for the hydrogeologic framework. Because the different flow systems occupy different depth ranges, a single conceptual vertical sequence of hydrogeologic units -- as defined in the HydrogeologicUnit table -- may host water from two or even three different flow systems simultaneously. Two wells drilled to different depths at the same location can tap water of dramatically different age, chemistry, and temperature, because they intersect different systems. The framework's three-dimensional geometry determines which flow paths exist, but the flow-system hierarchy determines which paths are active and how much water moves along each one.

Toth received the first O. E. Meinzer Award from the Geological Society of America for this work, recognizing it as "a distinguished contribution to the science of hydrogeology" (Marton, 2009).

### 20.11.2 Hydraulic Continuity -- The Foundation of Regional Flow

The existence of nested flow systems presupposes that water can move vertically through the entire sedimentary sequence -- including the confining layers that the framework identifies as aquitards. This property is called **hydraulic continuity**, a concept whose modern formulation is also due to Toth (1990, 1995). Hydraulic continuity means that a pressure change at any point in the saturated zone propagates, at finite speed and with diminishing amplitude, to every other point. No geological layer is perfectly impermeable over geological time and basin-scale distances.

The evidence for hydraulic continuity is now overwhelming. Neuman and Witherspoon (1972) demonstrated in field tests that a hydraulic response can be detected through a 49-meter-thick aquitard approximately 30 days after pumping begins -- the layer appears "perfectly sealed" during short pumping tests but "open" when tested over longer periods. In the Great Hungarian Plain, Rónai (1985) showed that pressure fluctuations observed at the surface propagate to depths of several hundred meters within days, proving that the entire Quaternary and Upper Pannonian sequence behaves as a single hydraulically continuous system. Halász (1995) used potential-theory analysis to demonstrate that the dome-shaped piezometric surface beneath the Nyirseg recharge area could not exist unless there is continuous vertical leakage through the layered sediments.

For framework construction, hydraulic continuity means that the aquitards identified in the HydrogeologicUnit table are not barriers but rather resistors: they slow vertical flow without stopping it. This distinction is critical for assigning hydraulic properties to framework units and for subsequent flow modeling (Chapter 21). As Marton (2009) emphasizes, even the Pannonian clays and marls classified as confining units transmit water vertically at rates that are small on a local scale but significant on a regional scale, sustaining the nested flow systems that control the distribution of fresh, thermal, and saline water throughout the basin.

### 20.11.3 Gravity-Driven Flow in the Pannonian Basin

The Pannonian Basin provides a textbook-scale illustration of Toth's flow-system theory. According to Erdélyi (1975, 1978) and Rónai (1985), and synthesized by Toth and Almasi (2001), the subsurface water in the Hungarian part of the basin is driven by two forces:

1. **Gravity**, arising from the topographic relief of the basin surface, which drives the upper ~400--1,700 m of flow in an open (unconfined) regional flow system recharged by precipitation.

2. **Tectonic compression**, which in the deeper overpressured zone (below ~1,700 m) squeezes pore water upward through the compacting sediments.

The two zones interact through the regional aquitards of the Algyo and Endrod Formations. The relatively saline (6--25 g/L) deep waters migrating upward from the compressional zone are deflected laterally by the descending gravity-driven flow from the recharge areas, and the two water masses mix in the discharge zones, where they create saline marshes, alkaline lakes, and the characteristic sodic ("szikes") soils of the Great Plain (Toth, 2006).

The principal recharge areas are the two elevated sand plateaus of the Great Plain: the **Nyirseg** in the northeast and the **Duna-Tisza Interfluve** in the center. Beneath these plateaus, potentiometric levels decrease with depth -- the diagnostic signature of downward flow -- with potential differences of 30--35 m between the shallow water table and the deep Lower Pleistocene aquifer (Halász, 1995). The discharge areas are the low-lying parts of the Great Plain, particularly the Tisza River valley, where potentiometric levels increase with depth, artesian conditions develop, and upward flow brings thermal, mineralized water toward the surface.

In GIS terms, the recharge and discharge zones can be mapped by analyzing the vertical distribution of hydraulic heads from nested monitoring wells. Where the shallow head exceeds the deep head (downward gradient), the area is classified as recharge; where the deep head exceeds the shallow head (upward gradient), it is classified as discharge. Interpolating these point-based classifications across the basin using GeoArea polygons and linking them to the HydrogeologicUnit framework provides a powerful tool for water management: recharge zones require protection from contamination (because pollutants migrate downward into the aquifer system), while discharge zones are the locations where groundwater-dependent ecosystems and thermal springs must be sustained.

## 20.12 Detailed Hydrostratigraphic Model of the Pannonian Basin

### 20.12.1 A Layer-by-Layer Description

Section 20.7 introduced the five-unit framework of the Great Plain in broad terms. Here we provide a more detailed description of each unit's lithology, thickness, and hydraulic properties, drawing on the comprehensive review by Marton (2009).

**Pre-Tertiary Basement (HorizonID 0).** The basin floor consists of Paleozoic metamorphic and crystalline rocks (granite, phyllite, schist, gneiss, amphibolite) and Mesozoic carbonates (Triassic limestone and dolomite, Cretaceous carbonate platforms). In the crystalline units, primary (intergranular) porosity is negligible; groundwater occurs only in fracture zones along structural lineaments, yielding small and unpredictable supplies. The fractured Mesozoic carbonates -- particularly the Triassic karst systems of the Transdanubian Mountains and Northern Uplands -- are far more productive, forming some of Hungary's most important thermal karst aquifer systems (e.g., the Budapest thermal springs at ~30--70 degrees C). Hydraulic conductivity in the fractured carbonates varies by orders of magnitude over short distances, making them difficult to represent as a single GeoRasters surface; a dual-porosity modeling approach is often needed. In the Great Plain, the basement surface lies at depths ranging from 500 m on structural horst blocks to over 7,000 m in the deepest sub-basins (Mako Trough), and its rugged, fault-controlled topography is one of the most important features that any 3D framework must capture.

**Lower Pannonian Confining Complex (HorizonID 1).** Above the basement, the Lower Pannonian (or Kunsági) deposits consist of deep-water lacustrine marls and calcareous clays of the Endrod Formation, overlain by the shelf-slope transitional deposits of the Algyo Formation. These are predominantly fine-grained, with hydraulic conductivities in the range of $10^{-9}$ to $10^{-7}$ m/s. However, turbiditic sandstone intercalations within the Endrod Formation can locally form deep confined aquifers that produce hot ($>$80 degrees C), saline (6--25 g/L) water. Total thickness ranges from a few hundred meters on basement highs to over 2,000 m in deep sub-basins. In the context of Toth's flow-system theory, this unit forms the principal regional aquitard that separates the overpressured deep zone from the gravity-driven upper zone.

**Upper Pannonian Thermal Aquifer System (HorizonID 2).** The most important hydrogeologic unit for thermal water management, this corresponds to the Ujfalu Formation and its equivalents -- delta-front and shelf deposits that record the progressive infilling of Lake Pannon. The unit consists of interbedded sands and clays, with individual sand bodies ranging from 2 to 25 m in thickness and extending laterally as sheet-like deposits over distances of kilometers to tens of kilometers (Marton, 2009). Locally, adjacent sand bodies merge ("coalesce") to form continuous, hydraulically connected thermal reservoirs. The unit reaches its greatest development in the southeastern Great Plain (Hodmezovasarhely-Mako area), where over 1,500 m of Upper Pannonian sediments have been penetrated. Hydraulic conductivity of the sand layers is typically $10^{-5}$ to $10^{-4}$ m/s; transmissivity values of 200--500 m$^2$/d are common at production wells. Water temperature ranges from 30 to 80 degrees C depending on depth, and 95% of Hungary's water wells in the Great Plain are completed in this unit. Hungary's 1,289 registered thermal wells (as of 2000) produce 80--100 million m$^3$ of thermal water annually, predominantly from this formation (Marton, 2009).

**Pliocene Fluvial and Aeolian Deposits (HorizonID 3).** Above the Upper Pannonian lacustrine sequence, the uppermost Pliocene ("Levantei") deposits represent the transition from lake to land. In the Jászság Depression and the Koros Basin, this unit reaches 300--500 m thickness and consists of varicolored ("tarka") clays with limited aquifer potential. In the Danube structural graben, by contrast, the Pliocene deposits are sandier and function as productive aquifers. In the Nyirseg, the Pliocene sands grade upward into the Lower Pleistocene and form a single hydraulic system that constitutes one of the region's most important water supplies (Marton, 2009). Hydraulic conductivity varies widely, from $10^{-8}$ m/s in the clay-dominated Jászság facies to $10^{-4}$ m/s in the sand-dominated Nyirseg facies.

**Quaternary Alluvial Deposits (HorizonID 4).** The youngest unit, 50--300 m thick, consists of fluvial sands, gravels, silts, and aeolian loess. The Great Plain contains several sub-basins, each with a distinctive depositional history. The Lower Pleistocene is dominated by coarse-grained fluvial deposits -- gravelly sands with hydraulic conductivities of $10^{-4}$ to $10^{-3}$ m/s -- that form the best water-bearing layers in the shallow aquifer system. The Middle Pleistocene records a period of reduced subsidence and finer-grained sedimentation (clays, silts), while the Upper Pleistocene shows renewed coarse deposition, though with somewhat lower well yields than the deeper horizons. Approximately 80% of Hungary's drilled water wells are completed in Quaternary deposits (Marton, 2009). In the urbanized areas of the Great Plain, these shallow aquifers are the most vulnerable to contamination and the most important to protect.

### 20.12.2 Representing Lateral Heterogeneity in the Framework

The layer-cake summary above conceals the most challenging aspect of the Pannonian hydrostratigraphy: the extreme lateral variability of sand and clay bodies within each unit. The deltaic depositional environment of the Upper Pannonian produced sand lobes that shift laterally over short distances. A borehole drilled in one location may encounter 100 m of net sand in the thermal aquifer, while a borehole 10 km away may encounter predominantly clay at the same stratigraphic level.

This heterogeneity has direct consequences for the GeoRasters catalog. A single formation-top surface for the thermal aquifer system captures the regional geometry but not the internal sand-clay distribution. For site-scale modeling (individual well fields, geothermal projects), it is necessary to add **property surfaces** to the catalog: a net-sand-thickness raster, a sand-to-clay ratio raster, or -- where well-log data are dense enough -- individual sand-body surfaces for the principal reservoir intervals. The HorizonID system accommodates this by assigning sub-horizon indices (e.g., 2.1, 2.2, 2.3) to individual sand packages within the thermal aquifer, allowing the framework to be refined without disrupting the regional structure.

## 20.13 Leaky Aquifer Systems and Vertical Interconnection

### 20.13.1 The Theory of Leakage Between Layers

Classical hydrogeology -- as formulated by Theis (1935) -- treated confined aquifers as perfectly sealed layers bounded by impermeable formations. Water entered the aquifer only at its outcrop and left only through wells. The theory of **leaky aquifer systems** (sometimes called semi-confined systems) overturned this assumption by showing that aquitards transmit water vertically, continuously, and in significant quantities.

The mathematical theory of leaky aquifers was developed primarily by Jacob (1946), Hantush (1956, 1964), and Neuman and Witherspoon (1969, 1972). In the Hantush-Jacob model, a confined aquifer is overlain by a semi-permeable layer (aquitard) that separates it from an upper water source (an unconfined aquifer or surface water body). When a well is pumped, drawdown in the confined aquifer creates a vertical head difference across the aquitard, which induces vertical leakage. The leakage rate per unit area is:

$$q = \frac{K'}{m'} \cdot \Delta h$$

where $K'$ is the vertical hydraulic conductivity of the aquitard, $m'$ is its thickness, and $\Delta h$ is the head difference across it. The ratio $K'/m'$ is the **leakance** (or leakage coefficient), with dimensions of $T^{-1}$.

The **leakage factor** $B$, a characteristic length scale, is defined as:

$$B = \sqrt{\frac{T \cdot m'}{K'}} = \sqrt{\frac{T}{b}}$$

where $T$ is the transmissivity of the aquifer and $b = K'/m'$ is the leakance. A large leakage factor means that leakage is weak relative to the aquifer's ability to transmit water laterally; a small leakage factor means leakage is strong and drawdown is limited.

The significance of this theory for framework construction is profound. It means that every aquitard in the HydrogeologicUnit table has a quantifiable leakage rate, and the framework must store not only the geometry of confining units but also their vertical hydraulic conductivity and thickness -- the parameters that determine leakance. In the GeoRasters catalog, this translates to property surfaces for aquitard $K'$ and $m'$ alongside the traditional formation-top surfaces.

### 20.13.2 Multi-Layer Leaky Systems in the Great Plain

The Pannonian Basin is a classic multi-layer leaky system. Marton (2009) provides measured leakance values from several Hungarian sites:

- Roswell artesian basin (New Mexico, USA): $b$ = $4.8 \times 10^{-8}$ to $10^{-10}$ s$^{-1}$ (Hantush, 1956)
- Southern Illinois Pleistocene sediments: $b$ = $3 \times 10^{-7}$ to $8 \times 10^{-9}$ s$^{-1}$ (Walton, 1960)
- Debrecen Waterworks I, second aquitard: $b$ = $1.34 \times 10^{-4}$ d$^{-1}$ (Marton, 2000)

The Debrecen case is particularly instructive. The city's water supply is drawn from the Lower Pleistocene principal aquifer (the "vimuves reteg" or waterworks layer), which lies at 200--350 m depth beneath the city. Above it, a Middle Pleistocene aquitard separates it from shallower aquifers. Marton (2000, 2009) developed a numerical model using finite-difference calculations on a 500 m grid to quantify the leakage. The model showed that:

- The average leakage intensity into the Lower Pleistocene aquifer beneath Debrecen Waterworks I is approximately 0.49 m/year
- This represents roughly 53--56% of the total water extracted by the wells
- In the center of the well field, leakage intensity reaches 1.5 m/year

These results demonstrate that what appeared to be a "confined" aquifer is, in reality, drawing more than half of its yield from vertical leakage through the overlying aquitard. For the framework, this means that modeling the Debrecen well field with a single isolated aquifer layer would be fundamentally wrong; the multi-layer leaky structure must be represented.

The same principle applies throughout the Great Plain. The Quaternary sediments are, in Marton's (2009) words, "quintessentially leaky systems" ("kifejezetten atszivargo osszletek"). The Nyirseg, the Duna-Tisza Interfluve, and the basin margins -- all topographically elevated recharge areas -- are locations where vertical leakage from the surface downward through multiple semi-permeable layers sustains the deeper aquifers. In the discharge zones (the Tisza valley, the southern Great Plain), the leakage direction reverses: deep, pressurized water leaks upward through the aquitards, feeding springs, maintaining high water tables, and creating the sodic soils and alkaline lakes characteristic of the Alfold lowlands.

## 20.14 Groundwater Age Dating and Its Role in Framework Validation

### 20.14.1 The Concept of Groundwater Age

The "age" of groundwater -- the time elapsed since the water last had intimate contact with the Earth's atmosphere -- is a hydrogeological parameter of extraordinary diagnostic value (Marton, 2009). As defined by the International Glossary of Hydrogeology (UNESCO): "Age of ground water: the time at which water was last in intimate contact with the earth's atmosphere." When measured at different wells and depths within a hydrogeologic framework, groundwater age maps the invisible pathways of the flow system. Young water indicates active recharge and short flow paths; old water indicates deep regional flow and long residence times.

### 20.14.2 Isotopic Methods for Age Determination

Three principal isotopic systems are used in Hungarian hydrogeological practice:

**Carbon-14 ($^{14}$C) dating** is the most widely applied method for waters with ages ranging from about 1,000 to 30,000 years. The technique measures the residual radioactivity of dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC) in the groundwater. The age is computed using the Ingerson-Pearson model (1964):

$$t = -8{,}270 \times \ln\left(\frac{A}{A_0}\right)$$

where $A$ is the measured percent Modern Carbon (pMC) activity and $A_0$ is the initial DIC activity at the time of recharge. The initial activity depends on the geochemical conditions at the recharge point -- particularly the degree of limestone dissolution, which dilutes the atmospheric $^{14}$C signal with "dead" (zero-activity) carbonate carbon. In Central European settings, the initial activity is typically taken as 70--90 pMC, with Vogel (1970) recommending $85 \pm 5$% pMC as a standard value. In the Hungarian Great Plain, measured values in Pleistocene aquifer waters range from 50 to 70 pMC, corresponding to ages of several thousand to over 20,000 years (Marton, 2009).

A critical caveat is that mixing of waters of different ages produces $^{14}$C activities that yield misleading "apparent ages." Matthes, Münnich, and Sonntag (1976) showed that a mixture of 90% very old water (with zero $^{14}$C) and 10% modern water yields an apparent age of approximately 18,400 years -- neither the true age of the old component nor the young one. Marton (2009) emphasizes that $^{14}$C ages in leaky aquifer systems must always be interpreted in the context of the mixing induced by vertical leakage.

**Tritium ($^3$H) dating** is applicable to waters younger than about 50 years. Tritium entered the hydrological cycle primarily from atmospheric nuclear weapons testing in the 1950s and 1960s, creating a distinct "bomb pulse" that peaked around 1963. The presence of tritium in a groundwater sample indicates that at least some component of the water was recharged after 1952. The absence of tritium indicates water that predates the nuclear era -- though it does not specify how much older. In the Debrecen area, tritium measurements were used alongside $^{14}$C to track the ingress of young, shallow water into the deep waterworks aquifer through leakage (Marton, 2009).

**Stable isotope analysis ($^2$H and $^{18}$O)** does not provide absolute ages but distinguishes water of different origins. The deuterium excess and oxygen-18 content of groundwater reflect the climatic conditions at the time of recharge. Waters recharged during the last glacial period (Pleistocene, ~10,000--100,000 years ago) have characteristically depleted $^2$H and $^{18}$O values compared to modern precipitation. In the Great Plain, the stable isotope signature has been used to distinguish glacial-age deep waters from Holocene-age shallow waters, providing an independent check on the $^{14}$C chronology (Marton, 2009).

**Helium age dating** is an emerging technique for very old waters (>30,000 years) where $^{14}$C has decayed below detectable levels. Radiogenic $^4$He accumulates in groundwater from the decay of uranium and thorium in the surrounding rock matrix. The helium concentration increases with residence time, providing a "clock" that extends far beyond the $^{14}$C limit. Marton (2009) discusses the role of helium as a tracer for vertical water circulation, noting that anomalously high helium concentrations in shallow aquifers can indicate upward leakage of very old, deep water.

### 20.14.3 Hungarian Results: Age Dating in the Great Plain

The MTA ATOMKI (Institute for Nuclear Research, Debrecen) has measured $^{14}$C, tritium, and stable isotope concentrations in hundreds of Great Plain groundwater samples since the 1980s, providing one of the most comprehensive age-dating datasets for any sedimentary basin in Europe.

Key findings from Marton's (2009) synthesis:

1. **Recharge areas show young water at all depths.** In the Nyirseg, Pleistocene aquifer waters at 100--250 m depth have $^{14}$C ages of 3,000--7,000 years, confirming active downward leakage through the semi-permeable layers.

2. **Discharge areas show old water at shallow depths.** In the central Great Plain (e.g., Jászladány monitoring wells), even relatively shallow (200--300 m) aquifers contain water with $^{14}$C ages exceeding 15,000 years, confirming that regional flow paths transport water over great distances before it discharges.

3. **Pumping changes the age distribution.** At the Debrecen Waterworks, $^{14}$C measurements in the 1980s showed that the "waterworks layer" water was becoming progressively younger over time. The explanation: intensive pumping increased the vertical leakage of younger water from the overlying Middle Pleistocene aquifer ($^{14}$C activity ~40 pMC, age ~3,000 years). When pumping was reduced in the 1990s, the effect reversed -- the water became older again. Using the mixing equation, Marton calculated that the fraction of leakage-derived young water was approximately 50--56% of total production -- consistent with the independent hydraulic calculation described in Section 20.13.2. This is a remarkable cross-validation of two entirely independent methods.

4. **Stable isotopes confirm glacial-age origins for deep thermal waters.** The depleted deuterium and $^{18}$O signatures of deep (>500 m) thermal waters indicate recharge during Pleistocene glacial periods, consistent with $^{14}$C ages approaching or exceeding the method's ~30,000-year limit.

## 20.15 Framework Model Validation Using Field Data

### 20.15.1 Why Validation Matters

A hydrogeologic framework is a model -- an interpretation of the subsurface based on limited data. No matter how carefully the borehole picks have been quality-controlled, the surfaces interpolated, and the volumes constructed, the framework remains a hypothesis about what exists between and beyond the data points. Validation is the process of testing that hypothesis against independent evidence.

In the context of this chapter, "independent" means data that were not used in constructing the framework. If the framework was built from borehole lithology logs and formation-top picks, then pump test results, isotopic measurements, and water chemistry data are independent. If these independent datasets are consistent with the framework's predictions about connectivity, flow directions, and residence times, confidence in the framework increases. If they are inconsistent, the framework must be revised.

### 20.15.2 Pump Test Validation

The most direct validation of a framework's representation of aquifer connectivity comes from pump tests. When a well is pumped at a constant rate, the drawdown propagates outward through the aquifer and -- if the framework correctly identifies leaky boundaries -- also upward and downward through the aquitards. The observed drawdown in monitoring wells at various distances and depths can be compared with analytical or numerical predictions based on the framework geometry and assigned hydraulic properties.

In the Debrecen example, the framework identifies a Lower Pleistocene aquifer separated from a Middle Pleistocene aquifer by a semi-permeable aquitard. If the framework is correct, pumping the lower aquifer should produce:

- Rapid drawdown in nearby wells completed in the same aquifer
- Delayed, attenuated drawdown in wells completed in the overlying aquifer (the leaky signal)
- No drawdown in very shallow wells completed in the unconfined Quaternary aquifer (if the upper aquitard is sufficiently thick)

Marton (2009) reports that this is exactly what was observed. The leakance values derived from the pump test analysis ($b \approx 1.34 \times 10^{-4}$ d$^{-1}$) are consistent with the aquitard thickness (~10--20 m) and vertical hydraulic conductivity ($K' \approx 10^{-7}$ m/s) stored in the framework, providing a quantitative confirmation of the framework's structural and property assignments.

### 20.15.3 Isotopic and Geochemical Validation

Section 20.14.3 already demonstrated how $^{14}$C age dating validates the framework's representation of leakage. But isotopic and geochemical data can validate other aspects of the framework as well:

- **Flow direction.** If the framework predicts flow from the Nyirseg recharge area toward the Tisza discharge area, then $^{14}$C ages should increase systematically from northwest to southeast along the predicted flow path. They do.

- **Vertical connectivity.** If the framework predicts hydraulic continuity through the entire Quaternary-Pannonian sequence, then the stable isotope signature of deep waters should reflect past surface climatic conditions (not geochemical processes operating at depth). The depleted $^2$H and $^{18}$O values observed in the deep Great Plain waters confirm this prediction -- the water is meteoric (precipitation-derived), not connate.

- **Flow-system hierarchy.** If nested flow systems exist as predicted by Toth's theory, then wells at different depths in the same location should show different water chemistries, ages, and temperatures corresponding to different flow systems. Monitoring well nests throughout the Great Plain confirm this: shallow wells contain young, fresh, cool water (local system); intermediate wells contain older, more mineralized, warmer water (intermediate system); and deep wells contain very old, saline, hot water (regional system or connate).

- **Leakage-induced mixing.** The mixing of young and old water predicted by the leaky aquifer theory produces characteristic signatures in both $^{14}$C and tritium. The presence of detectable tritium in a supposedly "old" aquifer is a red flag that either the framework's representation of the confining unit is wrong (it is more permeable or discontinuous than modeled) or that the well is drawing mixed water from multiple horizons. In the Debrecen case, the simultaneous detection of low-level tritium and relatively young $^{14}$C ages in the deep aquifer confirmed that leakage -- not a flaw in the framework -- was responsible for the mixing.

### 20.15.4 The Potential-Yield Diagnostic ($\phi$--$q$ Analysis)

Marton (2009) introduced a practical empirical method for characterizing the hydraulic role of individual aquifer layers within the flow system. The method plots the potentiometric head ($\phi$) and specific well yield ($q$) measured at nested monitoring wells as a function of depth. The relationship between these two curves reveals whether a given layer is acting as a hydraulic "sink" (absorbing water from its surroundings) or a "source" (releasing water to its surroundings):

- Where the $\phi$ and $q$ trends are mirror images of each other (decreasing $\phi$ with increasing $q$, or vice versa), the layer is acting as a sink -- consistent with a recharge area where water flows downward into high-permeability layers.

- Where the $\phi$ and $q$ trends are parallel (both increasing or both decreasing together), the layer is acting as a source -- consistent with a discharge area where pressurized water is released upward.

This diagnostic has been applied to monitoring well data from across the Great Plain -- Nyirgelse, Berettyoujfalu, Szentes, Tiszakécske, Szeged -- confirming the theoretical predictions of the flow-system model and providing field-based validation of the framework's assignment of hydraulic roles to each unit (Marton, 2009). For GIS implementation, the $\phi$--$q$ analysis can be performed systematically across all monitoring well nests in the database and the results classified into recharge, discharge, and transitional zones, which are then mapped as GeoArea polygon features linked to the framework.
