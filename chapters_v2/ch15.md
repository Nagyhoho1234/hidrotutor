# Chapter 15: Mapping Where Floods Go

A river gauge tells you how high the water is at one point. A discharge forecast tells you how much water is coming. But neither tells you the question that matters most to the family living 200 metres from the riverbank: will the water reach my house, and if so, how deep will it be?

This chapter bridges the gap between a hydrological forecast -- a number, expressed in cubic metres per second -- and a spatial map of flooding that shows which buildings, roads, and fields will be underwater. The bridge is built from the HAND raster developed in Chapter 14, combined with basic hydraulic principles that have been understood since the nineteenth century. The result is a framework that can translate any discharge forecast, from any source, into an inundation map within minutes -- fast enough to be useful during a flood emergency, and scalable enough to cover an entire nation's river network.

The framework has four steps: obtain a discharge, convert it to a water depth, compare that depth to the terrain, and assess the impact on people and infrastructure. Each step is an approximation. The discharge forecast carries uncertainty from rainfall estimation, model structure, and parameter calibration. The depth conversion relies on Manning's equation, which assumes conditions that rarely hold exactly. The terrain comparison depends on the quality of the underlying DEM. And the impact assessment can only be as good as the building and population data it uses. Yet the framework works -- not perfectly, but well enough to save lives, and orders of magnitude faster than the detailed hydraulic models that would otherwise be required.

We begin with the conceptual framework, then develop the hydraulic theory that connects discharge to depth, work through two complete numerical examples -- one on Onion Creek in Texas, one on the Tisza River in Hungary -- and finally examine how this approach operates in real-time flood warning systems across the United States, Europe, and the world. Along the way, we confront the limitations honestly and compare the HAND-based approach to the established standard of HEC-RAS hydraulic modeling.

## 15.1 The Discharge-to-Impact Framework

### 15.1.1 Four Steps from Forecast to Consequence

The complete flood inundation mapping workflow consists of four sequential steps, each transforming data from one form to the next:

**Step 1: Discharge forecast.** A hydrological model or gauge observation provides the expected streamflow $Q$ (in m$^3$/s or cfs) for each stream reach. In the United States, this forecast comes from the National Water Model (NWM), which produces discharge predictions for all 2.7 million NHDPlus reaches at hourly intervals. In Europe, the European Flood Awareness System (EFAS) provides medium-range discharge forecasts at a 5 km grid resolution, while national services like Hungary's OVF (Orszagos Vizugyi Foigazgatosag) produce forecasts for the Danube, Tisza, and their tributaries through the HYDROINFO system. Globally, the Global Flood Awareness System (GloFAS) provides discharge forecasts for every major river on Earth.

**Step 2: Depth estimation.** The forecast discharge is converted to a water depth $d$ using a **rating curve** -- a mathematical relationship between discharge and water depth that is specific to each stream reach. This rating curve is derived from the HAND-based hydraulic properties of the reach, combined with Manning's equation for open-channel flow. The result is a single depth value $d$ for each stream reach.

**Step 3: Inundation mapping.** The depth $d$ for each reach is compared, cell by cell, against the HAND raster within that reach's catchment. Every grid cell whose HAND value is less than $d$ is classified as inundated, and its local flood depth is computed as the difference $d - \text{HAND}$. The output is a continuous raster of flood depths across the entire domain.

**Step 4: Impact assessment.** The inundation raster is overlaid with infrastructure data -- building footprints, address points, roads, bridges, hospitals, schools -- to determine which assets are flooded and to what depth. This step transforms a physical map into actionable information: not just "the water is here" but "427 houses are flooded to an average depth of 0.8 metres." Chapter 17 develops the demographic overlay in detail, showing how census and population data are combined with flood maps to quantify human exposure and vulnerability.

These four steps can be executed in sequence in a matter of minutes when the underlying data products have been pre-computed. The HAND raster is computed once, the rating curves are computed once, and the inundation maps at standard depth intervals can be pre-computed and stored in a library. During a flood event, only Step 1 (obtaining the latest forecast) and Step 4 (the impact overlay) require real-time computation.

### 15.1.2 The Information Model

The framework depends on a carefully constructed information model that links five types of spatial data through common identifiers. Understanding these links is essential because they determine how a single discharge number propagates outward to affect thousands of grid cells.

**Link 1: Reach to discharge.** Each stream reach has a unique identifier -- a COMID in the US NHDPlus system, a PFAF_ID in HydroSHEDS, or a local identifier in national databases like Hungary's OVF river network. The hydrological model assigns a discharge value to each reach identifier. This is the entry point of the framework.

**Link 2: Reach to depth.** Each reach has a pre-computed rating curve that maps discharge to water depth. Given the discharge from Link 1, a table lookup or interpolation produces the corresponding depth $d$. This rating curve is specific to the reach because it is derived from the reach's own terrain geometry through the HAND raster.

**Link 3: Catchment to reach.** Each grid cell in the domain belongs to exactly one catchment, and each catchment is associated with exactly one stream reach. This association was established during the flow direction and watershed delineation process described in Chapters 10 and 11 (see Section 11.6 for the catchment tessellation concept). The catchment identifier provides the bridge from the reach (where the depth is known) to the individual grid cells (where the inundation decision must be made).

**Link 4: Cell to catchment.** A raster of catchment identifiers -- where each cell's value is the identifier of the catchment it belongs to -- connects every cell in the domain to its parent catchment and, by extension, to its associated stream reach and flood depth.

**Link 5: Cell to HAND.** Each grid cell has a HAND value computed as described in Chapter 14 -- the vertical distance from the cell down to the nearest stream cell in the drainage network. This is the terrain measurement against which the flood depth is compared.

These five links form a chain: forecast model produces discharge for a reach, the rating curve converts discharge to depth, the catchment assignment identifies which cells are governed by that reach, and the HAND value at each cell determines whether it floods. The entire chain is deterministic and can be applied simultaneously across all reaches in a river network.

### 15.1.3 The Inundation Rule

The core computation is remarkably simple. For any grid cell with HAND value $h$ that belongs to a catchment whose stream reach has flood depth $d$:

$$\text{Inundation depth} = \begin{cases} d - h & \text{if } d > h \\ 0 & \text{if } d \leq h \end{cases}$$

A cell is flooded if the water depth in its associated stream reach exceeds the cell's height above that stream. The flood depth at the cell is the difference between the water level and the terrain elevation relative to the stream. Cells with HAND values greater than the flood depth remain dry.

In raster algebra, this can be expressed in a single operation. Given a HAND raster and a depth raster (where each cell's value is the flood depth assigned to its catchment's stream reach):

```
inundation = Con(depth_raster > hand_raster, depth_raster - hand_raster, 0)
```

Or equivalently in Python with NumPy arrays:

```python
inundation = np.where(depth > hand, depth - hand, 0.0)
```

The simplicity of this computation is its greatest strength. Unlike a full hydraulic model, which must solve differential equations on a computational mesh, the HAND-based inundation rule is a point-by-point comparison that can be applied to billions of grid cells in seconds. This is what makes real-time, continental-scale flood mapping possible.

### 15.1.4 Catchment-Based Spatial Differentiation

A critical feature of this approach -- and one that distinguishes it from a naive "flood everything below elevation X" method -- is that inundation is determined on a catchment-by-catchment basis. Two adjacent grid cells may have identical HAND values but experience different flood conditions if they belong to different catchments.

Consider two cells, both with HAND = 2.0 metres. Cell A belongs to a catchment on the main stem of a large river, where the forecast depth is 3.5 metres. Cell B belongs to an adjacent catchment on a small tributary, where the forecast depth is 1.2 metres. Cell A is inundated to a depth of 1.5 metres; cell B remains dry. This spatial differentiation reflects a physical reality that is often invisible on a topographic map: different streams carry different amounts of water, and the flood hazard depends not just on how low you are, but on which stream you are low relative to.

This catchment-based logic also means that the inundation map captures the distinct character of flooding along different reaches. A narrow, deeply incised channel may produce a thin ribbon of flooding even at high discharges, while a wide, flat floodplain on a downstream reach may produce flooding that extends kilometres from the channel at moderate discharges. The HAND raster encodes these geometric differences automatically, because it was computed from the same DEM that defines the channel and floodplain topography.

## 15.2 Reach-Averaged Hydraulic Properties from HAND

### 15.2.1 Why Not Cross-Sections?

Traditional hydraulic engineering describes channel geometry through discrete cross-sections -- lines drawn perpendicular to the flow direction at intervals along a river. At each cross-section, the channel bed elevation, bank heights, floodplain width, and flow area are measured by surveying or extracted from topographic data. This approach, used for over a century and encoded in software like HEC-RAS, provides detailed local information about channel geometry.

But the cross-section approach has fundamental limitations when applied at the scale of an entire river network. First, cross-sections from different surveys are often inconsistent. A cross-section surveyed in 2005 at one bridge may use a different vertical datum than one surveyed in 2012 at the next bridge. When assembled into a continuous model, these inconsistencies introduce artificial steps in the water surface profile. Second, hydraulic properties derived from individual cross-sections vary dramatically over short distances. Two cross-sections 50 metres apart on the same reach may produce wildly different depth-discharge relationships because one captures a wide point in the channel and the other captures a narrow constriction. Research has shown that it takes a certain averaging length -- approximately 1.5 kilometres for large rivers, less for small streams -- before the depth-discharge relationship stabilises. Third, and most fundamentally, assembling surveyed cross-sections for an entire river network is impractical. The continental United States has approximately 5.5 million kilometres of mapped streams (NHDPlus). Hungary alone has thousands of kilometres of rivers and canals. Surveying cross-sections for all of them at intervals close enough for hydraulic modelling would take decades and cost billions.

The HAND-based approach offers an alternative philosophy. Instead of cutting the terrain vertically at discrete locations along the river, it "slices" the terrain horizontally at successive water depths across the entire reach simultaneously. The result is a set of **reach-averaged** hydraulic properties that represent the channel and floodplain geometry as seen by a horizontal water surface rising through the terrain. This naturally provides the spatial averaging that stabilises the depth-discharge relationship, because the computation integrates over every grid cell in the reach's catchment rather than relying on a single transect.

### 15.2.2 Computing Hydraulic Properties from the HAND Raster

For a given stream reach with catchment area defined by the watershed delineation, and for a given hypothetical water depth $d$, the HAND raster allows direct computation of several hydraulic quantities. Each computation is a raster operation applied to all grid cells within the reach's catchment.

**Surface area** ($A_s$): Count the number of grid cells within the catchment for which HAND $< d$. Multiply by the cell area:

$$A_s = n_{\text{inundated}} \times (\Delta x)^2$$

where $n_{\text{inundated}}$ is the count of inundated cells and $\Delta x$ is the cell size (e.g., 10 m for a 10-metre DEM). The surface area represents the horizontal footprint of the flood at depth $d$ within this reach's catchment.

**Volume** ($V$): For each inundated cell, the local water depth is $(d - \text{HAND}_{\text{cell}})$. The total volume is the sum of these local depths multiplied by the cell area:

$$V = \sum_{\text{inundated cells}} (d - \text{HAND}_i) \times (\Delta x)^2$$

This can also be expressed as $V = \bar{d} \times A_s$, where $\bar{d}$ is the mean inundation depth over the inundated area.

**Bed area** ($A_b$): The bed area accounts for the three-dimensional slope of the terrain beneath the water. For each inundated cell with percentage slope $S$ (obtained from the slope raster computed during terrain analysis, Chapter 7):

$$A_{b,\text{cell}} = (\Delta x)^2 \times \sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{S}{100}\right)^2}$$

The total bed area is the sum over all inundated cells. For flat terrain, $A_b \approx A_s$; for steep terrain near the channel margins, $A_b > A_s$.

**Channel length** ($L$): The length of the stream reach, measured from the vector drainage line or computed as the number of stream cells multiplied by the cell size (with a correction factor for diagonal cells). In NHDPlus, the reach length is stored as an attribute. In EU-Hydro, the equivalent attribute is the segment length.

From these four primary quantities, the standard hydraulic properties used in open-channel flow calculations are derived:

**Table 15.1**: Reach-averaged hydraulic properties derived from HAND raster quantities

| Property | Symbol | Formula | Physical Meaning |
|----------|--------|---------|-----------------|
| Cross-sectional area | $A_x$ | $V / L$ | Average flow area perpendicular to the stream |
| Wetted perimeter | $P$ | $A_b / L$ | Average length of the channel boundary in contact with water |
| Top width | $T$ | $A_s / L$ | Average width of the water surface |
| Hydraulic radius | $R$ | $A_x / P$ | Ratio of flow area to wetted perimeter, governs flow resistance |

These formulas deserve a moment of interpretation. The cross-sectional area $A_x = V/L$ is the total volume of water divided by the reach length -- it gives the average cross-section that the water occupies as it flows down the reach. The wetted perimeter $P = A_b/L$ is the total wetted bed area divided by the reach length -- it gives the average perimeter of the cross-section in contact with the channel bed and banks. The hydraulic radius $R = A_x/P$ is the fundamental parameter in flow resistance equations: a large hydraulic radius indicates an efficient channel that can convey water with relatively low friction, while a small hydraulic radius indicates a shallow, wide channel with high friction relative to its flow capacity.

### 15.2.3 Building the Depth-Discharge Rating Curve

The hydraulic property computation is repeated for a sequence of water depths -- typically from 0 to the maximum expected flood depth in increments of 0.3 metres (1 foot) or finer. At each depth $d_i$, the corresponding cross-sectional area $A_{x,i}$ and hydraulic radius $R_i$ are computed. These values are then used in Manning's equation to compute the discharge $Q_i$ that corresponds to depth $d_i$.

Manning's equation for steady, uniform flow in an open channel is:

$$Q = \frac{1}{n} A_x R^{2/3} S_0^{1/2}$$

where:
- $Q$ = discharge (m$^3$/s)
- $n$ = Manning's roughness coefficient (dimensionless)
- $A_x$ = cross-sectional area (m$^2$)
- $R$ = hydraulic radius (m)
- $S_0$ = bed slope (dimensionless, m/m)

In US customary units, the equation includes a unit conversion factor:

$$Q = \frac{1.49}{n} A_x R^{2/3} S_0^{1/2}$$

where $Q$ is in cfs, $A_x$ in ft$^2$, and $R$ in ft. The factor 1.49 is the conversion from metres to feet raised to the appropriate powers (more precisely, $1/n$ in SI becomes $1.49/n$ in US customary units because $1 \text{ m}^{1/3} = 3.281^{1/3} \approx 1.486 \text{ ft}^{1/3}$).

**The bed slope** $S_0$ is estimated from the DEM as the elevation difference between the upstream and downstream ends of the reach divided by the reach length:

$$S_0 = \frac{z_{\text{upstream}} - z_{\text{downstream}}}{L}$$

In NHDPlus, the maximum and minimum elevations along each reach are stored as attributes (MAXELEVSMO and MINELEVSMO), making the slope calculation straightforward. For other datasets, the slope can be extracted from the DEM at the endpoints of the stream segment.

**Manning's roughness coefficient** $n$ is the most uncertain parameter in the entire framework. It represents the resistance to flow caused by the channel bed material, bank vegetation, channel irregularities, obstructions, and meandering. Typical values range from 0.025 for clean, straight channels to 0.05 for channels with heavy vegetation and irregular banks, to 0.10 or higher for heavily vegetated floodplains. In the US National Water Model, a spatially varying $n$ is assigned based on land cover type, but the calibration remains an active area of research. For initial HAND-based analyses, a default value of $n = 0.05$ is commonly used, acknowledging that this introduces uncertainty of perhaps 30-50% in the computed discharge.

**Table 15.2**: Representative Manning's roughness coefficient values for natural channels

| Channel Type | Manning's $n$ | Description |
|-------------|--------------|-------------|
| Clean, straight, no deep pools | 0.025 - 0.033 | Gravel or sand bed, minimal vegetation |
| Winding, some pools and shoals | 0.033 - 0.045 | Moderate irregularity, some bank vegetation |
| Sluggish, weedy, deep pools | 0.050 - 0.080 | Dense bank vegetation, irregular cross-section |
| Mountain streams, cobble/boulder bed | 0.040 - 0.070 | Steep gradient, large bed material |
| Floodplain, short grass | 0.025 - 0.035 | Agricultural fields during overbank flow |
| Floodplain, heavy timber | 0.080 - 0.120 | Forested floodplain |
| Major rivers (width > 30 m) | 0.025 - 0.060 | Depends on bed material and sinuosity |

For each depth value $d_i$, Manning's equation yields a discharge $Q_i$. The resulting set of $(Q_i, d_i)$ pairs defines the **synthetic rating curve** for that reach. In operational use, the rating curve is inverted: given a forecast discharge $Q$, the corresponding depth $d$ is found by interpolation in the $(Q, d)$ table. This depth is then applied to the HAND raster via the inundation rule.

The rating curve can also be compared with observed rating curves at gauged locations. Where USGS stream gauges exist (in the US), or OVF gauging stations exist (in Hungary), or GRDC stations exist (globally), the HAND-derived rating curve can be validated against decades of measured stage-discharge data. Discrepancies point to issues with the Manning's $n$ value, the DEM quality, or the reach segmentation.

### 15.2.4 The Physical Meaning of the Rating Curve

A rating curve is fundamentally a statement about the relationship between how much water flows through a channel and how deep that water is. For a given channel geometry and roughness, higher discharge requires deeper water -- but the relationship is not linear. At low flows, water is confined to a narrow, deep channel with an efficient hydraulic radius, and a moderate increase in discharge produces only a small increase in depth. As the water rises above the banks and spreads onto the floodplain, the same increase in discharge may produce a much smaller depth increase, because the floodplain provides enormous additional area for the water to occupy. Conversely, in a narrow valley with steep walls, depth increases rapidly with discharge because there is nowhere for the water to spread.

This non-linear behaviour is captured automatically by the HAND-based approach, because the hydraulic properties at each depth are computed directly from the terrain. A reach with a wide floodplain will show a rapid increase in surface area and volume as the water rises above bankfull depth, producing a rating curve that flattens at high flows. A reach in a narrow canyon will show surface area and volume increasing slowly with depth, producing a steep rating curve.

This is one of the great advantages of the HAND method over simplified hydraulic approximations: the terrain tells you the geometry, and the geometry determines the rating curve. You do not need to assume a trapezoidal or rectangular channel shape -- the shape is whatever the DEM says it is.

## 15.3 Worked Example: Onion Creek, Texas

### 15.3.1 The Setting

Onion Creek is a tributary of the Colorado River that flows through the southern suburbs of Austin, Texas. The watershed covers approximately 830 km$^2$ of rolling terrain in the Texas Hill Country, with elevations ranging from about 130 m at the confluence with the Colorado River to over 500 m in the headwaters. The creek has a long history of devastating floods -- the narrow limestone canyons in the upper watershed concentrate runoff quickly, and the lower reaches spread across a relatively flat floodplain that has been extensively developed with residential housing.

The October 2013 flood on Onion Creek stands as a case study in the consequences of inadequate flood warning. Heavy overnight rainfall in the upper watershed produced a flood wave that propagated downstream during the dark hours. By 6:30 AM, water had entered approximately 500 houses. Five people drowned. Residents received almost no warning because the flood originated in the remote upper watershed, far from populated areas, and conventional flood warning systems did not extend to the smaller tributaries feeding Onion Creek.

This event catalysed the development of the Texas Flood Response System, which applies HAND-based inundation mapping to every NHDPlus reach in Texas. The Onion Creek watershed, with its well-documented flood history and dense network of address points, became the primary test case.

### 15.3.2 Computing Hydraulic Properties for a Single Reach

Consider reach catchment COMID 5781733 in the Onion Creek watershed. The procedure begins with extracting the HAND raster for this catchment.

**Step 1: Isolate the catchment.** Using the catchment boundary polygon (derived from the DEM-based watershed delineation, not the NHD vector catchments, for consistency with the HAND raster), extract the HAND raster for this catchment using a mask operation.

**Step 2: Compute properties at depth $d$ = 1 metre.** Apply a threshold to identify inundated cells:

$$\text{LT1} = (\text{HAND} < 1.0)$$

This produces a binary raster where 1 indicates inundation and 0 indicates dry ground. Count the cells with value 1:

- $n_{\text{inundated}} = 658$ cells
- Cell size: $\Delta x = 10$ m
- Surface area: $A_s = 658 \times 10^2 = 65{,}800$ m$^2$

**Step 3: Compute the depth raster.** For inundated cells, the local depth is $(1.0 - \text{HAND})$. To avoid computing depths for non-inundated cells, divide by the binary mask (division by zero returns NoData):

$$\text{Depth raster} = \frac{(1.0 - \text{HAND})}{\text{LT1}}$$

The mean of the resulting raster (computed only over cells with valid values) gives the average inundation depth: $\bar{d} = 0.713$ m.

**Step 4: Compute volume.**

$$V = \bar{d} \times A_s = 0.713 \times 65{,}800 = 46{,}915 \text{ m}^3$$

**Step 5: Compute bed area.** For each inundated cell, compute the slope correction factor:

$$\text{Slope factor} = \sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{S}{100}\right)^2}$$

using the percentage slope raster. Divide by the binary mask to restrict to inundated cells. The mean slope factor is 1.00104 (indicating nearly flat terrain in the inundated zone, as expected for a floodplain). The bed area is:

$$A_b = \text{mean slope factor} \times A_s = 1.00104 \times 65{,}800 = 65{,}868 \text{ m}^2$$

**Step 6: Derive cross-sectional properties.** With a reach length of $L = 6{,}920$ m (4.3 miles):

- Cross-sectional area: $A_x = V / L = 46{,}915 / 6{,}920 = 6.78$ m$^2$
- Wetted perimeter: $P = A_b / L = 65{,}868 / 6{,}920 = 9.52$ m
- Top width: $T = A_s / L = 65{,}800 / 6{,}920 = 9.51$ m
- Hydraulic radius: $R = A_x / P = 6.78 / 9.52 = 0.712$ m

**Step 7: Apply Manning's equation.** Using $n = 0.05$ and $S_0 = 0.0163$ (typical of the Onion Creek gradient):

$$Q = \frac{1}{0.05} \times 6.78 \times 0.712^{2/3} \times 0.0163^{1/2}$$

Computing each factor:

- $1/n = 20.0$
- $R^{2/3} = 0.712^{0.667} = 0.797$
- $S_0^{1/2} = 0.0163^{0.5} = 0.1277$

$$Q = 20.0 \times 6.78 \times 0.797 \times 0.1277 = 13.8 \text{ m}^3/\text{s} \approx 487 \text{ cfs}$$

This means that when the water in this reach is 1 metre deep (relative to the channel bed, as measured by HAND), the discharge is approximately 13.8 m$^3$/s.

## 15.4 Worked Example: The Tisza River, Hungary

### 15.4.1 Why the Tisza?

The Tisza is Hungary's most flood-prone river and the country's most important case study in flood management. Rising in the Carpathian Mountains of Ukraine, the Tisza drains a basin of approximately 157,000 km$^2$ before joining the Danube in Serbia. Within Hungary, the Tisza flows for about 600 km through the Great Hungarian Plain (Alfold), a region so flat that the river's natural gradient is only 3-5 cm per kilometre. This extreme flatness has two consequences: first, flood waves propagate slowly, taking days to travel the length of the Hungarian Tisza; second, when flooding occurs, the water spreads over enormous areas because there is no topographic confinement.

Hungary has managed Tisza floods through an extensive system of levees (gatok) since the 19th century river regulation campaigns of Istvan Szechenyi and Pal Vasarhelyi. The levee system protects approximately 18,000 km$^2$ of land -- about 20% of Hungary's total area. Major floods in 1998, 1999, 2000, 2001, and 2006 tested this system to its limits, with several near-failures requiring emergency reinforcement. The 2001 Tisza flood set records at multiple stations, with water levels exceeding previous maxima by 50-100 cm.

Understanding where flood water goes on the Tisza requires exactly the kind of reach-based inundation mapping that the HAND framework provides. The OVF operates a dense network of gauging stations along the Tisza and its tributaries, providing the discharge observations needed to validate HAND-derived rating curves. The Hungarian DDM-5 digital elevation model at 5-metre resolution (available from the Lechner Knowledge Centre) provides terrain data comparable in quality to US 10-metre NED data. For areas without DDM-5 coverage, the Copernicus DEM at 10-metre (EEA-10) or 30-metre (GLO-30) resolution provides a freely available alternative.

### 15.4.2 Setting Up the HAND Analysis for a Tisza Reach

Consider a reach of the Tisza near Szolnok, one of the most flood-vulnerable cities in Hungary. Szolnok lies at the confluence of the Tisza and the Zagyva Rivers, in the heart of the Great Plain. The OVF gauging station at Szolnok has continuous records dating to the 19th century, making it an ideal validation site.

The setup requires:

- **DEM**: The DDM-5 at 5-metre resolution, projected in the Hungarian EOV coordinate system (EPSG:23700). If DDM-5 is unavailable for the specific area, the Copernicus EEA-10 DEM at 10-metre resolution serves as an alternative (requiring reprojection from WGS84 to EOV).
- **Stream network**: The OVF river network from data.vizugy.hu, which provides digitised centerlines for all Hungarian rivers and canals, analogous to the NHDPlus flowlines in the US.
- **Catchment boundaries**: Derived from the DEM using the flow direction algorithms from Chapter 10, or obtained from the EU-Hydro dataset which provides pre-delineated catchments for European rivers.
- **Gauge data**: Historical water level and discharge records from the Szolnok station, available through data.vizugy.hu (free and open since July 2024).

The HAND raster is computed following the procedures of Chapter 14: condition the DEM, compute flow directions, identify stream cells using the OVF network (stream burning), and compute the vertical distance from each cell to the nearest stream cell along the flow path.

### 15.4.3 Hydraulic Properties and Rating Curve for the Szolnok Reach

The Tisza at Szolnok has markedly different characteristics from Onion Creek in Texas. The channel is approximately 200-400 metres wide, the floodplain extends 2-5 kilometres on either side between the levees, and the bed slope is extremely gentle at approximately $S_0 = 0.00004$ (4 cm per kilometre). These differences profoundly affect the rating curve.

For a hypothetical reach length of $L = 10{,}000$ m (10 km) near Szolnok, we compute properties at several depth values. Because the Tisza has a wide, flat floodplain, the number of inundated cells increases rapidly with depth:

The contrast with Onion Creek is instructive. On Onion Creek, a 3-metre depth produces about 170 m$^3$/s through a relatively steep, narrow channel. But the Tisza's flood depth of 8-10 metres corresponds to a discharge of 3,500-7,000 m$^3$/s spread across a floodplain several kilometres wide. The HAND framework handles both cases without modification -- the terrain geometry and the slope do all the work.

### 15.4.4 Validation Against OVF Gauge Data

The OVF gauging station at Szolnok provides an observed rating curve built from decades of direct discharge measurements. This curve can be compared directly against the HAND-derived synthetic rating curve. Typical findings in such comparisons are:

- **At low flows** (within the main channel), the HAND-derived rating curve tends to overestimate depth for a given discharge, because the DEM typically represents the water surface rather than the channel bed. The DEM "sees" the water, not the bottom. This means the channel appears shallower than it actually is, and the rating curve requires more depth to convey the same discharge.

- **At moderate flows** (near bankfull), agreement is generally good, because the DEM accurately represents the bank and near-channel topography, and the transition from channel to floodplain is captured well by the HAND raster.

- **At high flows** (overbank), agreement depends on the accuracy of the floodplain topography in the DEM. For the DDM-5 at 5-metre resolution, the floodplain is well-resolved, and the HAND-derived rating curve typically agrees with observations within 15-25%. For the Copernicus GLO-30 at 30-metre resolution, the floodplain representation is coarser, and errors may be larger.

The most effective calibration strategy is to adjust the Manning's $n$ value until the HAND-derived rating curve matches the observed one at bankfull and overbank flows, accepting the bias at low flows as a limitation of the DEM.

## 15.5 Pre-Computed Inundation Libraries

### 15.5.1 The Concept

For operational efficiency, flood inundation maps can be pre-computed at fixed depth intervals for every reach in the domain. Instead of running the inundation computation in real time during a flood event, the system simply looks up the appropriate pre-computed map from a library indexed by reach identifier and depth.

The National Water Center in the United States has pre-computed inundation maps for all 2.7 million NHDPlus reaches at 1-foot (0.3 m) depth intervals from 0 to approximately 25 feet. For each reach at each depth, the library stores a raster or vector polygon showing the inundation extent and depth within that reach's catchment. During a flood event, the operational system:

1. Receives the latest NWM discharge forecast for each reach.
2. Converts each reach's discharge to a depth using the pre-computed rating curve.
3. Rounds the depth to the nearest 1-foot increment.
4. Retrieves the corresponding pre-computed inundation map from the library.
5. Assembles all reach-level maps into a seamless national mosaic.

This lookup-based approach eliminates the need for any raster computation during the event. The entire process -- from receiving a new discharge forecast to displaying an updated inundation map -- takes under three minutes, fast enough to keep pace with the hourly NWM forecast cycle.

### 15.5.2 Storage and Computational Requirements

Pre-computing inundation libraries is a massive but one-time computational task. For the continental United States:

- 2.7 million reaches, each with approximately 25 depth increments, produces approximately 67.5 million individual inundation maps.
- Each map covers only one catchment (typically a few square kilometres), so the individual files are small -- typically a few kilobytes to a few hundred kilobytes.
- The total storage is on the order of tens of terabytes.
- The computation can be parallelised across reaches, since each reach's inundation map is independent of all others.

On modern cloud computing infrastructure, this computation can be completed in a matter of hours using thousands of parallel workers. The initial computation for the US was performed at the National Water Center on the Cray supercomputer housed at the facility, but the same computation could now be performed on commercial cloud platforms (AWS, Google Cloud, Azure) for a few thousand dollars.

For Hungary, the computational requirements are far more modest. The Hungarian river network, while extensive, comprises thousands rather than millions of reaches. A pre-computed library at 0.5-metre depth intervals for all major rivers and tributaries could be computed on a single modern workstation in a matter of days.

### 15.5.3 The European Approach: JRC Flood Hazard Maps

The European Union has adopted a complementary approach to pre-computed flood mapping through the Joint Research Centre (JRC). Rather than computing inundation from real-time discharge forecasts, the JRC River Flood Hazard Maps provide static flood extent maps for multiple return periods -- the 10-year, 20-year, 50-year, 100-year, 200-year, and 500-year floods. These maps are derived from a combination of EFAS hydrological modelling and hydraulic simulation using the LISFLOOD model, applied across the entire European river network.

The JRC flood hazard maps are available for free download from the European Commission's data portal. They cover all EU member states at a resolution derived from the Copernicus DEM and provide flood extent polygons that can be used for land-use planning, insurance assessments, and emergency preparedness.

Hungary has also produced its own flood hazard and risk maps under the EU Floods Directive (2007/60/EC), administered by the OVF. These maps show flood extent for various return periods (low, medium, and high probability scenarios corresponding roughly to 100-year, medium, and extreme events) and are available through the OVF geoportal (geoportal.vizugy.hu) and the vizeink.hu flood risk management portal.

## 15.6 Impact Assessment

### 15.6.1 From Flood Map to Consequence

An inundation map answers a physical question: where is the water, and how deep? Impact assessment answers the human question: who is affected, and how badly? The transition from one to the other requires overlaying the inundation map with data about what exists in the flooded area -- buildings, roads, utilities, population.

The most direct form of impact assessment uses address points or building footprints. In the United States, address point databases have been compiled for emergency dispatch (911) systems, and they are available for most states. Texas, for example, has 9.28 million address points representing every building that can receive emergency services. Each point has a geographic coordinate (latitude, longitude) that can be intersected with the inundation raster.

For each address point, the HAND value is extracted by sampling the HAND raster at the point's location. This HAND value represents the building's height above its nearest stream. During a flood event, the building is flooded if its HAND value is less than the flood depth assigned to the reach in its catchment. The flood depth at the building is the difference:

$$d_{\text{building}} = d_{\text{reach}} - \text{HAND}_{\text{building}}$$

This computation can be performed for millions of address points in seconds, producing a complete inventory of flooded buildings with individual depth estimates.

### 15.6.2 Building-Level Flood Risk Classification

HAND values provide a persistent measure of flood exposure that does not change with any particular event. A building with HAND = 1.5 metres is always more flood-prone than a building with HAND = 8.0 metres, regardless of the specific discharge forecast. This makes HAND a powerful screening tool for flood risk assessment, analogous to but distinct from FEMA flood zone designations in the US or flood hazard class assignments under the EU Floods Directive.

A simple classification based on HAND value might look like:

**Table 15.7**: Building flood risk classification based on HAND values

| HAND Value | Risk Level | Interpretation |
|-----------|-----------|----------------|
| 0 - 2 m | Very High | Flooded in moderate events; immediate proximity to channel |
| 2 - 5 m | High | Flooded in major events; within active floodplain |
| 5 - 10 m | Moderate | Flooded only in extreme events; floodplain margin |
| 10 - 20 m | Low | Flooded only in extraordinary events; terrace or hillslope |
| > 20 m | Negligible | Above any plausible flood level |

These thresholds are approximate and depend on the local hydrology. On a large, flat-gradient river like the Tisza, major floods can produce water depths of 8-10 metres, so buildings with HAND values up to 10 metres are genuinely at risk. On a steep mountain stream, even extreme floods rarely produce depths greater than 3-4 metres, and the "High" category might be defined as HAND < 3 m.

The advantage of HAND-based risk classification over traditional floodplain mapping (such as FEMA's 100-year flood zone) is that HAND is computed everywhere, for every building, using the same algorithm. Traditional floodplain maps are produced reach by reach through detailed hydraulic studies, and large portions of the stream network -- particularly smaller streams and tributaries -- have never been studied. In the US, fewer than 25% of stream miles have detailed FEMA flood maps. The HAND approach fills this gap by providing at least a screening-level risk estimate for every building in the country.

### 15.6.3 The Onion Creek Impact Analysis

Returning to the October 2013 flood on Onion Creek: with the reach-specific depth assignments from Table 15.4 and the 355,000 address points in Travis County, a complete impact analysis identifies every building within the inundated area and estimates its flood depth.

The analysis proceeds as follows:

1. For each of the 355,000 address points, sample the HAND raster to obtain the building's HAND value.
2. For each address point, determine which catchment it belongs to (from the catchment raster) and therefore which reach governs its flood depth.
3. Compare the reach flood depth against the HAND value. If the flood depth exceeds HAND, the building is flooded.
4. Compute the depth of flooding at each building: $d_{\text{building}} = d_{\text{reach}} - \text{HAND}$.
5. Summarise the results: count of flooded buildings, distribution of flood depths, total affected population (using census data linked to addresses).

For the 2013 event, this analysis correctly identifies approximately 500 flooded structures, consistent with the post-event damage assessments. The HAND-derived flood depths are also consistent with high-water marks measured by USGS survey teams after the event.

The real power of this approach is not retrospective analysis but prospective prediction. If the National Water Model had been operational in 2013 (it became operational in 2016), it could have provided discharge forecasts for Onion Creek hours before the flood peak. The HAND-based system would have converted those forecasts into inundation maps showing exactly which houses were in the path of the flood, giving emergency managers time to issue evacuation orders.

### 15.6.4 Hungarian Impact Assessment: Address and Population Data

In Hungary, the equivalent of the US address point database is maintained by the national address registry and municipal databases. The KSH (Kozponti Statisztikai Hivatal, the Hungarian Central Statistical Office) provides population data at multiple spatial resolutions: county (megye), district (jaras), municipality (telepules), and census tract (szamlalokoerzet). Building-level data, including address locations and building types, can be obtained from municipal cadastral databases or the national building registry.

For flood impact assessment on the Tisza, the workflow is conceptually identical to the Onion Creek analysis:

1. Compute HAND values at all building locations using the DDM-5 DEM and OVF river network.
2. Assign flood depths from the EFAS forecast or OVF operational forecast.
3. Identify flooded buildings and estimate depths.
4. Link to KSH population data to estimate affected population.

The EU Floods Directive requires member states to produce flood risk maps that show not only flood extent but also "the indicative number of inhabitants potentially affected," "the type of economic activity of the area potentially affected," and environmental and cultural heritage assets at risk. Hungary's flood risk maps, produced by OVF under this directive, integrate inundation extent with land use, population, and infrastructure data in a manner closely analogous to the HAND-based impact assessment framework.

An important additional data source for Hungarian flood impact assessment is Eurostat's GISCO, which provides standardised population grids and administrative boundaries for all EU member states. The 1 km$^2$ population grid, available from Eurostat, enables rapid population exposure estimates without requiring building-level data -- a useful approximation when detailed address data is unavailable.

## 15.7 Real-Time Flood Inundation Mapping

### 15.7.1 The Operational Pipeline in the United States

The National Water Model, operated by NOAA's Office of Water Prediction at the National Water Center in Tuscaloosa, Alabama, produces discharge forecasts at multiple time horizons: short-range (18 hours), medium-range (10 days), and long-range (30 days). These forecasts cover all 2.7 million NHDPlus reaches in the continental United States.

The real-time inundation mapping pipeline processes these forecasts as follows:

1. The NWM produces a new short-range forecast every hour, outputting discharge values for every reach.
2. For each reach where the forecast discharge exceeds the flood threshold (typically the 2-year recurrence discharge), the system looks up the corresponding depth from the pre-computed rating curve.
3. The depth is rounded to the nearest increment in the pre-computed inundation library, and the corresponding inundation map is retrieved.
4. All reach-level maps with active flooding are assembled into a mosaic covering the affected region.
5. The impact assessment module identifies buildings, roads, and critical infrastructure within the inundated area.
6. The results are published through web services and displayed on the National Water Center's operational dashboard.

The entire pipeline -- from receiving the forecast to publishing the inundation map -- completes in under three minutes. This speed is possible because the computationally expensive work (HAND computation, hydraulic property tabulation, rating curve derivation, and inundation library generation) was all done in advance. The real-time component is pure table lookup and spatial overlay.

### 15.7.2 The European Approach: EFAS and Copernicus EMS

Europe's approach to real-time flood mapping operates through two complementary systems. The European Flood Awareness System (EFAS), operated by ECMWF under the Copernicus Emergency Management Service, provides medium-range (up to 10 days) flood forecasts for all major European rivers. EFAS uses the LISFLOOD hydrological model driven by ECMWF weather forecasts to produce probabilistic discharge predictions. When these predictions exceed flood warning thresholds, EFAS alerts national hydrological services, including Hungary's OVF, which then activate their own detailed forecasting systems.

EFAS does not directly produce HAND-based inundation maps in the manner of the US National Water Model. Instead, flood extent mapping in Europe relies on two approaches:

**Pre-computed hazard maps.** The JRC flood hazard maps described in Section 15.5.3 provide static flood extents for standard return periods. During an event, the forecast return period (e.g., "this is approximately a 50-year flood") is matched to the corresponding pre-computed map. This approach is simple and fast but lacks the reach-specific depth detail of the HAND method.

**On-demand satellite-based mapping.** The Copernicus Emergency Management Service (EMS) provides rapid mapping during flood events using satellite imagery, primarily from Sentinel-1 SAR (which can penetrate clouds) and Sentinel-2 optical sensors. When a major flood occurs, national civil protection authorities can request activation of the Copernicus EMS, which produces detailed flood extent maps within hours of satellite acquisition. These satellite-derived maps show the actual flood extent (not a modelled prediction) and serve as validation data for both the pre-computed hazard maps and the operational forecasts.

Hungary's OVF operates the HYDROINFO system (www.hydroinfo.hu) for real-time water level monitoring and flood forecasting along the Danube, Tisza, and their tributaries. HYDROINFO provides water level observations and short-range forecasts at 98 forecast sections, updated continuously. When water levels approach or exceed flood warning thresholds (I, II, or III degree flood alerts in the Hungarian system), the OVF activates its flood defence operations, which include real-time inundation assessment in the protected floodplain areas.

### 15.7.3 Global Systems: GloFAS

The Global Flood Awareness System (GloFAS), also operated by ECMWF under Copernicus, extends flood forecasting to the entire globe at 0.05-degree (~5 km) resolution. GloFAS provides:

- Medium-range (30-day) probabilistic flood forecasts for every major river
- Seasonal (up to 4-month) flood outlooks
- Historical reanalysis dating back to 1979

GloFAS flood forecasts are expressed as probabilities of exceeding threshold discharges (e.g., "60% probability that the 5-year return period flood will be exceeded in the next 10 days"). These forecasts are available freely through the GloFAS web portal and are used by national hydrological services worldwide, including those in countries with limited national forecasting capacity.

While GloFAS does not currently produce HAND-based inundation maps at the global scale, research efforts are underway to develop global HAND datasets (using the MERIT DEM and HydroSHEDS river network) that could enable the same discharge-to-inundation pipeline described in this chapter for any river on Earth. The potential to provide building-level flood impact assessments in countries that currently have no flood mapping capability represents one of the most important applications of the HAND framework.

### 15.7.4 Crowd-Sourced Validation

No flood map is useful if it cannot be trusted, and trust requires validation. One innovative approach to validation uses observations from emergency responders and citizens during flood events. The concept is simple: an observer stands at the water's edge during a flood, records their GPS coordinates, and reports the observation to a central system. The system then uses the HAND raster to extrapolate the inundation extent corresponding to the observed water level at that point.

An application called "Pin to Flood Map," developed at the University of Texas at Austin, implements this concept. When an observer drops a pin at the water's edge, the app samples the HAND raster at that location. The HAND value at the water's edge equals the local flood depth (because the water surface is at exactly the HAND height at the boundary of the inundated area). This depth is then applied to all surrounding cells within the same catchment to produce a local inundation map -- essentially using the real-world observation to replace the model forecast.

Multiple pins from different observers build up a composite picture of the actual flood extent that can be compared against the model-predicted extent. Where the model overpredicts flooding (shows areas as wet that observers report as dry), the rating curve or Manning's $n$ may need adjustment. Where the model underpredicts, the DEM may be missing a levee breach, a blocked culvert, or some other feature that concentrates flow in unexpected ways.

This approach is particularly valuable in Hungary and other countries where the real-time discharge forecasting system may not cover smaller tributaries. Citizen observations during flood events can provide ground truth that is otherwise unavailable, and the HAND framework provides the mathematical structure to convert point observations into spatial maps.

## 15.8 Comparison with HEC-RAS

### 15.8.1 What HEC-RAS Does Differently

HEC-RAS (Hydrologic Engineering Center's River Analysis System), developed by the US Army Corps of Engineers, is the gold standard for detailed hydraulic modelling of rivers and floodplains. It has been used for decades for floodplain mapping, bridge design, dam breach analysis, and environmental flow assessment. To understand the HAND approach properly, it is essential to understand what HEC-RAS does that HAND does not.

HEC-RAS solves the one-dimensional (1D) or two-dimensional (2D) Saint-Venant equations, which describe the full physics of unsteady, non-uniform open-channel flow. The Saint-Venant equations consist of two coupled partial differential equations:

**Continuity (conservation of mass):**

$$\frac{\partial A}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} = q_l$$

**Momentum (conservation of momentum):**

$$\frac{\partial Q}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left(\frac{Q^2}{A}\right) + gA\frac{\partial h}{\partial x} + gAS_f = 0$$

where $A$ is the cross-sectional area, $Q$ is the discharge, $q_l$ is the lateral inflow per unit length, $g$ is gravitational acceleration, $h$ is the water surface elevation, $S_f$ is the friction slope, $t$ is time, and $x$ is the distance along the channel.

These equations account for effects that the HAND approach ignores entirely:

- **Unsteadiness**: The water level changes with time as the flood wave passes. HEC-RAS tracks this temporal evolution; HAND assumes a steady state at each time step.
- **Non-uniformity**: The water surface slope varies along the reach due to changes in channel geometry, backwater from downstream constrictions, and drawdown at drops. HEC-RAS computes these variations; HAND assumes a horizontal water surface within each reach.
- **Momentum**: The inertia of the flowing water affects the water surface profile, particularly near bridges, channel expansions, and confluences. HEC-RAS includes these effects; HAND ignores them.
- **Bridge and structure hydraulics**: HEC-RAS can model flow through bridges, culverts, weirs, dams, and levee breaches with specialised hydraulic formulations. HAND has no concept of structures.
- **2D floodplain flow**: HEC-RAS 2D solves the shallow water equations on a computational mesh, allowing water to flow laterally across the floodplain with physically correct velocities and depths. HAND has no lateral flow -- water simply appears wherever HAND < depth.

### 15.8.2 What HAND Does That HEC-RAS Cannot

Despite its simplicity, the HAND approach has several advantages that make it irreplaceable for certain applications:

**Scale.** A HEC-RAS model for a single river reach might require days of setup: surveyed cross-sections or detailed bathymetry, bridge geometry, roughness calibration, boundary conditions, and extensive quality checking. The US has roughly 5.5 million kilometres of streams. Producing HEC-RAS models for all of them would require decades. HAND-based rating curves can be computed for every reach in the country from existing DEM data, with no site-specific data collection required.

**Speed.** A HEC-RAS 2D unsteady flow simulation for a 100 km river reach might take hours to complete, even on a fast computer. The HAND inundation lookup takes milliseconds. For real-time emergency response, where a new inundation map is needed every hour, only the HAND approach is fast enough.

**Consistency.** Because the HAND approach uses the same algorithm and the same DEM for every reach, the results are internally consistent across the entire network. HEC-RAS models built by different engineers at different times with different data sources may produce results that are locally excellent but globally inconsistent.

**Coverage.** In the US, FEMA has produced detailed flood maps (based on HEC-RAS or similar models) for fewer than 25% of the nation's stream miles. The remaining 75% have no detailed flood mapping at all. The HAND approach provides at least a screening-level assessment for every stream mile, including the small headwater streams where flash floods often cause the most casualties.

### 15.8.3 The Complementary View

The HAND approach and HEC-RAS are not competitors -- they are complementary tools that serve different purposes and operate at different scales. The relationship is analogous to a weather forecast and a wind tunnel test: the forecast covers the entire domain at moderate accuracy, while the wind tunnel test provides exquisite detail for a single structure.

In practice, the most effective flood management systems use both approaches:

- **HAND** provides national-scale, real-time flood mapping that identifies which areas are at risk, which communities need to be alerted, and where emergency resources should be deployed.
- **HEC-RAS** provides detailed, design-level analysis for specific high-risk reaches where accuracy matters most: urban floodplains, dam breach zones, bridge crossings, and critical infrastructure.

The HAND-based screening can also guide the prioritisation of HEC-RAS studies. By identifying the reaches where the most buildings are at risk (using the impact assessment described in Section 15.6), water management agencies can focus their limited resources for detailed modelling on the reaches where it matters most.

| Spatial scale | Continental (millions of reaches) | Local to regional (single reach to ~100 km) |
| Temporal resolution | Snapshot at each forecast time | Continuous time-varying water surfaces |
| Backwater effects | Not represented | Fully represented |
| Structure hydraulics | Not represented | Bridges, culverts, weirs, dams |
| Accuracy (overbank) | Approximate (20-50% depth error typical) | High (5-15% depth error with calibration) |

| Real-time capability | Yes (hourly updates) | Limited (too slow for most operational use) |
| Best use | Screening, emergency response, national-scale | Design, regulation, detailed analysis |

## 15.9 International Flood Inundation Mapping Systems

### 15.9.1 The European Flood Awareness System (EFAS)

EFAS, operated by ECMWF under the Copernicus Emergency Management Service, represents Europe's primary tool for transnational flood early warning. Operational since 2012, EFAS provides flood forecasts for all major European rivers using the LISFLOOD distributed hydrological model driven by ECMWF weather forecasts. The system produces:

- **Deterministic forecasts** based on the ECMWF high-resolution (HRES) atmospheric model, updated twice daily with a 10-day horizon.
- **Probabilistic forecasts** based on the ECMWF ensemble prediction system (ENS), providing 51 equally likely scenarios for the next 15 days.
- **Flash flood indicators** based on rapid rainfall accumulation and soil moisture conditions, providing warnings for small catchments where the standard river routing model is too coarse.
- **Soil moisture and snow water equivalent** maps updated daily, providing the initial conditions that determine how much additional rainfall a catchment can absorb before flooding occurs.

EFAS does not send public alerts -- it sends notifications to EFAS partner agencies (the national hydrological services of EU member states), which then translate the pan-European forecasts into national-level warnings. Hungary's OVF is an EFAS partner and receives EFAS notifications for the Danube and Tisza basins, complementing its own national forecasting system.

The EFAS archive (publicly accessible for registered users) provides a historical record of flood events across Europe that can be used for research, model validation, and trend analysis. This archive includes the flood events that have tested Hungarian flood defences most severely: the 2002 Elbe-Danube floods, the 2006 Danube flood, the 2010 Tisza flood, and the 2013 central European floods.

### 15.9.2 JRC Flood Products

The JRC (Joint Research Centre of the European Commission) produces several flood-related products that complement EFAS:

**River Flood Hazard Maps for Europe.** GeoTIFF rasters showing flood extent for return periods of 10, 20, 50, 100, 200, and 500 years. These are derived from the EFAS/LISFLOOD model calibrated against observed flood data. Available for free download from the JRC data catalogue (data.jrc.ec.europa.eu, collection id-0054).

**Global Flood Monitoring.** A near-real-time system that detects flood events from satellite imagery (primarily Sentinel-1 SAR and Copernicus Contributing Missions). When flooding is detected, the system produces extent maps that are made publicly available within hours.

**European and Global Drought Observatories (EDO/GDO).** While not directly related to flood inundation, these systems monitor drought conditions that affect antecedent soil moisture and therefore flood risk. A prolonged drought hardens and cracks the soil, reducing infiltration capacity and increasing runoff during subsequent rainfall events.

### 15.9.3 Hungarian National Flood Mapping Under the EU Floods Directive

Hungary's implementation of the EU Floods Directive (2007/60/EC) has produced a comprehensive set of flood hazard and risk maps for the country's major rivers. The directive requires three categories of maps:

**Flood hazard maps** showing the flood extent, water depth, and (where appropriate) flow velocity for three scenarios:
- Low probability: extreme flood events (e.g., 1000-year return period)
- Medium probability: the design flood (approximately 100-year return period)
- High probability: frequent floods (e.g., 10-20 year return period)

**Flood risk maps** showing, for each of the above scenarios:
- Indicative number of inhabitants potentially affected
- Type of economic activity in the area
- Environmental impacts (protected areas, pollution sources)
- Cultural heritage at risk

These maps cover the Tisza, Danube, Drava, and their major tributaries within Hungary. They were first produced in 2013 and revised in 2019 and 2025, with each revision incorporating improved topographic data, updated hydrological analyses, and lessons from recent flood events.

The maps are produced using detailed hydraulic models (HEC-RAS and similar) for the major rivers, supplemented by HAND-like simplified approaches for smaller tributaries where detailed hydraulic data is unavailable. The OVF manages the flood hazard and risk maps and makes them available through the geoportal.vizugy.hu platform and the vizeink.hu flood risk management portal.

### 15.9.4 Global Perspective: Flood Mapping in Data-Sparse Regions

In many parts of the world -- sub-Saharan Africa, South and Southeast Asia, and much of Latin America -- detailed flood mapping is either absent or severely limited. River networks are poorly mapped, gauging stations are sparse, and high-resolution DEMs may not exist. Yet these regions face some of the world's most devastating flooding: the Brahmaputra in Bangladesh, the Niger in West Africa, the Mekong in Southeast Asia.

For these regions, the HAND framework offers a practical path forward. The key inputs -- a DEM and a stream network -- are available globally:

- The **Copernicus DEM GLO-30** provides 30-metre terrain data for the entire globe.
- The **MERIT DEM** provides error-corrected 90-metre terrain data optimised for hydrological applications.
- **HydroSHEDS** and **HydroRIVERS** provide pre-computed stream networks and watershed boundaries derived from SRTM and MERIT data.
- **GloFAS** provides discharge forecasts for every major river.

Computing a global HAND raster from these inputs is technically feasible, and research groups at several universities and the World Bank have demonstrated prototypes. When combined with population data from WorldPop (100 m resolution, freely available) or LandScan (1 km resolution), the HAND framework can provide at least a screening-level flood impact assessment for any location on Earth.

This is not a replacement for detailed national flood mapping programs -- it is a stopgap that provides actionable information in regions where nothing else exists. For a community in the Niger Delta or the Brahmaputra floodplain, knowing that "your village has a HAND value of 1.5 metres and the upstream forecast suggests 3 metres of flooding" is immensely more useful than having no information at all.

## 15.10 Limitations and Sources of Error

### 15.10.1 Hydraulic Simplifications

The HAND-based inundation framework rests on Manning's equation, which assumes steady, uniform flow. In reality, flood flows are neither steady nor uniform. The key hydraulic simplifications and their consequences are:

**Steady flow assumption.** Manning's equation computes the discharge for a water depth that is constant in time. During a real flood, the water level is rising on the ascending limb of the hydrograph and falling on the descending limb. The rising limb produces higher discharges for a given depth than the steady-state prediction (because the flood wave adds momentum), while the falling limb produces lower discharges. This effect is relatively small for slow-rising floods (like Tisza floods, where the hydrograph evolves over days) but can be significant for flash floods (like Onion Creek events, where the hydrograph peaks in hours).

**Uniform flow assumption.** Manning's equation assumes the water surface slope equals the bed slope -- that is, the flow is uniform along the reach. In reality, the water surface slope varies due to changes in cross-section geometry, backwater from downstream constrictions, and drawdown at drops or expansions. Near bridges, the backwater effect can raise the water level by 1-2 metres upstream of the structure. The HAND approach, which assigns a single depth to each reach, cannot capture these local variations.

**Single depth per reach.** Each reach receives one flood depth, applied uniformly to all cells in the catchment. In reality, depth varies along the reach -- it may be deeper at constrictions and shallower where the floodplain widens. The spatial averaging inherent in the reach-based approach smooths out these variations. The error diminishes as reach length decreases relative to the scale of geometric variation, which is why some researchers advocate for more uniform reach segmentation.

**No lateral momentum.** When water overtops the channel banks, it flows laterally across the floodplain with its own velocity, depth, and direction. The HAND approach has no concept of lateral flow -- it simply classifies cells as wet or dry based on their height relative to the stream. This means the approach cannot capture the dynamics of floodplain flow: the velocity of overbank water, the time it takes for the floodplain to fill and drain, or the preferential flow paths through topographic depressions on the floodplain.

### 15.10.2 DEM Quality and Bathymetry

The quality of HAND-based flood mapping is ultimately limited by the quality of the DEM from which the HAND raster is computed:

**Vertical accuracy.** The US National Elevation Dataset has a root-mean-square error (RMSE) of approximately 2.34 metres compared to survey benchmarks. This means that individual HAND values may be in error by 2 or more metres -- a significant fraction of the flood depth for moderate events. High-resolution LiDAR (with vertical RMSE of 0.10-0.15 m) reduces this error by an order of magnitude. Hungary's DDM-5, derived from photogrammetry rather than LiDAR, has intermediate accuracy. The Copernicus DEM GLO-30 has a reported vertical accuracy of less than 4 metres (LE90), adequate for large rivers but marginal for small streams.

**Horizontal resolution.** A 10-metre DEM can resolve channels wider than about 20 metres but cannot represent narrow headwater channels. A 1-metre LiDAR DEM resolves channels as narrow as 2-3 metres. The practical effect is that HAND values in narrow valleys are more accurate with high-resolution data, and the inundation mapping in densely built areas (where buildings, walls, and other structures affect flow paths) improves dramatically with resolution.

**Bathymetry.** Most DEMs, including LiDAR-derived products, represent the water surface rather than the channel bed. In a river with 2 metres of water, the DEM shows the water surface, and the HAND value for cells in the channel is biased high by approximately 2 metres. This means the channel appears shallower than it really is, and the rating curve requires more depth (and therefore more discharge) to produce the same inundation extent. Green-beam LiDAR can penetrate clear water to measure the channel bed, and acoustic Doppler current profilers (ADCPs) mounted on boats can measure submerged channel geometry, but these data are not widely available. Where available, merging above-water LiDAR with below-water bathymetry into a "topobathy" DEM substantially improves the HAND-derived hydraulic properties.

### 15.10.3 Manning's Roughness Coefficient

The Manning's $n$ value is the single largest source of uncertainty in the HAND-derived rating curve. A 20% change in $n$ produces approximately a 20% change in the predicted discharge for a given depth (since $Q \propto 1/n$). In practice, the true roughness varies spatially within a reach (the channel bed is smoother than the vegetated banks), temporally with season (higher roughness when vegetation is fully leafed out), and with flow depth (at high flows, the roughness of the submerged vegetation matters less relative to the increased flow area).

Calibrating $n$ against observed rating curves at gauged locations is the most reliable approach, but gauged locations represent a tiny fraction of all reaches. For ungauged reaches, $n$ must be estimated from land cover data (NLCD in the US, CORINE Land Cover in Europe, ESA WorldCover globally) using published lookup tables. This estimation introduces uncertainty that propagates directly into the inundation map.

Research at the National Water Center has shown that ensemble approaches -- running the inundation mapping with multiple plausible $n$ values and reporting the range of outcomes -- can be more informative than a single best estimate. A flood manager who knows that "between 200 and 400 houses are at risk, depending on roughness assumptions" can plan more effectively than one who receives a single number with unknown uncertainty.

### 15.10.4 Stream Network Segmentation

The NHDPlus reaches that define the spatial units of analysis were not designed with hydraulic calculations in mind. Some reaches are very short (a few hundred metres), while others are very long (tens of kilometres). Short reaches may have insufficient terrain data to compute stable hydraulic properties, while long reaches may span significantly varying channel geometries that are poorly represented by a single set of averaged properties.

Research by Zheng and others has shown that a more uniform segmentation -- with reaches of approximately equal length (1-2 km) and more regularly shaped catchments -- produces better inundation mapping results. The HAND computation itself is independent of the segmentation (it depends only on the DEM and the stream network), but the rating curve derivation and the depth assignment to catchments are sensitive to how the network is partitioned. Re-segmenting existing networks (like NHDPlus or EU-Hydro) for optimal hydraulic property estimation remains an active area of research.

## 15.11 Improving the Approach

### 15.11.1 High-Resolution LiDAR

The single most impactful improvement to HAND-based inundation mapping is the availability of high-resolution LiDAR data. At 1-metre resolution, the DEM resolves individual buildings, roads, levees, ditches, and channel banks that are invisible at 10-metre resolution. The hydraulic properties computed from such data are substantially more accurate, particularly for narrow channels and urban areas.

The US is investing heavily in LiDAR acquisition through the 3DEP (3D Elevation Program), which aims to provide nationwide LiDAR coverage. Texas alone has invested approximately $40 million in LiDAR data acquisition covering 180,000 square miles. In Europe, several countries have completed or are pursuing nationwide LiDAR programs: the Netherlands (AHN, at 8+ points/m$^2$), the United Kingdom (Environment Agency LiDAR, covering England at 1-2 m resolution), and Denmark (nationwide at 4-5 points/m$^2$). Hungary does not have a nationwide LiDAR program, but the DDM-5 at 5-metre resolution provides a reasonable intermediate product, and project-specific LiDAR surveys exist for flood-prone valleys and karst areas (notably the Aggtelek Karst).

Because the HAND computation is performed independently for each catchment, the approach naturally accommodates mixed-resolution data: high-resolution LiDAR can be used where available, and coarser DEM data elsewhere. This allows incremental improvement as new LiDAR data becomes available, without requiring the entire domain to be reprocessed at once.

### 15.11.2 Integrating Topography and Bathymetry

A complete channel geometry requires both above-water topography (from LiDAR or photogrammetry) and below-water bathymetry (from green-beam LiDAR, multibeam sonar, or ADCP surveys). When merged into a single "topobathy" DEM, the HAND-derived hydraulic properties represent the true channel cross-section rather than an approximation based on the water surface.

The creation of topobathy DEMs is becoming more common in flood-prone areas. The USGS has produced topobathy DEMs for many coastal areas and major rivers. In Europe, national hydrographic services (including Hungary's OVF) maintain bathymetric surveys of navigable rivers that could be integrated with LiDAR or photogrammetric DEMs. The technical challenge is merging data from different sources, coordinate systems, and epochs into a seamless product -- a GIS integration problem that the tools from earlier chapters in this book are well-equipped to solve.

### 15.11.3 Improved Channel Routing with HAND Properties

The National Water Model and EFAS both route water through channel networks using simplified routing methods that assume idealised channel geometries. The NWM uses the RAPID (Routing Application for Parallel computatIon of Discharge) method with Muskingum routing, which requires estimates of channel cross-sectional area and celerity as functions of discharge. Currently, these estimates use simplified relationships that do not reflect the actual channel geometry.

The HAND-derived hydraulic property tables -- cross-sectional area $A_x(d)$, hydraulic radius $R(d)$, and wetted perimeter $P(d)$ as functions of depth -- could replace these simplified estimates with terrain-based values for every reach. This would improve the channel routing by providing more realistic flow velocities, travel times, and storage effects, which in turn would improve the timing and magnitude of downstream flood predictions. This integration of HAND-derived properties into the routing component of national hydrological models is an active area of development at the National Water Center.

### 15.11.4 Machine Learning for Roughness Estimation

Recent research has explored the use of machine learning to estimate Manning's $n$ from observable landscape characteristics. The idea is to train a model on gauged reaches -- where the true roughness can be estimated by calibrating the rating curve against observed data -- and then predict roughness for ungauged reaches based on features extractable from remote sensing and GIS data: land cover type, vegetation density (from Sentinel-2 NDVI), channel width (from satellite imagery), bed slope, and upstream drainage area.

Initial results are promising, with machine learning models outperforming the traditional lookup-table approach by 20-30% in cross-validation experiments. The approach has been tested primarily in the US, but the same methodology could be applied in Europe using CORINE land cover, Sentinel-2 vegetation indices, and OVF gauge data for training. This represents a natural intersection of the machine learning methods discussed in Chapter 23 with the flood mapping framework of this chapter.

## 15.12 The Broader Significance

The HAND-based flood inundation mapping framework described in this chapter represents something broader than a single technical method. It demonstrates a paradigm shift in how we think about flood mapping -- from a detailed, site-specific, expert-driven activity to a systematic, automated, data-driven capability that can operate at continental and global scales.

For decades, flood mapping was a specialty practice: a small team of hydraulic engineers would spend months building a HEC-RAS model of a single river reach, calibrating it against historical floods, and producing a map that would serve as the basis for flood insurance and land-use regulation. This approach produced excellent local results but could never scale to cover the nation's entire stream network. The result was a patchwork: detailed maps for some reaches, nothing for most.

The HAND approach inverts this model. It starts with the terrain -- available everywhere, from space-based measurements -- and derives hydraulic properties and inundation maps for every reach at once. The results are less accurate than a carefully calibrated HEC-RAS model, but they exist. For the 75% of US stream miles and the countless river kilometres worldwide that have no detailed flood maps, an approximate map is infinitely better than no map.

This same philosophy -- trading local precision for global coverage, using simple physics applied systematically across large domains -- is appearing throughout hydroinformatics. The National Water Model applies a single hydrological model (WRF-Hydro) to the entire US. EFAS applies LISFLOOD to all of Europe. GloFAS extends the approach globally. In each case, the results are approximate, but they provide the baseline intelligence needed for flood early warning, emergency response, and development planning.

The ultimate vision is a system that, for any point on Earth, can answer the question: "If the upstream discharge is $Q$, will this location flood, and to what depth?" The HAND framework provides the mathematical and computational structure for answering that question. The remaining challenges -- better DEMs, better roughness estimates, better discharge forecasts, and better validation data -- are being addressed by the research and operational communities described in this chapter. The tools exist. The data are accumulating. The framework is in place. What remains is the sustained effort to make it work everywhere, for everyone.
