# Chapter 7: Slope, Aspect, and the Shape of the Land

Every drop of rain that lands on a hillside faces the same question: which way is downhill? The answer depends on the slope of the surface -- its steepness and its orientation. These two quantities, slope and aspect, are the most fundamental derivatives of a digital elevation model, and they control nearly everything that matters in hydrology: the speed at which water moves across the landscape, the direction it travels, where it converges into channels, how much solar energy the surface absorbs, and how quickly soil erodes. A flat parking lot and a steep mountain gully may receive the same rainfall, but they generate entirely different hydrological responses, and the difference comes down to slope.

Chapter 5 introduced the grid as a way of representing terrain digitally, and Chapter 6 showed how map algebra enables cell-by-cell calculations across those grids. This chapter takes the next step: computing the shape of the land surface from the elevation values stored in a DEM. We will develop three distinct methods for calculating slope, each with different mathematical foundations, different assumptions, and different applications. The first -- the derivative method used by the ArcGIS Slope tool -- fits a smooth surface through the neighborhood of each cell and computes the gradient. The second -- the **D8 method** used by the ArcGIS Flow Direction tool -- finds the single steepest downhill path among eight neighbors. The third -- the **D-infinity method** published by Tarboton in 1997 -- removes the directional constraint of D8 by fitting triangular facets and allowing flow in any direction. These are not competing methods that give "better" or "worse" answers; they answer different questions, and understanding when to use each one is essential for any hydrological analysis.

Along the way, we will work through every algorithm in full mathematical detail with numerical examples, compare the results, and discuss how to automate these calculations for real-world projects. We conclude with a practical survey of the DEM data sources available in 2026, covering the United States, Hungary and Europe, and the rest of the world -- because none of these algorithms matter without good elevation data to feed them.

## 7.1 What Slope and Aspect Mean for Hydrology

### 7.1.1 Slope as a Driver of Water Movement

Slope is the rate of change of elevation with respect to horizontal distance. In everyday language, it is how steep the ground is. In mathematical terms, it is the magnitude of the gradient of the elevation surface. This single quantity influences a remarkably wide range of hydrological processes.

Surface runoff velocity is directly proportional to slope in virtually every empirical and physical flow equation. Manning's equation, the workhorse of open-channel hydraulics, expresses velocity as proportional to the square root of the energy slope. The Rational Method for peak discharge estimation treats the time of concentration -- the time it takes water to travel from the most distant point of a watershed to the outlet -- as a function of slope along the flow path. Steeper slopes mean faster travel times, higher peak flows, and more "flashy" hydrographs. When a flood forecaster needs to know how quickly a rainfall pulse will reach a river gauge, slope along the hillslope and channel is one of the first quantities they examine.

Erosion depends on slope even more strongly. The Universal Soil Loss Equation (USLE) and its successors include a slope-length factor that makes soil loss increase approximately as the square of slope steepness. A doubling of slope does not double erosion -- it roughly quadruples it. This nonlinear sensitivity means that accurate slope computation from DEMs is not merely a nice-to-have for erosion modeling; small errors in slope translate into large errors in predicted sediment yield.

Infiltration, too, is influenced by slope, though less directly. On steep surfaces, water moves downslope quickly, reducing the contact time between water and soil and limiting infiltration. On gentle slopes, water ponds or moves slowly, increasing infiltration opportunity time. Distributed hydrological models that partition rainfall into runoff and infiltration at each grid cell need slope as an input to get this partitioning right.

Finally, slope governs the spatial distribution of soil moisture. Water accumulates in low-slope areas -- valley bottoms, floodplains, topographic hollows -- creating saturated zones that contribute to storm runoff through the saturation-excess mechanism. The **topographic wetness index (TWI)**, one of the most widely used terrain attributes in hydrology, is defined as $\ln(a / \tan \beta)$, where $a$ is the upslope contributing area per unit contour length and $\beta$ is the local slope angle. Areas with high TWI values -- large contributing areas and gentle slopes -- are predicted to be saturated most frequently. This index underpins the TOPMODEL family of hydrological models and remains one of the most effective ways to map the spatial distribution of soil moisture from terrain data alone.

### 7.1.2 Aspect as a Directional Control

Aspect is the compass direction of the steepest downslope gradient -- the direction a ball would roll if placed on the surface. It answers the question "which way does this hillslope face?" and carries information that slope magnitude alone cannot convey.

In hydrology, aspect determines the direction of surface flow. This is the foundation of all watershed delineation: by tracing the direction of steepest descent from every cell in a DEM, we can determine which cells drain to a common outlet and thereby define watershed boundaries. The algorithms for doing this -- D8 and D-infinity flow direction, discussed in detail later in this chapter -- are fundamentally aspect calculations specialized for hydrological applications. Without aspect, there is no flow direction, and without flow direction, there is no watershed.

Aspect also controls the energy balance of the land surface, which has indirect but important hydrological consequences. In the Northern Hemisphere, south-facing slopes (aspect near 180 degrees) receive substantially more solar radiation than north-facing slopes. This affects evapotranspiration rates, soil temperature, snow accumulation and melt timing, and vegetation density. In mountainous watersheds, the difference in snowmelt timing between south-facing and north-facing slopes can be the dominant factor determining the shape of the spring runoff hydrograph. A south-facing slope may lose its snowpack weeks before an adjacent north-facing slope at the same elevation, producing a prolonged, multi-peaked melt season. The Carpathian Mountains of northern Hungary and the broader Carpathian arc that wraps around the Pannonian Basin provide a vivid example: their complex topography creates dramatic differences in snow retention between north-facing and south-facing valleys, directly influencing the timing and magnitude of spring floods on the upper Tisza River.

For agriculture and irrigation planning -- critical concerns in the Hungarian Great Plain (Alfold), where water scarcity during summer months is a recurring challenge -- aspect determines which fields dry out fastest, which slopes need supplemental irrigation first, and where micro-climate effects create frost pockets or heat traps. The precipitation patterns that supply this water are discussed in Chapter 8, which also shows how elevation (and therefore slope and aspect) systematically influences rainfall distribution through the orographic effect.

### 7.1.3 Representations of Slope and Aspect

Topographic slope can be expressed in several equivalent mathematical forms, and understanding the relationships between them is essential for interpreting the output of different GIS tools.

**As a surface derivative.** Given the elevation surface $z(x, y)$, the gradient is the vector of partial derivatives:

$$\nabla z = \left(\frac{\partial z}{\partial x},\ \frac{\partial z}{\partial y}\right)$$

This gradient vector points in the direction of steepest *ascent*. The slope is its magnitude, and the aspect is the direction of steepest *descent* (the reverse of the gradient direction).

**As a vector with x and y components.** The slope can be decomposed into components along the coordinate axes: $(S_x, S_y)$, where $S_x = \partial z / \partial x$ and $S_y = \partial z / \partial y$. This form is what the finite-difference algorithms actually compute.

**As a vector with magnitude and direction.** The slope magnitude $S$ and aspect angle $\alpha$ together form a polar representation:

$$S = \sqrt{S_x^2 + S_y^2}$$

$$\alpha = f(S_x, S_y)$$

where $f$ is a function that converts the Cartesian components to a compass bearing, with appropriate quadrant handling. These three representations are mathematically equivalent -- given any one, the others can be derived.

**Slope units.** Slope magnitude can be expressed in two common forms. As *percent rise* (or percent slope), it is the ratio of vertical rise to horizontal run, multiplied by 100: a slope of 0.45 corresponds to 45%. As *degrees*, it is the angle of inclination from the horizontal: if $S$ is the decimal slope, the angle is $\theta = \arctan(S) \times 180 / \pi$. Note that percent slope and degrees are not linearly related. A 100% slope corresponds to only 45 degrees, and slopes approaching vertical have percent values approaching infinity. Most GIS tools offer a choice between these two output units.

### 7.1.4 The Aspect Convention in GIS

In ArcGIS and most other GIS platforms, aspect is measured clockwise from north in the downslope direction:

- 0 degrees (or 360 degrees) = North
- 90 degrees = East
- 180 degrees = South
- 270 degrees = West

A flat area with no slope is assigned a special value of -1 in ArcGIS to indicate "no aspect." An aspect value of 145 degrees, for example, indicates a south-southeast-facing slope -- the surface tilts downward toward the south-southeast, and water on that surface would flow in that direction.

This convention is intuitive for most applications but creates a complication when comparing with the D-infinity method, which uses the mathematical convention of measuring angles counterclockwise from east. We will address this difference explicitly when we discuss D-infinity in Section 7.5.

## 7.2 Method 1: The Derivative Slope (ArcGIS Slope Tool)

### 7.2.1 The 3x3 Neighborhood and Weighted Finite Differences

The first and most commonly used method for computing slope from a DEM is the derivative method, implemented in the ArcGIS Slope and Aspect tools. This method estimates the partial derivatives of the elevation surface at each grid cell using a weighted finite-difference scheme applied to a 3x3 neighborhood of cells.

Consider a 3x3 block of cells centered on the cell of interest, which we label E. The eight surrounding cells are labeled A through I (skipping E), with the cell size denoted by $\delta$:

```
A  B  C
D  E  F
G  H  I
```

The x-axis points to the right (east) and the y-axis points upward (north), following the standard geographic convention. The algorithm computes partial derivatives in both coordinate directions using weighted averages of finite differences across the neighborhood.

**Slope in the x-direction (east-west):**

$$\frac{dz}{dx} = \frac{(A + 2D + G) - (C + 2F + I)}{8\delta}$$

**Slope in the y-direction (north-south):**

$$\frac{dz}{dy} = \frac{(G + 2H + I) - (A + 2B + C)}{8\delta}$$

These formulas deserve careful explanation, because understanding why they work this way illuminates what the Slope tool is actually computing.

### 7.2.2 Why the 1-2-1 Weighting?

The weighting scheme gives double weight to the cells directly adjacent to the center in the direction perpendicular to the derivative being computed. Consider the x-direction derivative. There are three independent estimates of $dz/dx$ that can be formed from the 3x3 neighborhood:

1. **Top row**: $(A - C) / (2\delta)$ -- the finite difference across the top of the neighborhood
2. **Middle row**: $(D - F) / (2\delta)$ -- the finite difference across the middle, passing directly through the center cell
3. **Bottom row**: $(G - I) / (2\delta)$ -- the finite difference across the bottom

The middle row passes through the cell of interest and should carry more weight than the top and bottom rows, which are one cell away. The algorithm weights these three estimates in the ratio 1:2:1, producing:

$$\frac{dz}{dx} = \frac{1 \cdot \frac{A - C}{2\delta} + 2 \cdot \frac{D - F}{2\delta} + 1 \cdot \frac{G - I}{2\delta}}{4}$$

Simplifying the denominator ($4 \times 2\delta = 8\delta$):

$$\frac{dz}{dx} = \frac{(A - C) + 2(D - F) + (G - I)}{8\delta} = \frac{(A + 2D + G) - (C + 2F + I)}{8\delta}$$

This is exactly the formula used by ArcGIS. The same logic applies to the y-direction, with the three column-wise differences weighted 1:2:1.

The 1-2-1 weighting has a deeper mathematical significance: it corresponds to a convolution with a **Sobel operator**, a well-known image processing filter for edge detection. The Sobel operator is mathematically equivalent to first smoothing the data with a [1, 2, 1] kernel in one direction (reducing noise) and then taking a centered finite difference in the perpendicular direction (estimating the derivative). This combination of smoothing and differentiation makes the derivative estimate more robust to noise in the elevation data than a simple centered difference at the center cell alone.

This matters in practice because DEMs are not perfect surfaces. They contain measurement noise, quantization artifacts (especially in older integer-valued DEMs), and systematic errors. A derivative computed from just two cells (e.g., $(D - F) / (2\delta)$) is extremely sensitive to noise in those cells. By incorporating all eight neighbors and weighting them appropriately, the Sobel-based method produces a smoother, more reliable slope estimate. This is why the ArcGIS Slope tool is the right choice for general terrain characterization, slope stability analysis, and applications where a robust, noise-resistant measure of surface steepness is needed.

### 7.2.3 Computing Slope Magnitude and Angle

Once the partial derivatives are computed, the slope magnitude and angle follow from vector calculus:

**Slope magnitude (as a decimal fraction):**

$$S = \sqrt{\left(\frac{dz}{dx}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{dz}{dy}\right)^2}$$

**Slope in degrees:**

$$\theta = \arctan(S) \times \frac{180}{\pi}$$

**Slope in percent rise:**

$$S_{\%} = S \times 100$$

Note that the slope magnitude $S$ is dimensionless when $z$, $x$, and $y$ are all in the same units (e.g., meters). If the DEM is in geographic coordinates (latitude/longitude in degrees) while elevation is in meters, the planar method will produce wildly incorrect results because the horizontal and vertical units differ. In this case, either reproject the DEM to a metric coordinate system first, or use the geodesic method available in ArcGIS Pro, which accounts for Earth's curvature and varying cell dimensions across latitude.

### 7.2.4 Aspect from the Derivative Method

Aspect is computed from the same partial derivatives:

$$\alpha = \arctan\left(\frac{dz/dx}{dz/dy}\right)$$

This formula looks simple, but the quadrant resolution requires care. The standard $\arctan$ function returns values only in the range $(-90^\circ, +90^\circ)$, which covers only two of the four possible quadrants. The two-argument $\text{atan2}$ function, available in all programming languages and GIS software, accepts both components separately and returns the correct angle in the full $(-180^\circ, +180^\circ)$ range. This result is then converted to the GIS convention of $(0^\circ, 360^\circ)$ measured clockwise from north.

The quadrant depends on the signs of the two partial derivatives:

**Table 7.1**: Quadrant determination for aspect from the signs of the partial derivatives

| $dz/dx$ | $dz/dy$ | Slope faces | Aspect range |
|----------|----------|-------------|--------------|
| Positive | Negative | Southeast | 90 -- 180 degrees |
| Positive | Positive | Northeast | 0 -- 90 degrees |
| Negative | Positive | Northwest | 270 -- 360 degrees |
| Negative | Negative | Southwest | 180 -- 270 degrees |

The physical interpretation is straightforward. A positive $dz/dx$ means the terrain rises toward the east, so the downslope direction has an eastward component. A negative $dz/dy$ means the terrain falls toward the north (remember, $y$ increases northward), so the downslope direction has a southward component. Combining these: the slope faces southeast, which corresponds to aspect values between 90 and 180 degrees.

### 7.2.5 Worked Example: Derivative Method

Let us work through a complete numerical example. Consider the following elevation grid with a cell size of $\delta = 30$ m:

```
80  74  63
69  67  56
60  52  48
```

The center cell E = 67 m, and we want to compute its slope and aspect.

**Step 1: Compute $dz/dx$ (east-west slope).**

Assigning the labels: A = 80, B = 74, C = 63, D = 69, E = 67, F = 56, G = 60, H = 52, I = 48.

$$\frac{dz}{dx} = \frac{(A + 2D + G) - (C + 2F + I)}{8\delta}$$

$$= \frac{(80 + 2 \times 69 + 60) - (63 + 2 \times 56 + 48)}{8 \times 30}$$

$$= \frac{(80 + 138 + 60) - (63 + 112 + 48)}{240}$$

$$= \frac{278 - 223}{240} = \frac{55}{240} = 0.229$$

The positive value means the terrain rises toward the west (the left side of the grid has higher elevations than the right side), so the downslope direction has an eastward component.

**Step 2: Compute $dz/dy$ (north-south slope).**

$$\frac{dz}{dy} = \frac{(G + 2H + I) - (A + 2B + C)}{8\delta}$$

$$= \frac{(60 + 2 \times 52 + 48) - (80 + 2 \times 74 + 63)}{8 \times 30}$$

$$= \frac{(60 + 104 + 48) - (80 + 148 + 63)}{240}$$

$$= \frac{212 - 291}{240} = \frac{-79}{240} = -0.329$$

The negative value means the terrain falls toward the north (the bottom row, which corresponds to the south in the standard display where row 1 is at the top, has lower elevations -- wait, we need to be careful here). In grid storage, row indices increase downward, but in geographic coordinates, $y$ increases northward. In this example, the top row (A, B, C) has higher elevations and the bottom row (G, H, I) has lower elevations. With $y$ increasing upward (northward), the formula $(G + 2H + I) - (A + 2B + C)$ being negative means the surface falls as we go south -- that is, the downslope direction has a southward component.

**Step 3: Compute slope magnitude.**

$$S = \sqrt{0.229^2 + (-0.329)^2} = \sqrt{0.0524 + 0.1082} = \sqrt{0.1607} = 0.401$$

In percent rise: $S_{\%} = 40.1\%$.

In degrees: $\theta = \arctan(0.401) \times 180/\pi = 21.8^\circ$.

**Step 4: Compute aspect.**

$$\alpha = \arctan\left(\frac{dz/dx}{dz/dy}\right) = \arctan\left(\frac{0.229}{-0.329}\right) = \arctan(-0.696)$$

The basic $\arctan$ returns $-34.8^\circ$. Now we resolve the quadrant. We have $dz/dx > 0$ (eastward component) and $dz/dy < 0$ (southward component), placing the slope in the southeast quadrant (Table 7.1). Adding 180 degrees to the basic arctangent result:

$$\alpha = -34.8^\circ + 180^\circ = 145.2^\circ$$

**Interpretation.** The center cell has a slope of 40.1% (21.8 degrees) facing 145.2 degrees -- south-southeast. This makes physical sense: the highest elevations are in the upper-left corner (80) and the lowest in the lower-right corner (48), so the terrain tilts from northwest to southeast.

### 7.2.6 Edge Cells and No-Data Boundaries

The derivative method requires the complete 3x3 neighborhood around each cell. Consequently, cells on the edge of the DEM grid -- the outermost row and column on all four sides -- cannot be evaluated, because one or more of their eight neighbors lies outside the grid. These edge cells are assigned no-data values in the output slope and aspect rasters.

This has practical implications. If your study area extends to the edge of your DEM, you will lose one cell's width of slope and aspect information around the entire perimeter. The solution is to acquire a DEM that extends beyond your study area by at least one cell in every direction, compute slope and aspect on the larger DEM, and then clip the results to your study area. For a DEM with 30-meter cells, this means your DEM should extend at least 30 meters beyond your study boundary on all sides -- a trivial margin in most cases, but one that is easy to overlook and frustrating to discover after the analysis is complete.

## 7.3 Method 2: The D8 Flow Direction Method

### 7.3.1 A Different Question

The derivative method answers the question "what is the average steepness and orientation of the surface around this cell?" The D8 method answers a different question: "if a drop of water lands on this cell, which neighboring cell does it flow to?" This distinction matters. The derivative method uses all eight neighbors -- including those at higher elevations -- to fit a smooth surface and estimate the gradient. The D8 method uses only *downslope* neighbors, because water does not flow uphill.

For hydrological applications -- watershed delineation, stream network extraction, flow accumulation -- the D8 method is the standard approach. It was first described by O'Callaghan and Mark in 1984 and remains the default algorithm in the ArcGIS Flow Direction tool. The "D8" name refers to the eight possible flow directions: the four cardinal directions (north, south, east, west) and the four diagonal directions (northeast, southeast, southwest, northwest).

### 7.3.2 The Algorithm

For each cell in the DEM, the D8 algorithm computes the slope to each of the eight neighboring cells as:

$$S_i = \frac{z_{\text{center}} - z_i}{d_i}$$

where $z_{\text{center}}$ is the elevation of the cell being evaluated, $z_i$ is the elevation of neighbor $i$, and $d_i$ is the distance to that neighbor:

- $d_i = \delta$ for cardinal neighbors (north, south, east, west)
- $d_i = \delta\sqrt{2}$ for diagonal neighbors (northeast, southeast, southwest, northwest)

The diagonal distance is longer by a factor of $\sqrt{2} \approx 1.414$ because the diagonal of a square with side $\delta$ has length $\delta\sqrt{2}$. This distance correction is essential: without it, the algorithm would systematically overestimate slopes to diagonal neighbors, because the elevation drop would be divided by too short a distance.

Only neighbors with elevations *lower* than the center cell produce positive slopes (downhill directions). The neighbor with the *maximum* positive slope is selected as the flow direction -- this is the steepest descent, the path that a drop of water on the surface would follow under gravity.

### 7.3.3 Flow Direction Encoding

The D8 flow direction is encoded as a power of 2, with each of the eight directions assigned a unique value:

```
32  64  128
16  --    1
 8   4    2
```

**Table 7.2**: D8 flow direction encoding

| Direction | Encoding | Angle from east |
|-----------|----------|-----------------|
| East | 1 | 0 degrees |
| Southeast | 2 | 315 degrees |
| South | 4 | 270 degrees |
| Southwest | 8 | 225 degrees |
| West | 16 | 180 degrees |
| Northwest | 32 | 135 degrees |
| North | 64 | 90 degrees |
| Northeast | 128 | 45 degrees |

The power-of-2 encoding is not arbitrary -- it was designed so that each direction is represented by a single bit in an 8-bit binary number. East (1) is the least significant bit, and northeast (128) is the most significant bit. This binary encoding makes it efficient to test whether a cell's flow direction includes a particular component, using bitwise operations. In practice, however, the D8 method assigns exactly one direction per cell, so only one bit is ever set.

The encoding starts from east and proceeds counterclockwise, which is the mathematical convention. This is different from the ArcGIS Aspect tool, which measures angles clockwise from north. Keeping track of angle conventions is one of the persistent nuisances of terrain analysis.

### 7.3.4 Worked Example: D8 Method

Using the same elevation grid as before (center cell E = 67, $\delta = 30$ m):

```
80  74  63
69  67  56
60  52  48
```

The center cell has elevation 67. We compute the slope to each neighbor and discard any neighbor with an equal or higher elevation.

**Neighbors with lower elevation:**

- F = 56 (East): $S = (67 - 56) / 30 = 11 / 30 = 0.367$
- I = 48 (Southeast): $S = (67 - 48) / (30\sqrt{2}) = 19 / 42.43 = 0.448$
- H = 52 (South): $S = (67 - 52) / 30 = 15 / 30 = 0.500$
- G = 60 (Southwest): $S = (67 - 60) / (30\sqrt{2}) = 7 / 42.43 = 0.165$
- C = 63 (Northeast): $S = (67 - 63) / (30\sqrt{2}) = 4 / 42.43 = 0.094$

**Neighbors with equal or higher elevation (no flow):**

- A = 80 (Northwest): higher
- B = 74 (North): higher
- D = 69 (West): higher

**Selecting the steepest descent:**

| Neighbor | Direction | Elevation | Slope |
|----------|-----------|-----------|-------|
| F | East | 56 | 0.367 |
| I | Southeast | 48 | 0.448 |
| H | South | 52 | **0.500** |
| G | Southwest | 60 | 0.165 |
| C | Northeast | 63 | 0.094 |

The steepest descent is to cell H (south), with slope 0.500 (50.0%). The D8 flow direction is encoded as **4** (south).

**Interpretation.** The D8 method directs all flow from this cell southward. Note that the southeast neighbor (I = 48) has the lowest elevation of any neighbor, but it does not receive the flow because the slope to the south (0.500) is steeper than the slope to the southeast (0.448). This is because the diagonal distance to I ($30\sqrt{2} = 42.43$ m) is longer than the cardinal distance to H (30 m), and the greater distance dilutes the elevation drop. The D8 method correctly identifies that the rate of descent is steeper to the south, even though the absolute elevation drop is greater to the southeast.

This illustrates a subtle but important point: flow direction is determined by *slope* (elevation drop per unit distance), not by *elevation difference* alone. The distance correction for diagonal cells changes the result in many real-world situations.

### 7.3.5 The Percentage Drop Output and Its Known Bug

The ArcGIS Flow Direction tool produces an optional **drop raster** that reports the percent slope in the assigned flow direction. This is simply $S_i \times 100$ for the selected steepest-descent neighbor.

There is a documented issue with this output. For diagonal flow directions, ArcGIS approximates $\sqrt{2} \approx 1.5$ when computing the diagonal distance for the drop raster. This means diagonal distances are treated as $1.5\delta$ rather than the correct $1.414\delta$, introducing an error of approximately 6% in the reported slope for diagonal flow directions. The flow directions themselves are computed using the correct $\sqrt{2}$ factor, so the direction assignments are unaffected -- only the reported percentage drop values for diagonal directions are wrong.

This bug was first reported in 2014 and persists in current versions of ArcGIS Pro. It serves as a useful reminder of why hand-verification of software results against test data is essential practice (discussed in Section 7.7.4). When your results depend on the actual slope value rather than just the flow direction, be aware of this discrepancy and consider computing slopes independently using the derivative method.

### 7.3.6 Limitations: Why Eight Directions Are Not Enough

The D8 method restricts flow to one of only eight possible directions, spaced at 45-degree intervals. This is a significant constraint. On a real landscape, the direction of steepest descent can be any angle between 0 and 360 degrees. When the true flow direction falls between two of the eight allowed D8 directions -- say, at 160 degrees, between south (180 degrees) and southeast (135 degrees in the encoding convention) -- the D8 method must choose one of them, introducing a directional error of up to 22.5 degrees.

On hillslopes, this directional quantization causes flow to artificially converge along the eight grid directions, creating parallel flow paths that follow the grid axes and diagonals rather than the natural topography. The resulting flow accumulation grid shows artificially straight flow lines and exaggerated convergence at certain cells. For applications that are sensitive to the spatial distribution of flow -- such as computing the topographic wetness index or delineating the contributing area of a specific hillslope location -- these artifacts can be significant.

The D8 method also assigns all flow to a single neighbor, even when the terrain between two neighbors suggests that flow should be split. Imagine a cell sitting at the top of a broad, symmetrical ridge: in reality, water would diverge to both sides of the ridge, but D8 forces all of it to one side. This makes D8 poorly suited for representing divergent flow on convex hillslopes.

These limitations motivated the development of the D-infinity method, which we examine next.

## 7.4 Method 3: The D-Infinity Method

### 7.4.1 Motivation and Theoretical Foundation

The D-infinity method was published by David Tarboton in 1997 in the journal *Water Resources Research*, in a paper titled "A New Method for the Determination of Flow Directions and Contributing Areas in Grid Digital Elevation Models." It was designed to overcome the two fundamental limitations of D8: the restriction to eight discrete directions and the inability to represent divergent flow.

The key insight is geometric. Instead of measuring the slope from the center cell to each neighbor independently, the D-infinity method divides the 3x3 neighborhood into eight triangular facets. Each facet is defined by the center cell and two adjacent neighbors -- one cardinal and one diagonal. A plane is fitted through the three elevation values of each facet, and the steepest downslope direction on that plane is computed. Because the plane is continuous, the resulting flow direction can be any angle, not just a multiple of 45 degrees.

The D-infinity method also enables proportional flow allocation. When the computed flow direction falls between two neighbors (say, between south and southeast), the method assigns a proportion of the flow to each neighbor based on the angle. If the flow direction is 10 degrees from south and 35 degrees from southeast, approximately 78% of the flow goes south and 22% goes southeast ($35/45 \approx 0.78$ and $10/45 \approx 0.22$). This proportional allocation produces smoother, more realistic contributing area patterns on hillslopes and eliminates many of the gridding artifacts that plague D8.

The D-infinity method is now implemented as an option in the ArcGIS Flow Direction tool (in addition to D8 and MFD -- multiple flow direction), and it is also available in the open-source TauDEM (Terrain Analysis Using Digital Elevation Models) software developed by Tarboton's research group at Utah State University.

### 7.4.2 The Triangular Facet Geometry

The eight neighbors of the center cell define eight triangular facets. Each facet has three vertices: the center cell ($z_0$), a cardinal neighbor ($z_1$), and a diagonal neighbor ($z_2$). The cardinal neighbor is at distance $\delta$ from the center, and the diagonal neighbor is at distance $\delta\sqrt{2}$. Within the facet's local coordinate system, the cardinal neighbor defines the x-axis direction, and the perpendicular direction (toward the diagonal neighbor) defines the y-axis.

The key observation is that the cardinal neighbor $z_1$ and the diagonal neighbor $z_2$ are separated by exactly $\delta$ in the direction perpendicular to the line from $z_0$ to $z_1$. This is because the diagonal neighbor sits at the corner of the grid cell, one cell away from the cardinal neighbor in the perpendicular direction. This geometry makes the within-facet calculations straightforward.

**Slope components within the facet:**

$$\frac{dz}{dx} = \frac{z_0 - z_1}{\delta}$$

$$\frac{dz}{dy} = \frac{z_1 - z_2}{\delta}$$

The x-component ($dz/dx$) is the slope from the center cell to the cardinal neighbor. The y-component ($dz/dy$) is the slope from the cardinal neighbor to the diagonal neighbor, measured perpendicular to the x-axis. Together, these two components define the tilt of the triangular facet.

**Flow angle within the facet:**

$$\alpha_1 = \arctan\left(\frac{dz/dy}{dz/dx}\right) = \arctan\left(\frac{z_1 - z_2}{z_0 - z_1}\right)$$

This angle $\alpha_1$ is measured from the x-axis of the facet (the direction from $z_0$ to $z_1$). It tells us how far the steepest direction on the facet deviates from the cardinal direction.

**Slope magnitude on the facet:**

$$S = \sqrt{\left(\frac{dz}{dx}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{dz}{dy}\right)^2} = \sqrt{\left(\frac{z_0 - z_1}{\delta}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{z_1 - z_2}{\delta}\right)^2}$$

### 7.4.3 Facet Numbering and the Global Angle Convention

The eight facets are numbered 0 through 7, starting from the east-facing facet and proceeding counterclockwise. Each facet spans 45 degrees of angular space. The numbering and geometry are summarized in the following table:

**Table 7.3**: D-infinity facet numbering, neighbors, and reference angles

| Facet | Cardinal neighbor ($z_1$) | Diagonal neighbor ($z_2$) | Reference angle (from east, CCW) |
|-------|--------------------------|--------------------------|----------------------------------|
| 0 | East | Northeast | 0 degrees |
| 1 | North | Northeast | 45 degrees |
| 2 | North | Northwest | 90 degrees |
| 3 | West | Northwest | 135 degrees |
| 4 | West | Southwest | 180 degrees |
| 5 | South | Southwest | 225 degrees |
| 6 | South | Southeast | 270 degrees |
| 7 | East | Southeast | 315 degrees |

The D-infinity method uses a different angle convention than the ArcGIS Aspect tool:

- **D-infinity**: Angles are measured **counterclockwise from east** (the standard mathematical convention). East = 0 degrees, North = 90 degrees, West = 180 degrees, South = 270 degrees.
- **ArcGIS Aspect**: Angles are measured **clockwise from north**. North = 0 degrees, East = 90 degrees, South = 180 degrees, West = 270 degrees.

To convert from the D-infinity convention to the ArcGIS convention: $\alpha_{\text{ArcGIS}} = 90 - \alpha_{\text{Dinf}}$ (adding 360 if the result is negative). To convert the other way: $\alpha_{\text{Dinf}} = 90 - \alpha_{\text{ArcGIS}}$ (adding 360 if negative).

The global flow direction for a given facet is computed by adding the facet's reference angle to the within-facet angle $\alpha_1$:

$$\theta_{\text{global}} = n \times 45^\circ + \alpha_1$$

where $n$ is the facet number (0 through 7).

The algorithm evaluates all eight facets, computes the slope magnitude for each, and selects the facet with the steepest downslope. The global angle from that facet becomes the D-infinity flow direction.

### 7.4.4 Boundary Conditions: When the Angle Falls Outside the Facet

Each facet spans 45 degrees, so the within-facet angle $\alpha_1$ must fall between 0 and 45 degrees for the direction to lie within the facet's angular range. Two boundary cases arise:

**Case 1: $\alpha_1 < 0$ degrees.** The computed steepest direction lies beyond the cardinal edge of the facet -- that is, closer to the previous facet's cardinal direction. In this case, the angle is clamped to 0 degrees, and the slope is recomputed along the cardinal edge:

$$S_{\text{clamped}} = \frac{z_0 - z_1}{\delta}$$

This is equivalent to the D8 slope to the cardinal neighbor.

**Case 2: $\alpha_1 > 45$ degrees.** The computed steepest direction lies beyond the diagonal edge of the facet. The angle is clamped to 45 degrees, and the slope is recomputed along the diagonal edge:

$$S_{\text{clamped}} = \frac{z_0 - z_2}{\delta\sqrt{2}}$$

This is equivalent to the D8 slope to the diagonal neighbor.

In both boundary cases, the D-infinity result degrades gracefully to a D8-equivalent direction. This means D-infinity is always at least as good as D8: when the steepest direction lies along a grid direction, D-infinity recovers it exactly, and when it lies between grid directions, D-infinity provides a more accurate representation.

### 7.4.5 Worked Example: D-Infinity Method

Using the same elevation grid ($z_0 = 67$, $\delta = 30$ m):

```
80  74  63
69  67  56
60  52  48
```

We need to identify which facet produces the steepest downslope direction. Let us evaluate facet 6, which involves the south cardinal neighbor ($z_1$ = H = 52) and the southeast diagonal neighbor ($z_2$ = I = 48). The reference angle for facet 6 is 270 degrees (counterclockwise from east, which corresponds to the south direction).

**Step 1: Compute within-facet slope components.**

$$\frac{dz}{dx} = \frac{z_0 - z_1}{\delta} = \frac{67 - 52}{30} = \frac{15}{30} = 0.500$$

$$\frac{dz}{dy} = \frac{z_1 - z_2}{\delta} = \frac{52 - 48}{30} = \frac{4}{30} = 0.133$$

**Step 2: Compute within-facet angle.**

$$\alpha_1 = \arctan\left(\frac{dz/dy}{dz/dx}\right) = \arctan\left(\frac{0.133}{0.500}\right) = \arctan(0.267) = 14.9^\circ$$

Since $0^\circ < 14.9^\circ < 45^\circ$, the angle falls within the valid range of the facet. No clamping is needed.

**Step 3: Compute slope magnitude.**

$$S = \sqrt{0.500^2 + 0.133^2} = \sqrt{0.2500 + 0.0178} = \sqrt{0.2678} = 0.517$$

**Step 4: Compute global angle.**

$$\theta = 270^\circ + 14.9^\circ = 284.9^\circ$$

This is 284.9 degrees counterclockwise from east. In the ArcGIS aspect convention (clockwise from north): $90 - 284.9 = -194.9 + 360 = 165.1$ degrees -- approximately south-southeast, consistent with the derivative method's result of 145.2 degrees (the difference arises because the methods use different neighborhoods and weighting).

**Step 5: Verify this is the steepest facet.** We should check the other facets to confirm that facet 6 produces the steepest slope. For completeness, let us check facet 7 (east cardinal, southeast diagonal) and facet 5 (south cardinal, southwest diagonal):

*Facet 7*: $z_1$ = F = 56 (east), $z_2$ = I = 48 (southeast).
- $dz/dx = (67 - 56)/30 = 0.367$
- $dz/dy = (56 - 48)/30 = 0.267$
- $\alpha_1 = \arctan(0.267/0.367) = \arctan(0.727) = 36.0^\circ$
- $S = \sqrt{0.367^2 + 0.267^2} = \sqrt{0.135 + 0.071} = 0.454$

*Facet 5*: $z_1$ = H = 52 (south), $z_2$ = G = 60 (southwest).
- $dz/dx = (67 - 52)/30 = 0.500$
- $dz/dy = (52 - 60)/30 = -0.267$
- $\alpha_1 = \arctan(-0.267/0.500) = \arctan(-0.533) = -28.1^\circ$
- Since $\alpha_1 < 0$, the angle is clamped to $0^\circ$, and $S = dz/dx = 0.500$

Facet 6 has the steepest slope (0.517) and is indeed the controlling facet.

**Interpretation.** The D-infinity method places the flow direction at 284.9 degrees (counterclockwise from east), with a slope of 51.7%. The direction points between south and southeast, roughly 14.9 degrees from due south toward southeast. This makes physical sense given the elevation pattern: the steepest descent is broadly southward but with a slight eastward component because the southeast corner (48) is lower than due south (52).

### 7.4.6 Boundary Condition Examples

To solidify understanding, let us examine cases where the computed angle falls outside the valid facet range.

**Example A: Angle exceeds 45 degrees.** Consider a grid with $\delta = 10$ m:

```
45  46  47
43  46  41
38  42  43
```

Center cell: $z_0 = 46$. Consider facet 7 (east cardinal, southeast diagonal): $z_1$ = 41 (east), $z_2$ = 43 (southeast).

- $dz/dx = (46 - 41)/10 = 0.500$
- $dz/dy = (41 - 43)/10 = -0.200$
- $\alpha_1 = \arctan(-0.200/0.500) = -21.8^\circ$

Since $\alpha_1 < 0$, the angle is clamped to $0^\circ$, and the slope is recomputed along the cardinal edge: $S = (46 - 41)/10 = 0.500$. The result collapses to the D8 direction: straight east.

Now consider facet 6 (south cardinal, southeast diagonal): $z_1$ = 42 (south), $z_2$ = 43 (southeast).

- $dz/dx = (46 - 42)/10 = 0.400$
- $dz/dy = (42 - 43)/10 = -0.100$
- $\alpha_1 = \arctan(-0.100/0.400) = -14.0^\circ$

Again $\alpha_1 < 0$: clamped to $0^\circ$, slope = 0.400. Not steeper than facet 7.

Now consider facet 5 (south cardinal, southwest diagonal): $z_1$ = 42 (south), $z_2$ = 38 (southwest).

- $dz/dx = (46 - 42)/10 = 0.400$
- $dz/dy = (42 - 38)/10 = 0.400$
- $\alpha_1 = \arctan(0.400/0.400) = 45.0^\circ$
- $S = \sqrt{0.400^2 + 0.400^2} = 0.566$

Here $\alpha_1 = 45^\circ$ exactly, which falls on the boundary. The flow direction points exactly toward the southwest diagonal neighbor. This is the steepest facet (slope 0.566), and the global angle is $225^\circ + 45^\circ = 270^\circ$ counterclockwise from east, which points due south in the CCW-from-east convention. Wait -- let us reconsider. Facet 5 has reference angle 225 degrees. Adding 45 degrees gives 270 degrees, which in the D-infinity convention (CCW from east) is due south. The D8 equivalent would be the southwest diagonal (because the facet angle of 45 degrees points along the diagonal edge). This example illustrates the subtlety of the boundary conditions.

**Example B: Angle is negative.** Consider:

```
45  46  44
43  42  48
38  47  48
```

Center cell: $z_0 = 42$. Consider facet 4 (west cardinal, southwest diagonal): $z_1$ = 43 (west), $z_2$ = 38 (southwest).

Here $z_1 = 43 > z_0 = 42$, so $dz/dx = (42 - 43)/10 = -0.100$. The center-to-cardinal slope is *uphill*, which means the steepest direction on this facet does not point toward the cardinal neighbor. The algorithm computes $\alpha_1 = \arctan((43 - 38)/(42 - 43)) = \arctan(5/(-1)) = \arctan(-5) = -78.7^\circ$. Since this is far below 0 degrees, it confirms that the steepest direction lies outside this facet.

However, facet 5 (south cardinal, southwest diagonal): $z_1$ = 47 (south), $z_2$ = 38 (southwest).
- $dz/dx = (42 - 47)/10 = -0.500$ (uphill to south)
- The negative x-component indicates the plane tilts *toward* the center from the south, so this facet doesn't produce a downslope direction.

This grid has limited downslope options. The algorithm evaluates all eight facets and selects the one with the steepest positive slope. The key lesson is that when no facet produces a downslope direction, the cell is a pit (a local minimum), and special handling is needed -- typically the Fill Sinks preprocessing step discussed in Chapter 9.

## 7.5 Comparing the Three Methods

### 7.5.1 Summary Results for the Test Grid

Let us assemble the results from all three methods applied to the same elevation grid (center cell = 67, cell size = 30 m):

**Table 7.4**: Comparison of slope and direction results from the three methods

| Property | Derivative (ArcGIS Slope) | D8 (Flow Direction) | D-infinity |
|----------|--------------------------|---------------------|------------|
| Slope value | 40.1% (21.8 degrees) | 50.0% | 51.7% |
| Direction | 145.2 degrees CW from N | South (code = 4) | 284.9 degrees CCW from E |
| Direction (normalized) | 145.2 degrees CW from N | 180.0 degrees CW from N | ~165.1 degrees CW from N |
| Neighbors used | All 8 | Only downslope (5 of 8) | Only downslope (via facets) |
| Output type | Continuous angle | Discrete (8 values) | Continuous angle |

Several observations emerge from this comparison.

First, the three methods produce different slope values: 40.1%, 50.0%, and 51.7%. The derivative method gives the lowest value because it incorporates all eight neighbors, including the high-elevation cells to the northwest that pull the average gradient down. The D8 and D-infinity methods give higher values because they focus on the steepest downslope direction, ignoring upslope neighbors entirely.

Second, the three methods produce different directions, though all point broadly south-southeast. The derivative method's 145.2 degrees reflects the average tilt of the entire 3x3 neighborhood. The D8 method's due-south direction is the nearest allowed direction to the true flow path. The D-infinity method's ~165.1 degrees (converting to the CW-from-N convention) represents the exact steepest direction on the steepest triangular facet.

Third, the directional resolution varies dramatically. The derivative method and D-infinity both produce continuous angles. D8 can only produce eight discrete values. For this particular example, the true flow direction is between south (180 degrees) and southeast (~135 degrees), and D8 must choose south -- introducing an angular error of roughly 15 degrees.

### 7.5.3 When to Use Each Method

The choice between these three methods is not a matter of preference -- it depends on the hydrological question being asked.

**Use the derivative method (ArcGIS Slope/Aspect)** when you need a general characterization of surface steepness that considers the entire local neighborhood. Typical applications include computing slope maps for erosion modeling (USLE/RUSLE), identifying areas prone to landslides, calculating solar radiation incidence for evapotranspiration estimation, classifying terrain into slope categories for land suitability analysis, and creating hillshade visualizations. The derivative method's use of all eight neighbors and its built-in noise reduction (via the Sobel weighting) make it the most robust choice for these applications.

**Use the D8 method** when you need to trace discrete flow paths from cells to their downstream neighbors. D8 is the standard method for watershed delineation, stream network extraction, and flow accumulation. Its restriction to eight directions means that every cell has exactly one downstream neighbor, creating an unambiguous tree structure of flow paths that converges into well-defined stream channels. This property is essential for algorithms that count how many upstream cells drain through each cell (flow accumulation) or trace all cells upstream of a given pour point (watershed delineation). The chapters that follow -- Chapter 9 on DEM preparation, Chapter 10 on flow direction and accumulation, and Chapter 11 on watershed delineation -- all build on the D8 method as their foundation.

**Use the D-infinity method** when you need accurate representation of flow direction on hillslopes, particularly for computing contributing area or the topographic wetness index. The D-infinity method's ability to split flow between two downslope neighbors produces smoother, more realistic contributing area patterns that avoid the artificial striping artifacts of D8. This makes it superior for hillslope hydrological analysis, for computing terrain indices used in soil moisture mapping, and for any application where the spatial pattern of upslope contributing area matters. D-infinity is also valuable for detecting subtle topographic features like convergent hollows and divergent ridges, because the continuous flow direction carries more information than D8's eight discrete values.

In practice, many projects use multiple methods. A typical workflow might compute the derivative slope for general terrain characterization and visualization, D8 flow direction for watershed delineation and stream extraction, and D-infinity flow direction for computing the topographic wetness index. The methods are complementary, not competing.

## 7.6 Practical Implementation in ArcGIS Pro

### 7.6.1 Computing Slope and Aspect

The **Slope** and **Aspect** tools are found in ArcGIS Pro under the Spatial Analyst toolbox, in the Surface toolset. Both tools take a DEM raster as input and produce a new raster as output. The interface requires the following parameters:

- **Input raster**: The DEM. This should be in a projected coordinate system with matching horizontal and vertical units (both in meters, or both in feet).
- **Output raster**: The file path for the resulting slope or aspect grid.
- **Output measurement** (Slope tool only): Choose between *Degree* and *Percent Rise*. Degrees are more intuitive for terrain visualization; percent rise is more common in engineering applications and in hydrological equations.
- **Method**: *Planar* or *Geodesic*. Planar assumes a flat coordinate system and is appropriate for DEMs in projected coordinates (UTM, State Plane, EOV, etc.). Geodesic accounts for Earth's curvature and is necessary when the DEM is in geographic coordinates (latitude/longitude).

The critical error to avoid: running the planar method on a DEM in geographic coordinates. In geographic coordinates, one degree of latitude represents approximately 111 km, while one degree of longitude varies from 111 km at the equator to 0 km at the poles. Because the DEM cell size is specified in degrees but the elevation values are in meters, the horizontal and vertical units are incompatible, and the planar method will produce nonsensical slope values. The geodesic method handles this automatically, but it is computationally more expensive. The best practice is to project your DEM to a metric coordinate system before computing slope and aspect.

For Hungarian projects, the natural choice is EOV (EPSG:23700), the Hungarian national projection, which provides conformal mapping with minimal distortion across the entire country. For European-wide analysis, ETRS89-LAEA (EPSG:3035) is the standard equal-area projection. For global or multi-national projects, UTM zones provide a good balance of low distortion and computational convenience.

### 7.6.2 Computing Flow Direction

The **Flow Direction** tool is in the Spatial Analyst toolbox under the Hydrology toolset. Its parameters include:

- **Input surface raster**: The DEM. For hydrological applications, this should be a filled DEM (after running the Fill tool to remove spurious sinks -- see Chapter 9).
- **Output flow direction raster**: The encoded flow direction grid.
- **Flow direction type**: *D8*, *D-Infinity*, or *MFD* (Multiple Flow Direction). D8 is the default.
- **Output drop raster** (optional, D8 only): A raster of percent slope in the flow direction.

When using D-infinity, the output is a floating-point raster (see the distinction between integer and floating-point grids in Chapter 5, Section 5.6) where each cell contains an angle in the range 0 to $2\pi$ radians (note: radians, not degrees). To convert to degrees, multiply by $180/\pi$. The angle is measured counterclockwise from east in the mathematical convention.

### 7.6.3 Automating Multi-Tool Workflows with Model Builder

When a project requires computing multiple terrain derivatives from the same DEM -- slope, aspect, D8 flow direction, D-infinity flow direction, flow accumulation, and contributing area -- running each tool manually becomes tedious and error-prone. ArcGIS Pro's **Model Builder** provides a visual programming environment for chaining tools into an automated workflow.

A typical terrain analysis model might proceed as follows:

1. **Input**: Raw DEM raster (the model parameter that the user specifies when running the model).
2. **Fill** the DEM to remove sinks (Hydrology toolset).
3. From the filled DEM, compute **Slope** (Surface toolset) and **Aspect** (Surface toolset) in parallel.
4. From the filled DEM, compute **Flow Direction** using both D8 and D-infinity options.
5. From the D8 flow direction, compute **Flow Accumulation** (Hydrology toolset).
6. **Output**: Five rasters -- slope, aspect, D8 flow direction, D-infinity flow direction, and flow accumulation.

To build this model:

1. Open the Catalog panel in ArcGIS Pro, expand your project's Toolbox, right-click it, and select **New > Model**.
2. Drag each tool from the Geoprocessing pane onto the model canvas: Fill, Slope, Aspect, Flow Direction (twice, for D8 and D-infinity), and Flow Accumulation.
3. Connect the inputs and outputs by drawing lines: the raw DEM feeds into Fill; the filled DEM output feeds into Slope, Aspect, and both Flow Direction tools; the D8 flow direction feeds into Flow Accumulation.
4. Double-click each tool on the canvas to configure its parameters (output file paths, slope units, flow direction type).
5. Right-click the input DEM element and select **Parameter** to make it a model parameter -- this allows the user to specify a different DEM each time the model is run.
6. Use **Auto Layout** to organize the diagram for readability.
7. Click **Run** to execute the entire sequence.

The completed model can be saved in the project toolbox and reused for different DEMs. It can also be exported as a Python script for further customization or batch processing. ### 7.6.4 Verifying Software Results Against Hand Calculations

Trust, but verify. This principle applies doubly to GIS software, where complex algorithms operate invisibly behind a simple dialog box. We have already seen that the ArcGIS Flow Direction tool contains a documented bug in its drop raster output. Other software has its own quirks. The only reliable way to ensure that you understand what a tool is computing -- and that it is computing correctly -- is to verify its output against hand calculations on a small test dataset.

The verification procedure is straightforward:

**Step 1: Create a small test DEM.** A 5-column by 4-row grid is sufficient. Design the elevation values so that you know what the correct slope, aspect, and flow direction should be at the interior cells. Include at least one cell with a clear single-direction flow (for easy D8 verification), one cell where the true direction falls between two D8 directions (for D-infinity verification), and one flat cell (for testing the no-aspect case).

**Step 2: Import the test DEM into ArcGIS.** Save the grid as an ASCII file in the standard Arc ASCII Grid format:

```
ncols         5
nrows         4
xllcorner     0
yllcorner     0
cellsize      30
NODATA_value  -9999
80  74  63  55  50
69  67  56  49  45
60  52  48  43  40
55  50  45  41  38
```

Import using the **ASCII to Raster** tool.

**Step 3: Run the tools.** Compute Slope, Aspect, and Flow Direction (both D8 and D-infinity) on the test DEM.

**Step 4: Compare.** Use the **Identify** tool or the attribute table to read the output values at each interior cell. Compare against your hand calculations. For each cell, verify:

- Slope (percent and degrees) from the ArcGIS Slope tool matches the derivative method formula.
- Aspect from the ArcGIS Aspect tool matches the derivative method formula.
- D8 flow direction code matches your hand-computed steepest descent neighbor.
- D-infinity flow direction angle matches your hand-computed facet analysis.

**Step 5: Document discrepancies.** If the software produces values that differ from your hand calculations, investigate the cause. It may be a software bug, a difference in convention (e.g., the y-axis direction), a rounding difference, or an error in your hand calculation. Document both the software and hand-computed values, and note which you believe to be correct and why.

## 7.7 Digital Elevation Model Data Sources

All slope and aspect calculations depend on the quality of the underlying DEM. A poor DEM produces poor terrain derivatives, regardless of how sophisticated the algorithm is. The resolution, accuracy, and provenance of the elevation data determine the reliability of every downstream analysis: slope, aspect, flow direction, watershed boundaries, flood inundation extent, and more. This section surveys the major DEM data sources available in 2026, organized by geographic scope.

### 7.7.1 United States: NED and 3DEP

The **USGS 3D Elevation Program (3DEP)** is the US national initiative for high-resolution elevation data. Its primary product is the National Elevation Dataset (NED), available at multiple resolutions:

- **1/3 arc-second (~10 m)**: Complete coverage of the contiguous United States, Hawaii, and parts of Alaska. This is the standard resolution for most hydrological analyses.
- **1 arc-second (~30 m)**: Complete coverage of the US. Useful for regional-scale analyses where 10-meter resolution is unnecessary.
- **1/9 arc-second (~3 m)**: Available for selected areas with LiDAR coverage. Provides rich topographic detail for detailed flood mapping and urban hydrology.

NED data can be downloaded from the **USGS National Map** viewer at:

```
https://apps.nationalmap.gov/
```

The data is also available as an image service through the **ArcGIS Living Atlas**, allowing users to access NED tiles directly within ArcGIS Pro without downloading files. The Living Atlas service can be clipped to any study area on the fly, which is convenient for quick analyses but slower than working with local data for intensive computations.

For areas with LiDAR coverage, the raw LiDAR point clouds are available through 3DEP and through the **OpenTopography** portal. LiDAR-derived DEMs at 1-meter resolution capture extraordinary terrain detail -- individual stream channels, road embankments, levees, and building footprints become visible in the elevation surface. Chapter 13 discusses LiDAR data in detail.

When working with NED tiles, the typical workflow involves downloading multiple tiles that cover the study area, mosaicking them into a single raster using the **Mosaic to New Raster** tool, clipping to the study boundary using the **Extract by Mask** tool, and reprojecting to the desired coordinate system using the **Project Raster** tool with bilinear or cubic convolution resampling.

### 7.7.2 Hungary: DDM-5

The **DDM-5** (Digitalis Domborzatmodell -- Digital Terrain Model) is Hungary's national DEM, produced and maintained by the **Lechner Knowledge Centre** (formerly FOMI, the Institute of Geodesy, Cartography and Remote Sensing). It provides 5-meter horizontal resolution across the entire country, making it one of the highest-resolution national DEMs in Central Europe.

Key characteristics of DDM-5:

- **Resolution**: 5 m x 5 m grid
- **Source data**: Derived from 1:10,000 scale topographic contour lines and stereophotogrammetric measurements
- **Coordinate system**: EOV (EPSG:23700), Hungary's national projection
- **Update history**: Original production in 2000, with updates in 2005, 2015, and 2022 to incorporate major terrain changes (highway construction, mining, river engineering works)
- **Accuracy**: Vertical accuracy better than 1 m in flat terrain, 2-3 m in hilly/mountainous areas

DDM-5 is not freely downloadable. It must be ordered through the **Lechner Geoshop** at:

```
https://lechnerkozpont.hu/oldal/domborzatmodell
```

or through the English-language portal:

```
https://lechnerkozpont.hu/en/oldal/spatial-data
```

Lechner also provides DDM-5 data via WMS/WMTS services for visualization purposes, though the full-resolution raster data requires a formal data order. For academic research, Lechner offers reduced-price or free access through cooperation agreements with Hungarian universities.

For the Great Hungarian Plain (Alfold), where terrain gradients are extremely gentle (typical slopes less than 1%), the 5-meter resolution and sub-meter vertical accuracy of DDM-5 are essential. Coarser DEMs like SRTM (30 m) simply cannot resolve the subtle elevation differences that control surface drainage in flat terrain. A 10-centimeter elevation difference over 500 meters -- a slope of 0.02% -- can determine whether water flows east or west across a field, and detecting this difference requires both high resolution and high vertical accuracy.

For Hungarian students working with DDM-5 in ArcGIS Pro, note that the data arrives in EOV coordinates. If you need to combine it with European datasets (Copernicus DEM, CORINE land cover, EU-Hydro), you will need to either reproject the DDM-5 to ETRS89-LAEA (EPSG:3035) or reproject the European data to EOV. The transformation from HD72 (the datum underlying EOV) to ETRS89 uses a 7-parameter Helmert transformation (EPSG:1449). ArcGIS Pro handles this transformation automatically if both coordinate systems are properly defined.

### 7.7.3 Europe and Global: Copernicus DEM

The **Copernicus DEM** is a digital surface model derived from the German-led TanDEM-X radar satellite mission (2011-2015). It is rapidly becoming the standard global DEM for new projects, superseding SRTM for most applications. Three product tiers are available:

- **EEA-10**: 10-meter resolution, covering 39 European countries including Hungary. Access is restricted to authorized users (government agencies, research institutions). This is the best freely available DEM for European hydrological analysis.
- **GLO-30**: 30-meter resolution, global coverage. Freely available to all registered users. This is the workhorse for global hydrological studies.
- **GLO-90**: 90-meter resolution, global coverage. Freely available. Useful for continental-scale analyses where 30 m resolution is unnecessary.

The Copernicus DEM is distributed through:

```
https://dataspace.copernicus.eu/
```

It is also available through OpenTopography, Amazon Web Services, Google Earth Engine, and Microsoft Planetary Computer, making it accessible through virtually every major geospatial platform.

Key advantages over SRTM: the Copernicus DEM has higher vertical accuracy (absolute LE90 < 4 m globally, < 2 m in flat terrain), more recent acquisition dates (2011-2015 vs. 2000 for SRTM), and better coverage in mountainous and high-latitude regions where SRTM suffered from radar shadow effects. For Hungary specifically, both the EEA-10 and GLO-30 products provide excellent coverage of the Pannonian Basin and surrounding Carpathian ranges.

### 7.7.4 Global: SRTM

The **Shuttle Radar Topography Mission (SRTM)** acquired near-global elevation data during an 11-day Space Shuttle mission in February 2000. For over two decades, it was the standard global DEM, and it remains widely used.

- **Resolution**: 1 arc-second (~30 m) globally; 3 arc-second (~90 m) version also available
- **Coverage**: 60 degrees N to 56 degrees S latitude (approximately 80% of Earth's land surface). Hungary is fully covered.
- **Accuracy**: Vertical accuracy approximately 6-10 m (absolute), 5 m (relative)
- **Access**: Free; available from NASA Earthdata, USGS EarthExplorer, and OpenTopography

SRTM download sources include:

```
https://portal.opentopography.org/raster?opentopoID=OTSRTM.082015.4326.1
https://dwtkns.com/srtm30m/  (interactive tile selector)
https://earthexplorer.usgs.gov/
```

SRTM's main limitations are its age (year-2000 data, predating significant land surface changes in many regions), its lower vertical accuracy compared to Copernicus DEM, and its nature as a digital surface model rather than a bare-earth terrain model -- it includes the heights of buildings and vegetation. For new projects, the Copernicus DEM GLO-30 is generally preferred unless backward compatibility with existing studies requires SRTM.

### 7.7.5 Other Global DEMs

Several additional global DEMs are available for specialized applications:

**ALOS World 3D (AW3D30)**, produced by JAXA (the Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency), provides 30-meter global coverage derived from optical stereo imagery. Current version 4.1 (March 2025). It is independent of radar-based DEMs and can be superior in areas where radar measurements are problematic (dense tropical forests, very steep terrain). Available at:

```
https://www.eorc.jaxa.jp/ALOS/en/dataset/aw3d30/aw3d30_e.htm
```

**MERIT DEM** (Multi-Error-Removed Improved-Terrain), developed by Dai Yamazaki at the University of Tokyo, is a 90-meter global DEM created by removing systematic errors (stripe noise, absolute bias, speckle, tree height bias) from existing spaceborne DEMs. It provides the highest accuracy of any global 90-meter DEM and is widely used in global hydrological modeling. Available at:

```
https://global-hydrodynamics.github.io/MERIT_DEM/
```

**ASTER GDEM v3**, a joint product of METI (Japan) and NASA, provides 30-meter near-global coverage (83 degrees N to 83 degrees S) from ASTER satellite stereo imagery. It has been largely superseded by Copernicus DEM and AW3D30 in terms of accuracy.

### 7.7.7 Hungarian Case Study: Choosing a DEM for the Upper Tisza

To illustrate the practical implications of DEM choice, consider a hypothetical project to compute slope and flow direction maps for the Upper Tisza catchment in northeastern Hungary. The Tisza enters Hungary from Ukraine near Tiszabecs and flows south through increasingly flat terrain toward the Great Plain. The upper reach has moderate relief (the Carpathian foothills), while the lower portion is extremely flat.

**Option 1: DDM-5.** The highest resolution (5 m), best vertical accuracy, and native EOV coordinates. However, the catchment extends into Ukraine, where DDM-5 coverage ends at the national border. For a purely Hungarian analysis, this is the best choice.

**Option 2: Copernicus EEA-10.** Ten-meter resolution with seamless cross-border coverage into Ukraine and Romania. Ideal for a transboundary analysis of the full upper Tisza watershed. Available in geographic coordinates (WGS84/EGM2008); must be reprojected to EOV or ETRS89-LAEA for metric slope calculations.

**Option 3: Copernicus GLO-30 or SRTM.** Thirty-meter resolution, freely available, adequate for regional-scale analysis. However, 30 m may be too coarse to resolve the subtle drainage patterns on the flat portions of the Tisza floodplain, where gradients can be as low as 0.01%.

The practical recommendation: use DDM-5 for the Hungarian portion and Copernicus EEA-10 for the cross-border areas, mosaicking the two datasets along the national boundary with appropriate coordinate transformation. This combined approach leverages the best available data for each region.

## 7.8 The Broader Role of Terrain Derivatives

### 7.8.1 Beyond Slope and Aspect: Curvature and Derived Indices

Slope and aspect are the first derivatives of the elevation surface, but they are not the only terrain descriptors of interest. The second derivatives -- the rates of change of slope and aspect -- provide information about the *curvature* of the terrain, which further characterizes how water concentrates or disperses across the landscape.

**Plan curvature** measures the rate of change of aspect along a contour line. Positive plan curvature indicates convergent topography (a hollow or valley), where flow lines come together. Negative plan curvature indicates divergent topography (a ridge or spur), where flow lines spread apart. Zero plan curvature is a planar hillslope. Plan curvature directly affects the spatial concentration of flow: convergent areas accumulate more upslope contributing area per unit width, generating higher flow velocities and greater erosion potential.

**Profile curvature** measures the rate of change of slope along the flow direction. Positive profile curvature indicates a concave profile (slope decreasing downhill), where flow decelerates and deposition tends to occur. Negative profile curvature indicates a convex profile (slope increasing downhill), where flow accelerates and erosion intensifies.

ArcGIS Pro computes both curvatures through the **Curvature** tool in the Surface toolset, using the same 3x3 neighborhood as the Slope tool. While curvature is beyond the scope of this chapter's detailed treatment, it is worth noting that the mathematical machinery developed here -- partial derivatives of elevation surfaces estimated by finite differences over grid neighborhoods -- extends naturally to these higher-order derivatives. Curvature maps are increasingly used in geomorphological classification, landslide susceptibility mapping, and precision agriculture.

### 7.8.2 The Topographic Wetness Index

The **topographic wetness index (TWI)**, introduced by Beven and Kirkby in 1979 as part of their TOPMODEL framework, combines slope with upslope contributing area to predict the spatial distribution of soil moisture:

$$\text{TWI} = \ln\left(\frac{a}{\tan \beta}\right)$$

where $a$ is the specific upslope contributing area (total contributing area divided by contour length, in meters) and $\beta$ is the local slope angle.

High TWI values occur where contributing areas are large and slopes are gentle -- valley bottoms, topographic hollows, areas at the base of long hillslopes. These are the locations most likely to be saturated and to generate saturation-excess runoff during storms. Low TWI values occur on steep ridges and upper hillslopes, where contributing areas are small and slopes are steep -- locations that drain quickly and rarely saturate.

TWI computation depends critically on both slope and contributing area, making the choice of flow direction algorithm particularly important. D-infinity flow directions produce smoother, more physically realistic contributing area patterns than D8, which in turn produces better TWI maps. The artificial flow path striping caused by D8 translates directly into striped TWI patterns that have no physical basis. For TWI computation, D-infinity is strongly preferred.

TWI has proven to be one of the most versatile terrain indices in hydrology and ecology. It is used to predict soil moisture patterns, identify wetland locations, map zones of nitrogen cycling in soils, predict vegetation species distributions, and delineate hydrologically sensitive areas for agricultural management. Its computation requires only a DEM and the algorithms described in this chapter, making it accessible to any analyst with basic GIS skills.

### 7.8.3 Slope in Hydrological Models

Virtually every spatially distributed hydrological model uses slope as an input. In the SCS Curve Number method (used by HEC-HMS and many other models), time of concentration is estimated from slope along the longest flow path. In the Green-Ampt infiltration model, the effective rainfall excess depends on slope through its effect on surface storage. In kinematic wave routing, the velocity of overland flow is proportional to $S^{1/2}$ (from Manning's equation with the energy slope approximated by the terrain slope). In erosion models like RUSLE, the slope factor $S$ amplifies soil loss predictions nonlinearly.

The choice of slope calculation method matters for model calibration. If a model was calibrated using derivative-method slopes, switching to D8 or D-infinity slopes will change the effective slope values throughout the watershed and may degrade model performance unless the model is recalibrated. This is a subtle but real issue that arises when transferring models between software platforms or when updating a model with a new DEM. Documentation of which slope method was used -- and which DEM -- is an essential part of hydrological model metadata.

## 7.9 International Perspective: DEM Infrastructure and Access

### 7.9.1 The Three-Tier Data Landscape

The global availability of DEM data has improved dramatically over the past two decades, but significant disparities remain. Understanding the data landscape through the three-tier framework introduced in Chapter 3 -- national, continental/regional, and global -- helps practitioners identify the best available data for any study area.

**Table 7.8**: DEM data access by geographic region (three-tier framework)

| Region | National DEM | Continental/Regional DEM | Global DEM |
|--------|-------------|--------------------------|-----------|
| United States | NED/3DEP (1-10 m, free) | -- | SRTM, Copernicus GLO-30 |
| Hungary | DDM-5 (5 m, ordered) | Copernicus EEA-10 (10 m) | SRTM, Copernicus GLO-30 |
| EU member states | Varies (national surveys) | Copernicus EEA-10 (10 m) | SRTM, Copernicus GLO-30 |
| India | CartoDEM (10 m, restricted) | -- | SRTM, Copernicus GLO-30, AW3D30 |
| China | National DEM (restricted) | -- | SRTM, Copernicus GLO-30, AW3D30 |
| Sub-Saharan Africa | Rare/fragmentary | -- | SRTM, Copernicus GLO-30, AW3D30, MERIT |
| Middle East | Varies by country | -- | SRTM, Copernicus GLO-30, AW3D30 |

For students and researchers in countries without a high-quality national DEM -- which includes much of Africa, parts of Asia, and portions of the Middle East -- the Copernicus DEM GLO-30 is the single most important data source. Its combination of global coverage, 30-meter resolution, free availability, and good vertical accuracy makes it the de facto standard for hydrological analysis in data-sparse regions. The MERIT DEM, at 90 meters, is preferred for large-scale modeling because its error-removal processing produces cleaner hydrological derivatives (flow direction, contributing area) than the raw Copernicus or SRTM data.

### 7.9.2 Practical Recommendations

For a new hydrological project, the DEM selection workflow is:

1. **Check for a national DEM.** National products are almost always superior to global datasets within their coverage area, because they are derived from higher-resolution source data and are often validated against local ground truth. DDM-5 for Hungary, NED for the US, AHN for the Netherlands, etc.

2. **Check for continental/regional coverage.** If the study crosses national boundaries or the national DEM is unavailable, the Copernicus EEA-10 provides excellent coverage across Europe. No comparable continental product exists for other regions.

3. **Fall back to global DEMs.** Copernicus GLO-30 is the current best choice for most applications. SRTM remains useful for backward compatibility with older studies. AW3D30 (JAXA) is an independent alternative derived from optical rather than radar data, which can be advantageous in certain terrain types. MERIT DEM at 90 m is preferred for large-basin modeling.

4. **Consider LiDAR for detailed work.** When available, LiDAR provides vastly superior elevation data for flood mapping, urban hydrology, and detailed channel geometry. Coverage is expanding rapidly in Europe and the US but remains spotty in developing countries.

5. **Always verify the coordinate system and vertical datum.** Global DEMs typically use geographic coordinates (WGS84) with the EGM2008 or EGM96 geoid as the vertical datum. National DEMs use national coordinate systems and datums. Mixing these without proper transformation will introduce systematic errors in slope and aspect calculations.

## 7.10 From Slope to Watershed: Looking Ahead

The slope and aspect calculations developed in this chapter are not ends in themselves -- they are the foundation for the watershed analysis workflow that occupies the next four chapters. Here is the chain of dependence:

- **This chapter**: Compute slope and flow direction from the DEM.
- **Chapter 9**: Prepare the DEM by filling sinks and burning in streams, so that the flow direction grid is hydrologically consistent.
- **Chapter 10**: Use D8 flow direction to compute flow accumulation (how many cells drain through each cell), identifying where streams form.
- **Chapter 11**: Use flow accumulation and flow direction to delineate watershed boundaries and extract stream networks.
- **Chapter 12**: Automate the entire workflow so it can be applied to any DEM with minimal manual intervention.

The mathematical concepts introduced here -- the gradient, finite-difference approximation, neighbor weighting, triangular facet geometry -- recur throughout this sequence. The D8 flow direction grid, in particular, is the single most important intermediate product in hydrological terrain analysis: from it, all subsequent watershed properties are derived.

Understanding the three slope methods also equips you to make informed choices later. When Chapter 10 asks you to compute flow accumulation, you will know why D8 flow direction is the standard input (because its one-direction-per-cell property creates unambiguous drainage trees). When you encounter the topographic wetness index in advanced hydrological modeling, you will know why D-infinity flow direction produces better results (because it avoids the artificial striping of D8 contributing areas). And when you compare the slope values reported by different GIS packages, you will know whether the differences reflect different algorithms (derivative vs. D8 vs. D-infinity) or a genuine discrepancy that warrants investigation.

The shape of the land determines where water goes. With the tools developed in this chapter, you can read that shape from a grid of numbers -- and that reading is the first step toward understanding every water process that follows.
