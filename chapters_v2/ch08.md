# Chapter 8: Measuring Rain Where It Falls

Rain does not fall evenly. It varies from hilltop to valley, from one side of a mountain range to the other, from one end of a city to the next. Yet nearly everything in hydrology begins with a precipitation number -- how much rain fell on this watershed, during this storm, over this season. Hydrological models need precipitation at every grid cell. Flood forecasts need precipitation totals over entire catchments. Water balance studies need long-term averages that capture the true spatial pattern of rainfall across complex terrain. The fundamental problem is that we measure rain at points -- at scattered gauge locations -- but we need rain as a continuous field, everywhere.

This chapter addresses the bridge between point measurements and continuous spatial fields. It covers the principal interpolation methods available in GIS, the criteria for choosing among them, the special challenges of interpolating precipitation in mountainous terrain, and the techniques for computing watershed-average values from both point data and gridded surfaces. Along the way, it examines the international landscape of gridded climate products -- from PRISM in the United States to E-OBS and CARPATCLIM in Europe to CHIRPS for the developing world -- that increasingly allow hydrologists to bypass raw interpolation entirely and work with pre-computed, quality-controlled precipitation fields. Chapter 5's scale triplet framework (extent, spacing, and support) provides the conceptual vocabulary for evaluating these products, and Chapter 6's map algebra tools provide the computational machinery for working with the resulting grids.

The methods presented here are not limited to precipitation. Temperature, humidity, soil moisture, snow water equivalent, and many other environmental variables share the same fundamental challenge: they are measured at discrete points but needed as continuous surfaces. The interpolation framework developed in this chapter applies to all of them. However, precipitation remains the most demanding application because of its high spatial variability, its relationship with topography, and its central role in nearly every hydrological calculation that follows in Parts III and IV of this book. The precipitation grids produced by these methods become inputs to the watershed analysis workflows of Chapters 9 through 12 and the flood modeling frameworks of Chapters 14 and 15.

## 8.1 The Interpolation Problem

### 8.1.1 From Points to Surfaces

Every rain gauge is a point measurement. A standard gauge captures rainfall at a single location, typically with an orifice of 200 cm$^2$ (the WMO standard) or 324 cm$^2$ (the US National Weather Service 8-inch gauge). The area sampled by a gauge is roughly $10^{-6}$ km$^2$. A medium-sized watershed might cover $10^3$ km$^2$. The ratio between the measurement area and the area of interest is on the order of one to a billion. Somehow, a handful of point measurements must represent the rainfall over an area nine orders of magnitude larger.

**Spatial interpolation** is the formal name for this process: estimating values at unmeasured locations from a limited set of known sample points, producing a continuous raster surface. The term "interpolation" implies that the estimation occurs within the convex hull of the sample points -- between known values, not beyond them. Estimation beyond the sample domain is called **extrapolation** and carries substantially higher uncertainty. In practice, most spatial interpolation methods will produce values outside the sample domain if the raster extent extends beyond the outermost gauges, but the estimates in those regions should be treated with caution.

The choice of interpolation method is not a purely technical decision. It reflects assumptions about the spatial behavior of the variable being interpolated. A method that assumes smooth, gradual transitions (like spline interpolation) implicitly says that the variable does not change abruptly in space. A method that accounts for spatial correlation structure (like kriging) implicitly says that nearby values are more similar than distant values, and quantifies exactly how similarity decays with distance. A method that incorporates elevation as a co-variable (like PRISM or co-kriging) says that the variable has a systematic relationship with topography. Every interpolation method embeds a model of reality, whether the analyst recognizes it or not.

### 8.1.2 What Makes Precipitation Difficult

Precipitation is arguably the most challenging environmental variable to interpolate. Unlike temperature, which varies smoothly across the landscape and has a predictable relationship with elevation (the environmental lapse rate of roughly $-6.5$ degrees Celsius per 1000 m), precipitation is inherently discontinuous. A convective storm cell may dump 50 mm of rain on one town while the neighboring town 10 km away receives nothing. Frontal precipitation is more spatially coherent but still varies substantially over distances of tens of kilometers. Orographic precipitation -- rainfall caused by air masses being forced upward by terrain -- introduces systematic spatial patterns that simple distance-based methods cannot capture.

The gauge network compounds the problem (recall the scale triplet from Chapter 5, Section 5.7: a rain gauge has near-zero spatial support, and gauge spacing determines what spatial variability can be resolved). Gauges are not randomly distributed -- they are placed where people live, which means they cluster in valleys and lowlands. Mountain ridges, where precipitation is often highest, typically have few or no gauges. This non-random spatial distribution creates a systematic bias: simple interpolation from valley gauges will underestimate precipitation at high elevations. In Hungary, where the terrain ranges from the flat Great Plain (Alfold, around 80-100 m elevation) to the Matra Mountains (Kekes peak at 1,014 m), this bias is modest but measurable. In more mountainous countries -- Austria, Switzerland, Nepal, Peru -- it can be dramatic.

Furthermore, precipitation has a skewed distribution. Many days have zero rainfall, and the distribution of non-zero rainfall amounts follows a roughly exponential or gamma distribution with a long right tail. This means that extreme events -- which are often the most important for flood hydrology -- are precisely the events least well captured by the gauge network, because they are localized and the gauges may miss them entirely.

## 8.2 Interpolation Methods

The following sections describe the principal interpolation methods available in modern GIS software. Each method embeds different assumptions about the spatial structure of the data, and each produces a characteristically different surface from the same set of sample points. Understanding these differences is essential for selecting an appropriate method for any given application.

### 8.2.1 Nearest Neighbor (Thiessen/Voronoi Polygons)

The simplest interpolation method assigns to every unmeasured location the value of the nearest sample point. The resulting surface is piecewise constant: within the region of influence of each sample point, the estimated value equals the sample value exactly. At the boundary between two regions, the value changes abruptly -- there is no transition, no smoothing, no blending.

In one dimension, this produces a staircase function. Imagine five rain gauges along a transect, recording values of 12, 18, 22, 15, and 20 mm. The nearest-neighbor interpolation assigns 12 mm to all locations closer to the first gauge than to any other, 18 mm to all locations closer to the second gauge, and so on. The transitions occur at the midpoints between consecutive gauges.

In two dimensions, the regions of constant value form **Thiessen polygons**, also known as **Voronoi polygons** or **Dirichlet tessellations**. Each polygon contains all points in the plane that are closer to its associated sample point than to any other sample point. The construction is purely geometric: it depends only on the locations of the sample points, not on their values. The same set of gauge locations produces the same Thiessen polygons whether the gauges are recording precipitation, temperature, or wind speed.

Thiessen polygons have a dual relationship with the **Delaunay triangulation** of the same point set. If you connect adjacent Thiessen polygon centers with straight lines, you get the Delaunay triangulation; if you construct perpendicular bisectors of the Delaunay edges, you get the Thiessen polygon boundaries. This duality is mathematically elegant and computationally useful, but the practical implication for hydrologists is simple: the Thiessen polygon construction is unique for any given set of points, producing a deterministic, reproducible partition of space.

The advantages of nearest-neighbor interpolation are simplicity and data preservation. The method requires no parameters, makes no assumptions about spatial continuity, and preserves the original data values exactly (the estimated value at any sample point equals the measured value). It is also the only appropriate method for **categorical data** -- if the variable being interpolated is land use type, soil class, or any other categorical attribute, averaging between categories is meaningless, and nearest-neighbor assignment is the only sensible approach.

The disadvantages are equally clear. The resulting surface is discontinuous, with abrupt jumps at polygon boundaries that have no physical basis. The method ignores all information about spatial trends, correlation structures, and auxiliary variables. It treats a gauge 1 km away and a gauge 50 km away identically -- whichever is nearest gets full weight, and the other gets zero weight. For precipitation, this means that the spatial pattern of the interpolated surface is entirely determined by the gauge locations, not by the rainfall process.

Despite these limitations, the Thiessen polygon method remains widely used for one specific purpose: computing area-weighted average precipitation over watersheds. This application is discussed in detail in Section 8.6.

### 8.2.2 Inverse Distance Weighting (IDW)

Inverse distance weighting estimates the value at an unmeasured location as a weighted average of surrounding sample points, where closer points receive more weight than distant points. The weight decreases as an inverse power of the distance:

$$\hat{z}(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i \cdot z_i}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i}, \quad w_i = \frac{1}{d_i^p}$$

where $\hat{z}(\mathbf{x})$ is the estimated value at location $\mathbf{x}$, $z_i$ is the measured value at sample point $i$, $d_i$ is the Euclidean distance from $\mathbf{x}$ to sample point $i$, and $p$ is the **power parameter** controlling how rapidly the influence of a sample point decays with distance.

The power parameter is the single most important user choice in IDW. When $p = 1$, the weight decays linearly with distance, producing a relatively smooth surface where distant points still exert appreciable influence. When $p = 2$ (the most common default), the weight decays as the square of distance, giving much more influence to nearby points. As $p \to \infty$, IDW converges to nearest-neighbor interpolation, because the closest point's weight overwhelms all others. In practice, values of $p$ between 1 and 3 are used, with $p = 2$ being the most common for hydrological applications.

The IDW surface passes exactly through each sample point: when $\mathbf{x}$ coincides with sample point $i$, the distance $d_i = 0$, and the weight $w_i \to \infty$, causing the estimate to equal $z_i$ exactly. Between sample points, the surface is smooth but exhibits a characteristic artifact known as the **"bull's-eye" effect**: concentric circular contours surround each sample point, creating a pattern that looks like a topographic map of isolated hills and depressions. This pattern has no physical basis -- it is purely an artifact of the distance-weighting scheme -- and can be misleading when the interpolated surface is used for analysis.

A related artifact occurs when a sample point is surrounded by other points with very different values. Suppose a gauge in a valley records 15 mm of rain while all surrounding gauges on ridges record 40-50 mm. The IDW surface will dip sharply toward 15 mm around the valley gauge, creating an isolated "pit" that may be physically reasonable (rain shadow) or may be a measurement artifact. IDW has no way to distinguish between these possibilities.

IDW can be configured to use only a subset of nearby points (a **search neighborhood**) rather than all sample points. This is controlled by specifying a maximum search radius, a minimum and maximum number of points, or both. Using a search neighborhood reduces computation time for large datasets and prevents distant, irrelevant points from influencing the estimate. However, it introduces the risk of leaving some areas with no nearby sample points, producing undefined estimates.

**Worked Example: IDW Interpolation**

Consider three rain gauges with the following coordinates and daily precipitation values:

| Gauge | Easting (km) | Northing (km) | Precipitation (mm) |
|-------|-------------|---------------|-------------------|
| A | 10.0 | 20.0 | 24.0 |
| B | 15.0 | 20.0 | 36.0 |
| C | 12.0 | 24.0 | 18.0 |

We wish to estimate precipitation at point $\mathbf{x} = (12.0, 22.0)$ using IDW with $p = 2$.

**Step 1: Compute distances.**

$$d_A = \sqrt{(12-10)^2 + (22-20)^2} = \sqrt{4 + 4} = 2.83 \text{ km}$$

$$d_B = \sqrt{(12-15)^2 + (22-20)^2} = \sqrt{9 + 4} = 3.61 \text{ km}$$

$$d_C = \sqrt{(12-12)^2 + (22-24)^2} = \sqrt{0 + 4} = 2.00 \text{ km}$$

**Step 2: Compute weights** ($w_i = 1/d_i^2$).

$$w_A = \frac{1}{2.83^2} = \frac{1}{8.00} = 0.1250$$

$$w_B = \frac{1}{3.61^2} = \frac{1}{13.03} = 0.0768$$

$$w_C = \frac{1}{2.00^2} = \frac{1}{4.00} = 0.2500$$

**Step 3: Compute the weighted average.**

$$\hat{z} = \frac{0.1250 \times 24.0 + 0.0768 \times 36.0 + 0.2500 \times 18.0}{0.1250 + 0.0768 + 0.2500}$$

$$= \frac{3.000 + 2.765 + 4.500}{0.4518} = \frac{10.265}{0.4518} = 22.7 \text{ mm}$$

**Interpretation**: The estimated precipitation at the target point is 22.7 mm. Gauge C, which is closest (2.00 km) and records only 18.0 mm, exerts the most influence -- its weight (0.2500) is twice that of gauge A and more than three times that of gauge B. The estimate is pulled below the simple arithmetic mean of the three gauges (26.0 mm) by the dominance of the nearby low-value gauge. This illustrates the core behavior of IDW: proximity dominates, and the power parameter controls how strongly.

### 8.2.3 Bilinear Interpolation

Bilinear interpolation fits a surface through four points arranged in a rectangle by performing linear interpolation first in one direction, then in the other. Given four corner values $z_{00}$, $z_{10}$, $z_{01}$, and $z_{11}$ at the corners of a rectangle, and a point $(x, y)$ expressed in normalized coordinates $(t, u)$ where $0 \le t \le 1$ and $0 \le u \le 1$, the bilinear interpolant is:

$$\hat{z}(t, u) = (1-t)(1-u) \cdot z_{00} + t(1-u) \cdot z_{10} + (1-t)u \cdot z_{01} + tu \cdot z_{11}$$

This can equivalently be written in polynomial form as:

$$\hat{z}(x, y) = a_0 + a_1 x + a_2 y + a_3 xy$$

where the four coefficients are determined by the four corner values. The surface is linear along any line parallel to the x-axis or y-axis, but the cross-product term $a_3 xy$ introduces a saddle shape between the corners. The surface passes exactly through all four corner values.

Bilinear interpolation is rarely used to create a surface directly from scattered gauge data, because it requires data on a regular grid. Its primary application in hydrology is **resampling**: converting a raster from one grid resolution to another, or reprojecting a raster from one coordinate system to another (the resampling process is covered in detail in Chapter 6, Section 6.6). When you reproject a DEM from geographic coordinates to a projected coordinate system, or resample a precipitation grid from 1 km to 250 m resolution, the GIS must estimate values at the new grid cell centers from the old grid. Bilinear interpolation uses the four surrounding cells in the original grid to compute each new cell value, producing a smooth result that avoids the staircase artifacts of nearest-neighbor resampling.

For continuous variables like elevation and temperature, bilinear resampling (or the closely related **cubic convolution** method, which uses 16 surrounding cells instead of 4) is strongly preferred over nearest-neighbor resampling. When a DEM is reprojected using nearest-neighbor resampling, the resulting surface shows systematic striping artifacts -- regular patterns of duplicate rows or columns caused by the discrete assignment of old cell values to new cell positions. These artifacts are cosmetically ugly and can introduce errors in derived products like slope, aspect, and flow direction. Bilinear and cubic convolution resampling eliminate these artifacts at the cost of a slight smoothing of the data.

### 8.2.4 Spline Interpolation

Spline methods fit piecewise polynomial functions to the sample data, producing a surface that is smooth not only within each piece but also at the junctions between pieces. The name comes from the flexible metal or rubber strips (splines) that draftsmen once used to draw smooth curves through a set of points -- the spline strip naturally minimizes bending energy, producing a curve that is as smooth as possible while passing through the specified points.

Mathematical splines formalize this physical analogy. The surface is constructed to minimize a **bending energy** functional, which penalizes curvature. The result is a surface that interpolates (passes through) the sample points while being as smooth as possible everywhere else. Two variants are common:

**Regularized splines** allow some deviation from the sample points in exchange for a globally smoother surface. A **weight parameter** controls the trade-off: higher weight produces a smoother surface that may not pass through the data points exactly; lower weight forces closer adherence to the data but allows more local bending. This is analogous to choosing between a stiff metal ruler (high weight) and a flexible rubber strip (low weight) for drawing a curve through a set of points.

**Tension splines** include an additional parameter that controls how tightly the surface is "stretched" between sample points. High tension produces a surface that stays closer to a linear interpolation between points; low tension allows the surface to develop peaks and troughs between points. The tension parameter is particularly important for precipitation interpolation because low tension can produce negative precipitation values or extreme peaks between gauges -- physically meaningless results that can propagate errors into subsequent hydrological calculations.

The principal advantage of spline interpolation is the smoothness of the resulting surface. For variables that are expected to vary smoothly in space -- groundwater heads, sea surface temperature, long-term average precipitation -- splines can produce aesthetically pleasing and physically reasonable surfaces. The principal disadvantage is the risk of **overshooting**: the surface may produce values outside the range of the input data, including physically impossible values like negative precipitation or temperatures below absolute zero. The analyst must always inspect the resulting surface for such artifacts and adjust parameters accordingly.

In GIS software, spline interpolation is available in both ArcGIS Pro (Spatial Analyst > Interpolation > Spline) and QGIS (through the GRASS `v.surf.rst` module or SAGA's Multilevel B-Spline Approximation). The GRASS implementation is particularly flexible, offering control over tension, smoothing, and segmentation parameters.

### 8.2.5 Kriging

**Kriging** is a geostatistical interpolation method that provides the **Best Linear Unbiased Estimate (BLUE)** of the surface at any point. Named after the South African mining engineer Danie Krige, who developed the foundational ideas in the 1950s for estimating gold ore concentrations, kriging was formalized mathematically by the French mathematician Georges Matheron in the 1960s. It has since become the gold standard for spatial interpolation in fields ranging from mining to meteorology to hydrology.

What distinguishes kriging from all other methods discussed in this chapter is its explicit treatment of **spatial correlation**. Rather than assuming an arbitrary weighting function (like IDW's inverse distance power), kriging determines the optimal weights from the data themselves by analyzing how the similarity between measurements changes with distance. This analysis is performed through the **variogram** (or, equivalently, the **semivariogram**), which plots the semivariance of the data as a function of lag distance.

The **empirical semivariogram** is computed from the data as:

$$\hat{\gamma}(h) = \frac{1}{2N(h)} \sum_{i=1}^{N(h)} [z(\mathbf{x}_i) - z(\mathbf{x}_i + h)]^2$$

where $\hat{\gamma}(h)$ is the estimated semivariance at lag distance $h$, $N(h)$ is the number of data pairs separated by distance $h$ (within some tolerance), and $z(\mathbf{x}_i)$ and $z(\mathbf{x}_i + h)$ are the measured values at two points separated by distance $h$. The empirical semivariogram is then fitted with a theoretical model -- typically spherical, exponential, or Gaussian -- characterized by three parameters:

- **Nugget** ($c_0$): The semivariance at zero distance, representing measurement error and micro-scale variability below the sampling resolution.
- **Sill** ($c_0 + c$): The semivariance at which the variogram levels off, representing the total variance of the data.
- **Range** ($a$): The distance at which the variogram reaches the sill, beyond which data points are effectively uncorrelated.

Once the variogram model is fitted, kriging uses it to compute optimal weights for each sample point, minimizing the estimation variance at every prediction location. The kriging equations simultaneously ensure that the estimator is **unbiased** (the expected estimation error is zero) and has **minimum variance** (the expected squared error is as small as possible). No other linear estimator can do better, given the assumed variogram model -- hence the "best linear unbiased" designation.

A unique advantage of kriging is that it produces not only the estimated value but also the **kriging variance** (or kriging standard error) at each prediction location. This variance map shows where the estimates are reliable (near gauges, where the kriging variance is low) and where they are uncertain (far from gauges, or in areas where the gauge network is sparse). No other interpolation method provides this built-in uncertainty quantification. For decision-making under uncertainty -- should we install another gauge here? is our precipitation estimate reliable enough for this flood forecast? -- the kriging variance map is invaluable.

Several variants of kriging exist for different situations:

- **Ordinary kriging** assumes a constant but unknown mean across the domain. This is the most common variant.
- **Universal kriging** (kriging with a trend) allows the mean to vary as a function of the coordinates, which is useful when there is a regional trend in the data.
- **Co-kriging** incorporates auxiliary variables that are correlated with the variable of interest. For precipitation, the most common auxiliary variable is elevation: co-kriging uses the dense DEM grid to improve precipitation estimates between gauges. This is particularly powerful in mountainous terrain where the orographic effect creates a strong elevation-precipitation relationship.
- **Empirical Bayesian Kriging** automates much of the variogram fitting process by estimating the variogram through a restricted maximum likelihood procedure, rather than requiring the analyst to manually select and fit a variogram model. This variant, available in ArcGIS Pro, makes kriging more accessible to non-specialists.

The principal disadvantage of kriging is its complexity. Variogram fitting requires judgment and experience, and a poorly fitted variogram will produce poor results. Kriging also assumes that the spatial correlation structure is stationary (the same everywhere in the domain), which may not hold for precipitation over large or topographically diverse areas. Finally, kriging can be computationally intensive for very large datasets, though modern implementations with local neighborhoods and efficient algorithms handle datasets of tens of thousands of points without difficulty.

### 8.2.6 Polynomial Trend Surfaces

A polynomial trend surface fits a global polynomial function to the sample data. A first-order (linear) trend surface is a tilted plane; a second-order (quadratic) trend surface can represent a dome, bowl, or saddle; higher orders allow increasingly complex shapes. The polynomial coefficients are determined by least-squares regression, minimizing the sum of squared residuals between the fitted surface and the measured values.

Unlike the previous methods, a polynomial trend surface typically does not pass through the sample points exactly (unless the degree of the polynomial equals or exceeds the number of sample points minus one). It is therefore a **smoothing** method rather than an **interpolating** method in the strict sense. A first-order surface captures only the regional trend -- for instance, precipitation increasing from east to west across a study area. Higher-order polynomials can capture more complex patterns, but they are prone to **overfitting**: the surface may oscillate wildly between data points, especially near the edges of the data domain, producing physically meaningless extreme values.

Polynomial trend surfaces are rarely used as standalone interpolation methods for hydrological variables. However, they play an important role as components of more sophisticated methods. Universal kriging, for example, fits a polynomial trend surface to capture the regional pattern and then applies kriging to the residuals (the deviations from the trend). This two-stage approach can capture both large-scale trends and local spatial correlation, producing better results than either method alone.

### 8.2.7 Comparing Methods: The Same Data, Different Surfaces

The choice of interpolation method matters. When the same set of sample points is interpolated using different methods, the resulting surfaces can differ substantially in their spatial character, their extreme values, and their suitability for downstream analysis. A useful exercise is to interpolate the same precipitation dataset using nearest neighbor, IDW, spline, and kriging, and compare the results side by side.

**Nearest neighbor** produces a blocky, mosaic-like surface with sharp discontinuities at polygon boundaries. The surface contains only the values present in the input data -- no new values are created. It is best suited for categorical data or for quick visualizations where exact spatial patterns are less important than general spatial attribution.

**IDW** produces a smooth surface with concentric "bull's-eye" contours around each sample point. The surface passes through the data points and transitions gradually between them, but the circular contour patterns are artifacts of the distance-weighting scheme and rarely reflect real spatial patterns. IDW is a reasonable default for moderate-density, irregularly distributed data when simplicity is valued over statistical rigor.

**Spline** produces a globally smooth surface that can capture complex spatial patterns. However, it may overshoot between sample points, producing values outside the data range, and is sensitive to parameter settings (tension, smoothing weight). It works well for smoothly varying variables with dense, regular sampling.

**Kriging** produces a smooth surface that reflects the spatial correlation structure of the data. It provides uncertainty estimates, can incorporate auxiliary variables, and is theoretically optimal for the assumed correlation model. It is the method of choice for sparse, irregularly distributed data when the analyst has the expertise (or the software automation) to fit the variogram properly.

The classic reference for comparing interpolation methods in hydrological applications is the work by Grayson and Bloschl (2000), *Spatial Patterns in Catchment Hydrology: Observations and Modelling*, published by Cambridge University Press. Their Chapter 2, "Spatial Observations and Interpolation," provides a thorough treatment with hydrological examples that remains relevant a quarter-century later.

## 8.3 Selecting an Interpolation Method

Choosing the right interpolation method is a decision that depends on the nature of the variable, the quality and density of the data, the availability of auxiliary information, and the intended use of the interpolated surface. There is no universally best method -- each has strengths and weaknesses, and the appropriate choice varies with the application.

### 8.3.1 Nature of the Variable

The first consideration is whether the variable is continuous or categorical. Continuous variables -- precipitation, temperature, elevation, soil moisture -- can take any value within their physical range and are expected to vary more or less smoothly in space. These are candidates for IDW, spline, or kriging interpolation. Categorical variables -- land use type, soil class, geological formation -- take discrete values that cannot be meaningfully averaged. The only appropriate interpolation method for categorical data is nearest neighbor.

Among continuous variables, the expected smoothness matters. Temperature varies smoothly at scales of tens of kilometers (except near coastlines and in complex terrain). Long-term average precipitation varies smoothly at regional scales but can be locally heterogeneous. Event-based precipitation -- a single storm -- can vary dramatically over distances of just a few kilometers. Methods that enforce global smoothness (low-tension splines, low-order polynomials) are appropriate for the first type but may smooth away real variability in the second and third types.

### 8.3.2 Density and Distribution of Samples

The density of the gauge network relative to the spatial variability of the phenomenon determines how much information the interpolation method has to work with. A dense, regularly spaced network can be adequately served by simple methods like IDW or bilinear interpolation. A sparse, irregularly distributed network benefits from kriging, which uses the spatial correlation structure to make the most of limited data.

The distribution of gauges matters as well. If gauges are clustered -- as precipitation gauges often are, concentrated in populated lowland areas -- then methods that account for this clustering (kriging with a declustering correction, or the use of search neighborhoods in IDW) will produce better results than methods that treat all gauges equally. A gauge cluster contributes redundant information: knowing the precipitation at one valley station adds little if you already know the precipitation at three other stations within 5 km. Kriging automatically accounts for this by assigning lower weights to clustered points.

### 8.3.3 Availability of Auxiliary Variables

When auxiliary variables are available -- particularly elevation for precipitation interpolation -- methods that can incorporate them will outperform methods that cannot. Co-kriging uses the spatial cross-correlation between the variable of interest and the auxiliary variable. Regression-kriging fits a regression model between the variable and the auxiliary variable, then applies kriging to the residuals. The PRISM approach uses local regression relationships that vary with slope, aspect, and elevation. All of these methods exploit the strong physical relationship between precipitation and topography to fill in the gaps between gauges.

In modern practice, gridded elevation data (DEMs) are available essentially everywhere at resolutions from 30 m (SRTM, Copernicus GLO-30) to 5 m (national DEMs like Hungary's DDM-5) or better (see Chapter 7, Section 7.7 for a comprehensive survey of DEM sources). This means that auxiliary-variable methods are always feasible for precipitation interpolation -- the auxiliary data is never the limiting factor. The limiting factor is the analyst's willingness to invest the additional effort, or the availability of software tools that automate the process.

### 8.3.4 Tolerance for Overshooting

Some interpolation methods can produce estimated values that exceed the range of the input data. A cubic spline, for example, may produce a precipitation value of $-2$ mm at a location between a gauge recording 0 mm and a gauge recording 5 mm. This is physically impossible and must be corrected (typically by clamping to zero). IDW and nearest-neighbor methods never overshoot -- the estimated value is always within the range of the nearby data values. Kriging can overshoot slightly, but the kriging equations constrain the estimate to be a weighted average of the data, which limits the magnitude of overshooting.

For applications where physically meaningful extreme values are important -- flood hydrology, drought monitoring, environmental compliance -- the analyst should either choose a method that does not overshoot or apply post-processing constraints to the interpolated surface.

### 8.3.5 Need for Uncertainty Estimates

If the application requires not just an estimate but a measure of how reliable that estimate is, kriging is the only standard interpolation method that provides this information directly. The kriging variance at each location quantifies the estimation uncertainty, and it can be used to construct confidence intervals, to identify locations where additional measurements would most reduce uncertainty, and to propagate precipitation uncertainty through downstream hydrological models.

For applications where uncertainty quantification is critical -- real-time flood forecasting, where the uncertainty in precipitation input directly affects the uncertainty in the flood forecast -- kriging or Bayesian approaches are strongly preferred. For applications where a single "best estimate" suffices -- computing long-term average precipitation for a water balance study -- simpler methods may be adequate.

**Table 8.2**: Decision guide for selecting an interpolation method

| Situation | Recommended Method | Rationale |
|-----------|-------------------|-----------|
| Categorical data (land use, soil type) | Nearest neighbor | Only method that handles discrete categories |
| Dense, regular grid resampling | Bilinear or cubic convolution | Designed for grid-to-grid transformation |
| Quick estimate, moderate data density | IDW ($p = 2$) | Simple, no parameters to fit, reasonable results |
| Smooth variable, dense data | Spline (with appropriate tension) | Produces globally smooth surface |
| Sparse data, no auxiliary variables | Ordinary kriging | Optimal use of limited data, provides uncertainty |
| Sparse data, elevation available | Co-kriging or regression-kriging | Exploits elevation-precipitation relationship |
| Operational setting, non-specialist users | Empirical Bayesian Kriging | Automates variogram fitting |
| Watershed-average precipitation from gauges | Thiessen polygons | Simple, reproducible, widely accepted |

## 8.4 The Orographic Effect and Precipitation Climatology

### 8.4.1 Why Mountains Change Everything

The **orographic effect** is the enhancement of precipitation caused by topographic lifting. When a moist air mass encounters a mountain range, it is forced upward. As it rises, it cools adiabatically, its capacity to hold water vapor decreases, and the excess moisture condenses and falls as precipitation on the windward side of the range. By the time the air descends on the leeward side, much of its moisture has been lost, producing a **rain shadow** -- a dry zone on the downwind side of the mountains.

This mechanism creates systematic spatial patterns in precipitation that are strongly correlated with topography. In the United States, the western slope of the Cascade Range in Washington state receives over 3,000 mm of annual precipitation, while the eastern slope, just 100 km away, receives less than 300 mm. In the Alps, the northern slopes of the Austrian and Swiss Alps are wetter than the southern slopes, and the inneralpine valleys (Valais, Engadin, Otztal) are distinctly dry. In the Carpathian Basin, the Matra and Bukk mountains of northern Hungary receive 700-800 mm/year, while the central Great Plain receives only 500-550 mm/year -- a modest gradient by global standards, but one that matters for agricultural water management and flood hydrology.

The practical implication for interpolation is profound. If gauge stations are concentrated in valleys and lowlands -- as they almost always are -- a simple distance-based interpolation method will underestimate precipitation at high elevations and overestimate it in rain shadows. The error can be enormous: in mountainous regions with sparse high-elevation gauges, the bias in annual precipitation can exceed 50%. This is not a theoretical concern -- it directly affects water balance calculations, reservoir inflow estimates, and flood forecasts.

### 8.4.2 PRISM: The US Approach

The **Parameter-elevation Regressions on Independent Slopes Model (PRISM)**, developed by Christopher Daly at the PRISM Climate Group at Oregon State University, is the most widely used approach for accounting for the orographic effect in the United States. PRISM does not simply regress precipitation against elevation; it develops local regression models that vary in space, accounting for slope orientation, proximity to coastlines, and the topographic position of each grid cell.

PRISM works by defining a "physiographic neighborhood" for each grid cell, selecting gauge stations that share similar topographic characteristics (slope aspect, elevation range, distance from coast), and fitting a local linear regression between gauge elevation and gauge precipitation within that neighborhood. The regression is then applied to the grid cell's own elevation to estimate precipitation. The key innovation is that the regression relationship changes from place to place, capturing the fact that the elevation-precipitation gradient is steeper on windward slopes than on leeward slopes, and different in coastal mountains than in continental interiors.

PRISM produces monthly and annual precipitation normals at 800 m (30 arc-second) and 4 km resolution for the entire contiguous United States, as well as daily time series. These products are the standard reference climatology for the US and are used extensively in hydrological modeling, agricultural planning, and climate impact assessment. PRISM data are freely available from the PRISM Climate Group website:

```
https://prism.oregonstate.edu/
```

### 8.4.3 European Gridded Climate Products: E-OBS and CARPATCLIM

Europe takes a different approach to gridded climate data, reflecting its institutional structure and multinational geography. Two products are particularly relevant for hydrological work in Hungary and Central Europe.

**E-OBS** is a daily gridded observational dataset covering Europe from 1950 to the present, produced by the Royal Netherlands Meteorological Institute (KNMI) as part of the European Climate Assessment & Dataset (ECA&D) project. E-OBS interpolates station data from national meteorological services across Europe onto a regular grid at 0.1-degree (~11 km) and 0.25-degree (~28 km) resolution. Variables include daily mean, minimum, and maximum temperature, precipitation amount, sea-level pressure, relative humidity, wind speed, and global radiation. The current version (v32.0e, November 2025) is distributed through the Copernicus Climate Data Store in NetCDF-4 format.

What makes E-OBS particularly valuable is its **ensemble approach**: rather than producing a single "best" estimate, E-OBS generates a 20-member ensemble, each representing a plausible realization of the gridded field given the station data and interpolation uncertainty. This allows users to assess the uncertainty in the gridded values -- a feature that is conceptually similar to kriging variance but implemented through ensemble spread. The ensemble approach is especially important in data-sparse regions or for extreme events, where the interpolation uncertainty may be substantial.

E-OBS uses a three-step interpolation process: (1) thin-plate smoothing splines for the long-term mean field (climatology), incorporating elevation as a co-variable; (2) kriging of daily anomalies (departures from the climatology); and (3) combination of the climatological field and the anomaly field. This approach captures the orographic effect through the climatological field while allowing the daily patterns to be driven by the gauge data.

E-OBS is freely available:

```
https://surfobs.climate.copernicus.eu/dataaccess/access_eobs.php
```

**CARPATCLIM** is a high-resolution daily gridded climate dataset specifically designed for the Greater Carpathian Region, covering the period 1961-2010 at 0.1-degree resolution. Produced by a consortium of nine countries led by the Hungarian Meteorological Service (OMSZ), CARPATCLIM is the most detailed climate atlas available for Hungary and its surroundings. It provides not only standard meteorological variables (temperature, precipitation) but also over 30 derived climate indices -- heat days, frost days, growing degree days, precipitation intensity, and many others.

The critical advantage of CARPATCLIM over E-OBS for Hungarian applications is the quality of the underlying station data. CARPATCLIM used homogenized and harmonized station records from all nine Carpathian countries, resolving cross-border inconsistencies that can affect European-wide datasets. The homogenization corrects for station relocations, instrument changes, and observation practice changes that would otherwise introduce artificial trends and discontinuities. For the period it covers (1961-2010), CARPATCLIM is the best available reference climatology for Hungary.

CARPATCLIM is freely available:

```
https://surfobs.climate.copernicus.eu/dataaccess/access_carpatclim.php
```

### 8.4.4 Global Precipitation Products: CHIRPS and GPM

For regions outside the US and Europe -- particularly in Africa, South and Southeast Asia, and Latin America, where ground-based gauge networks are sparse -- satellite-derived and satellite-merged precipitation products are essential.

**CHIRPS (Climate Hazards Group InfraRed Precipitation with Station data)** is a quasi-global rainfall dataset covering the period 1981 to the present at 0.05-degree (~5.3 km) resolution, available as daily, pentadal (5-day), and monthly products. Developed by the Climate Hazards Center at the University of California, Santa Barbara, CHIRPS combines satellite infrared estimates (which detect cold cloud tops associated with convective rainfall) with ground station data, using a modified inverse-distance weighting approach that accounts for station density and data quality. CHIRPS is specifically designed for trend analysis and drought monitoring in regions where gauge data is sparse -- it was originally developed with Africa in mind, but its coverage extends from 50 degrees South to 50 degrees North.

CHIRPS is widely used in agricultural drought monitoring (it is the primary precipitation input for the USAID Famine Early Warning Systems Network, FEWS NET), water resources assessment in developing countries, and climate change impact studies. Version 3, released in 2024, improves upon version 2 with better station integration and updated satellite calibration.

```
https://www.chc.ucsb.edu/data/chirps
```

**GPM/IMERG (Global Precipitation Measurement / Integrated Multi-satellitE Retrievals for GPM)** provides near-real-time and retrospective precipitation estimates at 0.1-degree (~10 km) resolution and half-hourly temporal resolution. The GPM mission, launched in 2014 as a joint NASA/JAXA effort, uses a constellation of satellites carrying microwave radiometers and radars to directly measure precipitation from space. The IMERG algorithm merges these satellite observations into a seamless global precipitation product.

GPM/IMERG has two key advantages over CHIRPS: higher temporal resolution (half-hourly versus daily) and direct microwave measurement of precipitation (rather than inference from infrared cloud-top temperatures). These make it particularly valuable for studying individual storm events and for regions where convective rainfall dominates. However, for long-term climatological studies, CHIRPS' longer record (back to 1981 versus GPM's 2000 start with TRMM predecessor data) is an advantage.

**ERA5** reanalysis data from ECMWF, described in Chapter 3, provides another global precipitation dataset at approximately 31 km resolution and hourly time steps, extending back to 1940. ERA5 is not an interpolation of gauge data -- it is the output of a numerical weather prediction model that assimilates observations from many sources (satellites, radiosondes, surface stations, aircraft). ERA5 precipitation is therefore a model product, not a direct observation, and it has known biases (it tends to overestimate light precipitation and underestimate heavy precipitation). However, its global coverage, temporal depth, and consistency make it a valuable complement to gauge-based products.

### 8.4.5 The Hypsometric Method: A Simple Alternative

When the resources or expertise for PRISM-type modeling are not available, the **hypsometric method** provides a simple but effective way to account for the elevation-precipitation relationship. The method proceeds in three steps:

1. **Develop a regression** between station elevation and annual (or monthly) precipitation from the available gauge data. For many regions, a linear regression $P = a + b \cdot h$ (where $P$ is precipitation and $h$ is elevation) captures the first-order orographic effect.

2. **Apply the regression** to each cell of the DEM, computing a precipitation value for each cell based on its elevation.

3. **Adjust the result** so that the gauge values are honored -- this is typically done by computing residuals (observed minus regression-predicted at gauge locations) and interpolating the residuals spatially, then adding the interpolated residual field to the regression-predicted field.

This three-step approach is essentially a simplified version of what PRISM and E-OBS do, but with a global rather than local regression. It works well when the elevation-precipitation relationship is approximately linear and does not vary much across the study area. It breaks down when the relationship differs substantially between windward and leeward slopes, or when precipitation patterns are controlled by factors other than elevation (proximity to moisture sources, prevailing wind direction, rain shadow effects).

**Worked Example: Hypsometric Precipitation Estimate for the Zagyva Watershed (Hungary)**

The Zagyva River drains approximately 5,677 km$^2$ in northern Hungary, flowing from the Matra and Bukk mountains southward to join the Tisza near Szolnok. We will use a simplified hypsometric approach to estimate mean annual precipitation across this watershed.

**Step 1: Station data.** Consider six OMSZ stations in and around the watershed:

**Table 8.4**: Station data for the Zagyva watershed hypsometric example

| Station | Elevation (m) | Mean Annual Precip. (mm) |
|---------|--------------|------------------------|
| Szolnok | 89 | 520 |
| Jaszbereny | 98 | 540 |
| Gyongyos | 170 | 580 |
| Salgotarjan | 315 | 640 |
| Parad | 410 | 690 |
| Kekes-teto | 1,010 | 800 |

**Step 2: Fit a linear regression.**

Using least-squares regression of precipitation on elevation:

$$P = 497 + 0.308 \times h$$

where $P$ is mean annual precipitation in mm and $h$ is elevation in m. The coefficient of determination is $R^2 = 0.97$, indicating a strong linear relationship. The slope indicates that precipitation increases by approximately 31 mm for every 100 m gain in elevation.

**Step 3: Apply to DEM cells.**

For a DEM cell at 500 m elevation:

$$P = 497 + 0.308 \times 500 = 497 + 154 = 651 \text{ mm}$$

For a DEM cell at 100 m elevation (the Alfold plains):

$$P = 497 + 0.308 \times 100 = 497 + 31 = 528 \text{ mm}$$

**Step 4: Compute watershed average.**

Using the DEM's hypsometric curve (the distribution of elevations within the watershed), with a mean watershed elevation of approximately 220 m:

$$\bar{P}_{\text{watershed}} = 497 + 0.308 \times 220 = 497 + 68 = 565 \text{ mm}$$

**Interpretation**: The estimated mean annual precipitation for the Zagyva watershed is about 565 mm. This is approximately 30 mm higher than what a simple average of the two lowland stations (Szolnok, Jaszbereny) would give, reflecting the contribution of the higher-elevation portions of the watershed that receive more rainfall. The difference is modest in this case because the Zagyva watershed is dominated by lowland areas, with only its northern headwaters reaching above 500 m. In a more mountainous watershed, the correction would be much larger.

## 8.5 Watershed Averaging Using Zonal Statistics

### 8.5.1 The Core Operation

Most hydrological calculations require spatially averaged values over defined areas -- mean precipitation over a watershed, mean elevation of a sub-basin, average temperature across a planning region. The GIS operation that computes these values is called **zonal statistics**: given a raster surface and a set of polygons defining zones, compute summary statistics (mean, minimum, maximum, standard deviation, sum, count) of the raster values within each polygon.

This operation is conceptually simple but practically powerful. In Chapter 6's classification of map algebra operations, zonal statistics are a **zonal operation** -- each output value is computed from all cells sharing a common zone identifier. Zonal statistics bridge the gap between the continuous raster representation of a variable (a precipitation surface, a DEM, a temperature grid) and the discrete polygon representation of analysis units (watersheds, administrative regions, planning zones). The result is a table that assigns one or more summary values to each polygon, which can then be joined back to the polygon feature class for mapping, further analysis, or export.

In ArcGIS Pro, the operation is performed with the **Zonal Statistics as Table** tool (Spatial Analyst > Zonal). The tool requires three inputs:

1. **Input zone data**: A polygon feature class (or a raster) defining the zones. For hydrological applications, this is typically a watershed or sub-basin feature class.
2. **Zone field**: The attribute that uniquely identifies each zone (e.g., HUC code, HydroID, watershed name).
3. **Input value raster**: The raster to be summarized (e.g., a DEM, a precipitation surface, a land cover grid).

The output is a table with one row per zone, containing the zone identifier, the count of raster cells within the zone, and the requested statistics (mean, min, max, range, standard deviation, sum). This table can be joined to the original polygon feature class using the zone field as the join key, making the statistics available as polygon attributes for mapping and further analysis.

In QGIS, the equivalent operation is performed through the **Zonal Statistics** tool in the Processing Toolbox, or through the GRASS `r.stats.zonal` module. The functionality is identical: raster values are summarized within polygon zones, and the results are appended as new attribute fields to the polygon layer.

### 8.5.2 Precision of Zonal Statistics

A natural question is: how precise are zonal statistics? If two independent workflows produce different raster surfaces of the same variable (for instance, two DEMs from different sources, or the same DEM resampled at different resolutions), how much will the zonal averages differ?

The answer, perhaps surprisingly, is that zonal statistics are extremely precise when the zone contains many raster cells. The following table illustrates this for average elevation computed over HUC10-equivalent sub-watersheds using two independent DEM sources:

**Table 8.5**: Precision of zonal elevation statistics -- comparison of two independent DEM computations

| Sub-watershed | Avg. Elev. Method 1 (m) | Avg. Elev. Method 2 (m) | Difference (cm) | Cell Count |
|---------------|------------------------|------------------------|-----------------|------------|
| SW-1 | 189.91 | 189.86 | -5.0 | 323,069 |
| SW-2 | 418.54 | 418.48 | -6.0 | 1,023,567 |
| SW-3 | 288.63 | 288.57 | -6.0 | 165,683 |
| SW-4 | 266.19 | 266.11 | -8.0 | 140,798 |
| SW-5 | 151.93 | 151.94 | +1.0 | 579,142 |
| SW-6 | 183.59 | 183.54 | -5.0 | 1,089,142 |
| **Average** | **249.80** | **249.75** | **-4.83** | **553,567** |

The differences between the two methods are approximately 5 cm in an average elevation of 250 m -- a relative error of about 0.002%. To put this in perspective, the Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) of the US National Elevation Dataset relative to ground truth is 2.34 m. For the Copernicus DEM GLO-30, the absolute vertical accuracy (LE90) is less than 4 m globally and approximately 1 m in well-mapped European areas. The computational precision of zonal statistics (centimeters) is two to three orders of magnitude better than the accuracy of the source elevation data (meters). This means that any observed differences in watershed-average values between independent analyses reflect differences in the source data, not errors in the zonal statistics computation itself.

This precision holds because zonal statistics involve averaging over very large numbers of cells. Even a small sub-watershed of 50 km$^2$, represented on a 30 m DEM, contains approximately 55,000 cells. The standard error of the mean decreases as $1/\sqrt{n}$, where $n$ is the number of cells. With $n = 55{,}000$, random errors in individual cells are suppressed by a factor of about 235. Only systematic errors -- biases that affect all cells in the same direction -- survive the averaging process.

### 8.5.3 Joining Results to Feature Classes

The output of Zonal Statistics as Table is a standalone table. To make the computed statistics usable for mapping and further spatial analysis, the table must be **joined** back to the original polygon feature class. This join operation matches rows in the table to features in the polygon layer using a common field -- typically the zone identifier.

In ArcGIS Pro, this is accomplished with the **Add Join** tool or by right-clicking the feature layer and selecting "Add Join." In QGIS, the equivalent is the **Join attributes by field value** tool in the Processing Toolbox, or the Joins tab in the layer properties dialog. Both platforms support temporary (in-memory) joins and permanent (export to new layer) joins.

After joining, the polygon feature class contains the zonal statistics as additional attribute fields. This enables direct visualization -- for example, symbolizing watersheds by mean annual precipitation using a graduated color scheme -- and further analysis -- for example, selecting all watersheds with mean elevation above 300 m for a mountain hydrology study.

The joined results can also be exported to tabular formats (CSV, Excel) for analysis in other software. In ArcGIS Pro, the **Table to Excel** tool converts any attribute table to an Excel workbook. In QGIS, any layer can be exported to CSV or other formats through the Export dialog.

## 8.6 The Thiessen Polygon Method for Watershed-Average Precipitation

### 8.6.1 The Method and Its Logic

The **Thiessen polygon method** is the oldest and most widely used technique for computing area-weighted average precipitation over a watershed from point gauge measurements. Developed by Alfred H. Thiessen of the US Weather Bureau in 1911, it remains a standard method in operational hydrology more than a century later, used by water management agencies worldwide.

The method's logic is straightforward. Each gauge is assigned a polygon encompassing all locations that are closer to that gauge than to any other gauge -- the Thiessen polygon, as described in Section 8.2.1. The watershed boundary is then overlaid on the Thiessen polygons, creating polygon fragments where each fragment inherits the precipitation value of its parent gauge and an area determined by the intersection geometry. The watershed-average precipitation is the area-weighted average of the gauge values:

$$\bar{P} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} P_i \cdot A_i}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} A_i}$$

where $P_i$ is the precipitation at gauge $i$, $A_i$ is the area of the intersection of gauge $i$'s Thiessen polygon with the watershed, and $n$ is the number of gauges whose Thiessen polygons intersect the watershed.

The method is a form of nearest-neighbor interpolation followed by spatial averaging. It assigns each point in the watershed to its nearest gauge, then integrates over the watershed area. The Thiessen polygon areas serve as weights: a gauge whose polygon covers 40% of the watershed area contributes 40% to the weighted average.

### 8.6.2 Step-by-Step Workflow in GIS

The following workflow implements the Thiessen polygon method in ArcGIS Pro. The equivalent operations are available in QGIS through the Processing Toolbox (Voronoi polygons are created with the **Voronoi Polygons** tool, and the overlay is performed with the **Intersection** tool).

**Step 1: Create Thiessen Polygons.** Use the **Create Thiessen Polygons** tool (Analysis > Proximity) with the gauge point feature class as input. Select "All fields" for output fields to ensure the precipitation attribute is carried to the output polygons. Set the processing environment extent to the watershed boundary (with a buffer) to ensure complete coverage.

**Step 2: Intersect with Watershed Boundaries.** Use the **Intersect** tool (Analysis > Overlay) with the Thiessen polygons and the watershed polygon layer as inputs. The output contains polygon fragments, each inheriting attributes from both inputs: the precipitation value from the Thiessen polygon and the watershed identifier from the watershed layer.

**Step 3: Compute Area-Precipitation Products.** Add a new field to the intersected feature class (e.g., `AP_Product`, type Double) and use **Calculate Field** to compute:

```
AP_Product = Precipitation * Shape_Area
```

This gives the numerator of the area-weighted average for each polygon fragment. Note that `Shape_Area` is automatically maintained by the geodatabase; it should be in square meters if the data is in a projected coordinate system with metric units.

**Step 4: Summarize by Watershed.** Use the **Summary Statistics** tool (Analysis > Statistics) to compute, for each watershed:

- `SUM(Shape_Area)` -- the total area of the watershed.
- `SUM(AP_Product)` -- the sum of area-precipitation products.

Group the summary by the watershed identifier field.

**Step 5: Compute Watershed-Average Precipitation.** Add a new field to the summary table and compute:

```
Mean_Precip = SUM_AP_Product / SUM_Shape_Area
```

This yields the Thiessen polygon estimate of mean precipitation for each watershed.

### 8.6.3 Worked Example: Thiessen Polygon Averaging

Consider a watershed with three precipitation gauges:

**Table 8.6**: Input data for Thiessen polygon worked example

| Gauge | Annual Precipitation (mm) | Thiessen Area within Watershed (km$^2$) |
|-------|-------------------------|--------------------------------------|
| G1 | 680 | 142 |
| G2 | 520 | 285 |
| G3 | 610 | 93 |
| **Total** | -- | **520** |

**Step 1: Compute area-precipitation products.**

$$\text{AP}_1 = 680 \times 142 = 96{,}560$$
$$\text{AP}_2 = 520 \times 285 = 148{,}200$$
$$\text{AP}_3 = 610 \times 93 = 56{,}730$$

**Step 2: Sum.**

$$\sum \text{AP} = 96{,}560 + 148{,}200 + 56{,}730 = 301{,}490$$

$$\sum A = 142 + 285 + 93 = 520 \text{ km}^2$$

**Step 3: Compute weighted average.**

$$\bar{P} = \frac{301{,}490}{520} = 579.8 \text{ mm}$$

**Interpretation**: The Thiessen-weighted average precipitation is 579.8 mm, which differs from the simple arithmetic mean of the three gauges ($\frac{680 + 520 + 610}{3} = 603.3$ mm) by 23.5 mm. The Thiessen average is lower because gauge G2, which records the lowest precipitation (520 mm), has the largest Thiessen polygon area within the watershed (285 km$^2$, or 54.8% of the total). The area weighting correctly gives more influence to the gauge that represents the largest portion of the watershed, even though its precipitation is the lowest.

### 8.6.4 Limitations of the Thiessen Polygon Method

The Thiessen polygon method, for all its simplicity and wide acceptance, has significant limitations that the analyst should be aware of.

First, it assumes that precipitation is constant within each Thiessen polygon -- a nearest-neighbor assumption that produces abrupt discontinuities at polygon boundaries. In reality, precipitation varies continuously in space, and the true pattern between two gauges is not a step function but a gradient. The Thiessen method cannot capture this gradient.

Second, the polygon boundaries are determined entirely by gauge geometry, not by any physical or climatological consideration. A ridge line between two valleys may create a natural precipitation boundary -- wetter on one side, drier on the other -- but the Thiessen boundary between two valley gauges will be a vertical bisector that may fall on the ridge, in the wrong valley, or at some arbitrary location that has no relationship to the actual precipitation pattern.

Third, and most critically, the Thiessen method ignores the orographic effect entirely. It treats precipitation as a flat, two-dimensional field determined solely by horizontal distance to gauges. In mountainous terrain, this can produce large errors because the method assigns high-elevation areas the precipitation value of the nearest low-elevation gauge, which may be much too low.

Fourth, the method is sensitive to the gauge network. Adding or removing a single gauge can substantially change the Thiessen polygon geometry and, consequently, the watershed-average estimate. This sensitivity is problematic for long-term studies where gauge networks change over time (stations are added, relocated, or decommissioned).

Despite these limitations, the Thiessen polygon method remains in widespread operational use because it is deterministic, reproducible, easy to explain to non-specialists, and requires no parameters or subjective choices beyond the selection of gauge stations. It is the method of record for many water management agencies and is accepted by courts and regulatory bodies. For legal and regulatory purposes, its simplicity and reproducibility are features, not bugs.

## 8.7 Surface-Based Alternatives to Thiessen Polygons

### 8.7.1 The Two-Step Approach

An increasingly common alternative to the Thiessen polygon method is the **surface-based** approach: first, interpolate the gauge data to create a continuous precipitation surface using one of the methods described in Section 8.2 (IDW, spline, kriging, or a regression-based method incorporating elevation); then, apply zonal statistics (Section 8.5) to compute the mean of the interpolated surface over each watershed.

This two-step approach has several advantages over the Thiessen method:

1. **Spatial smoothness**: The interpolated surface varies continuously in space, eliminating the abrupt discontinuities of the Thiessen method. The watershed-average value is a true spatial average of a continuous field, not a discrete weighted combination of a few gauge values.

2. **Incorporation of auxiliary variables**: If kriging, co-kriging, or regression-based methods are used, the interpolated surface can account for elevation, slope, aspect, and other topographic variables. This is particularly important in mountainous terrain, where the orographic effect creates systematic patterns that the Thiessen method ignores.

3. **Consistency between map and average**: The interpolated surface provides a spatially explicit precipitation map that can be inspected visually for reasonableness. The watershed-average value is then simply the integral of this map over the watershed area. With the Thiessen method, there is no intermediate map -- just a set of polygons with constant values.

4. **Compatibility with gridded climate products**: When pre-computed gridded precipitation products (E-OBS, CARPATCLIM, CHIRPS, PRISM) are available, the "interpolation" step has already been done by the product provider using sophisticated methods that incorporate station data, satellite observations, and elevation. The analyst simply downloads the gridded product and applies zonal statistics -- a one-step workflow that is simpler than the Thiessen method and produces better results.

### 8.7.2 When Pre-Computed Grids Are Available

In modern practice, the most common implementation of the surface-based approach does not involve performing interpolation at all. Instead, the analyst downloads a pre-computed gridded climate product and applies zonal statistics directly. This is the recommended workflow for most hydrological applications, because the gridded products have been developed by climate scientists using sophisticated methods, extensive station networks, and quality control procedures that are beyond the scope of a typical hydrological project.

For a hydrological study in Hungary, the recommended workflow is:

1. Download the CARPATCLIM daily precipitation grids (1961-2010, 0.1-degree resolution) or the E-OBS daily grids (1950-present, 0.1-degree resolution).
2. Compute the desired temporal aggregation (annual total, monthly total, event total) from the daily grids.
3. Apply Zonal Statistics to compute the spatial average over each watershed or sub-basin.

For a study in the United States, the equivalent workflow uses PRISM grids. For a study in Africa or tropical Asia, it uses CHIRPS or GPM/IMERG. For a global or continental-scale study, ERA5 provides a consistent framework.

### 8.7.3 Comparison of Thiessen and Surface-Based Methods

The choice between Thiessen polygons and surface-based methods depends on the application, the available data, and institutional requirements.

For **operational hydrology** with established procedures and regulatory requirements, the Thiessen method remains widely used because it is simple, reproducible, and well-documented. Many operational hydrological models (HEC-HMS, SWMM) accept Thiessen-weighted precipitation inputs directly. Changing to a surface-based method would require recalibrating the model and documenting the new procedure, which may not be justified for an operational system that is working adequately.

For **research and planning** applications, surface-based methods are generally preferred because they produce more physically realistic precipitation fields, especially in areas with significant topographic relief. The additional complexity is justified by the improved accuracy, and the availability of pre-computed gridded products means that the "additional complexity" is actually less than the Thiessen polygon workflow in many cases.

For **data-sparse regions** -- areas with few or no local gauges -- surface-based methods using satellite-merged products (CHIRPS, GPM/IMERG) are the only viable option. The Thiessen method requires local gauge data, and if no gauges exist within the watershed, it cannot be applied at all.

For **event-based analysis** -- estimating precipitation for a specific storm event -- the choice depends on whether the event was captured by the gauge network. If local gauges recorded the event, the Thiessen method provides a quick and reliable estimate. If the gauges missed the event (which happens frequently with convective storms), radar-based or satellite-based products may be the only source of spatial precipitation information.

## 8.8 Hungarian Case Study: Precipitation Averaging for the Upper Tisza Basin

### 8.8.1 Background

The Upper Tisza Basin, upstream of the gauging station at Szolnok, drains approximately 73,000 km$^2$ across Hungary, Slovakia, Ukraine, and Romania. It encompasses terrain ranging from the flat Hungarian Great Plain (80-100 m elevation) to the Carpathian mountain peaks exceeding 2,000 m in Romania and Ukraine. The basin is prone to major flooding -- the 2001 Tisza flood displaced tens of thousands of people, and the 2010 spring floods caused widespread damage across northeastern Hungary.

Estimating precipitation over this basin is a formidable interpolation challenge. The gauge network is dense in the Hungarian lowlands (OMSZ operates 136 stations across Hungary with 10-minute resolution) but sparse in the mountainous headwaters across the border. The orographic effect is dramatic: annual precipitation on the Carpathian ridges exceeds 1,200 mm, while the central Great Plain around Szolnok receives only about 500 mm. A simple average of the Hungarian lowland gauges would vastly underestimate the true basin-average precipitation.

### 8.8.2 Data Sources

For the Hungarian portion of the basin, OMSZ (the Hungarian Meteorological Service, now operating as HungaroMet Zrt.) provides station data through its climate services portal:

```
https://www.met.hu/en/eghajlat/
```

Climate summaries for major cities are freely available. Detailed station data for the full 136-station network can be obtained through data requests to OMSZ. The OVF (General Directorate of Water Management) also operates a precipitation monitoring network as part of the hydrographic database at:

```
https://data.vizugy.hu
```

For the full transboundary basin, CARPATCLIM provides the best available gridded dataset. Its 0.1-degree resolution, daily time step, and 1961-2010 coverage make it ideal for climatological analysis. Critically, CARPATCLIM was produced by a consortium that included the meteorological services of all nine Carpathian countries, meaning that cross-border station data was harmonized and homogenized -- a step that individual national datasets cannot provide.

For more recent periods (after 2010), E-OBS extends the analysis to the present day, though at somewhat lower quality due to the lack of the Carpathian-specific homogenization that CARPATCLIM provides.

### 8.8.3 Method Comparison

To illustrate the differences between methods, consider computing the mean annual precipitation for a HydroBASINS level-7 sub-basin in the headwaters of the Tisza (Carpathian Ukraine, approximately 2,500 km$^2$), using three approaches:

1. **Thiessen polygons from Hungarian gauges only**: Using the nearest OMSZ stations, all located in the Hungarian lowlands at 100-200 m elevation, the Thiessen method assigns these lowland values to the mountain sub-basin. Estimated mean annual precipitation: approximately 580 mm.

2. **IDW interpolation from CARPATCLIM grid points**: Using the CARPATCLIM grid cells within and around the sub-basin (which incorporate Ukrainian and Romanian gauge data and elevation adjustments), IDW interpolation produces a spatially varying surface. Estimated mean annual precipitation: approximately 920 mm.

3. **Zonal statistics from the CARPATCLIM grid directly**: Simply computing the mean of the CARPATCLIM grid cells within the sub-basin. Estimated mean annual precipitation: approximately 950 mm.

The Thiessen estimate (580 mm) underestimates the true basin-average precipitation by roughly 40% compared to the CARPATCLIM reference (950 mm). This is a catastrophic error for hydrological modeling -- it would mean underestimating the water input to the basin by almost half, making it impossible to close the water balance or predict flood peaks. The error arises entirely from the failure to account for the orographic effect and the absence of high-elevation gauges in the Hungarian-only network.

This example illustrates why international collaboration on climate data -- and products like CARPATCLIM that emerge from such collaboration -- is essential for transboundary river basins. Hungary cannot monitor the precipitation that falls on the Carpathian headwaters from Hungarian gauges alone. The water that causes floods in Szolnok originates as precipitation in the mountains of Ukraine and Romania.

## 8.9 Coordinate System Considerations for Spatial Interpolation

### 8.9.1 Why Projections Matter for Interpolation

Spatial interpolation is inherently a distance-based operation. Whether computing Thiessen polygon boundaries (based on nearest distance), IDW weights (inverse of distance), or kriging variograms (semivariance as a function of distance), every method depends on accurate distance calculations between points. This makes the choice of coordinate system critically important -- the same concern that Chapter 6, Section 6.11.5 identifies as the "coordinate system trap" for raster calculations applies with equal force to interpolation.

In a **geographic coordinate system** (longitude/latitude, e.g., WGS84 or ETRS89), coordinates are expressed in degrees. One degree of latitude corresponds to approximately 111 km everywhere on Earth, but one degree of longitude varies from 111 km at the equator to 0 km at the poles. At Hungary's latitude (~47 degrees North), one degree of longitude is approximately $111 \times \cos(47°) \approx 76$ km. This means that computing Euclidean distances directly from geographic coordinates introduces a directional bias: east-west distances are underestimated relative to north-south distances. Thiessen polygons constructed in geographic coordinates will be elongated in the east-west direction, which has no physical basis.

The solution is to work in a **projected coordinate system** that preserves distances as accurately as possible. For Hungary, the natural choices are:

- **HD72/EOV (EPSG:23700)**: Hungary's national coordinate system, an oblique Mercator projection centered on the country. Coordinates are in meters, and distance distortion within Hungary is minimal (scale factor 0.99993 at the central point). This is the standard projection for national datasets, including DDM-5 and OVF data.

- **ETRS89/LAEA (EPSG:3035)**: The standard European equal-area projection, used by Eurostat and Copernicus for pan-European analyses. This is an equal-area projection, meaning it preserves area accurately -- important for Thiessen polygon calculations and zonal statistics where area measurements are used as weights.

- **UTM Zone 34N (EPSG:32634)**: Covers most of Hungary. Preserves shape and distance within the zone, with maximum scale distortion of 0.04%. Good for local to regional studies.

For interpolation specifically, the key requirements are that distances are computed accurately and that areas are preserved for area-weighted operations. An equal-area projection (Albers Equal Area, Lambert Azimuthal Equal Area, or EOV for Hungary) is preferred for Thiessen polygon and zonal statistics workflows.

### 8.9.2 Resampling When Projecting Rasters

When a raster dataset must be reprojected -- for example, converting a CARPATCLIM precipitation grid from its native geographic coordinates (ETRS89) to EOV for combination with Hungarian national datasets -- the raster cells must be **resampled** to the new grid. The choice of resampling method matters:

- **Nearest neighbor**: Assigns each new cell the value of the nearest old cell. Fast but produces staircase artifacts in continuous data. Appropriate only for categorical rasters (land use, soil type).

- **Bilinear interpolation**: Computes each new cell value as a weighted average of the four nearest old cells. Produces smooth results with no staircase artifacts. The standard choice for continuous data like precipitation and temperature.

- **Cubic convolution**: Uses the 16 nearest old cells to compute each new cell value. Slightly sharper than bilinear, but can overshoot. Good for elevation data where preserving ridge and valley detail matters.

The general rule: use bilinear or cubic convolution for continuous variables (elevation, precipitation, temperature), and nearest neighbor only for categorical variables. Never use nearest neighbor for a DEM or precipitation raster -- the resulting artifacts will propagate into derived products (slope, aspect, flow direction, interpolated surfaces).

### 8.9.3 Minimizing Reprojection Steps

Each reprojection introduces a small amount of data degradation through the resampling process. Bilinear and cubic convolution resampling smooth the data slightly, and each successive reprojection compounds this smoothing. The practical recommendation is to plan the workflow to minimize the number of reprojection steps:

1. Identify the target coordinate system for the analysis (e.g., EOV for a Hungarian study, UTM for a cross-border study, ETRS89/LAEA for a European study).
2. Reproject all input datasets to the target system in a single step.
3. Perform all analysis in the target system.
4. If the final output must be in a different system (e.g., geographic coordinates for web mapping), reproject only the final product.

This workflow ensures that the source data undergoes at most two reprojections (source to analysis system, analysis system to output system), preserving data quality as much as possible.

## 8.10 Validation: Does the Precipitation Make Hydrological Sense?

### 8.10.1 The Runoff Ratio as a Consistency Check

When watershed-average precipitation has been computed -- whether by Thiessen polygons, surface-based methods, or zonal statistics from gridded products -- a basic validation is possible by comparing the precipitation estimate with observed streamflow. The **runoff ratio** is defined as:

$$R_r = \frac{\text{Mean annual runoff depth}}{\text{Mean annual precipitation}}$$

where the mean annual runoff depth is computed from the stream gauge record:

$$\text{Runoff depth} = \frac{Q \times T}{A}$$

with $Q$ as the mean annual discharge (m$^3$/s), $T$ as the number of seconds in one year ($3.156 \times 10^7$ s), and $A$ as the watershed area (m$^2$).

The runoff ratio is a dimensionless quantity that represents the fraction of precipitation that becomes streamflow. The complement, $1 - R_r$, represents the fraction lost to evapotranspiration, deep percolation, and other sinks. For typical temperate-climate watersheds:

- $R_r = 0.10 - 0.30$: Dry climate, high evapotranspiration (arid grasslands, Mediterranean climate)
- $R_r = 0.30 - 0.50$: Moderate climate, balanced water budget (much of Central Europe, including Hungary)
- $R_r = 0.50 - 0.70$: Wet climate, low evapotranspiration (maritime climates, high elevations)
- $R_r > 0.70$: Very wet or cool climate with low ET (alpine, subarctic)
- $R_r > 1.0$: **Physically impossible** under steady-state conditions -- indicates an error

A runoff ratio exceeding 1.0 means that more water is leaving the watershed as streamflow than is entering as precipitation, which violates conservation of mass. This can result from an underestimate of precipitation (e.g., using lowland gauges for a mountainous basin), an overestimate of the watershed area, or an error in the streamflow record (e.g., confusing units).

### 8.10.2 Worked Example: Runoff Ratio Validation

**Setting**: The Zagyva River at Szolnok. Watershed area $A = 5{,}677$ km$^2$. Mean annual discharge (from OVF records) $Q = 6.2$ m$^3$/s. Estimated mean annual precipitation (from the hypsometric method, Section 8.4.5) $\bar{P} = 565$ mm.

**Step 1: Compute mean annual runoff depth.**

$$\text{Runoff depth} = \frac{Q \times T}{A} = \frac{6.2 \times 3.156 \times 10^7}{5{,}677 \times 10^6}$$

$$= \frac{1.957 \times 10^8}{5.677 \times 10^9} = 0.0345 \text{ m} = 34.5 \text{ mm}$$

**Step 2: Compute runoff ratio.**

$$R_r = \frac{34.5}{565} = 0.061$$

**Interpretation**: The runoff ratio of 0.061 (about 6%) is very low, even for a lowland Hungarian watershed with high evapotranspiration. This suggests one of the following: (a) evapotranspiration is very high in the Zagyva basin (plausible -- the basin is dominated by agricultural land on the Great Plain); (b) significant groundwater outflow occurs that is not captured by the stream gauge; or (c) the precipitation estimate may be somewhat too high. For comparison, runoff ratios for other Hungarian rivers typically fall in the range of 0.05-0.15 for lowland basins and 0.15-0.35 for mountain basins. The Zagyva value is at the low end of the expected range but not physically impossible.

If the runoff ratio had been, say, 1.2, we would immediately know that something is wrong with either the precipitation, the discharge, or the area. This simple check is one of the most valuable diagnostic tools available to hydrologists and should be applied routinely whenever precipitation and streamflow data are combined.

## 8.11 Practical Recommendations

### 8.11.1 For Operational Hydrology

If you are maintaining an operational hydrological model with established procedures, continue using the Thiessen polygon method if it is the institutional standard. The method's simplicity, reproducibility, and legal defensibility outweigh its technical limitations for many operational applications. However, if you are setting up a new operational system or if your basin has significant topographic relief, seriously consider switching to a surface-based approach using gridded precipitation products. The computational effort is no greater -- in fact, it is often less, because you skip the Thiessen polygon construction -- and the results are more physically realistic.

### 8.11.2 For Research and Planning

Use gridded precipitation products whenever possible. For Hungary and Central Europe, CARPATCLIM (1961-2010) and E-OBS (1950-present) provide well-validated, freely available daily precipitation grids at approximately 11 km resolution. For the United States, PRISM is the standard. For Africa and tropical Asia, CHIRPS is the recommended product. For global studies, ERA5 provides consistent coverage.

Apply zonal statistics to extract watershed averages from these grids. This is simpler and more accurate than performing your own interpolation from raw gauge data, because the gridded products incorporate more station data, better quality control, and more sophisticated interpolation methods than any individual project can replicate.

### 8.11.3 For Data-Sparse Regions

If working in a region with few or no local rain gauges -- common in parts of Africa, Central Asia, and remote mountainous areas -- rely on satellite-merged products: CHIRPS for daily to monthly precipitation, GPM/IMERG for sub-daily precipitation and individual storm events, and ERA5 for a consistent global baseline. Be aware that these products have known biases (CHIRPS tends to underestimate extreme events; ERA5 overestimates light precipitation and underestimates heavy precipitation), and validate against any available local data.

If local gauges exist but are sparse, kriging with elevation as a co-variable (co-kriging or regression-kriging) will provide the best interpolation, because it makes optimal use of both the sparse gauge data and the dense DEM information.

### 8.11.4 For Cross-Border and Transboundary Studies

Always use international or pan-European products rather than national data alone. For Europe, E-OBS and ERA5 provide consistent, cross-border coverage. For the Carpathian region, CARPATCLIM is superior. For global transboundary basins, ERA5, CHIRPS, and GPM/IMERG provide consistent coverage across political boundaries. Supplement with GRDC discharge data for validation.

The institutional architecture of European water management -- the Water Framework Directive, EFAS, and pan-European data products -- reflects the recognition that water does not respect political boundaries. The precipitation that falls on the Carpathian Mountains in Romania and Ukraine becomes the floodwater that threatens Hungarian cities along the Tisza. Hydrological analysis must be equally boundary-blind.

## 8.12 The State of Practice in 2026

The landscape of precipitation estimation has changed dramatically in the past two decades. Twenty years ago, most hydrologists performed their own spatial interpolation from raw gauge data, and the Thiessen polygon method was the default for watershed averaging. Today, the proliferation of high-quality gridded precipitation products has shifted the standard practice from "interpolate your own" to "download and average."

This shift has been driven by three developments. First, global and regional climate data infrastructures -- Copernicus, NASA Earthdata, the Climate Data Store -- have made gridded products freely and instantly accessible through web portals and APIs. A European hydrologist can download 70 years of daily gridded precipitation for their study area in minutes, at no cost. Second, the methods used to produce these products have become far more sophisticated than what any individual project team could implement: they incorporate thousands of gauges, satellite observations, numerical model output, and statistical quality control procedures. Third, the computing power needed to generate, store, and distribute these products has become cheap enough that they can be offered as public goods.

The practical consequence is that the interpolation methods described in this chapter -- IDW, spline, kriging -- are increasingly used by the product developers rather than the end users. The hydrologist's task has shifted from interpolation to product selection (which gridded dataset is best for my region and application?) and spatial averaging (how do I extract watershed means from a gridded product?). The Thiessen polygon method survives in operational contexts where institutional inertia favors established procedures, but for new analyses, surface-based methods using pre-computed grids are the standard.

This does not mean that understanding interpolation is unnecessary. Quite the opposite -- understanding the methods is essential for evaluating the products. Knowing that E-OBS uses splines with elevation as a co-variable for the climatological field tells you that its precipitation estimates in mountainous areas will be better than a simple IDW from valley gauges. Knowing that CHIRPS merges satellite infrared with gauge data tells you that it may perform poorly for cold-season precipitation at high latitudes, where infrared-based estimates are unreliable. Knowing that ERA5 is a model product, not an interpolation of observations, tells you that its precipitation can be systematically biased in ways that gauge-based products are not. The interpolation concepts in this chapter are the vocabulary for making these judgments.

Looking forward, two trends are reshaping the field. First, **machine learning** methods are being applied to spatial interpolation, using deep neural networks to learn complex, nonlinear relationships between precipitation and its predictors (elevation, atmospheric fields, satellite imagery, land surface characteristics). Early results suggest that these methods can outperform traditional approaches, particularly for extreme events, though they require large training datasets and substantial computational resources. Second, **real-time blending** of multiple precipitation sources -- gauges, radar, satellite, and model output -- is becoming standard in operational forecasting systems like EFAS and the US National Water Model. These systems do not rely on a single interpolation method; they combine all available information in a statistically optimal framework, producing precipitation fields that are updated hourly and fed directly into hydrological models.

For the practicing hydrologist, the message is clear: know the methods, use the products, and always validate against streamflow. The precipitation surface is only a means to an end -- the ultimate test is whether it produces a hydrological model that matches observed river behavior.
