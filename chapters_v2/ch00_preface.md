# Preface and Introduction

## Preface

This book exists because of a collision — a slow-motion collision between three fields that spent decades developing in parallel and are now, in the mid-2020s, crashing into each other with extraordinary force. Hydrology, the ancient science of understanding water. Geographic information systems, the technology of mapping and spatial reasoning. And artificial intelligence, the newcomer that arrived not with a gentle knock but by kicking the door down. The State of Hidroinformatics in 2026 is my attempt to stand at the point of impact and make sense of what is happening.

I should be honest about how this book came to be. For years I taught courses at the University of Debrecen — Debreceni Egyetem — in the Department of Environmental Science, where my students needed to understand how water behaves across landscapes, how to measure it, model it, and predict what it will do next. The foundational material came from an extraordinary source: the GIS in Water Resources course developed by David Maidment and David Tarboton at the University of Texas at Austin and Utah State University, respectively. Their lecture notes, exercises, and datasets formed the backbone of a generation of hydroinformatics education worldwide. When I first adapted their material for Hungarian students, I realized two things simultaneously. First, that the content was brilliant — clear, systematic, rooted in real-world applications. Second, that it was deeply American. The datasets were American. The case studies were American. The regulatory frameworks, the measurement networks, the institutional structures — all American. My students in Debrecen, looking out the window at the Tisza River and its flood-prone tributaries, needed something that spoke to their landscape, their data infrastructure, their professional future.

That gap — between excellent American pedagogy and the reality of practicing hydroinformatics in Hungary, in Europe, and increasingly in the developing world — is what drove me to write this book. But the gap turned out to be only the beginning. As I worked on adapting and expanding the material over several years, the field itself was transforming beneath my feet. Cloud-based GIS platforms replaced desktop software as the primary tool for large-scale analysis. Satellite missions like Copernicus Sentinel delivered terabytes of free, high-resolution Earth observation data every day. And then, starting around 2023 and accelerating dramatically through 2025 and into 2026, artificial intelligence changed the rules of the game entirely. Machine learning models began outperforming physics-based hydrological models in streamflow prediction. Large language models became capable of reading scientific papers, writing code, and even running hydrological simulations with minimal human intervention. Agentic AI systems — autonomous software agents that can design experiments, execute models, evaluate results, and iterate — moved from science fiction to operational reality.

I found myself in the position of writing a book about a field that was being reinvented in real time. The choice was either to wait — and risk being perpetually behind — or to write what I could see happening right now, in 2026, with the full acknowledgment that some of what I describe will be outdated by 2028. I chose to write. A snapshot is still valuable if it is honest about what it captures.

### 0.1 The Debreceni Egyetem Context

The University of Debrecen sits in the eastern Hungarian Great Plain — the Alföld — a landscape that is, in hydrological terms, one of the most interesting and challenging in Central Europe. The Tisza River, Hungary's second-largest after the Danube, drains a catchment that spans five countries. Its floods are legendary. The 2001 Tisza flood displaced tens of thousands of people; the 2006 flood came close to overwhelming Budapest's flood defenses. Groundwater in the Alföld is shallow, vulnerable to contamination from agriculture, and critical for drinking water supply in communities that lack access to surface water treatment. Drought, once considered a problem for Mediterranean Europe, has become a recurring crisis on the Hungarian plain, with 2022 and 2024 bringing agricultural losses measured in the hundreds of millions of euros.

This is the landscape my students will work in. They need to understand American datasets like the National Hydrography Dataset (NHDPlus) not because they will use them directly, but because the methods built on those datasets — flow direction algorithms, watershed delineation, flood frequency analysis — are universal. They also need to understand the European Flood Awareness System (EFAS), the Copernicus Land Monitoring Service, the Hungarian Water Directorate's (OVF) measurement network, and the increasingly rich set of global datasets that serve countries without the luxury of dense ground-based monitoring. This book tries to serve all of these needs simultaneously, using a three-tier approach that I will explain in detail in the "How to Use This Book" section below.

The Department of Environmental Science at Debreceni Egyetem has a tradition of applied, interdisciplinary work. Our students come from backgrounds in geography, biology, chemistry, and engineering. They are not, in general, programmers. They are scientists who need to use computational tools to solve environmental problems. This book reflects that reality. While Chapter 12 introduces scripting and automation, and Part VI deals extensively with AI and machine learning, the core of the book assumes no programming expertise. The reader needs a basic understanding of hydrology — what a watershed is, how precipitation becomes runoff, what an aquifer does — and some familiarity with GIS concepts. Everything else is built from the ground up.

### 0.2 Acknowledgment of Foundations

No book emerges from nothing, and this one owes its deepest intellectual debt to David Maidment and David Tarboton. Maidment's vision of "GIS in Water Resources" as a unified field — not GIS applied to water, but a fundamentally spatial approach to water science — shaped everything that follows. His development of Arc Hydro as both a data model and a conceptual framework for organizing water information remains one of the most influential contributions to hydroinformatics. Tarboton's work on terrain analysis, particularly the TauDEM software package for flow direction and watershed delineation, provided the algorithmic foundations that underpin Part III of this book. Their open approach to education — sharing lecture notes, datasets, and exercises freely — made it possible for an instructor in Debrecen to teach world-class hydroinformatics content to students who had never set foot in Texas.

I must also acknowledge the broader community of researchers and practitioners whose work informs every chapter. The developers of HEC-RAS and HEC-HMS at the US Army Corps of Engineers' Hydrologic Engineering Center. The MODFLOW development team at the US Geological Survey. The scientists at the European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts (ECMWF) who built and operate the Global Flood Awareness System (GloFAS) and EFAS. The teams behind the Copernicus satellite program and its downstream services. The machine learning researchers — Kratzert, Nearing, and their colleagues — who demonstrated that Long Short-Term Memory networks could match or exceed the performance of calibrated process-based models for streamflow prediction, a result that shook the foundations of hydrological modeling. And the open-source community: QGIS developers, Python library maintainers, the people who build and maintain the infrastructure that makes modern hydroinformatics possible without million-dollar software licenses.

### 0.3 Writing for Hungary, Reaching the World

This book was written primarily for Hungarian students and professionals, but it was written in English and designed to be useful globally. This was a deliberate choice, and it requires explanation.

Hungary's scientific community is small but remarkably productive. Hungarian water engineers have a long and distinguished history — the regulation of the Tisza River in the 19th century was one of the largest hydraulic engineering projects in European history. But the language barrier is real. Much of the best hydroinformatics content exists only in English, and Hungarian students need to be able to engage with it directly. Writing this book in English means my students learn the technical vocabulary in the language they will encounter it in journals, conferences, and international collaborations.

At the same time, writing in English opens the book to a much larger audience. I have been struck, over the years, by how many students and professionals in the Middle East, South Asia, and Sub-Saharan Africa face the same challenge my Hungarian students face: they learn from American or British textbooks that assume access to datasets, software, and institutional infrastructure that simply does not exist in their countries. A student in Bangladesh trying to learn watershed delineation from a textbook that assumes access to the USGS 3DEP program and ArcGIS Pro licenses is not being well served. This book tries to serve that student too, by systematically presenting global alternatives — free satellite-derived DEMs like SRTM and Copernicus DEM, open-source software like QGIS and Google Earth Engine, and global hydrological datasets like HydroSHEDS, CHIRPS, and ERA5.

The three-tier approach works as follows. When introducing a concept, dataset, or tool, I first present the US example, because the American systems are often the most mature and best-documented, and because much of the foundational literature assumes them. Immediately after, I present the Hungarian or European equivalent — this is the "home" example for the book's primary audience. Finally, I present global alternatives for readers working in other contexts. This is not tokenistic internationalization. Every tier receives substantive treatment, with real URLs, real data access procedures, and real worked examples where possible.

### 0.4 The Role of AI in Creating This Book

I want to be transparent about something that would be dishonest to hide: artificial intelligence played a significant role in the creation of this book. Not as a ghostwriter — every idea, every pedagogical choice, every judgment about what matters and what does not, is mine. But as a tool, AI was indispensable.

I used large language models to help draft and revise prose, to check technical accuracy against published sources, to generate initial versions of worked examples that I then verified and refined, and to identify gaps in my coverage. I used AI-assisted search to find and verify data source URLs, publication dates, and institutional details across dozens of countries. I used code-generation tools to prototype the data processing workflows described in several chapters. And I used AI to help manage the sheer scale of the project — 25 chapters, hundreds of references, thousands of cross-references and data source citations.

This transparency is not an apology. I believe that using AI tools in scientific writing is no different, in principle, from using a calculator in engineering or a spell-checker in journalism. The tool does not replace the expert; it amplifies the expert's capacity. But the reader deserves to know how the sausage was made, especially in a book that devotes its final four chapters to the role of AI in hydroinformatics. It would be hypocritical to write about AI as a tool for water science while pretending that no AI was involved in writing about it.

That said, every factual claim in this book has been verified against primary sources to the best of my ability. Every URL was checked. Every dataset description was confirmed against the provider's documentation. Every worked example was computed and validated. AI can hallucinate — generate plausible-sounding but false information — and I have worked hard to ensure that none of those hallucinations survived into the final text. Where I am uncertain about a fact, I say so. Where information is likely to change (software versions, data portal URLs), I flag it and point the reader to the online version of this book, which is updated regularly.

### 0.5 How This Book Is Organized

The State of Hidroinformatics in 2026 is organized in six parts, each building on the previous but also designed to be readable independently.

**Part I: The Water Information Landscape** (Chapters 1-4) sets the stage. It explains why hydroinformatics matters, how spatial data represents water systems, where to find hydrological data around the world, and what GIS can do for water science. These chapters assume no prior knowledge of GIS and minimal knowledge of hydrology. A water manager, a policy analyst, or a journalist could read Part I and come away with a solid understanding of what hydroinformatics is and why it matters.

**Part II: Reading the Landscape** (Chapters 5-8) introduces the fundamental techniques for working with spatial data: how digital elevation models represent terrain, how map algebra allows computation with spatial layers, how terrain derivatives like slope and aspect control water movement, and how rainfall is measured and interpolated across space. This is where the book becomes technical, but every concept is grounded in physical intuition and illustrated with real-world examples.

**Part III: Following the Water Downhill** (Chapters 9-12) covers the core of computational hydrology: preparing digital elevation data for hydrological analysis, computing flow directions and accumulations, delineating watersheds automatically, and automating these workflows with scripting. This is the heart of the Maidment/Tarboton legacy, and these chapters receive the most detailed treatment.

**Part IV: When Water Becomes Dangerous** (Chapters 13-17) applies everything from the previous parts to the most urgent application of hydroinformatics: flood analysis. It covers LiDAR terrain mapping, the Height Above Nearest Drainage (HAND) method for identifying flood-prone terrain, inundation mapping and damage estimation, real-time flood forecasting systems, and population exposure analysis. These chapters are the most directly practical in the book — the ones most likely to be used by someone trying to save lives and property.

**Part V: The Hidden Water** (Chapters 18-21) extends the spatial approach underground, covering 3D subsurface GIS, groundwater data management, hydrogeological framework modeling, and groundwater flow simulation with MODFLOW. This part draws heavily on the Arc Hydro Groundwater framework and addresses a critical gap in most hydroinformatics curricula, which tend to focus exclusively on surface water.

**Part VI: The Intelligent Water System** (Chapters 22-25) addresses the frontier: how AI and machine learning are transforming water science. It covers model calibration as the fundamental challenge, practical machine learning applications in hydrology, the emerging capability of agentic AI for autonomous modeling, and a forward-looking chapter on the future of water intelligence. These chapters are written for 2026 and will inevitably date faster than the rest of the book, but they capture a moment of genuine transformation that deserves documentation.

## How to Use This Book

### 0.7 A Road Map Through Six Parts

This book contains 25 chapters organized in six parts, and while it is designed to be read sequentially, not every reader needs to start at Chapter 1 and proceed linearly to Chapter 25. The parts are structured to serve different needs, and understanding the architecture of the book will help you find what you need efficiently.

**Part I: The Water Information Landscape** (Chapters 1 through 4) is the foundation. If you are new to hydroinformatics — if you are a water engineer who has not used GIS, a GIS professional who has not worked with water data, or a student at the beginning of a course — start here. Chapter 1 establishes why the field matters and what problems it solves. Chapter 2 covers the spatial fundamentals: coordinate systems, projections, and how maps represent reality. Chapter 3 is a comprehensive tour of water data sources across the United States, the European Union, Hungary, and the wider world — this chapter alone justifies the book for many readers, as finding the right data is often the hardest part of any hydroinformatics project. Chapter 4 explains what GIS actually does for water science, moving beyond the "GIS is a tool for making maps" misconception to show it as a spatial analysis and modeling platform.

**Part II: Reading the Landscape** (Chapters 5 through 8) builds the technical vocabulary and conceptual framework for working with gridded spatial data. Chapter 5 explains how digital elevation models work — how a grid of numbers becomes a representation of terrain, and what the choices about resolution and source mean for your analysis. Chapter 6 introduces map algebra, the mathematical framework for combining spatial layers, which is one of those concepts that sounds abstract until you realize it is the mechanism behind nearly every spatial calculation in hydrology. Chapter 7 develops terrain derivatives — slope, aspect, curvature — and explains why they matter for understanding where water goes. Chapter 8 addresses precipitation measurement and interpolation, a topic where the gap between what data you want and what data you have is often vast, and where the choices you make about interpolation methods have real consequences for your results.

**Part III: Following the Water Downhill** (Chapters 9 through 12) is the methodological heart of the book. These four chapters take you from a raw digital elevation model to a fully delineated watershed with a computed stream network, ready for hydrological modeling. Chapter 9 explains why raw DEMs do not work for hydrology and how to fix them — pit filling, stream burning, and other conditioning techniques that are essential but rarely explained clearly. Chapter 10 covers flow direction and flow accumulation algorithms, the computational engine that extracts drainage patterns from terrain data. Chapter 11 shows how to delineate watersheds automatically and how to validate the results against mapped stream networks. Chapter 12 introduces automation and scripting, showing how to process not one watershed but thousands, which is the reality of operational hydroinformatics. If you are an experienced GIS user looking to add hydrological analysis to your skills, you might start here, referring back to Part II as needed.

**Part IV: When Water Becomes Dangerous** (Chapters 13 through 17) is where the book becomes most directly applied. These five chapters cover flood analysis from data acquisition through damage estimation. Chapter 13 explains LiDAR technology and why high-resolution terrain data changed everything about flood mapping. Chapter 14 introduces the HAND (Height Above Nearest Drainage) method, an elegant approach to identifying flood-prone terrain that requires no hydraulic modeling. Chapter 15 covers inundation mapping — the process of determining which areas flood at a given river stage — using both traditional hydraulic models and the simplified HAND-based approach. Chapter 16 surveys operational flood forecasting systems worldwide, from the US National Water Model to the European EFAS and GloFAS systems, with detailed coverage of Hungarian flood forecasting. Chapter 17 addresses the human dimension: who lives in the flood zone, how to estimate exposure and vulnerability, and how hydroinformatics supports emergency response. If you are a practicing flood engineer or emergency manager, Part IV can be read as a standalone resource.

**Part V: The Hidden Water** (Chapters 18 through 21) extends the spatial thinking of the previous parts into the subsurface. Groundwater is often treated as a separate discipline from surface water hydrology, but the tools and concepts of hydroinformatics apply equally to both. Chapter 18 introduces 3D subsurface GIS — how to represent geological layers, aquifers, and confining units in a spatial framework. Chapter 19 covers groundwater data: wells, boreholes, water level measurements, and how to manage and quality-control these datasets. Chapter 20 shows how to build a hydrogeological framework model — a 3D representation of the subsurface that forms the basis for groundwater flow modeling. Chapter 21 covers MODFLOW, the industry-standard groundwater flow simulator, showing how GIS-based framework models connect to numerical simulation. If you are a hydrogeologist, these four chapters provide a complete workflow from data to model.

**Part VI: The Intelligent Water System** (Chapters 22 through 25) addresses the most rapidly evolving part of the field. Chapter 22 frames the calibration problem — the fundamental challenge of making hydrological models match observed reality — and surveys traditional approaches. Chapter 23 introduces practical machine learning applications in hydrology: LSTM networks for streamflow prediction, neural networks for groundwater level forecasting, satellite-based evapotranspiration estimation, and more. Chapter 24 — the most forward-looking chapter in the book — covers agentic AI: autonomous software agents that can set up, run, evaluate, and iteratively improve hydrological models without continuous human supervision. Chapter 25 looks ahead to digital twins, real-time data assimilation, and the convergence of sensors, models, and AI into integrated water intelligence systems. If you are an AI researcher looking for application domains, or a hydrologist wondering what AI can actually do for you today, Part VI is where to go.

### 0.8 Prerequisites

This book assumes that the reader brings two things to the table, and provides everything else.

The first is a **basic understanding of hydrology**. You should know what a watershed is — the area of land that drains to a common point. You should understand, in general terms, how precipitation becomes streamflow: rain falls, some infiltrates into the ground, some runs off the surface, and eventually it reaches a stream or river. You should know what an aquifer is, what a water table is, and why groundwater matters. A single introductory course in hydrology, environmental science, or physical geography is more than sufficient. If you have ever looked at a flooded river and wondered how much water was flowing, or looked at a well and wondered where the water came from, you have enough background.

The second is **familiarity with GIS concepts**. You do not need to be an expert. You should know what a geographic information system does in general terms — that it stores, displays, and analyzes spatial data. You should have some experience opening a GIS application (ArcGIS, QGIS, or even Google Earth), loading data, and looking at it. You should understand, roughly, what raster and vector data are. If you have taken any course that involved making a map on a computer, you are prepared.

What you do **not** need is programming experience. Several chapters, particularly Chapter 12 and Parts V and VI, involve computational concepts, and Chapter 12 introduces Python scripting. But the book is written so that a reader with no programming background can understand the concepts, the workflows, and the results without writing a single line of code. The code examples are provided for readers who want to implement the techniques themselves, but they are not prerequisites for understanding the material. Worked examples are presented with full intermediate steps and interpretations, so you can follow the logic even if you have never seen a Python script.

### 0.9 The Three-Tier Approach

One of the persistent problems in hydroinformatics education is geographic parochialism. Most textbooks are written from the perspective of one country — usually the United States — and assume that readers have access to that country's data infrastructure, software licenses, and institutional frameworks. This is a disservice to the majority of the world's water professionals, who work in very different contexts.

This book addresses the problem with a systematic three-tier approach to datasets, tools, and institutional frameworks. The three tiers are presented consistently throughout the book, often in equivalence tables but also woven into the narrative text.

**Tier 1: United States.** The US examples are retained from the original Maidment/Tarboton course material because they are exceptionally well-documented, the datasets are freely available, and the systems (USGS streamflow monitoring, NHDPlus, the National Water Model) represent the global state of the art. When you encounter a US example, it is because the method was developed or first demonstrated in the US context, and the US data makes the explanation clearest. The US tier is the reference standard — not because America is more important, but because its water data infrastructure is the most mature and best-documented in the world.

**Tier 2: Hungary and the European Union.** This is the "home" tier for the book's primary audience. Hungarian examples use real data from the General Directorate of Water Management (OVF), the Hungarian Meteorological Service (OMSZ), the Vízügyi Alapadatok (water management basic data) system, and the National Spatial Data Infrastructure. European examples draw on the Copernicus program (Sentinel satellites, Copernicus DEM, the Climate Data Store), the European Flood Awareness System (EFAS), the Water Information System for Europe (WISE), and the data infrastructure mandated by the EU Water Framework Directive. These are not token additions — the Hungarian and European examples receive the same depth of treatment as the US examples, with real data access procedures, worked calculations, and interpretation of results.

**Tier 3: Global.** For readers outside the US and Europe — and there are many — each chapter provides global alternatives. These include satellite-derived datasets that cover the entire planet (SRTM, ASTER GDEM, Copernicus DEM for elevation; CHIRPS, GPM-IMERG for precipitation; ERA5 for meteorological reanalysis), open-source software that requires no licenses (QGIS, GRASS GIS, Google Earth Engine), and global hydrological frameworks (HydroSHEDS, GloFAS, the WMO Hydrological Observing System). The global tier is not an afterthought. For a student in Ethiopia or a water manager in Vietnam, it may be the most important tier in the book.

Equivalence tables — presenting the US, European, and global versions of datasets and tools side by side — appear in most chapters and are indexed in Appendix A for quick reference.

### 0.10 Online Resources

This book exists in two forms. The version you may be reading now — whether printed or as a PDF — is a snapshot, fixed at the time of publication. But the primary form of this book is its online edition, which offers several capabilities that print cannot.

**The RAG Tutor.** The online edition is equipped with a retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) system that allows you to ask questions in natural language and receive answers drawn from the book's content, with citations to specific chapters and sections. You can ask, for example, "How do I delineate a watershed using QGIS?" and receive a synthesized answer drawing from Chapters 9, 10, 11, and 12, with links to the relevant sections. The RAG system does not generate information beyond what is in the book — it retrieves and synthesizes existing content, reducing the risk of hallucination that affects general-purpose AI chatbots.

**Data download links.** Every dataset mentioned in the book is accompanied by a structured reference block containing the dataset name, providing organization, URL, spatial and temporal coverage, file format, and access requirements (free, registration required, institutional access). These reference blocks are maintained in the online edition and updated when URLs change — a sadly common occurrence with government data portals.

**The figure gallery.** The online edition includes all figures in full resolution, with interactive versions where appropriate. Maps can be zoomed and panned. Time series can be explored interactively. 3D visualizations of terrain and subsurface models can be rotated. These interactive features are not gimmicks — they genuinely aid understanding, particularly for complex spatial data.

**Concept map and search.** The online edition includes a navigable concept map that shows how chapters and topics relate to each other, and a full-text search that goes beyond simple keyword matching to understand the intent behind your query. If you search for "flood forecasting," you will find not only Chapter 16 (which is explicitly about flood forecasting) but also relevant sections in Chapter 8 (precipitation measurement), Chapter 14 (HAND terrain analysis), and Chapter 23 (AI-based flood prediction).

### 0.11 Conventions Used in This Book

To help you navigate the material efficiently, this book uses a consistent set of formatting conventions.

**Bold terms** indicate the first use of a technical term that is defined in context. These terms also appear in the Glossary (Appendix D). When you encounter a bold term, the surrounding text contains its definition. For example, when you first encounter **flow accumulation** in Chapter 10, the text will explain what it means, how it is computed, and why it matters.

**Figure and table numbering** follows the pattern X.Y, where X is the chapter number and Y is the sequential number within that chapter. Figure 10.3 is the third figure in Chapter 10. Table 7.2 is the second table in Chapter 7. This numbering is consistent throughout the book and used in all cross-references.

**Cross-references** between chapters are frequent and deliberate. Hydroinformatics is not a linear progression — concepts from early chapters reappear in new contexts later. When a concept from another chapter is relevant, you will see a reference like "see Chapter 10 for a detailed treatment of flow direction algorithms" or "this builds on the map algebra concepts introduced in Chapter 6." These cross-references are hyperlinked in the online edition.

**Equivalence tables** use a standard three-column format: United States | Hungary/EU | Global. These tables appear wherever datasets, tools, or institutional frameworks are compared across regions.

**Worked examples** are set apart from the main text and follow a consistent structure: (1) problem statement with explicit input data, (2) formula or method with actual values substituted, (3) intermediate computational steps, (4) final result with units, and (5) interpretation explaining what the result means in physical terms. Every worked example can be reproduced by the reader using the data and methods described.

**Code blocks** contain software commands, file paths, API calls, or data format specifications. These are always presented in monospaced font within dedicated blocks, never embedded in flowing prose. Code blocks are provided for reference and reproducibility; understanding them is not required to follow the conceptual argument.

**Data source reference blocks** appear throughout the text wherever a specific dataset is introduced. They contain standardized metadata: the dataset name, the providing organization, the access URL, the spatial and temporal coverage, the file format, and any access requirements. In the online edition, these blocks are searchable and aggregated in Appendix A.

**Mathematical notation** is consistent throughout. Variables are italic ($Q$ for discharge, $P$ for precipitation, $A$ for area). Vectors and matrices are bold. Units follow the SI system with common hydrological conventions (mm for precipitation depth, m$^3$/s for discharge, m/day for hydraulic conductivity). LaTeX notation is used for all equations, with inline expressions in single dollar signs and displayed equations in double dollar signs.

### 0.12 A Word on the Title

The attentive reader will have noticed that the title uses "Hidroinformatics" with an 'i' rather than the English "Hydroinformatics" with a 'y.' This is deliberate. In Hungarian, the word is *hidroinformatika*, and the hybrid spelling is a small reminder that this book, while written in English, grows from Hungarian soil. It is also, admittedly, a way to make the title distinctive and memorable. If it bothers the purist, I ask for forgiveness. Language, like water, finds its own path.
