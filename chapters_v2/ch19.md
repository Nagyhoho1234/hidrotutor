# Chapter 19: Wells, Boreholes, and Aquifer Maps

Groundwater is the world's largest accessible freshwater reserve, supplying roughly half of all drinking water, around forty percent of irrigation water, and sustaining baseflow in rivers during dry seasons. Yet unlike a river or a lake, an aquifer cannot be photographed from a satellite. Everything we know about the subsurface comes from indirect evidence: geologic maps that document the rock formations exposed at the surface, borehole logs that record the sequence of materials a drill bit encounters on its way down, and well databases that catalog the locations, depths, and yields of thousands of extraction and monitoring points. These three categories of information -- geologic maps, borehole records, and well inventories -- form the observational backbone of every hydrogeological investigation. Without them, the 3D subsurface data structures introduced in Chapter 18 would have no observations to populate, and the simulation tools discussed in Chapter 20 and Chapter 21 would have nothing to calibrate against.

This chapter examines how each of these data types is structured, stored, and analyzed within a geographic information system. We begin with aquifer feature classes and the maps that delineate aquifer boundaries, then move to well databases and the attributes that describe a well's identity, depth, usage, and relationship to aquifers. From there we descend into the borehole itself, exploring the BoreholeLog table, BorePoint and BoreLine three-dimensional features, and the concept of stratigraphic picks. We then look at geologic map databases -- the foundation upon which aquifer delineation rests. Throughout, we follow the three-tier approach established in earlier chapters: United States examples drawn from the USGS and state water agencies, Hungarian and European Union examples centered on SZTFH (the Supervisory Authority for Regulatory Affairs, formerly MBFSZ) and OVF (the General Directorate of Water Management), and global datasets including WHYMAP, IGRAC, and the UNESCO transboundary aquifer inventories.

## 19.1 Aquifer Maps and Aquifer Feature Classes

### 19.1.1 What an Aquifer Map Represents

An aquifer, in the formal definition adopted by the United States Geological Survey, is a geologic formation, group of formations, or part of a formation that contains sufficient saturated permeable material to yield significant quantities of water to wells and springs. This definition is deceptively simple. In practice, the boundary of an aquifer is a conceptual construct that depends on the scale of mapping, the purpose of the investigation, and the density of subsurface data available. A national-scale aquifer map of the United States, for example, groups hundreds of distinct geologic formations into roughly seventy principal aquifer systems, each colored and labeled on a single sheet. A county-level hydrogeological map of the same region may subdivide one of those principal aquifers into a dozen named members with distinct hydraulic properties.

Aquifer maps are cartographic products that portray the lateral extent of aquifer units, often distinguishing between outcrop zones (where the aquifer-forming rock is exposed at the land surface) and subcrop zones (where the aquifer is buried beneath younger formations). Many maps also show confining units -- layers of low-permeability material that separate aquifers vertically. The spatial relationship between geologic formations and aquifer boundaries is intimate: aquifer maps are, in essence, interpretive overlays on geologic maps, filtered through a hydrogeological lens. ### 19.1.2 Aquifer Feature Classes in the Geodatabase

In a GIS environment, aquifer boundaries are stored as polygon feature classes. Each polygon represents either an entire aquifer or a zone within an aquifer (such as the outcrop of the Edwards Aquifer in south-central Texas, or the confined zone of the same aquifer farther to the south). The feature class carries a set of attributes that enable identification, classification, and linkage to external databases.

The core attributes of an Aquifer feature class typically include the following fields:

**Table 19.1**: Core attributes of the Aquifer polygon feature class

| Attribute | Data Type | Description |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| HydroID | Integer | Unique feature identifier within the geodatabase, used for creating relationships between feature classes |
| HydroCode | Text | Permanent public identifier linking the feature to external information systems (e.g., state aquifer codes) |
| Name | Text | Display name of the aquifer or aquifer zone, used for labeling and legend construction |
| HGUID | Integer | Hydrogeologic unit identifier, relating the aquifer feature to a detailed entry in the HydrogeologicUnit table |
| HGUCode | Text | Text code for the hydrogeologic unit, used for symbolization and cross-referencing |
| FType | Text | Feature type, distinguishing zones such as "Outcrop," "Confined," "Subcrop," or "Recharge Zone" |

The HGUID field deserves particular attention. A single aquifer may be represented by dozens of polygon features in the geodatabase -- the Edwards Aquifer dataset in Texas, for instance, contains hundreds of polygons of varying sizes. All of these polygons share the same HGUID value, which links them to one record in the HydrogeologicUnit table. This table (described in greater detail in Chapter 20) stores the aquifer's name, lithological description, typical hydraulic conductivity range, and other properties. Through the HGUID, a user can select all features belonging to the Edwards Aquifer, regardless of how many polygons make up its spatial representation, and retrieve the aquifer's hydrogeological properties in a single query.

The HydroCode attribute provides the bridge to external databases. In Texas, each major aquifer is assigned a numeric code by the Texas Water Development Board; the Edwards Aquifer is code 11. By storing this code as the HydroCode, the geodatabase preserves the original identification scheme and allows data exchange between the GIS and the state's relational database. The same principle applies internationally: in Hungary, aquifer bodies defined under the EU Water Framework Directive carry alphanumeric codes assigned by the national water management authority.

### 19.1.3 US Aquifer Mapping: The USGS Principal Aquifers

The most comprehensive aquifer mapping effort in the United States is the USGS Principal Aquifers map, which identifies and delineates roughly seventy aquifer systems across the nation. This dataset, available as a polygon shapefile, classifies aquifers by rock type (unconsolidated sand and gravel, semiconsolidated sand, sandstone, carbonate rock, igneous and metamorphic rock) and by their regional significance. The map was originally published in the Ground Water Atlas of the United States (USGS Hydrologic Atlas 730) and has been updated digitally.

At the state level, mapping becomes more detailed. The Texas Water Development Board, for example, maintains datasets for nine major aquifers and twenty-two minor aquifers, each delineated as a polygon feature class with attributes for outcrop and subcrop zones. The Edwards Aquifer of south-central Texas, which supplies water to over two million people including the city of San Antonio, has been mapped in particularly fine detail, with polygon features distinguishing the recharge zone, the artesian zone, and the freshwater-saline water interface. Wells drilled into the Edwards Aquifer can be linked to these polygon features through the AquiferID attribute, creating a direct spatial relationship between the extraction point and the aquifer unit from which it draws.

Other states maintain similarly detailed aquifer maps. California's Department of Water Resources delineates groundwater basins and subbasins in the Bulletin 118 dataset, a critical resource for compliance with the Sustainable Groundwater Management Act of 2014. Florida's geological survey has mapped the Floridan Aquifer System -- one of the most productive in the world -- with particular attention to the confining units that separate the upper and lower Floridan aquifers.

### 19.1.4 Hungarian Aquifer Mapping: SZTFH and EU Water Framework Directive Bodies

Hungary's hydrogeological setting is dominated by the Pannonian Basin, a large sedimentary basin filled with several kilometers of Neogene and Quaternary sediments. The basin contains multiple aquifer systems stacked vertically: shallow Quaternary alluvial aquifers in the Great Plain (Alfold), intermediate Pannonian sand and sandstone aquifers, and deep thermal water-bearing formations that feed Hungary's famous thermal baths. The karstic aquifers of the Transdanubian Mountains (Dunantuli-kozephegyseg) and the Bukk Mountains in the northeast form a separate, critically important system that supplies drinking water to cities including Budapest.

The primary custodian of geological and hydrogeological map data in Hungary is SZTFH (Szabalyozott Tevekenysegek Felugyeleti Hatosaga -- Supervisory Authority for Regulatory Affairs), which absorbed the former Hungarian Mining and Geological Survey (MBFSZ, Magyar Banyaszati es Foldtani Szolgalat). SZTFH maintains the national geological map database accessible through its geoportal at map.mbfsz.gov.hu. This portal provides access to geological maps at scales from 1:100,000 to 1:500,000, hydrogeological maps showing aquifer vulnerability and groundwater resources, and borehole databases containing records of tens of thousands of exploration and water wells drilled since the late nineteenth century.

Under the EU Water Framework Directive (2000/60/EC), Hungary has delineated 185 groundwater bodies grouped into four main types: porous aquifers (alluvial and Pannonian sediments), karstic aquifers (Mesozoic limestones and dolomites), combined porous-karstic systems, and thermal water bodies. Each groundwater body has been assigned a quantitative status (good or poor, based on the balance between abstraction and recharge) and a chemical status (good or poor, based on pollutant concentrations). These groundwater bodies are reported to the European Environment Agency through the Water Information System for Europe (WISE) and are accessible through the EEA's WFD database at discodata.eea.europa.eu.

The distinction between geological mapping and WFD groundwater body delineation is important to understand. Geological maps describe the physical distribution of rock types; groundwater bodies are administrative-regulatory units defined for water management purposes. A single groundwater body may encompass several geological formations if they are hydraulically connected, or a single formation may be split into multiple groundwater bodies if regional differences in water quality or usage patterns warrant separate management. The GIS practitioner working with Hungarian groundwater data must therefore navigate both systems: SZTFH geological maps for the physical framework, and OVF/WFD groundwater body delineations for the regulatory framework.

### 19.1.5 Global Aquifer Mapping: WHYMAP and IGRAC

At the global scale, two initiatives have attempted to create consistent, worldwide aquifer maps. The World-wide Hydrogeological Mapping and Assessment Programme (WHYMAP), coordinated by the German Federal Institute for Geosciences and Natural Resources (BGR) in partnership with UNESCO, has produced a series of maps including the Groundwater Resources Map of the World at 1:25,000,000 scale. This map classifies aquifer systems by productivity and recharge conditions, providing a first-order global overview of where groundwater is abundant and where it is scarce.

IGRAC (International Groundwater Resources Assessment Centre), based in Delft, the Netherlands, operates under the auspices of UNESCO and the World Meteorological Organization. IGRAC maintains the Global Groundwater Information System (GGIS), a web-based platform that aggregates national groundwater data into a searchable, map-based interface. GGIS provides information on aquifer types, groundwater levels, and abstraction rates for countries that have contributed data. While coverage is uneven -- data-rich countries like the Netherlands and Australia are well represented, while many developing countries have sparse entries -- GGIS represents the most ambitious attempt to create a truly global groundwater database.

A particularly significant product of the WHYMAP/IGRAC collaboration is the Transboundary Aquifer Map. Of the world's approximately six hundred identified transboundary aquifer systems, many are shared between countries with different data standards, mapping conventions, and political sensitivities about water resources. The UNESCO ISARM (Internationally Shared Aquifer Resources Management) initiative has inventoried these systems continent by continent. In Europe alone, over one hundred and fifty transboundary aquifers have been identified, including several shared by Hungary with its neighbors -- the thermal water systems shared with Slovakia and Romania, and the alluvial aquifers of the Danube and Tisza that cross multiple national boundaries.

## 19.2 Well Databases and Well Attributes

### 19.2.1 The Central Role of Wells in Hydrogeology

Water wells are among the oldest and most important structures made by humans. Archaeological evidence from Cyprus, China, and the Levant shows that communities have been digging wells for at least eight thousand years. Wells provided reliable access to groundwater even during dry periods when surface water disappeared, and they enabled the development of settlements in arid regions that could not otherwise support permanent habitation. Today, at least 1.5 billion people worldwide rely on groundwater accessed through wells as their primary source of drinking water, and the number is growing as surface water becomes increasingly stressed by climate change and population growth.

For the hydrogeologist, every well is both a data point and a window into the subsurface. The location and depth of a well tell us where groundwater has been found and at what depth. Water level measurements in monitoring wells reveal the shape of the water table and the direction of groundwater flow. Pumping tests conducted at production wells yield estimates of aquifer transmissivity and storativity. Water quality samples collected from wells document the chemical composition of the groundwater and the presence or absence of contamination. Borehole logs recorded during drilling describe the sequence of geological materials encountered, providing direct evidence of subsurface stratigraphy. No other single type of infrastructure provides so many different kinds of hydrogeological information.

Cities, counties, states, and national agencies document and issue permits for well construction. Over decades and centuries, these records accumulate into well databases containing information on tens of thousands to millions of wells. These databases are among the most valuable assets in groundwater management, yet they are also among the most heterogeneous. A well record from 1920 may contain nothing more than a location description ("200 meters north of the old mill"), a depth, and the driller's name. A modern well record may include GPS coordinates accurate to a few centimeters, detailed lithological logs, geophysical measurements, pumping test results, and years of water level and water quality monitoring data. Integrating these records into a consistent, spatially enabled database is one of the fundamental challenges of groundwater informatics.

### 19.2.2 The Well Feature Class: Structure and Attributes

In a GIS framework, wells are represented as point features. Each point corresponds to the surface location of a well, and the point's attributes describe the well's properties. The Well feature class in the Arc Hydro Groundwater data model provides a minimum set of attributes designed for identification, three-dimensional representation, and linkage to aquifer and hydrogeologic units. This minimum set can be expanded for each project to include additional fields such as owner, driller, water use category, permit number, or casing diameter.

**Table 19.3**: Core attributes of the Well point feature class

| Attribute | Data Type | Description |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| HydroID | Integer | Unique identifier within the geodatabase |
| HydroCode | Text | Permanent public identifier (e.g., state well number) |
| LandElev | Float | Land surface elevation at the well location, in meters or feet above a reference datum |
| WellDepth | Float | Total depth of the well below land surface |
| AquiferID | Integer | Links the well to an Aquifer feature through the Aquifer's HydroID |
| AqCode | Text | Aquifer code from an external classification system |
| HGUID | Integer | Hydrogeologic unit identifier, linking to the HydrogeologicUnit table |
| FType | Text | Well type classification (e.g., Production, Monitoring, Injection, Domestic, Irrigation) |

The LandElev and WellDepth attributes together define the three-dimensional extent of the well. A well with a LandElev of 250 meters above sea level and a WellDepth of 120 meters extends from 250 meters down to 130 meters elevation. This simple representation -- a vertical line from the surface to the well bottom -- provides the geometric framework for visualizing wells in three-dimensional scenes and for determining which geological formations or aquifer units the well intersects.

The AquiferID attribute establishes the relationship between wells and aquifers. In the geodatabase, this is a one-to-many relationship: a single Aquifer polygon can be associated with many Well points, but in the simplest case each well is associated with one primary aquifer. When a well is screened across multiple aquifer units -- a common situation in areas with stacked aquifer systems -- additional AquiferID fields can be added (AquiferID2, AquiferID3) to capture these multiple associations. Alternatively, a many-to-many relationship table can be created, though this adds complexity to queries and is typically reserved for projects where multi-aquifer wells are the norm rather than the exception.

The FType attribute classifies wells by their purpose. A coded value domain restricts the values that can be entered, ensuring consistency across the database. Common well types include Production (extracting water for supply), Monitoring (measuring water levels and quality), Injection (recharging water or disposing of waste fluids), Domestic (private household supply), Irrigation (agricultural water supply), and Industrial (water for manufacturing processes). In the Texas Water Development Board database, for example, water use is encoded with single-letter codes (I for irrigation, H for domestic, P for public supply), which must be translated into the standardized FType values when importing data into the geodatabase.

### 19.2.3 US Well Databases: NWIS and State Systems

The largest well database in the United States is the USGS National Water Information System (NWIS), which contains records for approximately 1.5 million sites including groundwater wells, springs, and test holes. Each site in NWIS is identified by a fifteen-digit site number based on latitude and longitude, and the database stores information on well construction, aquifer assignment, water levels, and water quality. NWIS data are freely available through the USGS Water Services web interface and API.

State-level well databases often contain more detailed information than NWIS because they are fed by well-drilling permit systems that require drillers to submit completion reports. The Texas Water Development Board groundwater database, for example, contains records for approximately 130,000 wells in Texas. Each well is identified by a state well number and described by roughly fifty attributes organized in a relational database structure. A central "welldata" table stores the well's location, elevation, depth, owner, driller, and associated aquifer codes. This central table is linked through the state well number to subsidiary tables containing information on well casing intervals, water level measurements, and water quality analyses. The Texas Water Information Integration and Dissemination (WIID) system provides a web-based mapping interface for querying and visualizing these data.

Similar systems exist in other states. Michigan's Wellogic database contains records for over 500,000 wells and is searchable through a web-based GIS interface. California's Online System for Well Completion Reports (OSWCR) provides access to digitized driller's logs. Illinois maintains a comprehensive well database through its State Water Survey. The common theme across all these systems is the use of a central well table with a unique identifier that links to subsidiary tables containing time-varying and depth-varying data.

### 19.2.4 Hungarian Well Databases: OVF Monitoring Network and SZTFH Borehole Records

Hungary's groundwater monitoring infrastructure is maintained primarily by OVF, the General Directorate of Water Management. OVF operates a national network of groundwater monitoring wells that measure water levels and, at selected sites, water quality. The monitoring data are stored in the Central Hydrographic Database (Vizrajzi Adattar, abbreviated VRA) and have been made freely available since July 2024 through the data.vizugy.hu portal. This portal provides time series of groundwater levels from monitoring wells alongside surface water data, making it the closest Hungarian equivalent to the USGS NWIS.

The SZTFH borehole database represents a separate but complementary data source. While OVF focuses on operational monitoring, SZTFH maintains the geological borehole registry -- a catalog of exploration wells, geological survey boreholes, mining exploration holes, and water wells drilled since systematic record-keeping began in the late nineteenth century. The SZTFH database contains tens of thousands of borehole records with varying levels of detail. Older records may include only the location, depth, and a summary lithological log; modern records include detailed geological and geophysical logs, hydrogeological test results, and GPS-verified coordinates.

Hungary's thermal water wells deserve special mention. The country sits atop one of Europe's richest thermal water resources, with over 1,300 thermal wells (defined as wells producing water above 30 degrees Celsius) and approximately 270 operational thermal baths. The deepest thermal wells in Hungary reach depths exceeding 2,000 meters and produce water at temperatures above 90 degrees Celsius. These wells are documented in a specialized database maintained by SZTFH, with attributes including water temperature, yield, chemical composition, and the geological formation from which the thermal water is produced. The spatial distribution of thermal wells across Hungary correlates strongly with the depth to the Pannonian basement and the thickness of the insulating sedimentary fill -- a relationship that can be explored and visualized using GIS tools.

### 19.2.5 Well Data Standards and Interoperability

The heterogeneity of well databases worldwide has motivated several standardization efforts. The American Society for Testing and Materials (ASTM) published Standard D5254, which defines a minimum set of data elements to identify a groundwater site. This standard specifies that every well record should include, at minimum, a unique identifier, geographic coordinates with a stated datum and accuracy, land surface elevation, well depth, well use, and the date of construction. While seemingly basic, many historical well databases lack one or more of these elements, and the ASTM standard provides a target toward which data managers can work when modernizing their records.

In Australia, the National Groundwater Data Transfer Standard defines data structures and exchange formats for groundwater data used across Australian states and territories. This standard is particularly significant because it was designed from the outset for digital exchange, whereas many older standards assumed paper-based record-keeping.

The European INSPIRE Directive (2007/2/EC) has driven standardization of geospatial data across EU member states, including groundwater data. Under INSPIRE, the Geology theme defines schemas for boreholes and hydrogeological units that are legally binding for data exchange between EU member states. The GeoSciML (Geoscience Markup Language) standard, developed by the Commission for the Management and Application of Geoscience Information (CGI), provides an XML-based exchange format for geological data including borehole logs and geological maps. GroundWaterML 2.0, a companion standard to GeoSciML, specifically addresses the exchange of groundwater data including well locations, aquifer properties, and water level measurements. These standards are increasingly adopted by national geological surveys and water management agencies, facilitating cross-border data sharing for transboundary aquifer studies.

The OGC WaterML 2.0 standard, discussed in Chapter 3, provides a framework for encoding water observation data including groundwater level time series. Together, WaterML 2.0, GroundWaterML 2.0, and GeoSciML form a comprehensive suite of standards that cover the full range of groundwater data types -- from point observations of water levels, through well and borehole descriptions, to geological and hydrogeological maps. The practical adoption of these standards remains uneven, but the trajectory is clear: as web services and APIs replace bulk file downloads as the primary mechanism for data distribution, standardized exchange formats become essential rather than optional.

| Standard | Scope | Governing Body | Key Features |
|----------|-------|----------------|--------------|
| ASTM D5254 | Minimum well identification | ASTM International | Defines mandatory attributes for groundwater site identification |
| Australian National GW Data Transfer Standard | National well data exchange | Australian National Groundwater Committee | Comprehensive data structures for all well-related information |
| INSPIRE Geology Theme | EU geospatial data exchange | European Commission | Legally binding schemas for boreholes and hydrogeological units |

## 19.3 Borehole Logs: Recording the Subsurface

### 19.3.1 What a Borehole Log Contains

When a well is drilled, the drilling process generates a wealth of information about the subsurface. The most fundamental record is the lithological log (also called a driller's log or geological log), which describes the sequence of earth materials encountered as the drill advances. A typical lithological log entry looks like this:

```
Depth (m)    Material Description
0 - 3        Topsoil, brown, organic
3 - 8        Clay, gray, stiff
8 - 15       Sand, fine to medium, water-bearing
15 - 22      Sand and gravel, coarse, water-bearing
22 - 30      Clay, blue-gray, stiff (confining unit)
30 - 45      Limestone, fractured, water-bearing
45 - 50      Shale, dark gray, compact
```

Each line in this log represents a depth interval characterized by a lithological description. The descriptions are qualitative -- "fine to medium sand" rather than a measured grain-size distribution -- and they reflect the driller's experience and vocabulary. This is both the strength and the weakness of lithological logs: they provide direct evidence of subsurface conditions at a specific location, but the descriptions are subjective and vary from driller to driller. A formation described as "sandy clay" by one driller might be called "clayey sand" by another, and these two descriptions carry different hydrogeological implications.

Beyond the lithological log, modern boreholes may generate several additional types of records. Geophysical logs use instruments lowered into the borehole to measure physical properties of the surrounding rock. Common geophysical log types include gamma ray (measuring natural radioactivity, useful for distinguishing clay from sand), resistivity (measuring electrical resistance, related to porosity and fluid salinity), spontaneous potential (measuring natural electrical potentials at formation boundaries), and sonic logs (measuring acoustic travel time, related to porosity and rock type). These logs produce continuous traces of the measured property versus depth, providing far more detail than the driller's lithological descriptions.

Well completion logs document the construction of the well: the depths at which different casing diameters are installed, the positions of screens or perforations that allow water to enter the well, the gravel pack surrounding the screen, and the cement seals that isolate different zones. This information is critical for interpreting water level measurements (which may reflect conditions at the screen depth rather than at the water table) and for understanding potential cross-contamination pathways between aquifer units.

### 19.3.2 The BoreholeLog Table

In the geodatabase, borehole log data are stored in a table rather than a feature class because the individual log entries do not have independent x,y coordinates -- they inherit their horizontal position from the associated Well point feature. The BoreholeLog table represents vertical information recorded along boreholes, with each row corresponding to a depth interval or a point measurement.

The key attributes of the BoreholeLog table are shown in Table 19.5.

**Table 19.5**: Attributes of the BoreholeLog table

| Attribute | Data Type | Description |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| WellID | Integer | References the HydroID of the associated Well feature |
| RefElev | Float | Reference elevation (typically land surface elevation at the well) from which depth measurements are taken |
| FromDepth | Float | Top of the interval, measured as depth below the reference elevation |
| ToDepth | Float | Bottom of the interval, measured as depth below the reference elevation |
| TopElev | Float | Top elevation of the interval in absolute elevation units (e.g., meters above sea level) |
| BottomElev | Float | Bottom elevation of the interval in absolute elevation units |
| ElevUnits | Text | Units of elevation (meters, feet) |
| Material | Text | Description of the earth material observed in this interval |
| HGUID | Integer | Hydrogeologic unit identifier, classifying the interval into a defined unit |
| HGUCode | Text | Text code for the hydrogeologic unit |
| LogType | Text | Type of log data (e.g., "Lithology," "Well Completion," "Hydrostratigraphy") |

The distinction between relative and absolute elevations is fundamental. Raw borehole data are almost always recorded as depth below the land surface -- the driller starts at zero and measures downward. These values are stored in FromDepth and ToDepth. However, depth-below-surface values cannot be directly compared between wells because each well has a different land surface elevation. To integrate data across multiple wells -- for example, to interpolate the top of an aquifer between borehole control points -- absolute elevations must be used. The TopElev and BottomElev fields store elevations referenced to a common datum (typically mean sea level), calculated by subtracting the depth values from the reference elevation:

$$TopElev = RefElev - FromDepth$$

$$BottomElev = RefElev - ToDepth$$

For example, consider a well with a land surface elevation (RefElev) of 245.0 meters above sea level. A sand layer encountered between 12.0 meters and 25.0 meters depth would have:

$$TopElev = 245.0 - 12.0 = 233.0 \text{ m a.s.l.}$$

$$BottomElev = 245.0 - 25.0 = 220.0 \text{ m a.s.l.}$$

This transformation is straightforward but essential. Without it, borehole data remain isolated observations that cannot be correlated across a study area. With it, the data become inputs to spatial interpolation algorithms that can generate continuous surfaces representing formation tops and bottoms -- the foundation of the three-dimensional geological models discussed in Chapter 20.

The LogType attribute allows the same BoreholeLog table to store different types of vertical data for the same well. A single well might have a lithological log (LogType = "Lithology"), a well completion log (LogType = "Completion"), and a hydrostratigraphic interpretation (LogType = "Hydrostratigraphy"). By filtering on LogType, the user can display and analyze each type of information separately.

### 19.3.3 From Raw Logs to Hydrostratigraphic Interpretation

The raw material descriptions in a borehole log must be interpreted and classified before they become useful for hydrogeological modeling. This interpretation process involves grouping the raw lithological intervals into named stratigraphic or hydrostratigraphic units based on regional geological knowledge, correlation with neighboring boreholes, and the hydrogeological significance of the materials.

Consider a borehole in the Hungarian Great Plain. The driller's log might record the following sequence: topsoil (0-1 m), clay (1-5 m), fine sand (5-12 m), silty clay (12-18 m), medium sand (18-35 m), clay (35-42 m), coarse sand and gravel (42-60 m), dense clay (60-75 m). A hydrogeologist familiar with the regional stratigraphy would interpret this as: a shallow Holocene aquifer (the fine sand at 5-12 m), separated by a clay aquitard from an upper Pleistocene aquifer (the medium sand at 18-35 m), separated by another clay layer from a lower Pleistocene gravel aquifer (the coarse sand and gravel at 42-60 m), resting on a Pannonian confining unit (the dense clay below 60 m).

This interpretation transforms the raw lithological data into a hydrostratigraphic classification that can be stored in the BoreholeLog table using the HGUID and HGUCode fields. The Material field retains the original driller's description for reference, while the HGUID and HGUCode fields encode the interpreted classification. This dual representation preserves the primary data while enabling the classified data to be used for mapping and modeling.

The interpretation process is inherently subjective, and different hydrogeologists may classify the same borehole differently. This is particularly true in areas like the Great Plain where the Quaternary sediments exhibit complex lateral and vertical variability and where formation boundaries are gradational rather than sharp. Standardized stratigraphic frameworks help constrain interpretations, but professional judgment remains essential. When building a hydrostratigraphic model from multiple boreholes, it is good practice to document the interpretation criteria and to include uncertainty estimates -- acknowledging, for example, that the boundary between the upper and lower Pleistocene aquifers is placed at a particular depth based on a grain-size change that could alternatively be interpreted as an intra-formational variation.

## 19.4 BorePoint and BoreLine: Three-Dimensional Borehole Features

### 19.4.1 Why 3D Features Matter

The BoreholeLog table stores vertical borehole data in a purely tabular form -- rows of numbers and text without inherent geometry. To visualize and analyze these data in three dimensions, the tabular records must be converted into the Z-enabled geometric features described in Chapter 18 (Section 18.2) that can be displayed in a 3D scene, queried spatially, and used as inputs to geostatistical interpolation. This is the role of BorePoint and BoreLine feature classes: they transform the numbers in the BoreholeLog table into visible, queryable 3D objects within the GIS.

BorePoint is a z-enabled point feature class (PointZ) that represents point observations along a borehole. Each BorePoint feature has x, y, and z coordinates, where x and y are inherited from the associated Well feature and z is derived from the elevation attribute. Typical uses of BorePoint include representing stratigraphic picks (the elevation at which a formation boundary is encountered), sampling ports, or specific measurement locations along the borehole.

BoreLine is a z-enabled polyline feature class (PolylineZ) that represents interval data along a borehole. Each BoreLine feature is a vertical line segment with a top elevation and a bottom elevation, representing a depth interval characterized by a particular material, formation, or well construction element. A complete borehole representation consists of a stack of BoreLine features, one for each interval in the BoreholeLog table, connected end-to-end from the land surface to the well bottom.

Both feature classes carry the same classification attributes as the BoreholeLog table -- WellID, Material, HGUID, HGUCode, and FType -- enabling them to be symbolized by formation, queried by hydrogeologic unit, and related back to the parent Well feature.

### 19.4.2 Creating BorePoint Features: Stratigraphic Picks

A **stratigraphic pick** is a point in three-dimensional space where a particular geological boundary is encountered in a borehole. The term comes from the practice of "picking" formation tops on geophysical logs -- literally marking the depth at which the log signature indicates a transition from one formation to another. In the context of the BoreholeLog table, a stratigraphic pick corresponds to the top elevation (TopElev) of a particular hydrogeologic unit.

Stratigraphic picks are powerful tools for building three-dimensional geological models. If we extract the top elevation of a specific formation from every borehole in a study area, we obtain a set of scattered data points that can be interpolated to produce a continuous surface representing the top of that formation, using the interpolation methods described in Chapter 8. Repeating this process for multiple formations yields a set of stacked surfaces that define the three-dimensional architecture of the subsurface -- the topic of Chapter 20, which describes the complete workflow from picks to surfaces to volumes.

To create BorePoint features from stratigraphic picks, the GIS tool reads the BoreholeLog table, identifies records that match the target HGUID, extracts the TopElev value, combines it with the x,y coordinates of the associated Well feature, and creates a PointZ feature at the resulting three-dimensional location. The process is automated in tools such as the Arc Hydro Groundwater toolset, which includes a "Make BorePoints" geoprocessing tool that generates BorePoint features from BoreholeLog records in a single operation.

A worked example illustrates the process. Suppose we have three wells in a study area, and we want to extract the top of the "Upper Pleistocene Aquifer" (HGUID = 7) from each:

### 19.4.3 Creating BoreLine Features: Visualizing the Borehole Column

BoreLine features provide a richer visualization than BorePoint features because they represent the full extent of each interval rather than a single point. When displayed in a 3D scene, a set of BoreLine features for a single well appears as a vertical bar divided into color-coded segments, each segment corresponding to a different formation or well construction element. When multiple wells are displayed together, the result is a "fence diagram" of sorts -- a set of vertical bars distributed across the landscape, allowing the viewer to visually correlate formations between wells.

The creation of BoreLine features follows a similar process to BorePoint creation. The GIS tool reads each record in the BoreholeLog table, retrieves the x,y coordinates from the associated Well feature, and constructs a vertical polyline from the TopElev to the BottomElev. The resulting feature is attributed with the Material, HGUID, HGUCode, and LogType values from the source record.

When creating BoreLine features for well completion data, the intervals represent casing sections, screens, grout seals, and open-hole segments rather than geological formations. The FType attribute distinguishes between these construction elements, and the symbolization can be customized to use standard well construction symbols -- a solid cylinder for casing, a slotted pattern for screens, a stippled pattern for gravel pack, and a cross-hatch for cement seals.

### 19.4.4 Practical Considerations in 3D Borehole Representation

Several practical issues arise when working with BorePoint and BoreLine features. The first is the assumption of verticality. The standard representation assumes that the borehole is perfectly vertical, so all intervals share the x,y coordinates of the Well point feature. In reality, many boreholes deviate from vertical, especially deeper ones. Directional drilling surveys record the deviation of the borehole from vertical at regular depth intervals, and specialized software can compute the true three-dimensional trajectory of the borehole. In the standard Arc Hydro Groundwater data model, deviated boreholes are not explicitly supported, but the model can be extended by storing deviation survey data in a separate table and computing corrected x,y,z coordinates for each interval.

The second issue is the scale of visualization. When viewing wells in a 3D scene at the regional scale, the actual diameter of a borehole (typically 15-50 centimeters) is far too small to be visible. Wells must be rendered with exaggerated widths or as cylindrical symbols scaled for visibility rather than accuracy. Similarly, vertical exaggeration is often applied to make thin formations visible -- but this distorts the true geometry and can mislead viewers about the relative thickness of formations. The GIS practitioner must balance visual clarity against geometric fidelity, and should always note the exaggeration factor used in any published view.

The third issue is data density. In well-studied areas, borehole data may be available at high spatial density -- hundreds of wells per square kilometer in parts of the Netherlands, for example, or in the urban area of Budapest where decades of construction and geotechnical investigations have generated thousands of logs. In remote or underdeveloped areas, borehole data may be extremely sparse. The reliability of interpolated surfaces and three-dimensional models depends directly on borehole density, and areas between control points should be flagged as uncertain in any model output.

## 19.5 Geologic Map Databases: The Foundation

### 19.5.1 What a Geologic Map Database Contains

Geologic maps are the most common data products used to describe the geology of a region. They are cartographic products containing information about the kinds of earth materials present in a specific geographic area, the boundaries that separate them, and the geological structures that have deformed them. These maps represent three-dimensional geological features in a two-dimensional mapping environment, using specialized graphical elements such as cross-sections, stratigraphic columns, and graphic legends to convey the third dimension.

A geologic map database is the digital compilation of spatial and descriptive information from one or more geologic maps. The database converts the cartographic content of a paper map into georeferenced feature classes and attribute tables that can be queried, analyzed, and combined with other geospatial datasets. The fundamental data elements of a geologic map database are:

- **Polygon features** (GeologyArea): Represent map units -- the colored patches on a geologic map, each corresponding to a specific rock type or stratigraphic unit. Attributes include the unit abbreviation, name, lithological description, age, and hydrogeologic unit classification.

- **Line features** (GeologyLine): Represent contacts (boundaries between rock units), faults (fractures along which displacement has occurred), dikes, folds, and other linear geological features. Attributes include the feature type, certainty (whether the feature is mapped with confidence or is inferred), and displacement characteristics.

- **Point features** (GeologyPoint): Represent observation points such as bedding attitude measurements (strike and dip), sample locations, springs, caves, and sinkholes. Attributes include the feature type, orientation measurements, and descriptions.

Standards for digital geologic map databases have been developed by several organizations. In the United States, the USGS and the Association of American State Geologists maintain the National Geologic Map Database (NGMDB), which provides a standard for digital cartographic representation of geologic map features. The North American Geologic Map Data Model (NADM) defines standardized methodologies for storage, manipulation, and distribution of geologic map information. The Arc Geology geodatabase design provides a simplified ArcGIS implementation for representing geologic features, cross-sections, and legend graphics using sets of point, line, and polygon feature classes with common attributes.

### 19.5.2 Linking Geologic Maps to Hydrogeologic Units

The connection between geologic maps and hydrogeological analysis lies in the classification of rock units into hydrogeologic units. A hydrogeologic unit is a body of rock or sediment that is sufficiently distinct in its hydraulic properties to be treated as a single unit in a groundwater model. The classification is based on the permeability of the material: a permeable sandstone may be classified as an aquifer, an impermeable shale as a confining unit (aquitard), and a fractured limestone as a conduit aquifer.

The HGUID and HGUCode attributes in the geology feature classes provide the linkage between geologic map polygons and hydrogeologic units. Each geologic polygon (representing a mapped rock unit) can be assigned an HGUID value that relates it to an entry in the HydrogeologicUnit table. This assignment effectively translates the geologic map into a hydrogeologic map by reclassifying the mapped units according to their water-bearing properties.

This translation is not always one-to-one. A single geologic formation may function as an aquifer in one part of a study area (where it is fractured and well-connected) and as a confining unit in another (where fracturing is absent). Conversely, multiple geologic formations may be grouped into a single hydrogeologic unit if they have similar hydraulic properties and are hydraulically connected. The classification therefore requires regional hydrogeological knowledge and cannot be performed purely algorithmically. As discussed in Section 19.3.3, the process is interpretive and benefits from careful documentation of the criteria used.

### 19.5.3 Hungarian Geological Map Databases: SZTFH Portal

The SZTFH geological map portal (map.mbfsz.gov.hu) provides access to Hungary's national geological map database. The portal serves several categories of map data that are directly relevant to groundwater work:

**Pre-Quaternary geological maps** at 1:100,000 scale show the distribution of rock types beneath the Quaternary cover. For much of the Great Plain, the Quaternary sediments are thick (50-500 meters) and the underlying geology is known only from deep boreholes. These maps are therefore interpretive products based on borehole correlation rather than surface mapping.

**Quaternary geological maps** show the distribution of surficial sediments including alluvium, loess, wind-blown sand, and lacustrine deposits. These maps are particularly important for shallow groundwater studies because the Quaternary sediments host the most accessible aquifers.

**Hydrogeological maps** show aquifer vulnerability, groundwater depth, and groundwater quality zones. Hungary's national hydrogeological map (1:500,000) classifies the country into zones of high, moderate, and low groundwater vulnerability based on the thickness and permeability of the overlying protective layer.

**Engineering geological maps** provide information on foundation conditions, soil stability, and geotechnical hazards -- secondary to groundwater work but useful for understanding the near-surface environment.

The digitization of Hungary's geological map archive is an ongoing effort. Many historical maps, including the detailed 1:25,000 geological maps produced in the 1960s-1980s, exist only in paper form or as scanned images that have not yet been georeferenced and vectorized. The SZTFH portal provides progressive access to newly digitized sheets as they become available, but coverage at the most detailed scales remains incomplete. For hydrogeological investigations in areas where digital data are unavailable, the practitioner may need to visit SZTFH's physical archive in Budapest to access the original map sheets and manually digitize the relevant features.

### 19.5.4 Global Geological Map Databases

Several initiatives have attempted to create consistent geological map databases at the continental or global scale. The OneGeology initiative, launched at the 33rd International Geological Congress in 2008, aims to make geological map data from every country available through the internet using interoperable web services. OneGeology operates as a portal rather than a centralized database: each participating geological survey serves its own data through OGC-compliant web map services, and the OneGeology portal aggregates these services into a single interface. Coverage and detail vary dramatically between countries, but the initiative has established the technical framework for cross-border geological data sharing.

In Europe, the EGDI (European Geological Data Infrastructure) provides access to harmonized geological data from the geological surveys of EU member states. EGDI, hosted by EuroGeoSurveys, includes pan-European datasets for bedrock geology, quaternary geology, mineral resources, and groundwater. The geological data served through EGDI follow the GeoSciML standard, enabling interoperability between national datasets. For the groundwater practitioner working on transboundary studies -- for example, investigating the shared aquifer systems between Hungary and Romania in the eastern Great Plain -- EGDI provides a mechanism for obtaining geological data from both sides of the border in a common format.

The USGS has produced a range of geological map products at various scales. The Geologic Map of North America (1:5,000,000) provides a continental overview, while the State Geologic Map Compilation (SGMC) provides more detailed data at the state level. Many individual geological quadrangles at 1:24,000 scale are available through the NGMDB, though coverage is far from complete.

## 19.6 Relationships Between Features: Linking Wells, Aquifers, and Geology

### 19.6.1 The Aquifer-Well Relationship

The relationship between aquifer polygon features and well point features is one of the most important associations in a groundwater geodatabase. It answers the fundamental question: which aquifer does this well tap? In the data model, this relationship is implemented as a one-to-many association based on key fields. The AquiferID attribute of a Well feature is set equal to the HydroID of the Aquifer feature to which the well is assigned. This allows bidirectional queries: given an aquifer, find all wells that tap it; given a well, identify the aquifer from which it produces.

The assignment of wells to aquifers can be performed in several ways. The simplest is spatial overlay: wells that fall within the polygon boundary of an aquifer are assigned to that aquifer. This works well for unconfined aquifers where the outcrop area defines the zone in which wells access the aquifer from above. It is less appropriate for confined aquifers, where wells may be located above the aquifer's subcrop area but penetrate through overlying formations to reach the aquifer at depth. In these cases, the assignment must be based on the well's screen interval and the known depth of the aquifer at the well's location, rather than on the surface projection alone.

When a well is screened across multiple aquifer units, the simple one-to-many relationship is insufficient. Several solutions exist. The most common is to add additional AquiferID fields to the Well feature class (AquiferID2, AquiferID3), each linked to a different Aquifer feature through a separate relationship. This approach is simple to implement and query but limits the number of aquifers per well to a fixed maximum. An alternative is to create a junction table that implements a many-to-many relationship between Wells and Aquifers, where each row in the table represents one well-aquifer association. This approach is more flexible but more complex to manage.

### 19.6.2 The Well-BoreholeLog Relationship

The relationship between a Well feature and its BoreholeLog records is a one-to-many association: one well can have many log entries. The WellID attribute in the BoreholeLog table matches the HydroID of the Well feature, creating the link. This relationship allows the user to select a well on the map and retrieve all associated borehole data -- lithological descriptions, completion intervals, hydrostratigraphic classifications -- in a single query.

The importance of this relationship becomes clear when working with large databases. The Texas Water Development Board database, for example, contains approximately 130,000 wells, each of which may have dozens of log entries. Navigating this dataset requires efficient indexing on the WellID field to ensure that related records can be retrieved without scanning the entire table.

### 19.6.3 The Geology-Aquifer-Well Chain

The full chain of relationships in a groundwater geodatabase runs from geological map features through aquifer classifications to individual wells:

1. **GeologyArea** polygons represent mapped rock units with HGUID classifying them into hydrogeologic units.
2. **Aquifer** polygons represent the spatial extent of aquifer boundaries, also classified by HGUID.
3. **Well** points are linked to Aquifer features through AquiferID and to hydrogeologic units through HGUID.
4. **BoreholeLog** records describe the vertical sequence at each well, with HGUID classifying each interval.
5. **BorePoint** and **BoreLine** features provide the 3D geometric representation of borehole data.

This chain ensures that every observation in the database -- from a polygon on a geological map to a depth interval in a borehole log -- can be traced to a specific hydrogeologic unit. The HydrogeologicUnit table (detailed in Chapter 20) serves as the central lookup table that defines the properties of each unit and provides the common key (HGUID) that ties the entire system together.

## 19.7 Hungarian Context: Groundwater Data Infrastructure

### 19.7.1 The SZTFH Geological Data Portal (map.mbfsz.gov.hu)

The SZTFH portal at map.mbfsz.gov.hu is the primary access point for Hungary's geological and hydrogeological spatial data. The portal provides a web-based GIS interface for browsing, querying, and downloading geological map layers. Among the datasets available are:

**Surface geological maps**: Polygon features showing the distribution of rock and sediment types at the land surface. For the mountainous regions of Hungary (Bakony, Vertes, Mecsek, Bukk), these maps show the outcrop patterns of Mesozoic limestones, dolomites, and Paleogene-Neogene volcanic rocks. For the Great Plain, they show the distribution of Quaternary alluvial, aeolian, and lacustrine sediments.

**Subsurface geological maps**: Interpretive maps of the geology beneath the Quaternary cover, compiled from borehole data. These maps are essential for understanding the deeper aquifer systems that underlie the thick Quaternary sediments of the Alfold.

**Borehole database**: Point features representing the locations of geological and hydrogeological boreholes. Each point is linked to a borehole record that may include lithological logs, sample descriptions, and geophysical measurements. The database contains records spanning more than a century of exploration activity.

**Mineral and geothermal resource maps**: Including maps of geothermal gradient, heat flow, and thermal water occurrence. These are directly relevant to the thermal well database discussed in Section 19.2.4.

The portal supports WMS (Web Map Service) and WFS (Web Feature Service) interfaces, allowing data to be consumed directly by desktop GIS software without manual download. For the GIS practitioner, this means that SZTFH geological layers can be added to an ArcGIS or QGIS project as live services, always reflecting the most current data available.

A practical limitation is that the richest geological data -- detailed lithological logs, geophysical measurements, and unpublished borehole reports -- often exist only in SZTFH's internal archive and are not available through the web portal. For detailed hydrogeological investigations, particularly in the planning phase of major groundwater development projects, a formal data request to SZTFH may be necessary to access the complete borehole record for a study area.

### 19.7.2 OVF Groundwater Monitoring Wells

OVF maintains a network of groundwater monitoring wells distributed across Hungary. These wells are distinct from production wells in that their sole purpose is observation: they are instrumented with water level sensors (increasingly telemetric, transmitting data in real time) and are sampled periodically for water quality. The monitoring network serves several purposes:

**Water table mapping**: Regular water level measurements at monitoring wells allow the construction of potentiometric surface maps showing the shape of the water table and the direction of groundwater flow. These maps are fundamental tools for groundwater management planning.

**Trend detection**: Long-term water level time series from monitoring wells reveal trends in groundwater storage. Rising or falling water tables over years to decades indicate changes in the balance between recharge and abstraction, potentially signaling overexploitation or the effects of climate change on recharge.

**WFD compliance**: Under the EU Water Framework Directive, Hungary is required to assess the quantitative and chemical status of its 185 groundwater bodies. The monitoring well network provides the data for these assessments. Wells are assigned to specific groundwater bodies, and their measurements are aggregated to determine whether each body is in "good" or "poor" status.

The monitoring data are available through data.vizugy.hu, the OVF data portal. Users can query groundwater level time series by station, by region, or by groundwater body. The data include both historical records (some extending back several decades) and near-real-time measurements from telemetric stations. This makes the OVF portal the closest Hungarian equivalent to the USGS NWIS for groundwater data access.

### 19.7.3 Hungarian Thermal Wells and Geothermal Resources

Hungary's geothermal endowment is exceptional by European standards. The country's heat flow density averages approximately 90-100 mW/m-squared, roughly twice the continental average, due to the thin crust beneath the Pannonian Basin and the elevated temperature of the asthenospheric mantle. This high heat flow produces a geothermal gradient of approximately 45-50 degrees Celsius per kilometer -- meaning that for every kilometer of depth, the temperature increases by nearly fifty degrees. Water from wells deeper than about 600-700 meters typically exceeds 30 degrees Celsius, the conventional threshold for thermal water classification.

The exploitation of thermal water in Hungary has a history spanning centuries, from the Ottoman-era baths of Budapest to the modern network of thermal spas that attracts millions of visitors annually. As of 2025, Hungary has over 1,300 registered thermal wells and approximately 270 operating thermal baths. The largest thermal well fields are in Hajduszoboszlo (northeast Great Plain), Heviz (western Hungary, Europe's largest biologically active thermal lake), Budapest (multiple thermal spring systems along the Danube), and Szentes-Hodmezovasarhely (southeast Great Plain).

From a data management perspective, thermal wells are stored in the same geodatabase framework as other wells, but with additional attributes specific to geothermal resources:

**Table 19.8**: Additional attributes for thermal well features in Hungarian databases

| Attribute | Description | Typical Range |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|
| WaterTemp | Wellhead water temperature in degrees Celsius | 30-98 |
| MaxTemp | Maximum measured temperature at depth | 35-170 |
| Yield | Production rate in liters per minute | 50-3000 |
| TDS | Total dissolved solids in mg/L | 500-15,000 |
| WaterType | Hydrochemical classification (e.g., Na-HCO3, Ca-Mg-HCO3) | Varies by depth and aquifer |

The spatial analysis of thermal wells using GIS reveals clear patterns. Wells producing from Pannonian sandstone aquifers tend to have lower temperatures (30-60 degrees Celsius) and higher yields, while wells producing from fractured Mesozoic carbonates can have higher temperatures (50-90+ degrees Celsius) but more variable yields depending on the fracture network encountered. Mapping these patterns helps guide the siting of new thermal wells and the sustainable management of existing ones.

## 19.8 Global Perspective: Transboundary Aquifers and International Databases

### 19.8.1 WHYMAP: The World Hydrogeological Map

The World-wide Hydrogeological Mapping and Assessment Programme (WHYMAP) has produced the most widely used global map of groundwater resources. The programme, coordinated by BGR (Bundesanstalt fur Geowissenschaften und Rohstoffe) in Hannover, Germany, with support from UNESCO, IAH, IGRAC, and other partners, has been active since the early 2000s. Its flagship product is the Groundwater Resources Map of the World at 1:25,000,000 scale, which classifies the world's land surface into hydrogeological provinces based on the type and productivity of the underlying aquifer system.

The WHYMAP classification distinguishes between:
- **Major groundwater basins**: Large sedimentary basins with significant groundwater resources (e.g., the Ogallala Aquifer of the US Great Plains, the Guarani Aquifer of South America, the Great Artesian Basin of Australia).
- **Regions with complex hydrogeological structure**: Areas where multiple aquifer types coexist, often with complex geology (e.g., the Alps, the Carpathian Basin).
- **Regions with limited groundwater resources**: Areas with crystalline basement rock or thin soil cover where groundwater availability is limited (e.g., the Canadian Shield, much of sub-Saharan Africa's crystalline regions).
- **Areas of major groundwater recharge and discharge**: Zones where significant recharge or discharge of groundwater occurs.

The WHYMAP map is available as a wall poster, a PDF, and as GIS data layers downloadable from the BGR website. For educational purposes and for initial reconnaissance of a study area, the map provides an invaluable first overview. However, its very coarse scale means that it cannot replace national or regional aquifer maps for any practical engineering purpose.

### 19.8.2 IGRAC and the Global Groundwater Information System

IGRAC operates the Global Groundwater Information System (GGIS), accessible at ggis.un-igrac.org. GGIS aggregates groundwater data from participating countries and makes it available through a web-based GIS interface. The system includes several data layers:

**Aquifer maps**: Polygons showing the extent and type of major aquifer systems, compiled from national hydrogeological maps contributed by participating geological surveys.

**Groundwater monitoring**: Point data from national groundwater monitoring networks, including water level time series and basic water quality indicators. The Global Groundwater Monitoring Network (GGMN), managed by IGRAC, coordinates the collection and sharing of monitoring data from over thirty countries.

**Transboundary aquifer inventories**: Polygons delineating transboundary aquifer systems identified through the UNESCO ISARM programme, with attributes describing the aquifer type, shared countries, and management status.

**Groundwater use**: Estimates of groundwater abstraction rates by country and by sector (domestic, agricultural, industrial), compiled from national statistics and FAO AQUASTAT data.

For the international student, GGIS provides a starting point for accessing groundwater information about any country. The depth and quality of available data vary enormously -- some countries have contributed detailed well databases and monitoring records, while others are represented only by coarse national-level estimates. Nevertheless, GGIS is the only platform that attempts to provide a single access point for global groundwater data, and its coverage improves as more countries participate.

### 19.8.3 Transboundary Aquifers: The Shared Resource Challenge

Groundwater does not respect political boundaries. An aquifer that extends beneath two or more countries is a transboundary aquifer, and its management requires cooperation between the sharing states. The UNESCO ISARM (Internationally Shared Aquifer Resources Management) initiative has conducted inventories of transboundary aquifers on every continent, identifying approximately 600 such systems worldwide. In Europe, over 150 transboundary aquifers have been inventoried, many of them in the complex geological setting of central and southeastern Europe.

Hungary shares several significant transboundary aquifer systems with its neighbors:

**The Pannonian thermal water system** (shared with Slovakia, Romania, Serbia, and Croatia): The deep Pannonian sediments contain thermal water resources that extend across national boundaries. The thermal water basin beneath the Hungarian-Slovak border region is a particularly well-studied example, where coordinated monitoring has been established to prevent overexploitation on either side.

**The Danube alluvial aquifer** (shared with Slovakia, Croatia, and Serbia): The alluvial sediments deposited by the Danube and its tributaries form productive shallow aquifers that are critical for water supply on both banks. The management of these aquifers requires consideration of river-aquifer interactions and the impact of river regulation on recharge.

**The Drava-Mura alluvial aquifer** (shared with Croatia and Slovenia): Similar to the Danube system, the alluvial aquifers of the Drava and Mura rivers provide water supply to communities on both sides of the Hungarian-Croatian border.

The management of these shared resources is governed by bilateral and multilateral agreements, supported by data sharing between national water management agencies. The GIS framework described in this chapter -- with its standardized feature classes, common identification systems, and web service-based data exchange -- provides the technical foundation for such cooperation. The adoption of INSPIRE-compliant data standards by EU member states has significantly facilitated cross-border data sharing, though practical challenges remain in harmonizing historical datasets collected under different national standards.

## 19.9 The Nyirség Leaky Aquifer System: A Regional Hydrogeology Case Study

The concepts introduced in the preceding sections -- aquifer mapping, well databases, borehole logs, potentiometric surface construction, and geological cross-sections -- come together powerfully in a real-world investigation. The Nyirség region of northeastern Hungary provides an ideal case study because it has been investigated over five decades using progressively more sophisticated methods, and the resulting dataset illustrates virtually every GIS-based hydrogeological technique discussed in this textbook.

### 19.9.1 Geological Setting and Aquifer Architecture

The Nyirség is a roughly 5,000 km^2^ sand plateau in the northeastern corner of the Great Hungarian Plain, centered on the city of Debrecen (population approximately 200,000). The region is underlain by Quaternary sediments -- alternating layers of sand, gravel, silt, and clay -- deposited by ancient alluvial fans from the Carpathian Mountains. These sediments fill a tectonic basin that has been subsiding since the Pliocene, reaching a total Quaternary thickness of 100--300 meters depending on the location of tectonic depressions.

The Pleistocene sedimentary sequence in the Nyirség can be divided into three principal hydrostratigraphic units, each of which corresponds to a named aquifer separated by semi-confining clay layers (Marton, 2009):

- **Upper Pleistocene aquifer**: Fine to medium sand, typically encountered between 5 and 30 meters depth. This unit hosts the water table and is directly connected to the surface through the vadose zone. It is vulnerable to contamination from agricultural and urban sources.

- **Middle Pleistocene aquifer**: Medium to coarse sand, found between approximately 30 and 80 meters depth. This unit is partially confined by overlying clay layers and provides water to many smaller municipal supply wells.

- **Lower Pleistocene aquifer**: Coarse sand and gravel, encountered between approximately 80 and 150+ meters depth. This is the principal drinking water aquifer for the city of Debrecen and other major towns in the region. It is the target of most production wells and the focus of intensive hydrogeological investigation.

A key finding of decades of research in the Nyirség is that the clay layers separating these aquifer units, long assumed to be impermeable, are in fact **leaky** -- they transmit water slowly but continuously from the upper aquifers downward into the lower Pleistocene production aquifer. This makes the Nyirség a classic example of a **leaky aquifer system** (also called a semi-confined or aquitard-drained system), in which vertical flow through low-permeability layers is a critical component of the regional water balance. The discovery of this leakage fundamentally changed the understanding of the system's recharge mechanism and sustainability (Marton, 2009).

### 19.9.2 The Monitoring Well Network Near Debrecen

The city of Debrecen operates four major wellfields (designated I, II, III, and IV) that extract water from the Lower Pleistocene aquifer. To understand the hydrogeological impacts of this extraction, a network of monitoring wells was established in the vicinity of the wellfields. These monitoring wells are instrumented at multiple depth intervals -- typically as nested piezometers or clustered well pairs where two or three wells are drilled at the same surface location but screened at different depths (e.g., one in the Upper Pleistocene at 20 m, one in the Middle Pleistocene at 60 m, and one in the Lower Pleistocene at 120 m).

This multi-level monitoring design is critical for understanding vertical hydraulic gradients -- the difference in potentiometric head between vertically adjacent aquifer units at the same x,y location. When the head in the Upper Pleistocene is higher than the head in the Lower Pleistocene at the same location, the vertical gradient is downward, indicating recharge by leakage. When the gradient reverses (Lower Pleistocene head exceeds Upper Pleistocene head), conditions favor upward discharge -- the natural state in low-lying areas near rivers.

Data from monitoring wells near Debrecen's wellfields reveal that intensive pumping from the Lower Pleistocene aquifer has progressively increased the downward hydraulic gradient, accelerating leakage from the upper aquifers. In areas that were formerly neutral (zero gradient) or discharge zones, the gradient has reversed to become downward, effectively converting the landscape from a groundwater discharge area to a recharge area driven by pumping-induced leakage (Marton, 2009). This has profound implications for water quality, because the accelerated downward flow can carry contaminants from the shallow aquifer and land surface into the deep production aquifer over time.

### 19.9.3 Potentiometric Surface Mapping and GIS Analysis

Using the monitoring well database, potentiometric surface maps of the Lower Pleistocene aquifer have been constructed for multiple time periods: 1966, 1986, and 2006. These maps were generated using kriging interpolation (the natural neighbor option) in GIS, with 62 control points in 1966, 105 in 1986, and 72 in 2006. The resulting surfaces reveal the evolution of the regional flow field over a forty-year period of intensive groundwater extraction followed by reduced pumping (Marton, 2009).

The key observations from this time series of potentiometric maps are:

1. **1966**: The potentiometric surface slopes concentrically outward from the center of the Nyirség, indicating that the central highlands are the primary recharge area and groundwater flows radially toward the surrounding lowlands. A mild depression around Debrecen is already visible, reflecting the onset of wellfield pumping.

2. **1986**: The potentiometric surface shows a dramatic deepening of the cone of depression around Debrecen, with a maximum drawdown of approximately 28 meters at Wellfield I. Closed potentiometric contours surround the city, indicating that groundwater now flows toward Debrecen from all directions -- a fundamental reversal from the original radial outflow pattern. The influence of pumping is detectable up to 35 km from the wellfields.

3. **2006**: Following a roughly 40% reduction in extraction since the early 1990s (driven by industrial decline and water conservation), the potentiometric surface near the wellfields has partially recovered (approximately 5--7 m of rise). However, at distances greater than 10--15 km from the wellfields, potentiometric levels continue to decline -- a counterintuitive finding that reflects the slow propagation of pressure changes through the leaky aquifer system, analogous to a system of communicating vessels reaching a new equilibrium.

This time series of potentiometric maps exemplifies the power of GIS for groundwater management: by storing monitoring data in a well database, applying spatial interpolation, and comparing surfaces across time, managers can visualize the regional impact of pumping and evaluate the effectiveness of conservation measures. The maps were created using the Surfer geostatistical package and standard GIS tools, with the Grid Node Editor function used to extract interpolated values at specific coordinate locations for quantitative comparison.

### 19.9.4 Isotopic Dating: Groundwater Age and Recharge Rates

One of the most important contributions of the Nyirség research program was the application of isotope hydrology to determine groundwater ages and recharge rates. Beginning in 1973, water samples from monitoring and production wells across the region were analyzed for tritium (^3^H) and deuterium (^2^H) at laboratories in Karlsruhe (Germany), Vienna (Austria), and later at the ATOMKI nuclear research laboratory in Debrecen (Marton, 2009).

Tritium, a radioactive isotope of hydrogen with a half-life of 12.3 years, entered the hydrological cycle in large quantities during atmospheric nuclear weapons testing in the 1950s and 1960s. Its presence in groundwater indicates that at least some component of the water entered the subsurface after 1952. The absence of tritium indicates water that is older than roughly 60--70 years. Stable isotopes (deuterium and oxygen-18) provide complementary information about the origin and recharge conditions of the water.

The isotope results from the Nyirség revealed several critical findings:

- **Shallow wells** (Upper Pleistocene, 10--30 m depth) contained high tritium concentrations, confirming recent recharge from modern precipitation.

- **Mid-depth wells** (Middle Pleistocene, 40--80 m) showed intermediate tritium values, indicating a mixture of modern and older water -- consistent with the leaky aquifer model in which modern water percolates slowly through the semi-confining clay.

- **Deep wells** (Lower Pleistocene, 100--150+ m) showed very low or undetectable tritium in areas distant from pumping centers, indicating water ages of thousands of years. However, in the vicinity of intensively pumped wellfields, tritium was detectable even in deep wells -- a dramatic confirmation that pumping accelerates downward leakage and draws young, potentially contaminated water into the deep aquifer.

- **Recharge rate estimates** derived from the isotope data indicated natural vertical recharge through the aquitards of approximately 7 +/- 1 mm/year averaged over the entire Nyirség, with higher rates of approximately 20 +/- 2 mm/year in the central highland area. These rates were fundamental inputs for calibrating numerical groundwater flow models of the region.

The isotope data also showed that groundwater ages increased systematically with distance from the recharge area, as expected in a regional flow system. The age gradient provided independent confirmation of the flow directions inferred from the potentiometric surface maps.

### 19.9.5 Pleistocene Basement Mapping: Tectonic Controls on Aquifer Geometry

The three-dimensional architecture of the Nyirség aquifer system is strongly influenced by tectonic movements that have deformed the Pleistocene sediments since their deposition. Using borehole log data from 322 wells screened in the Lower Pleistocene, a map of the Pleistocene basement surface was constructed using kriging interpolation (natural neighbor option) in GIS (Marton, 2009).

The resulting basement map reveals a remarkably rugged surface with relief exceeding 160 meters -- more than twice the relief of the present land surface. The deepest tectonic depressions, reaching -157 m below sea level, are found near Nyirbátor and Nagyecsed in the eastern part of the region. Near Debrecen, the basement lies at -40 to -50 m below sea level, approximately 100--120 meters higher than the eastern depressions. This pattern indicates significant differential tectonic subsidence since the Pleistocene, with the eastern Nyirség sinking faster than the western part.

The tectonic fault lines identified from the basement map have direct hydraulic significance. Near Debrecen, a major north-south fault zone along the Toco Valley creates a hydraulic barrier that separates wellfields on either side. Wells on opposite sides of this fault, separated by only 3.5 km horizontally and screened at similar depths of approximately -950 m below sea level, show potentiometric head differences of up to 96 meters (Szanyi, 2004, cited in Marton, 2009) -- dramatic evidence that faults can compartmentalize aquifer systems and must be accounted for in any GIS-based groundwater model.

## 19.10 Groundwater Chemistry and Hydrochemical Mapping

Understanding the chemical composition of groundwater is essential for water supply management, contamination assessment, and regulatory compliance. GIS provides the tools to map, analyze, and visualize spatial patterns in water chemistry -- from simple single-parameter maps to complex multivariate hydrochemical facies analyses.

### 19.10.1 Major Ion Composition of Hungarian Groundwaters

The chemical composition of groundwater reflects the history of interactions between water and the geological materials through which it has flowed. Precipitation that recharges the aquifer system is dilute -- typically containing only 10--40 mg/L of total dissolved solids (TDS) acquired from atmospheric gases and aerosols. As this water percolates through the soil and underlying rock, it dissolves minerals and acquires dissolved ions whose concentrations increase with residence time and the reactivity of the host formations.

The major ions in natural groundwater are typically the cations calcium (Ca^2+^), magnesium (Mg^2+^), sodium (Na^+^), and potassium (K^+^), and the anions bicarbonate (HCO~3~^-^), sulfate (SO~4~^2-^), and chloride (Cl^-^). The relative proportions of these ions define the **hydrochemical facies** of the water and provide information about the geological environment from which the water originates.

Hungarian groundwaters span a wide range of hydrochemical facies depending on the aquifer type (Marton, 2009):

- **Shallow alluvial aquifers** (Quaternary sands and gravels in the Great Plain): Typically Ca-Mg-HCO~3~ type, reflecting the dissolution of carbonates in the soil and shallow sediments. Total hardness is moderate (150--400 mg/L as CaCO~3~). These waters are often affected by elevated nitrate concentrations from agricultural activity.

- **Deep Pannonian sandstone aquifers**: Typically Na-HCO~3~ type, with higher TDS (500--2,000 mg/L) and elevated sodium from ion exchange processes in which calcium in the water is exchanged for sodium on clay mineral surfaces. These waters are often soft but may exceed drinking water limits for sodium, ammonium, and iron/manganese.

- **Karstic aquifers** (Transdanubian and Bukk Mountains): Ca-HCO~3~ type, hard water with TDS of 300--600 mg/L, reflecting limestone and dolomite dissolution. These waters are generally of excellent quality but are vulnerable to surface contamination through karst conduits.

- **Thermal waters** (deep Pannonian and Mesozoic formations): Highly variable chemistry depending on depth and host rock. Waters from Pannonian sandstones at 1,000--2,000 m depth may be Na-Cl or Na-HCO~3~ type with TDS of 2,000--15,000 mg/L. Thermal waters from fractured Triassic dolomites (e.g., Hévíz) may be Ca-Mg-SO~4~-HCO~3~ type. Silica, fluoride, and various trace elements (boron, lithium, strontium) are often present at elevated concentrations.

### 19.10.2 Hydrochemical Facies Mapping with Piper Diagrams

The **Piper diagram** (also known as a trilinear diagram) is the standard visualization tool for classifying groundwater samples by their major ion composition. The diagram consists of two triangles -- one for cations (Ca, Mg, Na+K) and one for anions (HCO~3~, SO~4~, Cl) -- and a central diamond that combines both. Each water sample plots as a single point in the diamond based on the relative proportions (in milliequivalents per liter) of its major ions. Samples with similar hydrochemical signatures cluster together, while those from different geological environments separate into distinct fields.

In a GIS context, Piper diagrams can be linked to spatial maps through a two-step workflow:

1. **Generate the Piper diagram** from water quality data stored in the well database. Each sample is classified into a hydrochemical facies (e.g., Ca-HCO~3~, Na-Cl, mixed type) based on its position in the diamond.

2. **Map the facies spatially** by symbolizing the well point features according to their assigned hydrochemical class. The resulting map shows the geographic distribution of water types, which typically correlates with aquifer lithology, depth, and flow path length.

This workflow is routinely applied in Hungary for WFD compliance assessments, where the chemical status of each of the 185 groundwater bodies must be evaluated. Spatial clustering of particular water types can reveal contamination plumes, geological boundaries, or mixing zones between aquifers.

### 19.10.3 Arsenic Contamination in the Great Hungarian Plain

One of the most significant groundwater quality challenges in Hungary -- and one with direct relevance to GIS-based risk mapping -- is the naturally elevated arsenic concentration in groundwater across large areas of the Great Plain (Alfold). Unlike most groundwater contamination, which results from human activity, the arsenic enrichment in Hungarian groundwater is geogenic: it originates from natural geochemical processes within the aquifer sediments themselves.

**Scale of the problem.** According to World Bank experts (Alaerts and Khouri, 2004, cited in Marton, 2009), approximately 400,000 people in Hungary are at health risk from arsenic in their drinking water. The affected area spans six counties in the southern and central Great Plain, with the most severe contamination in Békés, Csongrád, and Jász-Nagykun-Szolnok counties. Arsenic concentrations in untreated groundwater from the affected aquifers commonly range from 10 to 100 micrograms per liter (ug/L), with some wells exceeding 200 ug/L.

**EU drinking water limit.** The maximum allowable arsenic concentration in drinking water was lowered from 50 ug/L to 10 ug/L -- first by the World Health Organization (WHO) in 1993, then by the European Union in 1997 (EU Directive 98/83/EC). This tenfold reduction in the permissible limit dramatically expanded the number of water supply systems classified as non-compliant. As Marton (2009) notes, it is always far cheaper to remove the first 90% of a contaminant than the remaining 10%, so the cost implications of the stricter standard are severe.

**Mechanism of arsenic mobilization.** The arsenic in Great Plain groundwaters is associated with iron and manganese oxyhydroxide minerals in the Pleistocene and Pannonian sediments. Under the reducing (low-oxygen) conditions that prevail in the deeper aquifers, these minerals dissolve, releasing adsorbed arsenic into the groundwater. The process is enhanced by the presence of organic matter in the sediments, which drives microbial reduction reactions. The result is that arsenic concentrations tend to increase with depth and with distance along the flow path -- exactly the opposite pattern from surface-derived contamination, which decreases with depth.

**Regulatory distinction.** It is important to note that under the EU Water Framework Directive definition, arsenic enrichment from geogenic sources is technically not "pollution" (which the Directive defines as contamination from human activity). Nevertheless, the health risk must be addressed, and Hungary has implemented a national Drinking Water Quality Improvement Programme (Ivóvízminőség-javító Program) to bring affected water supplies into compliance -- through arsenic removal treatment, blending with arsenic-free water, or developing alternative water sources.

**GIS mapping of arsenic risk.** The spatial distribution of arsenic in groundwater can be mapped by interpolating arsenic concentration data from the well database across the affected region. The resulting maps delineate arsenic risk zones that guide investment decisions for water treatment infrastructure. Overlay analysis combining arsenic concentration maps with population density data identifies the communities at greatest risk. Further overlay with geological maps (specifically, the thickness and lithology of Pannonian sediments) helps explain the geochemical controls on arsenic distribution and predict arsenic levels in areas where water quality data are sparse.

## 19.11 Wellhead Protection Zones: EU Requirements and GIS Delineation

The protection of groundwater sources from contamination is a cornerstone of EU water policy. The Water Framework Directive (2000/60/EC) requires member states to identify drinking water protected areas and to establish safeguard zones around abstraction points. In Hungary, the specific regulatory framework for wellhead protection is defined by Government Decree 123/1997 (Korm. rendelet), which mandates the delineation of protection zones around all groundwater supply wells.

### 19.11.1 The Hungarian Wellhead Protection System

The Hungarian wellhead protection system defines three concentric zones around each water supply well or wellfield (Marton, 2009):

- **Inner protection zone** (belső védőidom): The immediate vicinity of the well, typically extending 10--50 meters from the wellhead. All activities other than water supply operations are prohibited within this zone. It corresponds roughly to the area within which a contaminant could reach the well within hours.

- **Outer protection zone** (külső védőidom): A larger zone delineated by the capture zone boundary -- the three-dimensional volume of aquifer from which water flows to the well over a defined time period (typically 50 years or the design lifetime of the well). Within this zone, activities that could contaminate the aquifer are restricted or regulated. Land use controls prohibit the establishment of new pollution sources (e.g., industrial facilities, waste disposal sites, intensive agriculture) and require monitoring of existing activities.

- **Hydrogeological protection zone** (hidrogeológiai védőövezet): The outermost zone, encompassing the full recharge area of the well or wellfield. Monitoring and land use planning within this zone are designed to detect emerging threats before they reach the abstraction point.

These zones are three-dimensional bodies (idomok, literally "shapes" or "forms") rather than simple surface projections, because the capture zone of a well extends both laterally and vertically through the aquifer. In a layered system like the Nyirség, the protection zone for a deep well may extend vertically through multiple aquitards and laterally for kilometers in the direction of regional flow.

### 19.11.2 GIS-Based Delineation Methods

The delineation of wellhead protection zones is fundamentally a spatial analysis task, and GIS plays a central role in every step of the process:

1. **Analytical methods**: For simple hydrogeological settings (single aquifer, uniform flow), the capture zone can be calculated using analytical equations and mapped as an elliptical or bullet-shaped zone in GIS. The zone's dimensions depend on the pumping rate, aquifer transmissivity, porosity, and regional hydraulic gradient.

2. **Numerical modeling**: For complex settings (layered aquifer systems, heterogeneous hydraulic properties, multiple pumping wells), the capture zone is determined using numerical groundwater flow models (discussed in Chapters 20 and 21). Particle tracking algorithms trace the paths of hypothetical water particles backward in time from the well to their recharge locations, defining the three-dimensional capture zone. The model output is imported into GIS as polygon features representing the surface projection of the capture zone.

3. **Travel time mapping**: Isochrone maps (lines of equal travel time) are generated from the particle tracking results, showing how long it would take a contaminant released at any point in the aquifer to reach the well. These isochrones define the boundaries of different protection zones -- for example, the 1-year isochrone might define the inner zone and the 50-year isochrone the outer zone.

4. **Vulnerability overlay**: The protection zone polygons are overlaid with land use maps, geological vulnerability maps (from SZTFH), and existing pollution source inventories to assess the risk to the water supply. Areas where the protection zone intersects a high-vulnerability geological setting (e.g., thin or absent confining layer) and an existing pollution source represent the highest-priority management targets.

In the Nyirség, the delineation of wellhead protection zones for Debrecen's four wellfields was particularly challenging because the leaky aquifer system means that the capture zone extends vertically through the semi-confining clay layers to the land surface -- a much larger area than would be defined if the clay layers were truly impermeable. Marton (2009) documents how the discovery of leakage through isotope studies fundamentally changed the protection zone delineation for these wellfields, expanding the area requiring monitoring and land use controls.

A practical complication noted by Marton (2009) is that in the Great Plain, where the average production from aquifer systems already slightly exceeds long-term recharge, the far-field influence of wellfields extends so far that it becomes impossible to establish complete, fully effective protection zones (védőidomok). This finding underscores the tension between groundwater exploitation and source protection -- a tension that GIS-based analysis makes visible and quantifiable.

## 19.12 Water Table Decline and Sustainability Concerns

### 19.12.1 Long-Term Potentiometric Trends in Northeastern Hungary

The long-term monitoring record from the Nyirség and the broader northeastern Great Plain provides a sobering illustration of the cumulative impact of decades of groundwater extraction. As documented by Marton (2009), potentiometric levels in the Lower Pleistocene aquifer -- the principal water supply horizon -- declined continuously from the 1960s through the late 1980s, a period during which Hungary's industrial and agricultural water demands drove extraction rates to their peak.

The magnitude of the decline varied with proximity to major wellfields. At Debrecen's Wellfield I, the potentiometric surface dropped by approximately 28 meters between 1952 and 1980, at an average rate of 1.0 m/year. By the mid-1980s, the combined cone of depression around Debrecen's four wellfields extended 35 km to the northeast and reached the country's border to the southeast. At the city of Hajdunánás, 40 km north of Debrecen, the Lower Pleistocene potentiometric level dropped approximately 5--6 meters between 1966 and 1986 -- attributable partly to local extraction but primarily to the regional drawdown propagating from Debrecen.

Across the broader northeastern Great Plain, regional potentiometric levels in the principal aquifer declined by approximately 5--10 meters between the 1960s and 1980s. In the deepest thermal water-bearing formations, the decline exceeded 50 meters in some areas (Marton, 2009).

### 19.12.2 Partial Recovery and Continuing Regional Decline

Following Hungary's economic transition in the early 1990s, water consumption dropped sharply. Industrial water use decreased dramatically, and public supply consumption declined as water pricing was reformed. Total groundwater extraction from the Lower Pleistocene aquifer near Debrecen fell from approximately 30 million m^3^/year in the 1980s to approximately 16 million m^3^/year by the late 1990s -- a reduction of nearly 50%.

The response of the potentiometric surface to this reduced pumping, however, was not the simple, uniform recovery that might have been expected. As documented by the 2006 potentiometric map (Section 19.9.3), recovery occurred only within approximately 10--15 km of the major wellfields. At the three Debrecen wellfield reference points, potentiometric levels rose by 5.0 to 7.0 meters between 1986 and 2006. At greater distances, however, potentiometric levels continued to decline despite the reduction in extraction.

This pattern is explained by the physics of the leaky aquifer system: pressure changes propagate slowly through the low-permeability aquitards, and the regional system behaves like a set of communicating vessels that require decades or more to reach a new equilibrium. The continued decline at distance reflects the system's ongoing adjustment to the cumulative extraction of the preceding decades, even as conditions near the wellfields begin to stabilize (Marton, 2009).

### 19.12.3 Sustainability Implications

The Nyirség case study illustrates a central challenge in groundwater management worldwide: the time lag between changes in extraction rates and the aquifer system's response. In leaky aquifer systems with thick, low-permeability confining layers, the effects of overextraction may continue to propagate for decades after pumping rates are reduced. This means that:

- **Early warning systems are essential.** By the time water table declines become severe enough to attract public attention, the underlying cause may have been operating for decades, and the recovery time will be correspondingly long. Regular monitoring and GIS-based trend analysis provide the early detection capability that enables timely intervention.

- **Regional models are necessary.** Local wellfield optimization is insufficient; the far-field effects of pumping must be evaluated at the regional scale using numerical models that account for vertical leakage, boundary conditions, and the full three-dimensional geometry of the aquifer system.

- **International cooperation is required.** The Nyirség aquifer system extends across the Hungarian-Romanian border, and the potentiometric surface maps in Marton (2009) show that the influence of Debrecen's pumping reaches the border. As both countries are EU members, the Water Framework Directive provides the legal framework for coordinated monitoring and management of this shared resource, but the practical implementation of cross-border data sharing remains an ongoing challenge.

- **Protection zones must account for leakage.** In a leaky system, the conventional assumption that deep aquifers are protected by impermeable confining layers is invalid. Wellhead protection zones must be delineated based on the actual leakage properties of the aquitards, as determined by field measurements and isotope studies, rather than on idealized geological models.

## 19.13 From Data to Models: The Role of Well and Borehole Data in Groundwater Modeling

### 19.13.1 How Well Data Feed the Modeling Workflow

The data types described in this chapter -- aquifer maps, well databases, borehole logs, and geological maps -- do not exist in isolation. They are the inputs to the hydrogeological modeling workflow described in Chapters 20 and 21. Understanding how each data type feeds into the modeling process helps the practitioner appreciate why careful data management is so important.

**Aquifer maps** define the lateral extent of the model domain and the boundaries of different hydrogeologic units. When a numerical groundwater flow model is constructed (Chapter 21), the model grid must be designed to align with aquifer boundaries, and different hydraulic properties must be assigned to different zones within the model. Aquifer polygon features provide the spatial framework for this zonation.

**Well locations and depths** provide the observational control for calibrating the model. Water level measurements from monitoring wells are compared against model predictions to assess whether the model accurately reproduces observed conditions. Production wells define the locations and rates of groundwater extraction that the model must account for. Injection wells define return flow locations.

**Borehole logs** provide the subsurface information needed to build the three-dimensional geological framework of the model. Stratigraphic picks extracted from borehole data are interpolated to produce continuous surfaces representing formation tops and bottoms, which in turn define the layer geometry of the numerical model. The Material and HGUID classifications in the BoreholeLog table guide the assignment of hydraulic properties to model layers.

**Geological maps** provide the surface expression of the subsurface geology, constraining the lateral extent of formations where they are exposed at the surface or are truncated by erosion. In areas between borehole control points, geological maps provide the primary information about the spatial distribution of rock types.

The quality of a groundwater model is ultimately limited by the quality of the input data. A model built from ten boreholes will have far greater uncertainty than one built from a hundred, regardless of how sophisticated the modeling software is. This is why the careful collection, management, and analysis of well and borehole data -- the subject of this chapter -- is not merely a data management exercise. It is the foundation upon which all subsequent hydrogeological analysis rests.

### 19.13.2 Data Gaps and Uncertainty

No study area has perfect data coverage. Even in well-studied regions, there are always areas between boreholes where the subsurface is unknown and must be interpolated. The spatial distribution of boreholes is almost always uneven -- concentrated in urban areas, along transportation corridors, and in economically important aquifer zones, and sparse in rural areas, mountainous terrain, and deep subsurface formations.

Several strategies help manage this uncertainty. Cross-validation, where each borehole is temporarily removed from the dataset and the value at its location is estimated from the remaining boreholes, provides a measure of interpolation accuracy. Geostatistical methods such as kriging produce not only an estimated surface but also a variance surface that quantifies the uncertainty at every point. The variance is lowest near borehole control points and highest in data-sparse areas, providing an objective map of where additional drilling would most reduce model uncertainty.

In Hungary, the borehole density varies dramatically between regions. The Budapest urban area has thousands of boreholes per hundred square kilometers, accumulated through decades of construction, metro tunneling, and geotechnical investigations. The central Tisza region, by contrast, may have only a few dozen boreholes per hundred square kilometers. A hydrogeological model of the Budapest area can rely on dense data and produce high-confidence results; a model of the central Tisza must acknowledge and quantify the substantial uncertainties arising from sparse data.

## 19.14 Practical Workflow: Building a Well and Aquifer Database

### 19.14.1 Step-by-Step Implementation

The following workflow summarizes the practical steps for creating a well and aquifer database in a GIS environment, drawing together the concepts discussed throughout this chapter.

**Step 1: Design the geodatabase.** Create the Aquifer polygon feature class, Well point feature class, BoreholeLog table, and BorePoint/BoreLine feature classes. Add project-specific attributes beyond the minimum set -- for example, well owner, water use, permit number, and thermal water attributes if applicable. Define coded value domains for FType, WaterUse, and other classification fields.

**Step 2: Import aquifer data.** Load aquifer boundary polygons from national or regional aquifer maps. For Hungary, this means downloading WFD groundwater body boundaries from EEA/WISE or SZTFH hydrogeological map layers. For the United States, this means obtaining USGS Principal Aquifer or state-level aquifer shapefiles. Assign HydroID values to uniquely identify each feature, and set HydroCode to the original external identifier.

**Step 3: Import well data.** Load well locations from national well databases. Source data often arrive as tabular files with coordinate columns, requiring the creation of point features from x,y coordinates (the "Make XY Event Layer" or equivalent geoprocessing tool). Import the minimum attribute set plus any project-specific fields. Assign HydroID values. Set HydroCode to the original well identifier (state well number, national well code, etc.).

**Step 4: Establish aquifer-well relationships.** Populate the AquiferID field in the Well feature class by matching wells to their corresponding Aquifer features. This can be done through spatial overlay (for wells within aquifer outcrop polygons), through lookup tables linking well codes to aquifer codes, or through manual assignment based on screen depth and local knowledge.

**Step 5: Import borehole data.** Load vertical data (lithological logs, completion intervals, hydrostratigraphy) into the BoreholeLog table. Set WellID to match the HydroID of the corresponding Well feature. Calculate absolute elevations (TopElev, BottomElev) from reference elevations and depth values.

**Step 6: Create 3D features.** Generate BorePoint features from stratigraphic picks and BoreLine features from interval data using automated geoprocessing tools. Symbolize by HGUID or Material for visualization in 3D scenes.

**Step 7: Quality assurance.** Check for orphan records (BoreholeLog entries with WellID values that do not match any Well feature), elevation inconsistencies (wells where the computed bottom elevation is above the land surface), and spatial outliers (wells plotted at obviously wrong locations). Review attribute completeness and correct coded value violations.

**Step 8: Visualization and products.** Create map products showing well distributions classified by type, aquifer boundary maps with well overlays, 3D borehole visualizations, and cross-sections through the study area. Export data for use in groundwater modeling software.

This workflow is iterative rather than strictly sequential. New data may become available during a project, requiring reimporting and reclassification. Quality issues discovered during visualization may require corrections in the source data. The geodatabase structure described in this chapter is designed to accommodate this iterative process through its flexible attribute system and well-defined relationships.
