# Chapter 9: Preparing the Digital Landscape

A digital elevation model is, at first glance, a deceptively simple thing: a grid of numbers, each representing the height of the land surface at a single point. From this elementary data structure, the entire machinery of computational hydrology can be set in motion -- flow directions, contributing areas, stream networks, watershed boundaries, and a host of derivative surfaces that underpin everything from flood forecasting to water resource planning. But there is a catch, and it is a significant one. A raw DEM, straight from its data provider, is almost never ready for hydrological analysis. It contains artifacts, depressions, and inconsistencies that break the fundamental assumption on which all terrain-based hydrology rests: that water flows continuously downhill from every point on the landscape to some outlet.

This chapter addresses the essential first step in any DEM-based hydrological workflow: conditioning the elevation data so that it supports continuous, uninterrupted drainage. The process is sometimes dismissed as a mere preprocessing technicality, but that characterization undersells its importance. The decisions made during DEM conditioning -- which depressions to fill, which barriers to carve, whether to burn in an external stream network -- propagate through every subsequent step of the analysis. A poorly conditioned DEM produces incorrect flow directions, which produce incorrect flow accumulations, which produce incorrect stream networks and watershed boundaries. By the time the error reaches a flood forecast or a water balance calculation, it may be unrecoverable.

We begin with the terrain flow analysis framework that provides the conceptual roadmap for the entire Part III sequence (Chapters 9 through 12). We then examine why raw DEMs fail for hydrology, explore the methods available for fixing them, and discuss the specialized software tools -- ArcGIS, TauDEM, and Arc Hydro -- that implement these methods. Throughout, we draw on examples from the United States, Hungary, and the global context to illustrate that DEM conditioning is a universal challenge, encountered wherever digital terrain data meets hydrological analysis.

## 9.1 The Terrain Flow Analysis Framework

Before diving into the details of DEM conditioning, it is worth stepping back to see the full picture. The terrain flow analysis framework is a systematic, sequential processing pipeline that transforms a raw DEM -- a "dumb" grid of elevation values -- into a rich hydrological information model containing streams, sub-watersheds, connectivity relationships, drainage areas, and many other quantities that water professionals need. Understanding this framework is essential because DEM conditioning is not an end in itself; it is the foundation upon which every subsequent step depends.

### 9.1.1 The Processing Pipeline

The framework proceeds through a well-defined sequence of steps, each building on the output of the previous one:

1. **DEM reconditioning** (optional) -- If an independently mapped stream network is available, the DEM can be modified to ensure that computed drainage patterns follow the known stream paths. This step uses techniques like stream burning or the AGREE method, discussed in Section 9.5.

2. **DEM conditioning (pit removal)** -- Remove depressions and artifacts that prevent continuous drainage. This is the core topic of this chapter.

3. **Flow direction computation** -- Determine the direction water flows from each grid cell based on the elevation of its neighbors. This is the subject of Chapter 10.

4. **Flow accumulation** -- Count the number of upstream cells draining through each cell. Cells with high accumulation values lie on streams; cells with low values lie on hillslopes.

5. **Stream definition** -- Apply a threshold to the flow accumulation grid to classify cells as stream or non-stream. The choice of threshold controls the density of the derived stream network.

6. **Stream segmentation** -- Divide the continuous stream raster into individual reaches at junction points. Each reach becomes a distinct segment with a unique identifier.

7. **Catchment grid delineation** -- Identify the contributing area draining to each stream segment. This produces a raster where each cell carries the identifier of the stream segment it drains to.

8. **Raster-to-vector conversion** -- Transform the raster representations of streams and catchments into vector features: polylines for drainage lines, polygons for catchment boundaries, and points for outlet locations.

The end result is a comprehensive hydrological data structure: from a simple grid of elevation values, one obtains a connected stream network with named reaches, a set of nested sub-watersheds with computed areas, a drainage topology that describes which sub-watershed flows into which, and the ability to trace flow paths from any point on the landscape to any downstream point. Watersheds -- the areas of land that drain to a common outlet -- are the most fundamental landscape units in hydrology, and this framework is the standard method for deriving them from digital terrain data.

### 9.1.2 Why This Sequence Matters

The sequential nature of the framework means that errors introduced early propagate through every subsequent step. If a pit is not removed during conditioning, the flow direction algorithm will route water into that pit and stop. The flow accumulation grid will show zero accumulation downstream of the pit, as if no water passes through. The stream network will have a gap. The watershed boundary will be incorrect. And any hydrological model calibrated against this flawed watershed delineation will produce unreliable results.

This chapter focuses on Steps 1 and 2 of the framework -- reconditioning and pit removal. These steps are where the analyst exercises the most judgment, because the decisions are not purely algorithmic. They require understanding the landscape: Is that depression a DEM artifact or a real sinkhole? Should the DEM follow a mapped stream network that might be cartographically generalized, or should it trust its own elevation values? Is the study area in a karst landscape where sinkholes are genuine hydrological features, or in a floodplain where every depression is an artifact of the LiDAR return from standing water?

The subsequent steps (Chapters 10 through 12) are more mechanical -- once the DEM is properly conditioned, the flow direction, accumulation, and watershed delineation algorithms proceed with relatively little human intervention. This makes DEM conditioning the step where domain knowledge matters most.

## 9.2 Why Raw DEMs Fail for Hydrology

A hydrologically conditioned DEM is one in which a non-increasing flow path exists from every grid cell to the domain outlet or edge. In plain terms: water can travel from any cell to the boundary without ever being forced to flow uphill. A raw DEM, as delivered by a data provider, virtually never satisfies this condition. It contains **pits** -- also called sinks or depressions -- that trap flow and prevent it from reaching the outlet.

### 9.2.1 The Anatomy of a Pit

A pit is a cell or connected group of cells that is completely surrounded by higher terrain. Every adjacent cell is at equal or higher elevation, so there is no downslope neighbor to receive flow. In a small DEM grid, a pit might be a single cell with elevation 95 surrounded by eight neighbors all at 100 or above. In a larger landscape, a pit might be a cluster of dozens of cells forming a closed basin -- think of a bowl pressed into the terrain surface, with no notch in the rim through which water could escape.

Two key terms describe the geometry of a pit. The **pit floor** is the lowest cell (or cells) within the depression. The **pour point** is the lowest cell on the rim that encloses the pit -- the point where water would first overflow if the pit were slowly filled like a bathtub. The difference in elevation between the pour point and the pit floor is the **pit depth**, and this quantity is central to understanding how much the DEM must be modified to resolve the pit.

Consider a concrete example. A 5-by-5 section of a DEM might look like this:

**Table 9.1**: A small DEM grid containing a single pit (values are elevations in meters)

| 102 | 101 | 100 | 101 | 103 |
|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|
| 101 |  98 |  97 |  99 | 102 |
| 100 |  96 |  93 |  95 | 101 |
| 101 |  97 |  95 |  98 | 102 |
| 103 | 100 |  99 | 101 | 104 |

The central cell at elevation 93 is the pit floor. Every path outward from this cell must first climb -- to 95, 96, or 97 -- before descending again. The pour point is the lowest cell on the enclosing rim, which in this case is the cell at elevation 99 on the bottom edge. The pit depth is 99 - 93 = 6 meters. To drain this pit by filling, every interior cell below 99 meters would need to be raised to 99.

### 9.2.2 Sources of Pits

Pits arise from two fundamentally different causes, and distinguishing between them is one of the central challenges of DEM conditioning.

**Artifact pits** are the product of errors and limitations in the DEM creation process. Every DEM is derived from some form of measurement -- photogrammetry, radar interferometry, LiDAR scanning, contour interpolation -- and each method introduces its own characteristic errors. Interpolation between sparse elevation points can create shallow artificial depressions where the mathematical surface dips below the true terrain. Radar-based DEMs like SRTM and the Copernicus DEM represent a surface that includes vegetation canopy and building roofs, creating apparent depressions where the radar return drops below the surrounding canopy level. Even high-resolution LiDAR DEMs contain pits caused by noise in the point cloud, classification errors (ground points misidentified as non-ground or vice versa), and the regular sampling that approximates an inherently irregular natural surface.

The scale of artifact pits ranges from trivial to substantial. In a 30-meter SRTM DEM, most artifact pits are shallow -- a few centimeters to a few meters -- and affect one or two cells. In a 1-meter LiDAR DEM, artifact pits are more numerous but generally shallower, often corresponding to features like road surfaces that sit slightly below surrounding curbs or sidewalks. At all resolutions, the vast majority of pits in a raw DEM are artifacts that should be removed.

**Real depressions** are genuine topographic features where the land surface forms a closed basin. These occur naturally in several landscape settings:

- **Karst terrain**, where the dissolution of limestone creates sinkholes and closed depressions. Hungary's Aggtelek Karst, a UNESCO World Heritage Site in the northeast of the country, contains hundreds of dolines (sinkholes) ranging from a few meters to tens of meters deep. The Peter Sinks in Logan Canyon, Utah, is another famous example -- a 62-meter-deep closed depression where record-low temperatures have been observed due to cold-air pooling.

- **Aeolian (wind-formed) landscapes**, where dune fields and deflation hollows create undulating terrain with numerous closed basins. The Texas Panhandle and the Nebraska Sand Hills in the United States, and parts of the Hungarian Great Plain (Alfold) where wind-blown sand forms characteristic landforms, exemplify this type.

- **Glacially scoured terrain**, where kettle holes, moraines, and other glacial deposits create innumerable small lakes and closed basins. Scandinavia, the Canadian Shield, and the Alpine forelands all exhibit this pattern.

- **Tectonic and volcanic landscapes**, where fault scarps, calderas, and lava flows create large-scale closed basins. The Dead Sea basin, the Danakil Depression in Ethiopia, and parts of the East African Rift are dramatic examples.

- **Human-modified landscapes**, where road embankments, levees, dams, and other structures create artificial barriers that the DEM faithfully records. A highway embankment crossing a shallow valley creates a real barrier to surface flow, but for the purposes of watershed delineation, the culvert beneath the road should provide drainage continuity.

The challenge is that the DEM itself provides no label distinguishing artifact pits from real depressions. A 3-meter-deep pit in a karst landscape looks identical in the grid data to a 3-meter-deep artifact in a radar DEM. The analyst must bring external knowledge -- of the landscape type, the DEM source, and the analysis objectives -- to make appropriate conditioning decisions.

### 9.2.3 The Consequences of Unconditioned DEMs

If pits are left in the DEM, the consequences for hydrological analysis are severe. When a flow direction algorithm encounters a pit cell, it has nowhere to route the flow. The cell becomes a terminal sink. All cells whose flow paths lead to this pit become part of an isolated sub-watershed that has no connection to the outlet. The flow accumulation grid will show these isolated sub-watersheds as separate drainage units, disconnected from the main network.

In practice, this means that the computed watershed for a given outlet point will have holes in it -- islands of terrain that the algorithm considers to be internally draining rather than contributing to the outlet. These holes can be small (a single pit cell) or large (a real depression encompassing hundreds of cells). The total drainage area will be underestimated, the stream network will have gaps, and any rainfall-runoff calculation based on the flawed watershed will produce incorrect results.

## 9.3 Pit Filling

Pit filling is the most widely used DEM conditioning method. Its principle is straightforward: raise the elevation of every cell within a depression until the pit drains to its pour point and water can flow continuously out of the former depression. The result is a hydrologically conditioned DEM -- often called a "filled DEM" -- in which every cell has at least one non-increasing flow path to the domain edge.

### 9.3.1 The Filling Algorithm

The procedure works from the lowest pit upward. For each pit in the DEM:

1. Identify the pit floor and all cells that drain internally to it.
2. Identify the pour point -- the lowest cell on the rim of the enclosing depression.
3. Raise all cells within the depression to the elevation of the pour point.

After this operation, the former depression becomes a flat area at the pour point elevation. Water entering this flat area will eventually reach the pour point (by mechanisms discussed in Section 9.6 on flat area resolution) and continue downslope.

Returning to the example from Table 9.1, where the pit floor is at 93 m and the pour point is at 99 m, filling raises the five interior cells (at elevations 93, 95, 95, 96, 97) all to 99 m. The resulting grid has a flat area of 99 m cells where the pit used to be, and water can now pass through to the neighboring cells beyond the rim.

The algorithm handles nested pits -- pits within pits -- by processing from the smallest and shallowest upward. When a small pit is filled, it may merge with a neighboring pit to form a larger depression, which is then filled in turn. The classic implementation by Jenson and Domingue (1988) processes the DEM in a systematic scan that guarantees all pits are resolved. More efficient implementations by Planchon and Darboux (2002) and Wang and Liu (2006) reduce computation time significantly for large DEMs, making pit filling practical even for continental-scale datasets at high resolution.

### 9.3.2 Advantages of Pit Filling

Pit filling has several properties that explain its dominance as the standard conditioning method:

**Simplicity and robustness.** The algorithm is guaranteed to produce a hydrologically conditioned DEM. There are no convergence issues, no parameters to tune (other than an optional maximum depth, discussed below), and no edge cases that cause failure. Given any input DEM, pit filling will always produce a valid output.

**Universal availability.** Pit filling is implemented in every major GIS and hydrological software package. ArcGIS Pro provides it as the Fill tool in the Spatial Analyst Hydrology toolbox. TauDEM provides it as the PitRemove function. QGIS provides it through GRASS (r.fill.dir), SAGA (Fill Sinks), and WhiteboxTools (FillDepressions). There is no mainstream DEM processing software that lacks this capability.

**Minimal parameterization.** In its basic form, pit filling requires no user-specified parameters. The algorithm simply fills all pits to their pour point elevation. Some implementations offer a maximum depth (or z-limit) parameter that restricts filling to pits shallower than a specified threshold, preserving deeper depressions that are likely to be real topographic features. This single parameter provides a useful coarse filter but does not solve the fundamental problem of distinguishing artifacts from real features.

### 9.3.3 Limitations of Pit Filling

Despite its widespread use, pit filling has important limitations that the analyst must understand.

**Alteration of the DEM surface.** Filling raises cell elevations, sometimes substantially. For most of the domain, the changes are negligible -- a fraction of the DEM's vertical accuracy. But for large real depressions, filling can modify the surface dramatically. The Peter Sinks depression in Logan Canyon, Utah, has a depth of approximately 62 meters. Filling this feature raises hundreds of cells by tens of meters, fundamentally changing the topographic representation of the area. Whether this is appropriate depends on the analysis objectives. For watershed delineation, filling is defensible on the grounds that subsurface flow through the karst bedrock eventually reaches the river system, so the depression should be treated as hydrologically connected. For terrain analysis or geomorphological mapping, the alteration is unacceptable.

**Creation of flat areas.** After filling, the former depression becomes a flat area -- a region where all cells have the same elevation. Standard eight-direction flow direction algorithms cannot determine a unique downslope direction on flat terrain, because no neighbor is lower than the cell being processed. This requires a secondary algorithm (flat area resolution, Section 9.6) to assign flow directions across the flat area, introducing additional complexity and potential artifacts.

**Scale dependence.** The number and size of pits in a DEM depend strongly on resolution. A 90-meter SRTM DEM of a given area might contain a few hundred pits, most shallow. The same area at 1-meter LiDAR resolution might contain hundreds of thousands of pits, many of them real micro-topographic features (tire ruts, ditches, plowed furrows) that are irrelevant to watershed-scale hydrology but genuine at the field scale. Filling all of them produces a heavily smoothed surface that may lose important drainage information. The analyst must consider whether the analysis scale justifies removing all depressions or only those above a certain depth or area threshold.

### 9.3.4 Worked Example: Filling a Small DEM

Consider the following 7-by-7 DEM grid representing a hillslope with a shallow depression:

**Table 9.2**: Input DEM with a depression (elevations in meters above datum)

| 120 | 118 | 115 | 113 | 112 | 114 | 116 |
|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|
| 119 | 116 | 112 | 108 | 107 | 110 | 115 |
| 117 | 113 | 107 | 103 | 102 | 106 | 114 |
| 116 | 111 | 105 | **98** | 100 | 104 | 113 |
| 118 | 114 | 108 | 104 | 103 | 107 | 115 |
| 120 | 117 | 112 | 109 | 108 | 111 | 117 |
| 122 | 120 | 116 | 114 | 113 | 115 | 119 |

The cell at elevation 98 (bold) is the pit floor. Tracing outward, the pour point is the cell at elevation 107 on the northwest (row 2, column 3), because this is the lowest point on the rim of the enclosing depression through which water could escape toward the lower elevations in the upper-left corner.

The pit depth is 107 - 98 = 9 meters. All cells within the enclosed depression that are below 107 m -- the cells at 98, 100, 102, 103, 103, 104, 104, 105, 106 -- are raised to 107. After filling, a flat area of 107-meter cells occupies the former depression, and water can now pass from this flat area through the pour point and onward downhill.

The total volume of fill is:

$$V_{fill} = \sum_{i \in pit} (z_{pour} - z_i) \times A_{cell}$$

where $z_{pour} = 107$ m, $z_i$ is the original elevation of cell $i$, and $A_{cell}$ is the cell area. If the cell size is 10 meters (100 m$^2$ per cell), the fill volume for the nine cells below 107 m is:

$$V_{fill} = [(107-98) + (107-100) + (107-102) + (107-103) + (107-103) + (107-104) + (107-104) + (107-105) + (107-106)] \times 100$$

$$V_{fill} = [9 + 7 + 5 + 4 + 4 + 3 + 3 + 2 + 1] \times 100 = 38 \times 100 = 3{,}800 \text{ m}^3$$

This fill volume represents the storage capacity of the depression -- a useful quantity in its own right for surface water storage estimation in agricultural landscapes or stormwater management. The concept of terrain volume below a reference elevation reappears in Chapter 15, where HAND-derived volumes are used to compute reach-averaged hydraulic properties.

## 9.4 Carving (Breaching)

Carving -- also called breaching -- takes the opposite approach to pit filling. Instead of raising the pit floor to the pour point elevation, carving lowers the barrier cells along a drainage path from the pit interior through the rim to the nearest lower cell outside the depression. The result is a channel cut through the barrier, allowing the pit to drain without modifying the interior topography.

### 9.4.1 The Carving Procedure

The basic carving algorithm works as follows:

1. Identify the pit floor.
2. Find the shortest path from the pit floor to the nearest cell outside the depression that has a lower elevation.
3. Lower all cells along this path to create a monotonically decreasing path from the pit floor to the external cell.

In practice, the path is often traced from the pour point outward (since the pour point is already the lowest point on the rim, the path only needs to breach the rim and any cells immediately beyond it). The lowering can be done by setting the carved cells to a linear interpolation between the pit floor elevation and the elevation of the external target cell, ensuring a smooth gradient along the carved channel.

Returning to the grid in Table 9.1, where the pit floor is at 93 m, the pour point is at 99 m, and the cell beyond the pour point (outside the pit) is at, say, 98 m: carving would lower the pour point cell from 99 to some value between 93 and 98 (depending on the interpolation method), creating a narrow channel through which the pit drains. The interior cells at 93, 95, 96, and 97 remain unchanged.

### 9.4.2 When Carving Excels

Carving is particularly effective in situations where the barrier causing the pit is narrow and the depression behind it is real or at least topographically significant. Three common scenarios favor carving over filling:

**Road embankments in LiDAR DEMs.** High-resolution LiDAR DEMs faithfully capture the elevation of road surfaces, including embankments that cross valleys. A two-lane road with a 1-meter-high embankment can create a pit dozens of cells wide in a 1-meter DEM. The real landscape has a culvert beneath the road that provides drainage continuity, but the DEM shows only the road surface. Filling this pit would raise the entire upstream valley floor by a meter; carving simply cuts a narrow channel through the road embankment, mimicking the culvert.

**Levees and dikes along rivers.** Flood protection levees create long, continuous barriers in DEMs. The protected floodplain behind the levee often sits several meters below the levee crest, creating a linear pit that can extend for kilometers. Filling this pit would raise the entire floodplain to the levee crest elevation, which is absurd. Carving through the levee at appropriate drainage points is far more realistic.

**Railway embankments and bridge abutments.** Similar to road embankments, railway lines often run on raised embankments, and bridge abutments create apparent dams across river channels. Carving removes these artificial barriers while preserving the surrounding topography.

### 9.4.3 Limitations of Carving

Carving works best for narrow barriers. When the barrier is broad -- a wide ridge rather than a narrow embankment -- the carved channel may need to extend over many cells, creating an artificial valley that distorts the terrain more than filling would have. In the extreme case, carving through a mountain ridge to drain a real interior basin would produce a geologically absurd channel that never existed in nature.

Carving is also less widely implemented than filling in standard GIS software. ArcGIS Pro's Spatial Analyst does not include a dedicated carving tool (conditioning relies on the Fill tool). TauDEM likewise implements filling but not carving. Carving algorithms are available in WhiteboxTools (BreachDepressions, BreachDepressionsLeastCost) and in specialized hydrology packages, but their integration into mainstream workflows is less seamless.

## 9.5 Optimal Pit Removal

Soille (2004) proposed a method that seeks to minimize the total alteration to the DEM by combining filling and carving in an optimal way. Rather than committing entirely to one approach or the other, the algorithm finds the combination of cell elevation increases (partial filling) and decreases (partial carving) that produces a hydrologically conditioned DEM with the least total absolute change.

### 9.5.1 The Optimization Concept

The problem can be formulated as follows. Let $z_i$ be the original elevation of cell $i$ and $z_i'$ be the conditioned elevation. The conditioning must ensure that every cell has a non-increasing flow path to the domain boundary. Subject to this constraint, the algorithm minimizes:

$$\sum_{i} |z_i' - z_i|$$

This is the total absolute elevation change across all cells in the domain. The filled DEM and the carved DEM represent two extremes of the solution space. The purely filled DEM minimizes changes to the area outside the pit (zero changes) but maximizes changes inside the pit. The purely carved DEM minimizes changes inside the pit but may require extensive changes along the carved path. The optimal solution lies between these extremes, distributing the changes across both the pit interior and the barrier in a way that minimizes the total.

For a simple pit with a narrow barrier, the optimal solution is close to pure carving (lowering a few barrier cells is cheaper than raising many interior cells). For a small, shallow pit surrounded by broad terrain, the optimal solution is close to pure filling (raising one or two cells is cheaper than carving a long path). For intermediate cases, the solution involves partial filling and partial carving -- the pit floor rises somewhat, and the barrier is lowered somewhat, meeting in the middle.

### 9.5.2 Practical Status

Despite its theoretical elegance, optimal pit removal has not been widely adopted in operational practice. The algorithm is computationally more expensive than simple filling, particularly for large DEMs with many nested pits. More importantly, the practical improvement over filling is often modest -- for the small, shallow artifact pits that dominate most DEMs, filling alters the surface by fractions of a meter, and the "optimal" solution would differ by only millimeters. The method's benefits are most apparent for large real depressions where filling causes substantial surface modification, but these are precisely the features where the analyst often wants to make a deliberate, case-by-case decision rather than relying on an automated optimization.

In 2026, optimal pit removal remains available in a few specialized tools (notably Lindsay's WhiteboxTools, which implements several variants) but is not part of the standard toolbox in ArcGIS Pro, TauDEM, or QGIS's core processing algorithms. For most practitioners, the choice remains between filling and carving, with filling as the default.

## 9.6 Stream Burning and the AGREE Method

The conditioning methods discussed so far -- filling, carving, and optimal removal -- address pits within the DEM. But there is a second category of DEM conditioning that addresses a different problem: ensuring that the DEM-derived drainage patterns agree with independently mapped stream networks. This process is called **DEM reconditioning**, and it is applied before pit removal as an optional preliminary step.

### 9.6.1 Why DEM Reconditioning Is Needed

The stream network derived from a DEM by following flow directions may not match the stream network mapped by traditional survey or extracted from aerial photography. There are several reasons for this discrepancy:

**DEM resolution limitations.** A 30-meter DEM cannot resolve stream channels that are only a few meters wide. The terrain surface represented in the DEM may slope gently in a direction that differs from the actual channel location by hundreds of meters, particularly in flat terrain where elevation differences are comparable to the DEM's vertical accuracy.

**Radar artifacts.** DEMs derived from radar interferometry (SRTM, Copernicus DEM) represent the surface seen by the radar beam, which includes vegetation canopy and building roofs. In forested river valleys, the apparent elevation of the forested hillslope may be lower than the apparent elevation of the canopy above the stream channel, causing the DEM to route flow away from the actual channel.

**Flat terrain ambiguity.** In areas with very low relief -- large floodplains, deltas, coastal marshes -- the DEM may lack sufficient vertical precision to resolve the subtle topographic gradients that guide stream flow. The Tisza River floodplain in Hungary, for example, has slopes of less than 0.01% over vast areas, which is well below the accuracy of even high-quality DEMs.

**Anthropogenic modification.** Urban areas, agricultural drainage networks, and flood control structures modify drainage patterns in ways that the DEM surface alone cannot reveal. A stream that has been diverted through a concrete channel may flow in a direction that the natural topography does not support.

When an independently mapped stream network is available -- such as the National Hydrography Dataset (NHD) in the United States, the EU-Hydro dataset in Europe, or the HydroSHEDS global dataset -- DEM reconditioning forces the DEM-derived drainage to follow these known stream paths.

### 9.6.2 Simple Stream Burning

Stream burning is the simplest reconditioning approach. The procedure artificially modifies the DEM to create deep channels along the mapped stream network, ensuring that flow direction algorithms will route water along these channels.

The implementation is straightforward:

1. Convert the vector stream network to a raster grid at the same resolution as the DEM. Cells that intersect a mapped stream receive a value of 1; all other cells receive a value of 0.

2. Apply a raster calculation to modify the DEM:

```
DEM_burned = DEM + increment * (1 - stream_raster)
```

This formula adds a fixed elevation increment (typically 10 to 50 meters) to every cell that is NOT on a mapped stream. Stream cells retain their original elevation. The effect is to raise the entire landscape relative to the streams, creating artificial canyons along the mapped stream paths.

Alternatively, the same effect can be achieved by lowering stream cells:

```
DEM_burned = DEM - drop * stream_raster
```

Both approaches produce the same result: the stream cells sit in deep trenches that flow direction algorithms will follow reliably.

**Advantages of stream burning.** The method is simple, easy to implement (a single raster calculation in any GIS), and effective at forcing drainage alignment.

**Limitations of stream burning.** The approach creates abrupt elevation discontinuities at stream banks -- vertical cliffs where the terrain drops from the raised non-stream surface to the original stream-cell elevation. These artificial cliffs produce unrealistic slope values, incorrect hillslope flow patterns near the stream, and artifacts in any subsequent terrain analysis (slope, curvature, wetness index) that uses the conditioned DEM. The conditioned DEM is suitable only for flow direction and accumulation computation; it should not be used for other terrain analyses.

### 9.6.3 The AGREE Method

The AGREE method (AGREE Elevation Grid Modification Methodology) provides a more sophisticated alternative to simple stream burning. Developed by Hellweger (1997) as a student in David Maidment's research group at the University of Texas at Austin, AGREE addresses the abrupt-cliff problem by applying a smooth, distance-dependent taper from the surrounding terrain to the lowered stream elevation.

The method uses three inputs: the DEM, a rasterized stream network, and the Euclidean distance from each cell to the nearest stream cell. The conditioning is applied in two zones:

**Within the buffer zone** (typically 500 meters from the nearest stream), the DEM is progressively lowered as the distance to the stream decreases. The lowering increases linearly from zero at the buffer edge to the full drop amount at the stream bank. This creates a smooth, V-shaped depression along each stream segment.

**At the stream cells themselves**, the DEM is lowered by a fixed amount (typically 10 meters) below the original stream-cell elevation, ensuring that the stream sits at the bottom of the V-shaped depression.

The conditioning formula can be expressed as:

$$z_{conditioned} = z_{original} - d_{stream} \cdot s_{raster} - \frac{d_{stream} \cdot (r - dist)}{r} \cdot (dist < r) \cdot (1 - s_{raster})$$

where $z_{original}$ is the original DEM elevation, $d_{stream}$ is the drop applied to stream cells (e.g., 10 m), $s_{raster}$ is the stream raster (1 on stream, 0 elsewhere), $dist$ is the Euclidean distance to the nearest stream cell, and $r$ is the buffer radius (e.g., 500 m).

In practice, this is implemented via raster algebra in ArcGIS Raster Calculator or equivalent tools:

```
DEM_agree = DEM - 10 * stream_raster
            - 0.02 * (500 - distance) * (distance < 500) * (1 - stream_raster)
```

where the term `0.02 * (500 - distance)` produces a linear taper from 0 meters of lowering at 500 m distance to 10 meters of lowering right at the stream bank (since 0.02 * 500 = 10). The logical expression `(distance < 500)` evaluates to 1 within the buffer and 0 outside it, confining the taper to the near-stream zone.

### 9.6.4 Deciding Whether to Recondition

The decision to apply stream burning or the AGREE method depends on the relative quality and resolution of the DEM and the stream data. Three common scenarios illustrate the decision logic:

**High-quality DEM, uncertain stream data.** If the DEM has fine resolution and good vertical accuracy (e.g., a 1-meter LiDAR DEM), and the stream data is cartographically generalized or positionally inaccurate (e.g., a 1:100,000 stream map), reconditioning would force the DEM to follow an inaccurate stream path. In this case, the DEM-derived drainage should be trusted, and reconditioning should not be applied.

**Moderate DEM, high-quality stream data.** If the DEM has coarse resolution or significant artifacts (e.g., a 30-meter radar DEM in flat terrain), and the stream data is derived from detailed survey or high-resolution imagery, reconditioning ensures that the computed drainage follows the known channels. This is the most common scenario for applying the AGREE method.

**High-resolution LiDAR with infrastructure artifacts.** A LiDAR DEM may capture the bridge deck over a stream channel, making the stream appear to be blocked by a dam at each bridge crossing. In this case, targeted stream burning along the stream centerline beneath bridges is appropriate, while the rest of the DEM is left unmodified. This selective approach requires manual identification of problem locations or an automated bridge detection algorithm.

## 9.7 Examining Conditioning Effects

Good practice demands that the analyst examine the impact of conditioning on the DEM. Blindly applying pit filling or stream burning and proceeding to the next step without inspection is a recipe for undetected errors. The most informative diagnostic is the **fill-minus-DEM** difference surface.

### 9.7.1 The Fill-Minus-DEM Diagnostic

Computing the difference between the conditioned DEM and the original DEM produces a raster that reveals exactly where and by how much the conditioning modified the surface:

```
difference = DEM_filled - DEM_original
```

For a purely filled DEM, this difference is everywhere non-negative (filling only raises elevations). The resulting raster has three characteristic zones:

**Zero values** dominate the landscape. The vast majority of cells are unaffected by filling because they are not located in depressions. These cells appear as a uniform background in the difference map.

**Small positive values** (typically less than 1 meter, often just centimeters) indicate shallow artifact pits that have been filled. These are scattered across the landscape, often concentrated along stream paths where interpolation artifacts are most common.

**Large positive values** (meters to tens of meters) indicate deep depressions that have been filled. These may be real topographic features (karst sinkholes, kettle lakes, deflation hollows) or major DEM artifacts. Each large-value cluster deserves individual inspection to determine whether filling was appropriate.

The fill-minus-DEM raster is essentially a map of depression depth across the landscape. Displaying it with a stretched color ramp immediately reveals the locations and magnitudes of all depressions that were removed. This map should be examined carefully, particularly in landscapes known to contain real closed basins.

### 9.7.2 Case Study: Logan River Watershed, Utah

In the Logan River watershed in northern Utah, pit filling applied to the USGS 1/3 arc-second DEM raises most cells by less than 0.1 meter -- well within the DEM's stated accuracy of approximately 2.34 meters RMSE. The fill-minus-DEM surface is almost entirely zero. But one feature stands out dramatically: Peter Sinks, a closed karst depression in the Bear River Range east of Logan. This feature, approximately 600 meters across and 62 meters deep, is the deepest single-cell or multi-cell pit in the watershed. Filling raises its floor by 62 meters -- from approximately 2,446 meters to 2,508 meters -- creating a large flat area in the conditioned DEM.

Peter Sinks is a legitimate closed depression formed by karst dissolution of Paleozoic limestone. It is famous among meteorologists for record-low temperatures (the second-coldest recorded temperature in the contiguous United States, -56.3 degrees Celsius in 1985, was measured here), caused by cold air pooling in the enclosed basin. From a hydrological perspective, the depression is internally draining: precipitation falling into Peter Sinks infiltrates through the karst bedrock rather than flowing overland to the Bear River. Whether to fill this feature depends on the analysis objectives. For surface watershed delineation, filling ensures topographic continuity and accounts for the assumption that subsurface karst drainage eventually reaches the river system. For a surface runoff model, filling is inappropriate because surface water genuinely does not leave the basin.

### 9.7.3 Hungarian Example: The Aggtelek Karst

Hungary's most dramatic example of the fill-versus-reality dilemma is the Aggtelek Karst in the northern part of the country, along the border with Slovakia. This UNESCO World Heritage Site (shared with Slovakia's Slovak Karst as the "Caves of Aggtelek Karst and Slovak Karst") contains one of the most extensive karst systems in Central Europe, with over 700 documented caves including the 25-kilometer Baradla-Domica cave system.

The surface above the cave system is pockmarked with dolines -- circular or elliptical sinkholes formed by dissolution of the underlying Triassic limestone. These dolines range from a few meters to over 50 meters in diameter and from less than a meter to more than 15 meters deep. A LiDAR survey of the Aggtelek area (available through the Lechner Knowledge Centre at 2 points per square meter) reveals hundreds of individual dolines, each of which appears as a pit in the DEM.

Applying pit filling to this landscape raises the following questions:

**Should the dolines be filled?** For watershed-scale delineation of the Bodva or Sajo river basins that receive the karst spring discharge, yes -- filling ensures drainage continuity and correctly attributes the karst area to the downstream watershed. But for local-scale analysis of the karst drainage system itself, filling destroys the most important topographic features in the landscape.

**How deep are the fills?** Most dolines are relatively shallow (1-5 meters), but some are significantly deeper. The fill-minus-DEM diagnostic reveals these features as clusters of positive values against a zero background, providing a ready-made inventory of karst depressions.

**What about the relationship between surface and subsurface drainage?** The Aggtelek Karst illustrates a fundamental limitation of DEM-based hydrology: the implicit assumption that water flows on the surface. In karst terrain, a significant fraction (often the majority) of precipitation reaches the river system via subsurface conduit flow that may follow entirely different pathways from the surface topography. No amount of DEM conditioning can resolve this issue; it requires explicit karst hydrological modeling that is beyond the scope of terrain analysis.

The practical recommendation for the Aggtelek Karst, and for karst landscapes generally, is to fill all depressions for the purpose of watershed delineation (accepting the simplification that subsurface drainage roughly follows the topographic watershed) but to document the karst features and their potential influence on actual flow routing. The fill-minus-DEM diagnostic provides the documentation -- it maps every depression that was removed, with its depth, allowing subsequent analyses to account for the known karst storage.

## 9.8 Flat Area Resolution

Pit filling solves the problem of depressions but creates a new one: flat areas. After filling, every former depression becomes a zone of cells at identical elevation -- the pour point elevation. On this flat terrain, the standard eight-direction (D8) flow direction algorithm cannot determine a unique downslope direction, because no neighbor is lower than the cell being processed. If left unresolved, these flat areas produce artifacts in the flow accumulation grid: parallel flow paths, discontinuous streams, and incorrect drainage patterns.

### 9.8.1 The Flat Area Problem

Consider a 4-by-4 flat area, all cells at elevation 100, with a single pour point at one corner that leads to a cell at elevation 99. The D8 algorithm assigns flow direction based on the steepest descent to one of eight neighbors. For cells in the interior of the flat area, every neighbor has the same elevation (100), so there is no steepest descent. The algorithm either assigns an arbitrary direction or flags the cell as undefined.

Without flat area resolution, the flow accumulation through this region will be incorrect. Some cells may receive no upstream drainage at all (if the flow direction algorithm assigned them a direction that loops back to another flat cell), while others may receive artificially concentrated drainage (if the algorithm arbitrarily routed multiple cells' flow to the same neighbor).

### 9.8.2 Resolution Strategies

Several algorithms have been developed to assign consistent flow directions across flat areas. The most widely used approach, implemented in TauDEM and described by Garbrecht and Martz (1997), combines two gradients:

**Gradient toward lower terrain.** Cells closer to the pour point (the outlet of the flat area) receive a slight imposed slope toward the pour point. This ensures that water generally flows in the correct direction -- toward the exit.

**Gradient away from higher terrain.** Cells closer to the edges of the flat area that border on higher terrain receive a slight imposed slope away from those edges. This prevents water from pooling against the edges of the flat area and encourages it to flow toward the interior.

The combination of these two gradients produces a flow field across the flat area that mimics natural drainage: water flows away from the surrounding high ground and toward the outlet. The imposed gradients are infinitesimally small -- they do not change the actual elevation values, only the flow direction assignments -- so the conditioned DEM is not further modified.

TauDEM's implementation is particularly robust because it handles the common case of flat areas that border the domain edge (where external elevations are unknown) and flat areas with multiple outlets (where water should be distributed among several pour points). The algorithm ensures that every cell on a flat area receives a valid flow direction, and that the resulting flow paths converge smoothly toward the outlet(s) without creating artificial parallel channels.

### 9.8.3 Implications for Workflow

Flat area resolution is handled automatically by most modern DEM processing software. In TauDEM, the PitRemove function fills pits, and the subsequent D8FlowDir function includes flat area resolution. In ArcGIS Pro, the Fill tool fills pits, and the Flow Direction tool resolves flat areas. The analyst does not need to invoke flat area resolution as a separate step, but understanding the process is important for interpreting the results, particularly when large flat areas produce drainage patterns that look different from what the natural terrain would suggest.

## 9.9 Edge Contamination

When a DEM is clipped to a study area boundary and flow accumulation is computed, cells near the domain edge may have incorrect contributing area values. This phenomenon, known as **edge contamination**, arises because the true contributing area of a near-boundary cell may extend beyond the DEM extent into terrain that is not represented in the data.

### 9.9.1 How Edge Contamination Arises

Consider a cell near the southern edge of a DEM. This cell's true contributing area extends to the north, following the flow paths that converge on it. If the watershed boundary is far to the north, well within the DEM, the entire contributing area is captured and the flow accumulation is correct. But if the flow path enters the DEM from the south -- crossing the southern boundary -- the contributing area outside the DEM is unknown. The flow accumulation algorithm can count only the cells within the DEM, underestimating the true value.

The problem propagates inward along flow paths. If cell A is near the boundary and has unknown upstream area, then every cell downstream of A also has uncertain accumulation, because A's contribution is unknown. This chain of uncertainty can extend far into the domain interior along major flow paths that enter the domain from outside.

TauDEM handles edge contamination by explicitly marking affected cells as **no data**. When the algorithm encounters a boundary cell whose flow direction would originate from outside the domain, it marks that cell's accumulation as no data. Any downstream cell that receives flow from a no-data cell is also marked as no data. The result is streaks of no-data values extending inward from the domain boundary along contaminated flow paths.

### 9.9.2 Preventing Edge Contamination

The standard prevention strategy is to buffer the study area boundary before clipping the DEM. Instead of clipping the DEM exactly to the watershed boundary, the analyst adds a buffer of 1 to 5 kilometers (depending on the terrain and DEM resolution) around the watershed, processes the entire buffered DEM, and then clips the results to the original watershed boundary after flow accumulation is complete.

This ensures that all flow paths within the watershed originate from cells that are well inside the DEM domain, where the full upstream contributing area is captured. The buffer cells serve as a protective margin that absorbs the edge contamination effects.

**Practical recommendation:** Always process a DEM that extends at least one ridge-line beyond the watershed boundary on all sides. For small watersheds (less than 100 km$^2$), a 1-kilometer buffer is usually sufficient. For large basins where major tributaries may originate outside the immediate study area, a larger buffer or the processing of the entire containing basin may be necessary.

## 9.10 Software for DEM Conditioning

DEM conditioning and the broader terrain flow analysis framework are implemented in several software packages. This section describes the three most important tools for hydrological terrain analysis: ArcGIS Pro with its Spatial Analyst extension, TauDEM, and Arc Hydro. Because this chapter serves as the canonical description of these tools for the book, the discussion here is more detailed than in subsequent chapters, which will reference back to this section.

### 9.10.1 ArcGIS Pro and the Hydrology Toolbox

ArcGIS Pro, ESRI's current-generation desktop GIS platform, provides hydrological terrain analysis through the **Hydrology toolset** within the **Spatial Analyst** extension. The toolset contains the core functions for the terrain flow analysis framework:

- **Fill** -- Fills pits in a DEM. Accepts an optional z-limit parameter to restrict filling to pits shallower than a specified depth.
- **Flow Direction** -- Computes the D8 (eight-direction) flow direction grid, including flat area resolution.
- **Flow Accumulation** -- Computes the number of upstream cells draining through each cell.
- **Stream Link** -- Assigns unique identifiers to stream segments between junctions.
- **Stream Order** -- Computes Strahler or Shreve stream order.
- **Watershed** -- Delineates the contributing area above a specified pour point.
- **Basin** -- Delineates all drainage basins in the domain.
- **Snap Pour Point** -- Adjusts pour point locations to the nearest cell of high flow accumulation, correcting for spatial imprecision in outlet placement.

The toolset is well integrated into the ArcGIS Pro environment, with tools accessible through the Geoprocessing pane, the Python window (via ArcPy), and ModelBuilder for workflow automation. Documentation is available at:

```
https://pro.arcgis.com/en/pro-app/tool-reference/spatial-analyst/an-overview-of-the-hydrology-tools.htm
```

**Strengths of the ArcGIS approach.** The tools are mature, well-documented, and accessible through an intuitive graphical interface. For analysts already working in the ArcGIS ecosystem, the hydrology toolset integrates seamlessly with other Spatial Analyst functions (slope, aspect, reclassify, zonal statistics) and with the vector analysis tools needed for downstream processing.

**Limitations.** ArcGIS Spatial Analyst supports only the D8 flow direction model and only the filling approach for pit removal (no built-in carving or optimal removal). The tools operate on the local machine and cannot leverage parallel processing for large datasets. Stream burning and the AGREE method are not built-in tools but can be implemented through the Raster Calculator. ArcGIS Pro requires a commercial license with the Spatial Analyst extension.

ArcGIS Pro also offers **Ready-to-Use tools** accessible through ArcGIS Portal, which operate on pre-processed global DEMs in the cloud. The Watershed and Trace Downstream tools can perform quick preliminary watershed delineation and downstream tracing anywhere in the world using SRTM data, without the analyst needing to download and process a DEM locally. This cloud-based approach represents the software-as-a-service model that is increasingly common in GIS, and it is useful for rapid reconnaissance work, but it does not offer the control over conditioning parameters that local processing provides.

### 9.10.2 TauDEM: Terrain Analysis Using Digital Elevation Models

TauDEM (Terrain Analysis Using Digital Elevation Models) is an open-source software package developed by David Tarboton and his research group at Utah State University. It is the most technically advanced freely available tool for DEM-based hydrological analysis, and it serves as the computational engine behind much of the research and operational work in terrain-based hydrology worldwide.

**Architecture.** TauDEM is built as a set of C++ command-line executables, each performing a single function in the terrain analysis pipeline. The executables communicate through standard geospatial raster files (GeoTIFF format via the GDAL library), with a systematic naming convention for output files: `fel` for filled elevation, `p` for D8 flow direction, `ad8` for D8 flow accumulation, `src` for stream raster, `ord` for stream order, `w` for watershed grid, and so on. This modular architecture makes TauDEM easy to integrate into scripted workflows, automated pipelines, and web-based processing services.

**Parallel processing.** TauDEM's most distinctive technical feature is its MPI (Message Passing Interface) parallel implementation. The DEM is divided into horizontal stripes, each assigned to a separate processor core. The stripes communicate at their shared boundaries to ensure consistent results. On a modern multi-core workstation, this parallelization can reduce processing times by an order of magnitude for large DEMs. On a high-performance computing cluster, TauDEM can process continental-scale DEMs that would be infeasible on a single machine. The parallel implementation is particularly valuable for the pit-filling and flow-direction steps, which are the most computationally demanding parts of the pipeline.

**D-infinity flow model.** In addition to the standard D8 (eight-direction) flow model used by most software, TauDEM implements the D-infinity (Dinf) flow model developed by Tarboton (1997). Where D8 routes all flow from each cell to a single downslope neighbor, Dinf distributes flow among two neighbors based on the aspect angle, allowing flow to follow continuous paths that are not constrained to the eight cardinal and diagonal directions. This produces smoother flow accumulation surfaces and more realistic contributing area estimates, particularly on divergent hillslopes. TauDEM provides parallel versions of all Dinf functions.

**Key functions for DEM conditioning.** TauDEM provides the following functions relevant to this chapter:

- **PitRemove** -- Fills pits in the DEM. Handles geographic-coordinate DEMs (latitude/longitude) directly, without requiring projection to a metric coordinate system.
- **D8FlowDir** -- Computes D8 flow directions with built-in flat area resolution using the Garbrecht and Martz algorithm.
- **DinfFlowDir** -- Computes D-infinity flow directions.
- **AreaD8** and **AreaDinf** -- Compute contributing area using D8 and Dinf models, respectively.
- **StreamReach** and associated functions for stream network extraction and watershed delineation.

**Integration with GIS.** TauDEM is deployed as an ArcGIS Toolbox, with Python scripts that translate between the ArcGIS graphical interface and the underlying command-line executables. This allows ArcGIS Pro users to access TauDEM functions through the familiar Geoprocessing pane, with parameter entry forms and progress dialogs. TauDEM also integrates with QGIS through the Processing framework (as an external provider) and is available through several cloud-based platforms including the Community Surface Dynamics Modeling System (CSDMS), OpenTopography, and CyberGIS.

**Availability and documentation.** TauDEM is freely available under an open-source license from:

```
https://hydrology.usu.edu/taudem/
```

The website provides executable installers for Windows, source code for Linux/macOS compilation, documentation, and tutorial datasets. The software reads any raster format supported by GDAL, making it compatible with virtually all DEM data sources.

### 9.10.3 Arc Hydro

Arc Hydro is a data model and toolset for water resources applications within the ArcGIS platform, developed under the leadership of David Maidment at the University of Texas at Austin. While ArcGIS Spatial Analyst provides the basic hydrology tools and TauDEM provides the computational engine, Arc Hydro provides the organizational framework and advanced conditioning tools that tie the terrain analysis pipeline into a comprehensive water resources information system.

**The Arc Hydro data model.** At its core, Arc Hydro defines a standardized geodatabase schema for storing hydrological features and their relationships. The schema includes feature classes for hydro junctions, hydro edges (stream segments), catchments, monitoring points, and time series data, along with relationship classes that encode the connectivity and drainage topology. This data model was first described in the book *Arc Hydro: GIS for Water Resources* (Maidment, 2002) and has been continuously updated since.

The data model is significant because it provides a common language for hydrological GIS work. When an Arc Hydro geodatabase says that catchment 42 drains through stream segment 42 to junction 15, which is the outlet of stream segment 15 connecting to junction 7, that connectivity information is stored explicitly in the database and can be queried, navigated, and used by any tool that understands the schema. This is far more powerful than a collection of disconnected shapefiles and rasters.

**Arc Hydro tools for DEM conditioning.** Beyond the basic Fill tool available in Spatial Analyst, Arc Hydro provides several advanced conditioning tools:

- **DEM Reconditioning (AGREE)** -- A built-in implementation of the AGREE method, allowing the analyst to specify the stream network, buffer distance, and drop depth through a dialog rather than constructing the raster algebra expression manually.
- **Fill Sinks** -- An enhanced fill tool with options for incremental filling (imposing a slight gradient across filled flat areas rather than creating perfectly flat surfaces).
- **Level DEM** -- Tools for adjusting the DEM to match known water surface elevations at gauge locations.
- **Build Walls** -- A tool that raises DEM cells along a specified polyline (e.g., a watershed boundary) to enforce drainage divides, useful when the DEM does not clearly define the divide.

**Arc Hydro as a workflow framework.** Arc Hydro organizes the entire terrain analysis pipeline into a structured workflow accessible through a custom toolbar in ArcGIS Pro. The workflow guides the analyst through the steps -- reconditioning, pit filling, flow direction, flow accumulation, stream definition, catchment delineation, and vector conversion -- with each step's output automatically feeding into the next. The results are stored in a standardized Arc Hydro geodatabase, ready for subsequent analysis.

Arc Hydro is the largest toolset available for hydrological analysis within the commercial GIS framework. It extends the capabilities of ArcGIS Spatial Analyst with hundreds of additional tools covering not only terrain analysis but also network analysis, time series management, floodplain mapping, and groundwater modeling (the latter through the Arc Hydro Groundwater extension, discussed in Part V of this book).

**Availability.** Arc Hydro is distributed as a free add-in for ArcGIS Pro, available from the ESRI community:

```
https://resources.arcgis.com/en/communities/hydro/
```

It requires ArcGIS Pro with the Spatial Analyst extension. While the software itself is free, the underlying ArcGIS platform is commercial.

### 9.10.4 QGIS and Open-Source Alternatives

For analysts working outside the ESRI ecosystem, QGIS provides access to hydrological terrain analysis through several processing backends:

**GRASS GIS** (r.fill.dir, r.watershed, r.stream.extract) provides a comprehensive set of hydrological tools with robust algorithms, including the efficient Least Cost Path method for watershed delineation that does not require explicit pit filling.

**SAGA GIS** (Fill Sinks, Flow Direction, Flow Accumulation, Channel Network) offers an alternative set of algorithms accessible through the QGIS Processing framework.

## 9.11 International DEM Sources

DEM conditioning is a universal requirement, but the input DEM varies by region. The choice of DEM affects the number and severity of pits encountered, the resolution of the derived stream network, and the suitability of the results for different applications. This section provides a reference guide to the major DEM sources available internationally, complementing the US-focused discussion in the lecture notes.

### 9.11.1 US DEM Sources

The United States has the most comprehensive national elevation data program in the world. The **3D Elevation Program (3DEP)**, managed by the USGS, is systematically acquiring high-resolution elevation data for the entire country. The current national product is the **National Elevation Dataset (NED)**, available at 1/3 arc-second (approximately 10 meter) and 1 arc-second (approximately 30 meter) resolutions. The stated vertical accuracy of NED is approximately 2.34 meters RMSE relative to survey benchmarks. 3DEP is progressively replacing NED with LiDAR-derived 1-meter DEMs, with a target of national coverage by the late 2020s. Data is freely available through the USGS National Map Downloader and the OpenTopography portal.

### 9.11.2 Hungarian and European DEM Sources

Hungary's national DEM is the **DDM-5** (Digitalis Domborzatmodell, Digital Terrain Model), produced and maintained by the Lechner Knowledge Centre (formerly FOMI). DDM-5 provides 5-meter grid spacing in the EOV projection (EPSG:23700), derived from 1:10,000 topographic contour lines and stereophotogrammetry. It has been updated periodically (2000, 2005, 2015, 2022) to reflect major terrain changes such as highway construction and mining. DDM-5 is the highest-resolution national DEM available for Hungary, but it is not freely downloadable -- it must be ordered through the Lechner data services or the geoshop.hu portal.

For freely available alternatives, the **Copernicus DEM** from the European Space Agency provides the best option. Three products are available:

- **EEA-10**: 10-meter resolution, covering 39 European countries including Hungary. Access is restricted to qualified users.
- **GLO-30**: 30-meter resolution, global coverage, freely available to all users through the Copernicus Data Space Ecosystem.
- **GLO-90**: 90-meter resolution, global coverage, freely available.

The Copernicus DEM is derived from the TanDEM-X radar mission (2011-2015) and has a stated vertical accuracy of better than 4 meters (90% confidence) globally, with significantly better accuracy in Europe. For hydrological conditioning, the Copernicus GLO-30 is an excellent choice for Hungarian watersheds: it provides sufficient resolution for catchment-scale analysis, its vertical accuracy is comparable to or better than SRTM, and it is freely and easily accessible.

**Hungarian LiDAR data** exists for specific areas of particular interest -- the Aggtelek Karst, selected flood-prone valleys, and highway corridors -- but there is no comprehensive nationwide LiDAR program comparable to the US 3DEP. LiDAR coverage is fragmented and must be obtained through the Lechner Knowledge Centre or the relevant water directorate for the area of interest.

Developed by Dai Yamazaki at the University of Tokyo, MERIT removes multiple error components from SRTM and AW3D30 -- including stripe noise, absolute bias, speckle noise, and tree height bias -- producing a DEM that is significantly more accurate for hydrological analysis than either parent product. At 90-meter resolution, MERIT is coarser than the 30-meter products, but its reduced error content means that it requires less aggressive conditioning and produces more reliable flow directions, particularly in flat terrain.

### 9.11.4 Choosing a DEM for Hydrological Analysis

The choice of DEM involves trade-offs among resolution, accuracy, coverage, and accessibility. Higher resolution is not always better -- a 1-meter LiDAR DEM contains far more pits and artifacts than a 30-meter product, requires correspondingly more conditioning, and may resolve features (individual buildings, parked vehicles, vegetation) that are irrelevant to catchment-scale hydrology. The following guidelines help frame the decision:

**For watershed delineation at the catchment scale** (drainage areas above 10 km$^2$): 30-meter DEMs (Copernicus GLO-30, SRTM, NED) provide an excellent balance of resolution and manageability. Conditioning is straightforward and the derived stream networks match mapped streams well in moderate-to-steep terrain.

**For detailed local analysis** (drainage areas below 10 km$^2$, urban hydrology, floodplain mapping): High-resolution DEMs (LiDAR 1-5 m, DDM-5 at 5 m) are needed to resolve small channels, road drainage, and microtopography. Conditioning is more complex and may require carving, selective stream burning, and careful treatment of infrastructure features.

**For continental or global studies**: 90-meter products (Copernicus GLO-90, MERIT DEM, TanDEM-X 90m) are practical and sufficient. At this scale, the conditioning challenge is modest and the analysis can cover vast areas on a single workstation.

## 9.12 Cell Size, Projections, and Coordinate Considerations

Before conditioning a DEM, the analyst must address several practical considerations related to cell size, coordinate systems, and data preparation. These details may seem mundane, but errors at this stage propagate through the entire analysis.

### 9.12.1 Geographic vs. Projected Coordinates

DEMs delivered in geographic coordinates (latitude/longitude) have cell sizes expressed in decimal degrees rather than meters. A cell size of approximately 9.259 x 10$^{-5}$ degrees at 42 degrees north latitude corresponds to:

- **North-south dimension**: $R \times \Delta\phi = 6{,}370 \text{ km} \times 9.259 \times 10^{-5} \times \frac{\pi}{180} \approx 10.3 \text{ m}$

- **East-west dimension**: $R \times \cos(\phi) \times \Delta\lambda = 6{,}370 \text{ km} \times \cos(42°) \times 9.259 \times 10^{-5} \times \frac{\pi}{180} \approx 7.7 \text{ m}$

The cells are not square -- they are approximately 10.3 m in the north-south direction and 7.7 m in the east-west direction at this latitude. This non-square geometry must be accounted for when computing slopes, areas, and flow accumulation weights. At the equator, the cells are approximately square; at high latitudes, the east-west dimension shrinks significantly.

Two approaches handle this:

**Projection before processing.** The DEM is reprojected to a metric coordinate system (UTM, EOV, or another equal-area or conformal projection) and resampled to a regular integer cell size (e.g., 10 m). This simplifies all subsequent calculations because cell dimensions are uniform and in meters. However, reprojection introduces resampling artifacts -- the elevation values in the projected DEM are interpolated from the original, introducing a small amount of smoothing or distortion depending on the resampling method (bilinear interpolation, cubic convolution, or nearest neighbor).

**Processing in geographic coordinates.** Some software (notably TauDEM) can process DEMs in geographic coordinates directly, applying latitude-dependent corrections to slope and area calculations internally. This avoids resampling artifacts but requires the software to handle the non-square cell geometry correctly.

For Hungarian applications, DEMs obtained in the national EOV projection (EPSG:23700) are already in metric coordinates with square cells, so no reprojection is needed for processing with most tools. DEMs obtained from global sources (Copernicus, SRTM) in geographic coordinates should be either processed directly with TauDEM or projected to EOV or UTM Zone 34N (EPSG:32634, which covers most of Hungary) before processing with ArcGIS or QGIS tools.

### 9.12.2 Resampling Methods

When reprojecting a DEM, the choice of resampling method affects the quality of the output:

**Nearest neighbor** assigns the value of the closest input cell to each output cell. It preserves the original elevation values exactly but can introduce staircase artifacts on slopes and does not handle the geometric distortion of reprojection smoothly.

**Bilinear interpolation** computes a distance-weighted average of the four nearest input cells. It produces a smoother surface than nearest neighbor but slightly alters elevation values, potentially filling very shallow pits or creating new ones.

**Cubic convolution** uses 16 surrounding cells to fit a smooth surface and sample the output value. It produces the smoothest result and best preserves continuous surface properties (slope, curvature) but alters elevation values more than bilinear interpolation.

For hydrological DEM processing, **bilinear interpolation** is generally recommended as a compromise between smoothness and fidelity. Cubic convolution is preferred when the resampled DEM will also be used for slope and aspect analysis. Nearest neighbor should be avoided for DEM resampling unless exact preservation of integer elevation values is required.

## 9.13 A Practical Conditioning Workflow

To consolidate the concepts presented in this chapter, here is a recommended workflow for conditioning a DEM for hydrological analysis. The workflow applies regardless of the software platform, though the specific tool names will differ.

### 9.13.1 Step-by-Step Procedure

**Step 1: Obtain and prepare the DEM.** Download or access the DEM for the study area, including a buffer of at least 1 kilometer beyond the watershed boundary. Verify the coordinate system and, if necessary, reproject to a metric system using bilinear interpolation. Check for no-data cells, void areas, and obvious artifacts by visualizing the DEM with hillshade rendering.

**Step 2: Decide on reconditioning.** If a reliable, independently mapped stream network is available, consider applying the AGREE method (preferred) or simple stream burning to ensure the DEM drainage follows the known channels. If the DEM is high-resolution and high-quality, and no independent stream data of comparable quality is available, skip reconditioning.

**Step 3: Fill pits.** Apply the pit-filling algorithm to produce a hydrologically conditioned DEM. If the landscape is known to contain large real depressions (karst, aeolian, glacial), consider using a maximum depth parameter to preserve depressions deeper than a threshold (e.g., 10 meters), or fill all pits and document the large fills for subsequent interpretation.

**Step 4: Compute and examine the fill-minus-DEM diagnostic.** Subtract the original DEM from the filled DEM. Display the result with a stretched color ramp. Inspect for:
- Large fill depths that may indicate real depressions being incorrectly removed.
- Patterns of fill that suggest systematic DEM artifacts (e.g., striping in radar DEMs).
- Areas where reconditioning may have been too aggressive or insufficient.

**Step 5: Proceed to flow direction computation.** With the conditioned DEM in hand, compute flow directions as described in Chapter 10. The conditioning ensures that flow direction assignment will be unambiguous (after flat area resolution) and that every cell will have a path to the domain outlet.

### 9.13.2 Common Pitfalls

Several mistakes recur frequently in DEM conditioning workflows:

**Forgetting the buffer.** Clipping the DEM exactly to the watershed boundary and then conditioning produces edge contamination that corrupts flow accumulation values within the watershed. Always include a buffer.

**Conditioning twice.** Some analysts apply both stream burning and pit filling and then wonder why the filled DEM looks strange. This is correct procedure -- stream burning first, then pit filling -- but the analyst must understand that the fill-minus-DEM diagnostic will reflect both operations. The large "fills" along stream paths are not depressions being filled; they are the stream trench being backfilled by the filling algorithm in areas where the burned stream created local pits.

**Using the conditioned DEM for terrain analysis.** The filled (or burned-and-filled) DEM should be used only for flow direction and accumulation calculations. Slope, aspect, curvature, and other terrain derivatives should be computed from the original, unconditioned DEM, because the conditioning alters the terrain surface in ways that distort these quantities.

**Ignoring the coordinate system.** Processing a geographic-coordinate DEM with tools that assume metric coordinates will produce incorrect slope values, area calculations, and flow accumulation weights. Always verify that the software handles the coordinate system correctly.

## 9.14 Conditioning in Special Landscapes

Some landscape types present conditioning challenges that go beyond the standard fill-and-proceed approach. Two categories deserve special discussion: karst terrain (where real depressions are fundamental landscape features) and flat terrain (where the DEM lacks sufficient precision to resolve drainage patterns).

### 9.14.1 Karst Terrain

Karst landscapes, formed by the dissolution of soluble rocks (limestone, dolomite, gypsum, salt), present a fundamental challenge to DEM-based hydrology because they violate the assumption that water flows on the surface along topographically defined pathways. In karst terrain:

- Surface water disappears into sinkholes, swallow holes, and losing stream reaches.
- Subsurface conduit flow carries water through cave systems that may cross beneath surface drainage divides.
- Springs deliver water to the surface at locations that cannot be predicted from surface topography alone.
- The subsurface catchment may differ significantly from the surface catchment.

The Aggtelek Karst in Hungary is an instructive example. The Baradla cave system receives water from surface dolines over an area of approximately 28 km$^2$ and discharges it through springs at Josvafo, several kilometers from the doline field. The surface topographic watershed and the subsurface karst catchment are not identical -- water that falls on one side of a topographic ridge may flow through the cave system to emerge on the other side.

For DEM-based hydrological analysis in karst terrain, the recommended approach is pragmatic:

1. Fill all depressions to establish topographic continuity, recognizing that this is a simplification.
2. Use the fill-minus-DEM diagnostic to map the karst depressions.
3. Document the known karst features and their potential influence on flow routing.
4. For detailed karst hydrology (spring discharge prediction, contaminant transport), use specialized karst models that explicitly represent conduit flow -- DEM-based terrain analysis is not sufficient.

### 9.14.2 Extremely Flat Terrain

In landscapes with very low relief, DEM conditioning becomes challenging because the elevation differences that define flow paths are comparable to or smaller than the DEM's vertical accuracy. The Hungarian Great Plain (Alfold) is a prime example: over large areas, the terrain slope is less than 0.1%, and the elevation range across a 10-kilometer transect may be only 1-2 meters. At 30-meter resolution with a vertical accuracy of several meters, the DEM essentially does not resolve the topographic gradients that guide drainage.

In such terrain, pit filling can raise large areas by amounts that exceed the natural elevation range of the landscape. The fill-minus-DEM diagnostic may show filled regions covering hundreds or thousands of square kilometers -- not because there are deep depressions, but because the terrain is so flat that even small elevation inconsistencies create extensive interconnected depressions.

Strategies for flat terrain include:

**Stream burning.** In flat landscapes, the mapped stream network is often the most reliable indicator of drainage direction. Applying the AGREE method with a generous drop (10-20 meters) and buffer (500-1000 meters) forces the DEM to follow the known channels, even where the natural terrain gradient is too subtle for the DEM to resolve.

**Higher-resolution DEMs.** Where available, LiDAR-derived DEMs with sub-meter vertical accuracy may resolve the subtle gradients that coarser DEMs miss. However, LiDAR in flat terrain also captures microtopographic features (plowed furrows, tire tracks, irrigation channels) that create an enormous number of pits requiring aggressive conditioning.

**Hybrid approaches.** Some practitioners use a combination of stream burning along major channels, moderate filling for minor depressions, and careful manual editing of the DEM at critical drainage decision points (e.g., bifurcations where a river splits into two distributaries).

## 9.15 Global Perspective: DEM Conditioning Across Continents

The challenges of DEM conditioning are universal, but their character varies with landscape, data availability, and institutional context. A brief survey of conditioning challenges in different regions illustrates the global scope of the problem.

**Sub-Saharan Africa.** Many African countries lack high-resolution national DEMs, relying on SRTM (30 m) or Copernicus GLO-30 for hydrological analysis. In the Sahel, aeolian depressions create numerous real pits in the DEM that challenge standard filling approaches. In the Congo Basin, dense tropical forest canopy causes radar DEMs to overestimate terrain elevation, creating artifacts that require careful conditioning. The HydroSHEDS dataset, derived from conditioned SRTM data, provides pre-processed flow direction and accumulation grids for the entire continent, reducing the need for individual analysts to perform conditioning from scratch.

**South and Southeast Asia.** The monsoon river systems of the Ganges-Brahmaputra, Mekong, and Irrawaddy deltas present extreme flat-terrain challenges. Delta distributary channels create complex drainage patterns where flow direction depends on water level (and thus on season), not just on topography. In the Himalayan mountains upstream, steep terrain and deep valleys produce few conditioning challenges, but the transition zone where rivers emerge from the mountains onto the plains requires careful stream burning to resolve the abrupt change in drainage character.

**Middle East.** Arid landscapes with internal drainage (sabkhas, playas, wadis that terminate in desert sinks) challenge the assumption that all flow should reach an outlet. In these landscapes, filling all depressions may be inappropriate -- some basins genuinely have no surface outlet, and treating them as contributing to a distant river system misrepresents the hydrology. Selective filling (using a maximum depth parameter) or manual editing is often required.

## 9.16 The Road Ahead: From Conditioned DEM to Watershed

This chapter has addressed the essential first step in DEM-based hydrological analysis: preparing the digital landscape so that water can flow continuously from every cell to an outlet. We have seen that raw DEMs contain pits -- artifacts and real depressions -- that must be resolved before any flow routing can proceed. Pit filling is the standard approach, universally available and robust, though it creates flat areas and may over-modify landscapes with genuine closed basins. Carving and optimal removal offer alternatives for specific situations. Stream burning and the AGREE method reconcile DEM drainage with independently mapped stream networks. And the fill-minus-DEM diagnostic provides essential quality control.

With a conditioned DEM in hand, the next step is to determine the direction water flows from each cell. Chapter 10 introduces the eight-direction pour point model (D8) and the D-infinity model that form the basis of flow direction computation. The flow direction grid is the key derivative product of the conditioned DEM, and from it, the entire downstream analysis -- flow accumulation, stream definition, and watershed delineation (Chapter 11) -- follows logically.

The tools introduced in this chapter -- ArcGIS Spatial Analyst, TauDEM, and Arc Hydro -- will reappear throughout Part III and beyond. TauDEM's parallel processing architecture and D-infinity flow model will be discussed further in Chapter 10. Arc Hydro's geodatabase framework and watershed delineation tools will be the focus of Chapter 12, which covers the automation of the complete terrain analysis workflow. The software descriptions provided in Section 9.10 serve as the canonical reference for these tools throughout the book.
