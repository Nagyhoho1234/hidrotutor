# Chapter 14: How High Above the River?

When a river rises during a flood, the question that matters most to the people living nearby is not what the absolute elevation of the water is, nor what the discharge at some upstream gauge reads, but something far more immediate: will the water reach my house? The answer depends on a single vertical measurement -- how high the house sits above the river. A building three metres above the channel may be safe during a moderate flood but submerged during a severe one. A building thirty metres above the channel is safe from any conceivable riverine flood. This intuition -- that flood risk is fundamentally a question of relative elevation above the nearest watercourse -- is the foundation of one of the most powerful products in modern hydroinformatics: the Height Above Nearest Drainage, universally known by its acronym, HAND.

HAND transforms a digital elevation model from an absolute reference frame (elevation above sea level) into a stream-relative reference frame (elevation above the nearest stream channel). The transformation is conceptually simple but computationally demanding, requiring a fully conditioned DEM, carefully derived flow directions, a consistent stream network, and algorithms capable of propagating elevation differences across millions or billions of grid cells. The result is a raster surface where every pixel carries a number that directly answers the flood question: how high is this point above the river?

This chapter develops the HAND concept from first principles, describes its computation using D-infinity flow directions, walks through a fully worked numerical example, discusses practical implementation in GIS software, addresses the critical issue of stream network consistency, examines the properties of the resulting HAND raster, describes continental-scale computation efforts, and demonstrates the application of HAND to flood-prone terrain identification. Throughout, we build on the terrain analysis foundations established in Chapters 9 through 11 and prepare the reader for the flood inundation mapping methods of Chapter 15.

## 14.1 The Concept of Relative Elevation

### 14.1.1 Why Absolute Elevation Fails for Flood Assessment

A conventional digital elevation model reports the elevation of each grid cell above a geodetic datum -- NAVD88 in the United States, the Baltic Height System in Hungary, or the EGM2008 geoid for global datasets. These absolute elevations are essential for engineering design, geodetic surveying, and countless other purposes, but they are remarkably poor predictors of flood risk. The reason is straightforward: flooding is a local phenomenon governed by the relationship between a point and its nearest watercourse, not by the point's position relative to a distant datum.

Consider two locations in Hungary. The first is a point on the Tisza floodplain near Szolnok at an absolute elevation of 82 metres above the Baltic datum. The second is a point on the Buda Hills overlooking the Danube at an absolute elevation of 250 metres. Based on absolute elevation alone, the Szolnok point appears much "lower" and therefore presumably more flood-prone. But this reasoning ignores the local context entirely. The Tisza at Szolnok flows at approximately 79 metres elevation, so our first point sits just 3 metres above the river -- well within the range of a major flood. The Danube at Budapest flows at approximately 90 metres, so our second point, despite its much higher absolute elevation, sits 160 metres above the river and faces zero flood risk from the Danube.

The same logic applies everywhere rivers flow. Along the Mississippi River near St. Louis, a point at 130 metres absolute elevation on the floodplain may be in grave danger, while a point at 120 metres on a bluff overlooking a small tributary creek is perfectly safe. What matters is not how high you are above sea level, but how high you are above the nearest water surface. This is precisely what HAND measures.

### 14.1.2 From Absolute to Stream-Relative Coordinates

HAND performs a coordinate transformation on the elevation surface. Instead of reporting each cell's height above a geodetic datum, it reports each cell's height above the nearest stream channel, where "nearest" is defined not by straight-line distance but by the hydrologic flow path -- the route water would take as it flows downhill from that cell to the stream. This distinction matters because water does not travel in straight lines; it follows the topography, winding around ridges and through valleys.

The result of this transformation is sometimes called a "stream-normalized DEM" or a "relative DEM." Its properties are dramatically different from those of the original DEM. In the original DEM, elevations increase systematically with distance from the coast and with position in the landscape -- upstream reaches are higher than downstream reaches, mountains are higher than plains. In the HAND raster, these systematic trends vanish. Every stream cell, whether it sits at 3,000 metres elevation in the headwaters or at 50 metres near the outlet, receives a HAND value of zero. The floodplain cells adjacent to each stream reach receive small HAND values regardless of their absolute elevation. Hilltops and ridges receive large HAND values. The HAND surface is, in effect, a map of topographic position relative to the drainage network -- a quantity that geomorphologists call the "hillslope position" and that hydrologists recognize as a direct proxy for flood vulnerability.

This transformation has a second, equally important property: it makes the HAND surface directly comparable to flood depth. If a hydrological model or a real-time gauge reading tells us that the water depth in a particular stream reach is 4 metres, then every cell with a HAND value less than 4 metres along that reach is inundated, and the depth of inundation at each cell is simply 4 minus the HAND value. This arithmetic simplicity is what makes HAND so powerful for operational flood mapping, as we will explore in detail in Chapter 15.

### 14.1.3 The Family of Proximity Measures

HAND belongs to a broader family of terrain-derived proximity measures that describe a point's position relative to the drainage network and the drainage divide. These measures were formalized by Tesfa, Tarboton, Watson, Schreuders, Baker, and Wallace (2011), who demonstrated that they can all be computed as generalized weighted distances along D-infinity flow paths. For any point on the landscape, one can compute:

**Horizontal distance to the stream.** The planimetric distance along the flow path from the point to the nearest stream cell. A cell 500 metres horizontally from the stream (measured along the flow path) has more time for flood waters to reach it than one 50 metres away, though horizontal distance alone does not indicate whether the cell is above or below flood level.

**Vertical distance to the stream (HAND).** The elevation difference between the point and its nearest stream cell, computed along the flow path. This is the quantity we focus on in this chapter. It directly indicates the minimum flood depth required to inundate the point.

**Distance along the slope to the stream.** The actual path length measured along the terrain surface, accounting for both horizontal distance and slope. This measure is relevant to overland flow travel time and hydrograph timing: a cell on a long, gentle slope drains more slowly than one on a short, steep slope, even if both have the same HAND value.

**Horizontal distance to the ridge.** The distance from the point upslope to the nearest drainage divide, traced along the reverse of the flow direction. Combined with the distance to the stream, this measure allows one to determine where a point sits on the hillslope -- near the top, in the middle, or near the bottom.

**Vertical distance to the ridge.** The elevation difference between the point and the ridge above it. This quantity, combined with HAND, gives the total local relief of the hillslope: the sum of the vertical distance to the ridge and the vertical distance to the stream equals the total elevation difference between the ridge and the channel bottom along the flow path through that point.

These measures, taken together, provide a complete description of a point's position on the hillslope. A point near the ridge with high vertical distance to the stream and low distance to the ridge is in the upland zone. A point near the stream with low HAND and high distance to the ridge is in the lowland or floodplain zone. A point midway between ridge and stream is in the transitional hillslope zone. This kind of landscape classification is valuable for soil mapping, vegetation modeling, and land-use planning, but among all five measures, HAND has proven the most practically important because of its direct, quantitative relationship to flood inundation depth.

## 14.2 The Origin and Evolution of HAND

### 14.2.1 Brazilian Beginnings

The acronym HAND was coined by Antonio Donato Nobre, a Brazilian researcher who recognized that the vertical distance from the land surface to the nearest drainage channel was a powerful descriptor of Amazonian ecosystems. In the Amazon basin, the distinction between permanently flooded várzea forests, seasonally flooded igapó forests, and upland terra firme forests is governed almost entirely by their elevation above the nearest river. Nobre and colleagues published their work in the mid-2000s, demonstrating that a DEM-derived HAND surface could map these ecological zones across vast, poorly surveyed regions of the Amazon where field surveys were impractical.

The idea itself was not entirely new -- hydrologists had long recognized that relative elevation above a stream was a useful quantity -- but Nobre's contribution was to formalize it as a computable raster product and to demonstrate its practical utility at regional scale. The name "Height Above Nearest Drainage" was descriptive and memorable, and it stuck. Subsequent papers by Rennó, Nobre, Cuartas, Soares, Hodnett, Tomasella, and Waterloo (2008) developed the methodology further, establishing HAND as a standard tool in tropical hydrology and geomorphology.

### 14.2.2 From Regional Tool to Continental Infrastructure

The transformation of HAND from a research tool to an operational infrastructure product was driven largely by work at the University of Texas at Austin in collaboration with Utah State University. David Maidment, David Tarboton, and their students and collaborators recognized that HAND could serve as the terrain foundation for a national flood inundation mapping system. The key technical advances that enabled this transition were threefold.

First, Tarboton's TauDEM software provided a parallel implementation of D-infinity flow directions and generalized downstream distance functions that could process continental-scale DEMs on high-performance computing clusters. The algorithms described later in this chapter are implemented in TauDEM.

Second, Tesfa and colleagues (2011) formalized the theoretical framework for computing generalized proximity measures from DEMs, showing that HAND and its relatives could be computed as special cases of a single "flow algebra" framework applied to D-infinity flow fields (see Chapter 10 for the flow algebra concept).

Third, Zheng, Liu, Maidment, Tarboton, and Wang undertook the massive computational effort of producing HAND for the entire continental United States at 10-metre resolution, using the National Elevation Dataset processed on the Roger and Stampede supercomputers at the National Center for Supercomputing Applications (NCSA) and the Texas Advanced Computing Center (TACC). This continental HAND dataset became a cornerstone of the US National Water Model's flood inundation mapping capability.

Today, HAND is computed and used operationally by water agencies around the world. The concept has been applied at scales ranging from individual catchments to entire continents, in environments from the Amazon floodplain to the arid landscapes of the Middle East. Its simplicity, physical interpretability, and direct connection to flood depth make it one of the most widely adopted products of digital terrain analysis.

### 14.2.3 European and Hungarian Context

In Europe, the Joint Research Centre (JRC) of the European Commission has produced continental-scale flood hazard maps that rely, in part, on terrain-relative elevation concepts similar to HAND. The European Flood Awareness System (EFAS), operated by the Copernicus Emergency Management Service, uses terrain analysis products derived from the Copernicus DEM for its flood forecasting and inundation mapping capabilities.

For Hungary specifically, HAND is exceptionally relevant. The country's geography is dominated by two great river floodplains -- the Danube and the Tisza -- where vast areas of low-lying terrain sit only a few metres above normal river levels. The Great Hungarian Plain (Alfold), which covers more than half the country's area, has minimal topographic relief, and the distinction between flood-prone and flood-safe terrain often comes down to elevation differences of just 2 to 5 metres. In such a landscape, HAND provides information that absolute elevation simply cannot: it reveals which parts of the plain are genuinely close to river level and which, despite their low absolute elevation, sit on subtle topographic rises that place them above flood reach.

Hungarian water management has a long history of dealing with Tisza floods -- the catastrophic events of 1879, 1970, 1998, 2000, 2001, and 2006 all demonstrated that the Tisza floodplain requires constant vigilance and sophisticated flood mapping. The HAND concept offers a computationally efficient way to assess flood vulnerability across the entire Tisza system, complementing the detailed hydraulic models maintained by OVF (Orszagos Vizugyi Foigazgatosag, the General Directorate of Water Management) and the real-time monitoring data available through hydroinfo.hu.

## 14.3 Prerequisites: The Terrain Processing Chain

### 14.3.1 Building on Chapters 9, 10, and 11

HAND is the culminating product of the terrain flow analysis framework developed throughout Part III. It cannot be computed from a raw DEM alone; it requires every intermediate product from the processing chain described in Chapters 9 through 11: a hydrologically conditioned DEM (Chapter 9), D8 and D-infinity flow directions (Chapter 10), flow accumulation (Chapter 10), and a stream network with catchment delineation (Chapter 11). The reader should be thoroughly familiar with this complete processing chain before proceeding. Rather than re-derive those steps here, this section examines how the choices made during terrain conditioning affect the quality of the HAND result, and what additional considerations arise when the goal is HAND rather than watershed delineation.

### 14.3.2 The Critical Role of DEM Quality

HAND is only as good as the DEM from which it is derived. This may seem obvious, but it has practical implications that deserve emphasis. The vertical accuracy of the DEM directly limits the precision of HAND values. If the DEM has a root-mean-square elevation error of 2 metres (typical for the US National Elevation Dataset at 10-metre resolution), then HAND values are uncertain by approximately 2 metres. A computed HAND value of 1.5 metres might in reality be anywhere from -0.5 to 3.5 metres, which is the difference between a cell that is below the stream (an artifact) and one that is safely above a moderate flood.

For LiDAR-derived DEMs (Chapter 13), the vertical accuracy is typically 10 to 20 centimetres, which provides HAND values precise enough to distinguish individual terraces and levee features on the floodplain. The difference in practical utility is enormous: a HAND surface derived from a 10-metre NED DEM can identify broad flood-prone zones, while one derived from a 1-metre LiDAR DEM can identify individual buildings at risk.

The Copernicus EEA-10 DEM (10 metres, available for EU/EEA countries) and the freely available Copernicus GLO-30 (30 metres, global) provide alternatives for areas where national data are unavailable or for cross-border analyses.

### 14.3.3 Why D-infinity Matters for HAND

Chapter 10 introduced two flow direction models: the D8 model, which assigns each cell a single flow direction to one of eight neighbours, and the D-infinity model, which allows a continuous flow angle and proportional partitioning of flow between two downslope neighbours. For stream delineation and watershed boundary identification, the D8 model is usually sufficient because streams are inherently one-dimensional features and watershed boundaries are sharp divides. For HAND, however, the D-infinity model is strongly preferred.

The reason relates to how HAND values are computed on hillslopes. Under the D8 model, every cell drains to exactly one downstream neighbour, creating narrow, discrete flow paths that converge like the veins of a leaf. On a smooth hillslope where water in reality spreads out and flows downhill as a broad sheet, the D8 model forces all flow into artificially narrow channels. The resulting HAND surface shows "streaking" artifacts -- long, narrow lines of slightly different HAND values that follow the D8 flow paths and have no physical basis.

The D-infinity model eliminates these artifacts by distributing flow proportionally between two neighbours based on the continuous flow angle. On a smooth hillslope, cell A might send 60% of its flow to the southeast neighbour and 40% to the south neighbour. Its HAND value is then a weighted average of the HAND values computed via both flow paths. This weighted averaging produces a smooth HAND surface that better represents the physical reality of water spreading across the hillslope.

The mathematical details of D-infinity flow direction computation and proportional flow partitioning are developed in Chapters 7 and 10. What matters here is the practical consequence: always use D-infinity flow directions when computing HAND. The D8 model is acceptable for stream delineation, but HAND requires the smooth, artifact-free surface that only D-infinity provides.

## 14.4 The HAND Computation Algorithm

### 14.4.1 The Downstream Vertical Distance Problem

Computing HAND requires solving a specific type of "downstream accumulation" problem. For every cell in the DEM that is not classified as a stream cell, we need to determine the elevation of the stream cell (or cells) to which it eventually drains, and then compute the difference between its own elevation and that stream elevation. If flow were strictly D8 -- each cell draining to exactly one downstream neighbour -- the problem would be trivial: start at any non-stream cell, trace the unique flow path downhill until you reach a stream cell, and subtract the stream cell's elevation from the starting cell's elevation. Under D-infinity, the problem is more complex because each cell may drain to two downstream neighbours, each of which may drain to two more, creating a branching tree of flow paths that eventually converge on one or more stream cells.

The solution uses the flow algebra framework introduced in Chapter 10. Specifically, HAND is computed as a downstream accumulation of elevation differences using the D-infinity proportional flow partitions as weights. The computation proceeds from the stream network upward, sweeping through the landscape from low-lying stream cells to high ridge cells, rather than tracing paths from each cell down to the stream.

### 14.4.2 Algorithm: Upslope Sweep from the Stream Network

The HAND algorithm, as implemented in TauDEM's "D-infinity Distance Down" function, proceeds as follows:

**Step 1 -- Initialization.** Set HAND = 0 for all grid cells classified as stream cells. These are the known values that anchor the computation. Mark all non-stream cells as "not yet computed."

**Step 2 -- Build the ready queue.** A non-stream cell is "ready" for computation if all of its downslope receiving cells (as determined by the D-infinity flow direction) have already been computed. Since all stream cells are initialized to HAND = 0, the first ready cells are those non-stream cells immediately adjacent to the stream network whose D-infinity flow directions point entirely into stream cells. Place all ready cells in a processing queue.

**Step 3 -- Process each ready cell.** For each ready cell $i$ in the queue, identify the one or two downslope cells $k$ to which it drains and their D-infinity flow proportions $p_k$ (where $\sum p_k = 1$). Compute the HAND value as:

$$\text{HAND}_i = \sum_{k} p_k \cdot \left( \text{HAND}_k + (Z_i - Z_k) \right)$$

where $Z_i$ is the elevation of cell $i$ and $Z_k$ is the elevation of cell $k$. The term $(Z_i - Z_k)$ is the elevation drop from cell $i$ to its downslope neighbour $k$, and $\text{HAND}_k$ is the already-computed height of cell $k$ above the stream. The sum adds the local elevation drop to the previously accumulated height above the stream, weighted by the flow proportion going to each neighbour.

**Step 4 -- Propagate readiness.** After computing $\text{HAND}_i$, check all cells that drain into cell $i$'s neighbourhood. Specifically, for each of cell $i$'s upslope neighbours, check whether all of that neighbour's downslope receiving cells have now been computed. If so, add that neighbour to the ready queue.

**Step 5 -- Terminate.** The algorithm terminates when the ready queue is empty. At that point, every cell in the DEM has been assigned a HAND value: zero for stream cells, and a positive value for all non-stream cells.

The upslope-sweep structure is critical for efficiency. Rather than tracing individual flow paths from every cell down to the stream (which would involve redundant computation as paths overlap), the algorithm processes each cell exactly once, working outward from the known values at the stream network. This is analogous to the way flow accumulation is computed by sweeping downstream from ridges (Chapter 10), but in the opposite direction.

### 14.4.3 Worked Example: Computing HAND for Individual Cells

To make the algorithm concrete, let us work through two examples with actual numbers.

**Example 1: A cell adjacent to the stream.**

Consider a grid cell at position (3, 4) with elevation $Z = 436.48$ m. Its D-infinity flow direction points to two downstream neighbours:

- Cell A (to the south): $Z_A = 435.56$ m, HAND$_A = 0$ (on the stream), flow proportion $p_A = 0.33$
- Cell B (to the southeast): $Z_B = 435.35$ m, HAND$_B = 0$ (on the stream), flow proportion $p_B = 0.67$

The elevation differences are:

$$\Delta Z_A = Z - Z_A = 436.48 - 435.56 = 0.92 \text{ m}$$

$$\Delta Z_B = Z - Z_B = 436.48 - 435.35 = 1.13 \text{ m}$$

Applying the HAND formula:

$$\text{HAND} = p_A \cdot (\text{HAND}_A + \Delta Z_A) + p_B \cdot (\text{HAND}_B + \Delta Z_B)$$

$$= 0.33 \times (0 + 0.92) + 0.67 \times (0 + 1.13)$$

$$= 0.33 \times 0.92 + 0.67 \times 1.13$$

$$= 0.304 + 0.757 = 1.06 \text{ m}$$

This cell sits approximately 1.06 metres above the nearest stream. In the context of a major flood on the Tisza, where flood depths routinely exceed 3 metres above the channel, this cell would be inundated. On a small upland stream where floods rarely exceed 1 metre, this cell might remain dry in all but the most extreme events.

Note the role of the flow proportions. The cell sends 67% of its flow to the southeast neighbour and 33% to the south neighbour. The southeast path has a larger elevation drop (1.13 m vs. 0.92 m), but the weighted average takes both paths into account. If we had used D8 flow directions, the cell would drain entirely to one neighbour, and the HAND value would be either 0.92 m or 1.13 m rather than the blended value of 1.06 m.

**Example 2: A cell further from the stream.**

Now consider a cell higher on the hillslope at position (2, 3) with elevation $Z = 441.22$ m. Its two downstream neighbours are not on the stream -- they are cells whose HAND values have already been computed in previous iterations:

- Cell C: $Z_C = 439.12$ m, HAND$_C = 2.64$ m, flow proportion $p_C = 0.60$
- Cell D: $Z_D = 438.48$ m, HAND$_D = 1.88$ m, flow proportion $p_D = 0.40$

The elevation differences are:

$$\Delta Z_C = 441.22 - 439.12 = 2.10 \text{ m}$$

$$\Delta Z_D = 441.22 - 438.48 = 2.74 \text{ m}$$

Applying the formula:

$$\text{HAND} = 0.60 \times (2.64 + 2.10) + 0.40 \times (1.88 + 2.74)$$

$$= 0.60 \times 4.74 + 0.40 \times 4.62$$

$$= 2.844 + 1.848 = 4.69 \text{ m}$$

This cell sits 4.69 metres above the nearest stream. Notice how the HAND value accumulates: cell C is already 2.64 m above the stream, and our cell is another 2.10 m above cell C via that path. Through the other path, cell D is 1.88 m above the stream, and our cell is 2.74 m above cell D. The weighted combination gives 4.69 m, reflecting the contributions of both flow paths.

The interpretation is immediate: this cell would be inundated only if the flood depth in the nearest stream reach exceeds 4.69 metres. For a stream in the Tisza system where major floods can produce 5-6 metre depths above the channel, this cell is at moderate risk. For a small tributary with maximum flood depths of 2-3 metres, this cell is safe.

### 14.4.4 Understanding the Weighted Average

The weighted-average formulation deserves some reflection, because it produces HAND values that do not correspond to any single physical flow path. In Example 2, no water molecule actually follows a path with exactly 4.69 metres of elevation drop to the stream. Some water from our cell reaches the stream via Cell C with a total drop of 4.74 m, and some via Cell D with a drop of 4.62 m. The HAND value of 4.69 m is an average over these paths, weighted by the fraction of flow going to each.

This averaging has two practical consequences. First, it produces smoother HAND surfaces than any single-path method, eliminating the sharp boundaries and streaking artifacts that arise under D8 flow assignment. Second, it means that HAND values should be interpreted as "representative height above the stream" rather than "exact height along a specific flow path." For flood mapping purposes, this interpretation is appropriate: the flood water spreading laterally across a floodplain does not follow a single flow path either.

## 14.5 Stream Network Consistency

### 14.5.1 The Alignment Problem

The HAND computation is critically sensitive to the stream network from which it is computed. Specifically, the stream raster must be consistent with the DEM -- the stream cells must lie in the DEM's topographic valleys, not displaced onto adjacent higher ground. This requirement sounds obvious, but violating it is one of the most common errors in HAND computation, and it produces results that are badly wrong.

The problem arises because vector stream networks -- the NHDPlus in the United States, EU-Hydro in Europe, OpenStreetMap waterways globally -- were digitized from maps or satellite imagery independently of any particular DEM. When these vector lines are rasterized onto a DEM grid, the resulting stream cells may not coincide with the DEM's lowest points. A stream that in reality flows down the centre of a valley might, after rasterization, land on cells that are 2 or 3 metres above the valley floor. Every HAND value computed from such a displaced stream will be wrong by that amount -- potentially classifying floodplain cells as flood-safe when they are not.

The displacement problem is especially severe with coarser DEMs. On a 30-metre SRTM grid, a stream that is 20 metres wide occupies less than one cell width. The cell that the vector stream falls on might represent a weighted average of the stream channel, the near bank, and adjacent land, with an elevation considerably higher than the actual water surface. On a 1-metre LiDAR grid, the same stream occupies 20 cells and at least some of those cells will have elevations close to the water surface, but the vector line may still be offset by a cell or two.

The solution is never to use a rasterized vector stream network directly for HAND computation. Instead, derive the stream network from the DEM itself, using the flow accumulation approach described in Chapter 11. A DEM-derived stream, by construction, follows the DEM's topographic valleys. It may not match the real-world stream position exactly (especially in areas where the DEM has errors or where anthropogenic modifications have altered the terrain), but it is internally consistent with the elevation surface, which is what HAND requires.

### 14.5.2 Deriving Streams from DEM Using Vector Channel Heads

The challenge with DEM-derived streams is controlling where they begin. A simple contributing-area threshold (e.g., classify as stream every cell with more than 1,000 upstream cells) produces a stream network, but the extent of that network depends entirely on the threshold chosen. Too low a threshold creates streams everywhere, including on hillslopes where no real streams exist. Too high a threshold omits headwater streams that carry real flow. The optimal threshold varies with climate, geology, and topographic texture, as discussed in Chapter 11.

A more robust approach uses the vector stream network to define channel heads -- the upstream endpoints of mapped streams -- and then derives the rest of the network from the DEM. The procedure is:

**Step 1.** Extract the upstream endpoints (channel heads) of the vector stream network. In ArcGIS, the "Feature Vertices to Points" tool with the "start" option does this for each polyline feature. For NHDPlus, the channel head is the upstream end of each headwater flow line (identified by the "start flag" attribute). For EU-Hydro, the equivalent is the upstream terminus of each river segment.

**Step 2.** Rasterize the channel head points onto the same grid as the DEM, producing a binary raster with value 1 at channel-head cells and 0 everywhere else. The raster must have the same cell size, extent, and alignment as the DEM.

**Step 3.** Run weighted flow accumulation (D8) using the channel-head raster as the weight grid. The result counts, for each cell, how many channel heads lie upstream of it. Any cell with a count of 1 or more lies on a stream.

**Step 4.** Threshold the result at 1 to produce a binary stream raster (1 = stream, 0 = non-stream).

This approach ensures that streams begin at the locations mapped in the vector dataset but follow the DEM valleys downstream. The resulting network has the same topology and extent as the original vector network but is geometrically aligned with the DEM's topographic surface. This is the stream raster that should be used for HAND computation.

### 14.5.3 Dealing with Anthropogenic Obstructions

In developed landscapes, human-made structures frequently interfere with DEM-derived flow paths. Railway embankments, highway overpasses, levees, and dam structures create barriers in the DEM that do not block real water flow (which passes through culverts, bridges, and engineered openings). Standard pit-filling may cause the DEM-derived stream to detour around these barriers, sometimes by hundreds of metres, producing an incorrect stream alignment and correspondingly wrong HAND values.

The "soft burn-in" or "etching" approach addresses this problem by enforcing monotonically decreasing elevations along the known stream path. The procedure traces along the vector stream line following the indicated flow direction. Wherever the DEM elevation increases along the traced path (indicating a barrier or artifact), it is lowered to the previous elevation, effectively cutting a narrow channel through the obstruction. The rest of the DEM is left unchanged.

This is distinct from the full stream-burning approach discussed in Chapter 9, which lowers the DEM along the entire stream by a fixed amount. The soft burn-in makes only the minimum necessary adjustments to maintain downstream flow, preserving as much of the original DEM information as possible. TauDEM implements this procedure through its "Flow Direction Conditioning" function.

In Hungary, this problem is particularly relevant along the Tisza, where an extensive system of flood-control levees (gátak) creates continuous elevated barriers on both sides of the river. A DEM of the Tisza floodplain near Szolnok will show the levees as ridges that appear to block flow from the floodplain back to the river. Without etching or some form of DEM conditioning, the derived stream network may be displaced to one side of the levee system, and HAND values within the protected floodplain will be incorrect. The solution is to use the known river centreline for conditioning while retaining the levee features in the DEM for accurate HAND values on the protected side.

### 14.5.4 Handling Water Bodies

Permanent water bodies -- lakes, reservoirs, wide river channels, coastal wetlands -- present a special consideration for HAND computation. These areas have HAND values of zero or near-zero because they are either on the drainage network or immediately adjacent to it. If the goal is to identify flood-prone areas where transient flooding occurs during high-water events, then permanent water bodies must be distinguished from seasonally or event-flooded areas.

The practical approach is straightforward: use the mapped water body polygons from the relevant hydrographic dataset (NHDPlus water bodies in the US, EU-Hydro water bodies in Europe, OVF surface water maps in Hungary) as a mask. Areas classified as permanent water are excluded from the HAND-based flood analysis. The HAND surface is computed for the entire landscape including the water bodies (the algorithm does not distinguish wet from dry cells), but the interpretation step -- "which cells are flood-prone?" -- applies only to cells outside the permanent water mask.

In Hungary, Lake Balaton (the largest lake in Central Europe at 594 km²), Lake Fertő (Neusiedler See), and the extensive oxbow lakes (holtágak) along the Tisza all require this treatment. The HAND values within these permanent water bodies are physically meaningless -- the "height above nearest drainage" for a cell that is itself covered by water is a concept without practical content.

## 14.6 Practical Implementation in GIS

### 14.6.1 Required Inputs

Computing HAND in a GIS environment requires three inputs, all of which must be internally consistent -- derived from the same conditioned DEM and sharing the same grid extent, cell size, and alignment:

1. **A hydrologically conditioned DEM** (pit-filled, and optionally stream-burned or etched). This is the elevation surface from which HAND values are computed. Use the filled DEM, not the original, for reasons discussed in Section 14.6.3.

2. **D-infinity flow directions** computed from the conditioned DEM. These provide the proportional flow partitioning needed for smooth HAND values.

3. **A DEM-consistent stream raster** derived from the DEM's own flow accumulation or from channel-head weighted accumulation (Section 14.5.2). Stream cells have value 1; non-stream cells have value 0 or NoData.

All three inputs must share the same spatial reference, cell size, and grid alignment. A mismatch in any of these properties will produce incorrect HAND values without necessarily generating an error message -- the tool will compute something, but the results will be wrong.

### 14.6.2 The ArcGIS Pro Flow Distance Tool

In ArcGIS Pro, HAND is computed using the **Flow Distance** tool in the Spatial Analyst Hydrology toolbox. The tool is designed to compute distances along flow paths from cells to the stream network, with options for horizontal, vertical, or slope distance. For HAND, the settings are:

```
Tool:           Flow Distance (Spatial Analyst > Hydrology)
Input stream raster:      [binary stream raster, 1 = stream]
Input surface raster:     [filled DEM]
Input flow direction raster: [D-infinity flow directions]
Distance type:            VERTICAL
Flow direction type:      DINF
```

The output is a floating-point raster where each cell contains its HAND value in the same vertical units as the DEM (typically metres). Stream cells receive a value of 0. Cells that do not connect to any stream cell (for example, cells on islands or in internally drained basins that were not resolved during conditioning) receive NoData.

The computation time depends on the DEM size and the computing resources available. For a single HUC-10 watershed (~250 km²) at 10-metre resolution, the computation takes seconds to minutes. For a large river basin at high resolution, it may take hours and benefit from the parallel processing capabilities of TauDEM (Section 14.7.1).

### 14.6.3 Why Use the Filled DEM as the Surface Raster

A question that arises in practice is whether to use the original (unfilled) DEM or the hydrologically conditioned (filled) DEM as the surface raster for HAND computation. The recommendation is clear: use the filled DEM. The reasons are:

**Physical consistency.** The flow directions were computed from the filled DEM. Using the original DEM with filled-DEM flow directions creates an inconsistency: a cell might have a flow direction pointing to a neighbour that is actually higher in the original DEM (because the fill operation raised the neighbour's elevation). This can produce negative HAND values -- cells that appear to be below the stream -- which are physically nonsensical.

**Artifact removal.** Sinks in the original DEM are considered artifacts of the data collection process (see Chapter 9). The filled DEM represents our best approximation of the true land surface. HAND values computed from the filled DEM are therefore our best estimate of the true relative elevation.

**Monotonic accumulation.** Using the filled DEM ensures that HAND values increase monotonically as you move upslope from the stream. With the original DEM, cells in filled pits might have HAND values lower than their downstream neighbours, creating a non-physical pattern that complicates flood extent delineation.

The exception to this recommendation is when working with very high-resolution LiDAR DEMs where the pit-filling operation may significantly alter legitimate topographic features (such as natural depressions, levee-protected areas, or terraced agricultural land). In such cases, a gentle smoothing or breach-based conditioning approach (Chapter 9) may be preferable to aggressive filling, and the resulting conditioned DEM should still be used as the surface raster.

### 14.6.4 The TauDEM Alternative

For large-area computations or when ArcGIS Pro is not available, TauDEM (Terrain Analysis Using Digital Elevation Models) provides an open-source, command-line alternative that supports parallel processing on multi-core workstations and high-performance computing clusters. The relevant TauDEM function is **DinfDistDown** (D-infinity Distance Down), which computes vertical distance to the stream using D-infinity flow directions.

The TauDEM command-line syntax for HAND computation is:

```
mpiexec -n 8 DinfDistDown -ang dinf_flowdir.tif -fel filled_dem.tif ^
        -src stream_raster.tif -dd hand_output.tif -m ave v
```

The parameters are:

- `-ang`: D-infinity flow direction grid (angle in radians)
- `-fel`: Filled DEM (elevation surface)
- `-src`: Stream raster (1 = stream, 0 = non-stream)
- `-dd`: Output HAND raster
- `-m ave v`: Method = average, type = vertical (vertical distance, weighted average over D-infinity flow proportions)
- `-n 8`: Use 8 processors for parallel computation

TauDEM's parallel implementation uses MPI (Message Passing Interface) to distribute the computation across multiple processors. On a modern workstation with 16 or 32 cores, this can reduce computation time by an order of magnitude compared to single-threaded execution. For continental-scale computations, TauDEM has been run on supercomputers with thousands of cores.

### 14.6.5 QGIS and Open-Source Workflows

For users working with QGIS rather than ArcGIS Pro, HAND can be computed through several pathways. The most direct is to install TauDEM and call its functions through QGIS's Processing Toolbox or from the command line. WhiteboxTools, an open-source geospatial analysis library developed by John Lindsay at the University of Guelph, also provides HAND-related functions and integrates with QGIS through the WhiteboxTools for Processing plugin.

The GRASS GIS module `r.stream.distance` offers another open-source option, computing stream distance using the GRASS flow direction framework. While GRASS uses its own internal flow direction format rather than TauDEM's D-infinity angles, the underlying concept is the same.

For Python-based workflows, the pysheds library provides lightweight watershed delineation and HAND computation capabilities that do not require any GIS software at all. This is useful for automated pipelines and cloud-based processing.

## 14.7 Properties of the HAND Raster

### 14.7.1 Value Range and Interpretation

The HAND raster has properties markedly different from the DEM from which it was derived. Where the DEM reports absolute elevations that increase systematically from the watershed outlet to the headwaters and from valley floors to ridgetops, the HAND raster has no such systematic trend. Its values depend only on the local relationship between each cell and its nearest stream, independent of position within the watershed.

For a typical watershed, HAND values range from 0 (on the stream) to several tens or hundreds of metres on hilltops and ridges. The maximum HAND value is always less than or equal to the total relief of the watershed (the difference between the highest and lowest DEM elevations), and is typically much less. This is because HAND measures the local drop to the nearest stream, not the total drop to the watershed outlet. A cell on a ridge between two parallel streams may have a HAND value of only 30 metres even though the watershed has 400 metres of total relief.

To illustrate this with a concrete example from the published literature: for the Onion Creek watershed near Austin, Texas, DEM elevations range from approximately 113 m to 513 m (a total relief of 400 m), but HAND values range from 0 to approximately 122 m. The maximum HAND value occurs on an isolated hilltop that is far from any stream -- its nearest drainage point is a small tributary stream high in the watershed, and the elevation difference between the hilltop and that stream is only 122 m even though the hilltop sits 300+ metres above the watershed outlet.

For the Tisza floodplain in Hungary, the contrast is even more striking. Absolute elevations along the middle Tisza between Szolnok and Szeged range from roughly 76 m to 90 m (total relief of only 14 m in this extremely flat landscape). But HAND values in this area range from 0 to about 8-10 m, concentrated heavily in the 0-4 m range. This reflects the reality of the landscape: most of the Great Plain sits only a few metres above the Tisza's water surface, and the small elevation differences that separate flood-prone from flood-safe terrain are precisely what HAND reveals.

### 14.7.2 Visualisation Strategies

Effective visualization of HAND requires a classification scheme that highlights the low-value cells near streams where flood risk is concentrated. Using a linear colour ramp from 0 to the maximum value (perhaps 100+ metres) wastes most of the colour range on high-HAND cells that face no flood risk, leaving the critical low-HAND cells indistinguishable.

A recommended classification for flood-focused visualization:

**Table 14.5**: HAND classification for flood vulnerability mapping

| Class | HAND Range | Colour | Interpretation |
|-------|-----------|--------|----------------|
| 1 | 0 -- 1 m | Dark blue | Extreme flood risk; inundated by minor floods |
| 2 | 1 -- 2 m | Medium blue | Very high flood risk; frequent inundation |
| 3 | 2 -- 5 m | Light blue | High flood risk; inundated by moderate floods |
| 4 | 5 -- 10 m | Yellow | Moderate flood risk; inundated only by major floods |
| 5 | 10 -- 20 m | Orange | Low flood risk; only extreme events |
| 6 | > 20 m | Transparent or light grey | Negligible flood risk from riverine flooding |

Making the high-HAND cells transparent or very light allows the underlying terrain (hillshade, aerial imagery, or topographic basemap) to show through, creating a visualization where only the flood-relevant areas are visible. This is particularly effective when overlaid on a hillshade derived from the same DEM, as it provides topographic context while focusing attention on the areas of concern.

For the Tisza floodplain, where nearly the entire landscape falls in the 0-10 m HAND range, a finer classification with 0.5-metre intervals in the 0-5 m range may be more informative. The difference between 1.5 m and 3.0 m HAND on the Tisza plain can represent the difference between annual flooding and decadal flooding -- a distinction with enormous practical importance for land use planning and insurance assessment.

### 14.7.3 Comparison of D8 and D-infinity HAND

The differences between D8-derived and D-infinity-derived HAND are most visible on smooth hillslopes and in areas of low relief. On steep terrain with well-defined valleys, both methods produce similar results because the flow directions are unambiguous -- water clearly flows downhill along the valley axis, and the choice of flow algorithm makes little difference.

On the Great Hungarian Plain, however, where slopes are measured in fractions of a degree and the terrain has very little texture, the differences can be significant. The D8 algorithm creates parallel striping patterns on smooth surfaces because all cells in a row with the same D8 direction accumulate the same way. The D-infinity algorithm, by distributing flow proportionally based on the continuous slope angle, produces a smooth surface without these artifacts.

The practical difference for flood mapping is that D8 HAND surfaces produce ragged, stepwise flood extent boundaries when thresholded (e.g., "show all cells with HAND < 3 m"), while D-infinity HAND surfaces produce smooth, continuous boundaries that better represent the physical extent of flooding. In flat areas like the Tisza plain, where flood extent boundaries may sweep across kilometres of terrain for a 1-metre change in water level, this smoothness matters greatly for map readability and operational utility.

Both methods, however, are uncertain to the extent that the DEM itself is uncertain. The RMSE of the HAND surface is at least as large as the RMSE of the underlying DEM (and may be larger, since HAND involves the difference between two elevation values, both of which carry uncertainty). For the 10-metre NED with an RMSE of 2.34 m, HAND values are uncertain by at least 2-3 metres. The difference between D8 and D-infinity HAND -- typically a fraction of a metre -- is small compared to this uncertainty. The preference for D-infinity is therefore an aesthetic and theoretical one rather than a matter of demonstrated accuracy, but it is consistent with best practice and comes at negligible additional computational cost.

## 14.8 Continental-Scale HAND Computation

### 14.8.1 The US Continental HAND Dataset

The most ambitious HAND computation to date has been the production of a 10-metre HAND raster for the entire continental United States. This effort, led by Yan Liu and Xiaowen Wang at the University of Illinois in collaboration with the University of Texas at Austin, processed approximately 10 billion grid cells from the National Elevation Dataset through the full terrain analysis chain culminating in HAND.

The computation was performed on the Roger supercomputer at the National Center for Supercomputing Applications (NCSA) and the Stampede supercomputer at the Texas Advanced Computing Center (TACC). The processing chain for each HUC-6 watershed unit comprised:

1. Rasterization of NHDPlus flow lines to identify stream cells
2. Lowering of stream cell elevations (etching) to ensure flow continuity through barriers
3. Pit removal (filling) of the conditioned DEM
4. D8 and D-infinity flow direction computation
5. Weighted flow accumulation from NHD channel heads
6. Stream raster generation from the weighted accumulation
7. HAND computation using D-infinity vertical distance down

The continental dataset, when complete, was archived at TACC and made available for download. It provides the terrain foundation for the US National Water Model's flood inundation mapping capability: given a predicted flood depth for any NHDPlus stream reach, the HAND raster allows immediate determination of which cells are inundated and to what depth (as explored in Chapter 15).

### 14.8.2 European and Global Efforts

Following the US example, similar continental-scale terrain analysis products have been developed or are under development for other regions:

**Europe.** The Joint Research Centre (JRC) has produced river flood hazard maps for all of Europe using terrain analysis products derived from the Copernicus DEM. While the JRC's specific methodology differs from the TauDEM-based HAND approach (using the LISFLOOD hydrological-hydraulic model chain), the underlying terrain processing -- conditioning, flow direction, stream derivation -- follows the same principles. The European Flood Awareness System (EFAS) maintains operationally updated terrain products for its forecasting domain.

**Global.** The MERIT Hydro dataset, developed by Dai Yamazaki at the University of Tokyo, provides hydrologically conditioned elevation data and derived flow direction and flow accumulation products at 3 arc-second (~90 m) resolution globally. While MERIT Hydro does not include a pre-computed HAND surface, it provides all the inputs needed to compute one. The GloFAS (Global Flood Awareness System) uses terrain analysis products derived from MERIT DEM for its global flood forecasting.

**Hungary.** No pre-computed national HAND dataset currently exists for Hungary, but all the inputs are available: the DDM-5 provides a 5-metre DEM, the OVF river network provides stream geometry, and standard software (TauDEM or ArcGIS Pro) provides the processing tools. Computing HAND for the entire Hungarian territory at 5-metre resolution would be a substantial but feasible computation -- approximately 1.9 billion grid cells for Hungary's 93,000 km² area -- well within the capability of a modern multi-core workstation or a modest HPC allocation.

### 14.8.3 Computational Considerations

Continental-scale HAND computation involves several practical challenges beyond the sheer volume of data:

**Tiling and edge effects.** A continental DEM cannot be processed as a single raster -- it must be divided into tiles (typically following watershed boundaries such as HUC-6 or HUC-8 units in the US). Edge effects occur where flow paths cross tile boundaries. TauDEM handles this through domain decomposition with overlapping buffer zones, but the analyst must ensure that the tile boundaries do not cut across areas of interest.

**Memory management.** A 10-metre DEM of the continental US contains approximately 10 billion cells. At 4 bytes per cell (32-bit floating point), the DEM alone requires 40 gigabytes. The flow direction, flow accumulation, stream raster, and HAND raster each require comparable storage, for a total of ~200 GB for the complete processing chain. This exceeds the memory of typical workstations, requiring either tile-based processing or a computing cluster with distributed memory.

**Stream network consistency across tiles.** The stream network must be consistent across tile boundaries: a stream that exits one tile at its lowest point must enter the adjacent tile at the corresponding location. The NHDPlus ensures this through its reach-based topology, but DEM-derived streams at tile boundaries may not match perfectly. Quality control procedures are needed to identify and correct these inconsistencies.

**Storage and distribution.** The final HAND raster at 10-metre resolution for the US requires approximately 40 GB of storage in compressed GeoTIFF format. Distribution and access for this volume of data requires cloud hosting or specialized data archives such as TACC or the USGS ScienceBase platform.

## 14.9 Flood-Prone Terrain Identification

### 14.9.1 From HAND to Flood Risk

The most immediate application of HAND is identifying flood-prone terrain. At its simplest, any cell with a HAND value below a certain threshold is considered flood-prone. The question is: what threshold?

The answer depends on the specific stream and the magnitude of flooding being considered. A threshold of 2 metres identifies areas vulnerable to floods that raise water 2 metres above the channel -- frequent events on many rivers. A threshold of 10 metres identifies areas vulnerable only to extreme floods. There is no single "correct" threshold; the choice depends on the application.

For rapid screening and planning purposes, several rules of thumb have emerged from operational experience:

- **HAND < 2 m**: Areas at risk from annual to 10-year floods on most lowland rivers. These areas should generally not be developed or should be protected by flood defences.
- **HAND < 5 m**: Areas at risk from 50 to 100-year floods on major rivers. This corresponds roughly to the "100-year floodplain" concept used in regulatory flood mapping (FEMA in the US, EU Floods Directive in Europe).
- **HAND < 10 m**: Areas at risk from extreme events (500-year or greater). This corresponds to the outer boundary of flood risk considered in most planning frameworks.

These thresholds are approximate and vary with local conditions. On a steep mountain stream where flood depths rarely exceed 1-2 metres, even HAND < 2 m may overestimate the flood zone. On the lower Mississippi or the Tisza, where major floods can raise water 8-10 metres above the channel, HAND < 10 m is a realistic flood threshold.

### 14.9.2 HAND-Based Hydraulic Properties

Beyond simple flood-zone identification, HAND enables the estimation of reach-scale hydraulic properties that would otherwise require detailed field surveys. For any stream reach, the HAND raster provides a cross-sectional profile of the terrain: the distribution of HAND values in the cells draining to that reach describes the shape of the valley in which the stream flows.

From this cross-sectional information, one can compute, as functions of water depth $h$:

- **Inundated area** $A(h)$: The number of cells with HAND $< h$, multiplied by cell area
- **Wetted perimeter** $P(h)$: The length of the boundary between inundated and non-inundated cells at depth $h$
- **Top width** $T(h)$: The width of the water surface at depth $h$
- **Cross-sectional area** $A_x(h)$: The integral of $(h - \text{HAND})$ over all cells with HAND $< h$ along a cross-section
- **Hydraulic radius** $R(h) = A_x(h) / P(h)$: The ratio of cross-sectional area to wetted perimeter

These properties are inputs to Manning's equation and other hydraulic rating relationships that convert between water depth and discharge. Chapter 15 develops this application in detail, showing how HAND-derived hydraulic properties enable synthetic rating curves that approximate the results of detailed hydraulic modeling.

### 14.9.3 Limitations of HAND for Flood Mapping

HAND is powerful but approximate. Several important limitations should be understood:

**Steady-state assumption.** HAND assumes that flood water at a given depth in the stream instantaneously fills all terrain below that depth along the entire reach. In reality, flood waves propagate downstream over time, and the water surface profile along a reach is not flat but slopes in the downstream direction. HAND-based inundation maps therefore represent an upper bound on the actual flood extent -- they show everywhere that could potentially be flooded, not the time-varying extent of an actual flood event.

**No backwater or constriction effects.** HAND does not account for backwater effects at bridges, dams, or confluences, nor for flow constrictions at narrow valley sections. In reality, these features cause water to pile up upstream and spread across the floodplain in ways that a simple depth-HAND comparison cannot capture. Full hydraulic models (HEC-RAS, MIKE FLOOD, or the JRC's LISFLOOD) are needed for detailed inundation mapping in areas with significant hydraulic structures.

**Dependence on stream definition.** The HAND values for any cell depend on which cells are classified as streams. Changing the stream definition threshold changes the HAND surface. A more extensive stream network (lower threshold) tends to produce lower HAND values because each cell is closer to its nearest stream. A sparser network (higher threshold) produces higher HAND values. The choice of threshold must be appropriate for the scale of flooding being considered: for main-stem river flooding, only main-stem streams should be in the stream definition; for flash flooding on small tributaries, headwater streams must be included.

**DEM limitations.** As discussed in Section 14.3.2, HAND inherits all the limitations of the underlying DEM. In forested areas, a radar-derived DEM (SRTM, Copernicus DEM) reports the canopy height rather than the ground surface, producing HAND values that are too high. In urban areas, buildings and other structures may distort the DEM surface. LiDAR-derived bare-earth DEMs provide the most accurate HAND surfaces but are not available everywhere.

Despite these limitations, HAND's combination of simplicity, speed, and scalability makes it an invaluable screening tool. It is not a replacement for detailed hydraulic modeling, but it provides a first-order flood vulnerability assessment that can be computed anywhere a DEM exists, at any scale, in minutes to hours rather than the days to weeks required for hydraulic model setup and calibration.

## 14.10 Hungarian Case Study: HAND on the Tisza Floodplain

### 14.10.1 The Tisza: Hungary's Most Flood-Prone River

The Tisza (Tisa in Serbian, Tysa in Ukrainian) is the longest tributary of the Danube and Hungary's most important river after the Danube itself. Rising in the Carpathian Mountains of western Ukraine, it flows 966 km through Ukraine, Romania, Hungary, and Serbia before joining the Danube near Novi Sad. Within Hungary, the Tisza crosses 596 km of predominantly flat terrain, collecting tributaries from the Northern Hungarian Mountains and the Transylvanian Plateau.

The Tisza's flood characteristics are extreme by Central European standards. The river's catchment area (157,186 km²) extends across the entire eastern Carpathian arc, where intense rainfall and rapid snowmelt can produce enormous flood waves. These waves arrive on the Hungarian plain, where the river's slope decreases dramatically (from >1 m/km in the mountains to <0.05 m/km on the plain), causing the flood wave to slow and spread. The result is prolonged, deep flooding of the surrounding landscape.

Before the 19th-century river regulation works led by Pal Vasarhelyi and later Istvan Szechenyi, the Tisza's natural floodplain covered approximately 22,000 km² -- roughly a quarter of modern Hungary's total area. The regulation works, completed between 1846 and 1879, shortened the river by nearly 500 km through channel cutoffs (átvágások) and confined it between levees (gátak). The modern levee system protects most of the former floodplain, but the area between the levees (hullámtér) still experiences regular flooding, and levee breaches during extreme events can inundate vast areas of the protected floodplain (mentett ártér).

The most devastating modern Tisza floods occurred in 1879 (Szeged destroyed, 151 dead), 1970 (severe flooding across the middle Tisza), 1998-2001 (a series of major floods affecting the upper and middle Tisza), and 2006 (record water levels at several stations). These events underscore the ongoing need for accurate flood vulnerability mapping in the Tisza basin.

### 14.10.2 Computing HAND for the Middle Tisza

To illustrate the HAND workflow in a Hungarian context, consider the computation for a segment of the middle Tisza between Szolnok and Csongrád, a distance of approximately 120 km along the river. This reach crosses the heart of the Great Plain, where the terrain is extraordinarily flat and HAND is the only practical way to distinguish flood-vulnerable from flood-safe areas.

**Data sources:**

- DEM: DDM-5 at 5-metre resolution from Lechner Tudásközpont, in EOV projection (EPSG:23700). Alternative: Copernicus EEA-10 at 10-metre resolution, reprojected to EOV.
- Stream network: OVF river centreline (vízfolyás tengely) from data.vizugy.hu, used for channel-head extraction and soft burn-in.
- Software: TauDEM for parallel processing, or ArcGIS Pro Flow Distance tool.

**Processing steps:**

1. Extract the DEM for the study area (approximately 3,000 km²), buffered by at least 5 km beyond the area of interest to avoid edge effects.
2. Rasterize the OVF river centreline for the Tisza and its tributaries (Zagyva, Hármas-Körös, Alcsi-Holt-Tisza) onto the DEM grid.
3. Apply soft burn-in conditioning along the rasterized streams to ensure flow continuity through levees and road embankments.
4. Fill the conditioned DEM to remove remaining pits.
5. Compute D8 flow directions for stream delineation and D-infinity flow directions for HAND computation.
6. Compute D8 flow accumulation with the channel-head raster as weight to generate a DEM-consistent stream raster.
7. Compute HAND using D-infinity distance down.

**Expected results:**

On the Tisza floodplain, HAND values concentrate heavily in the 0-5 m range. The active channel (meder) and its immediate banks have HAND = 0. The area between the levees (hullámtér), typically 500 m to 3 km wide, has HAND values of 0-4 m. The protected floodplain immediately behind the levees has HAND values of 3-8 m, depending on the levee height and local terrain. Towns and villages on the plain, many of which were sited on natural river terraces or artificial mounds (kunhalmok), typically sit at HAND values of 4-8 m.

### 14.10.3 Interpreting HAND on the Tisza Plain

The HAND results for the Tisza floodplain reveal several features that are invisible in the conventional DEM:

**Levee effectiveness.** The levees appear as sharp discontinuities in the HAND surface. On the river side (hullámtér), HAND values are low (0-4 m). Immediately behind the levee, HAND values jump to 5-8 m because the protected area drains away from the levee toward secondary channels and drainage ditches rather than toward the Tisza itself. This HAND discontinuity directly measures the protective effect of the levee system: the higher the HAND value behind the levee, the greater the flood depth the levee can withstand before the protected area is at risk.

**Remnant floodplain features.** The pre-regulation Tisza had a complex system of oxbow lakes (morotvák, holtágak), backwater channels, and seasonal wetlands. These features remain visible in the HAND surface as elongated zones of low HAND values extending away from the modern channel, following the traces of abandoned meanders. Some of these old channels still function as drainage paths during extreme events, and their low HAND values correctly identify them as flood-vulnerable.

**Settlement vulnerability.** Hungarian settlements on the Tisza plain were historically sited on natural topographic highs -- river terraces, loess ridges, and artificial mounds. HAND values for these settlement sites are typically 4-8 m, confirming that traditional settlement placement reflected an intuitive understanding of flood risk. More recent development, however, has sometimes extended into areas with HAND values of 2-4 m, particularly in suburban zones and industrial areas. HAND mapping reveals these vulnerable extensions clearly.

**Agricultural zoning.** The HAND surface provides a natural zonation for agricultural land use. Areas with HAND < 2 m are suitable only for flood-tolerant uses (riparian forest, wet meadow, seasonal pasture). Areas with HAND 2-5 m can support annual crops but face periodic flood damage. Areas with HAND > 5 m are suitable for all agricultural uses, including orchards and permanent structures.

### 14.10.4 Comparison with Official Flood Maps

Hungary's official flood hazard maps, produced under the EU Floods Directive (2007/60/EC) by OVF, show flood extents for various return periods (10-year, 50-year, 100-year, and extreme scenarios). These maps are based on detailed hydraulic modeling (HEC-RAS or similar) calibrated to observed flood events and surveyed cross-sections. They represent the current standard for regulatory flood mapping in Hungary.

HAND-based flood extent estimates, while simpler, provide a useful complement to these official maps. The comparison typically shows:

- For the floodplain between the levees (hullámtér), HAND and the official maps agree well, because both identify the same low-lying terrain as flood-prone.
- For the protected floodplain (mentett ártér), HAND provides a worst-case scenario (what would happen if a levee breached), while the official maps show the expected extent assuming levees hold.
- For small tributaries not covered by the official maps, HAND provides the only available flood vulnerability information.

This complementarity makes HAND particularly valuable for emergency planning: it answers the question "what is the worst that could happen?" in a way that detailed hydraulic models, which typically assume infrastructure functions as designed, may not.

## 14.11 Global Perspective: HAND in Different Environments

### 14.11.1 Data Availability for Global HAND Computation

One of HAND's greatest strengths is that it can be computed anywhere a DEM exists. With the availability of global DEMs at 30-metre resolution (Copernicus GLO-30, SRTM) and hydrologically conditioned global flow data (MERIT Hydro, HydroSHEDS), HAND computation is feasible for any location on Earth. This is a dramatic advantage over detailed hydraulic modeling, which requires local survey data, calibration data, and expertise that are often unavailable in developing countries.

While its vertical accuracy (~4 m LE90) limits the precision of HAND values, the resulting flood vulnerability maps are still far more informative than anything available from absolute elevation alone. As national LiDAR programmes expand (India, Japan, South Korea, and several African nations have ongoing programmes), HAND precision will improve accordingly.

### 14.11.2 HAND in Arid and Semi-Arid Environments

HAND was originally developed for humid tropical and temperate environments where rivers carry perennial flow. In arid and semi-arid environments (much of the Middle East, North Africa, the Sahel, central Asia, and parts of Australia), the drainage network consists largely of ephemeral channels (wadis, arroyos) that carry water only during and immediately after rainfall events.

HAND is still applicable in these environments, but with modified interpretation. A low HAND value in an arid landscape indicates vulnerability to flash flooding -- brief, intense events triggered by convective storms that can fill a dry wadi with metres of water in minutes. The Copernicus DEM and SRTM provide adequate data for wadi delineation in most arid regions, though the lack of perennial flow means that stream definition must rely on contributing-area thresholds rather than on mapped stream networks (which are often incomplete or absent for ephemeral channels).

### 14.11.3 HAND in Mountainous Terrain

In steep mountainous terrain, HAND values increase rapidly with distance from the stream, and the flat-water assumption underlying HAND-based flood mapping becomes less valid. Mountain streams have steep gradients, turbulent flow, and water surfaces that slope significantly in the downstream direction. A cell with HAND = 3 m may be safe if it is upstream of a steep reach (where the water surface is lower than the average channel elevation) or flooded if it is upstream of a constriction that causes backwater effects.

Despite these limitations, HAND remains useful for mountain flood mapping because it identifies the valley bottoms and narrow floodplains where mountain communities are located. In the Hindu Kush, the Andes, the East African Rift, and the Carpathian Mountains, settlements cluster in valley floors where HAND values are low, and HAND mapping provides at least a first-order identification of vulnerable locations.

## 14.12 Integration with Flood Inundation Mapping

### 14.12.1 The Bridge to Chapter 15

HAND is not, by itself, a flood inundation map. It is the terrain foundation upon which flood inundation maps are built. The critical step that turns a HAND raster into a flood inundation map is the addition of water depth information: how deep is the water in each stream reach during a given flood event?

This water depth information can come from several sources:

- **Hydrological model output**: The US National Water Model, EFAS, or any rainfall-runoff model that produces discharge forecasts for individual stream reaches
- **Hydraulic model output**: HEC-RAS, MIKE 11/21, or LISFLOOD simulations that compute water surface profiles
- **Gauge observations**: Real-time water level readings from OVF stations (Hungary), USGS gauges (US), or any national hydrometric network
- **Historical records**: Observed flood levels for design events (100-year flood, 500-year flood)

Given the water depth $h$ in a stream reach, every cell draining to that reach with HAND $< h$ is classified as inundated, and the inundation depth at each cell is $h - \text{HAND}$. This simple arithmetic, repeated for every reach in the network, produces a flood inundation map for the entire domain. Chapter 15 develops this approach in full detail, including the construction of synthetic rating curves from HAND, the conversion between discharge and depth, and the assembly of reach-level inundation maps into watershed-scale and national-scale flood maps.

### 14.12.2 HAND as Infrastructure

The key insight of the HAND approach is that the expensive terrain processing -- DEM conditioning, flow direction computation, stream derivation, and HAND computation itself -- needs to be done only once for a given DEM. The resulting HAND raster is then a permanent piece of infrastructure that can be combined with any flood scenario, from any source, at any time. When a new flood forecast arrives from the National Water Model or EFAS, converting it to an inundation map requires only a simple comparison of predicted water depth against pre-computed HAND values -- an operation that can be performed in seconds.

This "pre-compute the terrain, apply the forecast in real time" architecture is what enables HAND-based flood mapping at continental scale with update cycles of minutes rather than hours. It is the foundation of the operational flood inundation mapping systems described in Chapter 15 and the real-time flood forecasting systems described in Chapter 16.

## 14.13 Key Properties and Applications Summary

HAND has several important properties that together explain its emergence as a cornerstone of operational hydroinformatics:

**Physical interpretability.** Unlike many terrain-derived indices (wetness index, topographic position index, slope-area diagrams), HAND has a direct physical meaning: it is the minimum flood depth in the nearest stream required to inundate a point. This makes it immediately understandable to engineers, planners, emergency managers, and the public.

**Computational efficiency.** HAND requires a single pass through the DEM (plus the prerequisite terrain processing chain shared with many other applications). Once computed, it is a static product that can be reused indefinitely with different flood scenarios.

**Scalability.** HAND has been computed at scales from individual small catchments to the entire continental United States. The algorithm is inherently parallelizable, enabling the use of multi-core workstations and HPC clusters for large-area processing.

**Data parsimony.** HAND requires only a DEM and a stream network definition. It does not require surveyed cross-sections, Manning's roughness coefficients, boundary conditions, or calibration data -- all of which are needed by detailed hydraulic models and are unavailable for most of the world's rivers.

**Compatibility with real-time forecasting.** The pre-computed HAND raster can be combined with real-time water depth forecasts to produce inundation maps in seconds. This speed is essential for operational flood warning systems where every minute counts.

These properties make HAND an indispensable tool in the modern hydroinformatician's toolkit. It does not replace detailed hydraulic modeling -- it complements it by providing rapid, scalable, first-order flood vulnerability assessment that can be applied anywhere, anytime, with minimal data requirements.
