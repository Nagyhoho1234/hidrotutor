# Chapter 4: GIS as a Water Tool

Water professionals do not need to become GIS experts. They need to understand what GIS can do for them -- what questions it can answer, what workflows it enables, and where it fits in the larger ecosystem of water resources analysis. This chapter is not a software manual. It is a capabilities overview: a practical tour of what geographic information systems offer to anyone working with water, from the engineer designing a stormwater network to the researcher modeling climate change impacts on a transboundary river basin.

The previous chapters established why hydroinformatics matters (Chapter 1), how we moved from paper maps to digital representations (Chapter 2), and where the data lives (Chapter 3). This chapter bridges the gap between having data and doing something useful with it. GIS is the workbench where water data becomes water knowledge -- where a digital elevation model becomes a watershed boundary, where a table of stream gauge coordinates becomes a map of monitoring coverage, where a flood inundation grid becomes an estimate of affected population.

The treatment here deliberately spans the full GIS ecosystem: desktop software like ArcGIS Pro and QGIS, server-based systems for enterprise data management, cloud platforms like Google Earth Engine and Microsoft Planetary Computer that put petabytes of data at your fingertips, and mobile applications that bring GIS to the field. The water professional of 2026 does not work with a single tool -- they move fluidly between desktop, cloud, and mobile environments depending on the task at hand.

We begin with the three fundamental views of GIS -- how it stores data, how it shows data, and how it analyses data -- and then work through the practical building blocks: geodatabases, vector and raster representations, symbology, querying, and the construction of a watershed base map. By the end, you should have a clear mental model of what GIS can do for your water work, even if you have never opened the software.

## 4.1 Three Views of GIS

The power of a geographic information system lies not in any single capability but in the integration of three complementary functions, introduced briefly in Section 1.4. Every modern GIS platform -- whether commercial or open-source, desktop or cloud-based -- organizes its capabilities around three core views: the geodatabase view for data management, the geovisualization view for mapping and display, and the geoprocessing view for spatial analysis. Understanding these three views is the key to understanding what GIS can do for water resources work.

### 4.1.1 The Geodatabase View: Data Management

The geodatabase view treats GIS as an intelligent spatial database. At its core, a GIS stores geographic features -- points, lines, polygons, and grids -- along with their attributes in structured collections. This is more than simple file storage. A geodatabase enforces rules: it ensures that stream network features connect properly at junctions, that watershed boundaries do not overlap, that coordinate systems are consistent across datasets, and that relationships between features are maintained. When you store a stream gauge as a point feature and the river reach it monitors as a line feature, the geodatabase can maintain the explicit relationship between them, so that querying the gauge automatically retrieves information about its reach, and vice versa.

For water professionals, the geodatabase view answers a fundamental organizational question: how do I keep all my spatial data -- DEMs, stream networks, watershed boundaries, gauge locations, soil maps, land cover grids -- organized, consistent, and accessible? The alternative is a folder full of shapefiles with cryptic names, inconsistent coordinate systems, and no documented relationships. Anyone who has inherited a colleague's project folder knows the difference.

The geodatabase view also handles data integrity. Feature datasets within a geodatabase enforce a common coordinate system for all their contents, so you cannot accidentally mix UTM Zone 34N data with UTM Zone 33N data in the same container. Domains restrict attribute values to valid ranges -- a field for Strahler stream order might be constrained to integers between 1 and 12, preventing data entry errors. Subtypes allow a single feature class to contain categorized features -- perennial and intermittent streams in the same river network layer, for instance -- with different default values and validation rules for each category.

### 4.1.2 The Geovisualization View: Mapping and Display

The geovisualization view treats GIS as a cartographic engine. This is often the first capability people associate with GIS: making maps. But modern geovisualization goes far beyond producing a static map for a report. It encompasses interactive exploration, dynamic symbolization, multi-scale display, three-dimensional visualization, and temporal animation.

For water resources work, geovisualization is the primary tool for understanding spatial patterns. A table of discharge values at 200 gauging stations is difficult to interpret; the same data displayed as graduated symbols on a map immediately reveals which river basins are wet and which are dry. A spreadsheet listing flood depths at 10,000 grid cells conveys nothing; a color-ramped inundation map overlaid on an aerial photograph tells you instantly which neighborhoods are underwater. The human visual system is extraordinarily good at detecting spatial patterns -- clusters, gradients, outliers, boundaries -- and geovisualization harnesses this ability.

Modern GIS platforms support sophisticated visualization techniques that are particularly valuable for hydrology. Hillshade rendering transforms a flat-looking DEM into a realistic terrain visualization where drainage patterns become immediately visible. Transparency controls allow you to overlay a flood inundation layer on satellite imagery, so you can see both the flood extent and the buildings it affects. Time-aware layers can animate river levels over the course of a flood event, or show how a drought develops week by week across a continent. Three-dimensional visualization can drape a watershed map over its DEM, making the relationship between topography and drainage patterns intuitive.

### 4.1.3 The Geoprocessing View: Spatial Analysis

The geoprocessing view treats GIS as an analytical engine. This is where the real power lies for water professionals. Geoprocessing tools take spatial data as input, perform computations that account for geographic relationships, and produce new spatial data as output. The key word is "spatial" -- these are operations that depend fundamentally on the locations and geometric relationships of features, not just their attributes.

For hydrology, geoprocessing is transformative. From a DEM, geoprocessing tools can compute slope and aspect at every cell, determine the direction water would flow across each cell, accumulate those flows to identify where streams form, trace upstream boundaries to delineate watersheds, and calculate morphometric parameters like drainage density and basin shape. These operations, which would take weeks by hand on a paper map, run in minutes on a modern computer. The entire workflow from raw elevation data to a complete watershed characterization is a geoprocessing chain -- a sequence of tools where each step's output feeds the next step's input.

Beyond terrain analysis, geoprocessing provides the spatial overlay operations that are central to water resources planning. Intersecting a flood zone with a parcel map produces a list of properties at risk. Buffering a stream network by its riparian setback distance identifies land use conflicts. Clipping a continental land cover dataset to a watershed boundary extracts the information relevant to your study area. Dissolving sub-watershed polygons by their parent watershed code aggregates detailed data to a coarser reporting level. These operations are the bread and butter of GIS-based water analysis, and they are described in detail in Chapters 5 through 12 as we work through specific hydrological workflows.

## 4.2 The GIS Ecosystem in 2026

The GIS landscape has changed dramatically over the past decade. What was once a single desktop application has expanded into an ecosystem of interconnected platforms spanning desktop, server, cloud, and mobile environments. A water professional in 2026 might use a desktop GIS to build a detailed hydraulic model, a cloud platform to process satellite imagery over a continental river basin, a web GIS to share flood risk maps with municipal planners, and a mobile app to collect field data at gauge locations -- all within the same project. Understanding this ecosystem is essential for choosing the right tool for each task.

### 4.2.1 Desktop GIS: The Professional Workbench

Desktop GIS remains the workhorse for detailed spatial analysis, cartographic production, and model building. Two platforms dominate the landscape: ESRI's ArcGIS Pro (commercial) and QGIS (open-source). Both are mature, feature-rich systems capable of handling the full range of water resources GIS tasks, from basic mapping to advanced hydrological modeling.

**ArcGIS Pro** is the current-generation desktop platform from ESRI, the company that has dominated the commercial GIS market since the 1980s. It replaced the long-running ArcMap application and brought a modernized interface, 64-bit processing, and tighter integration with ESRI's cloud platform (ArcGIS Online). For water resources specifically, ArcGIS Pro offers the Spatial Analyst extension for raster-based terrain analysis, the Arc Hydro toolset for hydrological data modeling (discussed in Section 4.8), and seamless access to ESRI's global data services. ArcGIS Pro organizes all work around a project -- a single folder containing the project file (`.aprx`), a default geodatabase, and a toolbox. This unified structure keeps all data, maps, and analysis tools together in one place.

The licensing model is subscription-based and tied to organizational accounts. University students typically access ArcGIS Pro through institutional site licenses. For professional organizations, costs are significant but include access to the entire ESRI ecosystem: desktop, server, online, and mobile platforms.

**QGIS** (formerly Quantum GIS) is the leading open-source desktop GIS, developed by a global community of contributors and released under the GNU General Public License. QGIS has matured enormously since its early days as a simple map viewer. The current long-term release provides capabilities that rival ArcGIS Pro for most water resources tasks: full support for vector and raster data, sophisticated cartographic tools, a powerful plugin ecosystem, and a built-in graphical modeler for automating workflows. QGIS reads and writes virtually every spatial data format in common use, including ESRI geodatabases (read access), GeoPackages (the OGC standard that serves as QGIS's native format), shapefiles, GeoTIFF, NetCDF, and dozens of others.

For hydrological analysis, QGIS offers several pathways. The GRASS GIS integration provides a comprehensive set of terrain analysis and watershed delineation tools through the Processing framework. The SAGA GIS tools, also accessible through Processing, offer additional hydrological algorithms. Dedicated plugins such as the QGIS HEC-RAS plugin, the PCRaster plugin for distributed hydrological modeling, and the WhiteboxTools plugin for advanced geomorphometric analysis extend QGIS's capabilities into specialized territory. QGIS also supports Python scripting through PyQGIS, enabling automation of complex workflows.

The practical difference between ArcGIS Pro and QGIS for water resources work has narrowed considerably. ArcGIS Pro retains advantages in enterprise data management (the file geodatabase and enterprise geodatabase remain industry standards), integration with ESRI's cloud services, and the availability of specialized extensions like Arc Hydro. QGIS offers the advantages of zero cost, platform independence (it runs on Windows, macOS, and Linux), transparency of algorithms, and a rapidly growing plugin ecosystem. Many water professionals use both: QGIS for day-to-day analysis and visualization, ArcGIS Pro for tasks that require specific ESRI tools or enterprise integration.

### 4.2.2 Server GIS: Enterprise Data Management

Server-based GIS fills a different need: managing large spatial databases, serving maps and data to many users simultaneously, and running geoprocessing tools on centralized infrastructure. In a water management agency or a national hydrological service, server GIS is the backbone that makes spatial data accessible across the organization.

**ArcGIS Enterprise** (formerly ArcGIS Server) is ESRI's server platform. It provides a web-based portal for publishing, discovering, and using geographic information across an organization. Water agencies use it to publish flood hazard maps, stream gauge data, and watershed boundaries as web services that can be consumed by desktop GIS clients, web applications, and mobile apps. The enterprise geodatabase -- backed by a relational database management system like PostgreSQL or Microsoft SQL Server -- handles multi-user editing, versioning, and concurrent access to large datasets.

**GeoServer** is the open-source equivalent, developed under the Open Geospatial Consortium (OGC) standards. GeoServer publishes spatial data as Web Map Services (WMS), Web Feature Services (WFS), and Web Coverage Services (WCS) that any OGC-compliant client can consume. Many national water agencies, particularly in Europe, use GeoServer to comply with the INSPIRE Directive, which requires EU member states to publish spatial data through standardized web services. Hungary's national spatial data infrastructure, accessible through the Lechner Knowledge Centre, relies on OGC-standard services that can be consumed by any GIS client.

For most water professionals, server GIS operates invisibly in the background. When you add a web service layer to your desktop GIS -- a basemap, a real-time gauge network, a flood warning layer -- you are consuming data from a server GIS. Understanding that this infrastructure exists helps you appreciate why certain data layers are available as services rather than downloads, and why some operations (like real-time flood map updates) require server infrastructure rather than desktop tools.

### 4.2.3 Cloud GIS: Planetary-Scale Analysis

Cloud-based GIS platforms represent the most significant shift in the GIS ecosystem since the move from command-line to graphical interfaces. Instead of downloading data to your computer and running analysis locally, cloud GIS brings the computation to the data. This matters enormously for water resources work, where the datasets involved -- satellite imagery archives, global DEMs, continental-scale climate reanalysis -- can be measured in petabytes.

**Google Earth Engine (GEE)** is the platform that pioneered this approach. Launched in 2010, GEE provides free access to a multi-petabyte archive of satellite imagery and geospatial datasets, along with a cloud-based computational infrastructure for analyzing them. For water resources, GEE is transformative. Tasks that would be impractical on a desktop -- classifying land cover across an entire river basin from 20 years of Landsat imagery, computing monthly precipitation anomalies from global gridded data, mapping surface water extent changes across a continent -- become feasible in hours or even minutes. GEE's JavaScript and Python APIs allow users to write analysis scripts that execute on Google's servers, with the data never leaving the cloud. The JRC Global Surface Water dataset, which maps surface water dynamics at 30-metre resolution for the entire Earth from 1984 to the present, was computed entirely on Google Earth Engine.

**Microsoft Planetary Computer** offers a similar cloud-based approach, built on the Azure cloud infrastructure and the STAC (SpatioTemporal Asset Catalog) standard. Planetary Computer hosts an extensive collection of environmental datasets -- Sentinel imagery, global DEMs, land cover maps, climate data -- and provides a JupyterHub environment for analysis. Its integration with the broader Python geospatial ecosystem (xarray, rioxarray, dask) makes it particularly attractive for users comfortable with Python-based scientific computing. For water resources, Planetary Computer provides direct access to the Copernicus DEM, Sentinel-2 imagery for water body mapping, ERA5 climate reanalysis data, and many other datasets relevant to hydrological analysis.

**ArcGIS Online** is ESRI's cloud platform, serving both as a content management system for sharing maps and as a platform for cloud-based analysis. For water resources, ArcGIS Online provides several ready-to-use tools that require no local data or processing: watershed delineation from cloud-hosted elevation data, elevation profile generation, and viewshed analysis. The watershed delineation tool is particularly noteworthy -- you click a point on the map, and the system delineates the upstream watershed in seconds using pre-processed global elevation data. For quick reconnaissance studies or for teaching purposes, this capability is remarkably powerful.

The choice between cloud platforms depends on the task, the scale, and the user's programming comfort. GEE excels at large-scale remote sensing analysis with its JavaScript-first API. Planetary Computer integrates well with Python scientific workflows. ArcGIS Online offers point-and-click cloud analysis without programming. All three provide access to global datasets that would be impractical to download and process locally.

### 4.2.4 Mobile GIS: Data Collection in the Field

Mobile GIS closes the loop between office analysis and field reality. Water resources work frequently requires field visits -- verifying stream locations, documenting flood damage, inspecting infrastructure, collecting water samples at georeferenced locations. Mobile GIS applications turn a smartphone or tablet into a data collection device that records geographic coordinates, photographs, and attribute information simultaneously, then synchronizes the collected data with the office GIS.

**ArcGIS Field Maps** (which consolidated the earlier Collector and Explorer apps) is ESRI's mobile platform. It allows field workers to view maps, navigate to features of interest, collect new features with GPS coordinates, update existing feature attributes, and capture photographs linked to specific locations. For water agencies, this means that a field team inspecting culverts after a flood can systematically record the location, condition, and photograph of each structure, with the data flowing directly into the enterprise geodatabase.

**QField** is the mobile companion to QGIS. It allows QGIS projects to be transferred to a mobile device for field data collection, with synchronization back to the desktop project. QField supports offline operation -- essential for fieldwork in remote areas without cellular coverage, which describes many watershed headwater areas. The QFieldCloud service provides synchronization infrastructure for teams.

**KoBoToolbox** and **ODK (Open Data Kit)** are open-source mobile data collection platforms widely used in developing countries for water, sanitation, and hygiene (WASH) surveys. While not full GIS applications, they support georeferenced data collection and integrate with GIS through standard data formats. For international water projects, particularly those funded by development agencies, these tools are often the primary means of spatial data collection.

## 4.3 The Geodatabase: Organizing Water Data

The geodatabase is the central concept in modern GIS data management. It is not merely a file format -- it is an architectural philosophy for organizing spatial information so that the relationships between geographic features are preserved, data integrity is enforced, and complex real-world systems can be modeled coherently. For water resources, where every feature is related to every other feature through the physical logic of the hydrological cycle, this organizational capability is not a luxury -- it is a necessity.

### 4.3.1 Feature Classes

A **feature class** (objektumosztaly) is a collection of geographic features that share the same geometry type (point, line, or polygon) and the same set of attributes. Think of it as a table in a database where every row represents a geographic object and every row has the same columns. The crucial difference from an ordinary database table is that one of those columns -- the Shape field -- contains the spatial geometry of the feature: the coordinates that define where it is and what shape it has.

In water resources, the most common feature classes map directly to physical entities. Stream reaches are stored as polyline feature classes, where each feature represents a segment of river between confluences. Watershed boundaries are stored as polygon feature classes, where each feature represents a drainage area. Stream gauges, wells, and sampling points are stored as point feature classes, where each feature represents a monitoring location. Cross-sections are stored as polyline feature classes, where each feature represents a surveyed transect across a channel.

Every feature in a feature class has a unique ObjectID assigned automatically by the geodatabase, plus a Shape field managed by the system. Beyond these system fields, you define whatever attribute fields the application requires. A stream reach feature class might include fields for reach name, Strahler order, length, mean annual discharge, contributing drainage area, and slope. A watershed polygon feature class might include fields for watershed code, name, total area, mean elevation, land cover percentages, and mean annual precipitation.

Feature classes can be stored directly in a geodatabase or grouped within feature datasets (Section 4.3.3). A standalone feature class defines its own coordinate system. A feature class inside a feature dataset inherits the coordinate system of the dataset, which enforces spatial consistency among related layers.

### 4.3.2 Raster Datasets

**Raster datasets** (raszteres adatok) store gridded information -- values arranged in a regular matrix of rows and columns, where each cell represents a fixed area on the ground. In water resources, raster datasets are pervasive. Digital elevation models are rasters. Rainfall grids are rasters. Land cover classifications are rasters. Soil property maps are rasters. Flow direction grids, flow accumulation grids, and watershed delineation grids are all rasters produced by geoprocessing operations on other rasters.

A raster dataset is defined by several fundamental properties. The **cell size** (or spatial resolution) determines how much ground each cell represents -- a 30-metre DEM has cells that are 30 by 30 metres on the ground, with each cell containing a single elevation value. The **extent** defines the geographic area covered -- the coordinates of the four corners of the grid. The **number of bands** indicates whether the raster contains a single value per cell (like an elevation model) or multiple values (like a multispectral satellite image with red, green, blue, and near-infrared bands). The **bit depth** determines the range of values each cell can store -- an 8-bit raster can hold integers from 0 to 255, while a 32-bit floating-point raster can hold decimal values with high precision.

A geodatabase can store raster datasets alongside vector feature classes, which is critical for water resources work where raster and vector data must be used together. The DEM (raster) defines the terrain from which stream networks (vector lines) and watershed boundaries (vector polygons) are derived. Storing them in the same geodatabase ensures they are managed together and that their spatial relationships are maintained.

### 4.3.3 Feature Datasets and Tables

A **feature dataset** is a container within a geodatabase that groups related feature classes under a single coordinate system. This is a deceptively simple concept with profound practical implications. When you create a feature dataset and assign it a coordinate system -- say, UTM Zone 34N with the ETRS89 datum -- every feature class you create within that dataset inherits that coordinate system. Data imported into the feature dataset from a different coordinate system is automatically reprojected. This eliminates one of the most common sources of error in GIS projects: mismatched coordinate systems between layers that are supposed to align.

For water resources projects, a feature dataset typically groups all the thematic layers for a study area: the watershed boundary, sub-watershed boundaries, stream network, gauge locations, cross-section lines, and any other vector features. By placing them all in a feature dataset with an appropriate projected coordinate system (projected, not geographic, so that length and area calculations produce values in metres and square metres rather than degrees), you guarantee spatial consistency across the entire project.

**Tables** (also called object classes) are the simplest containers in a geodatabase. A table is a collection of rows with the same attributes, where each row has a unique ObjectID, but -- critically -- no geometry. Tables store non-spatial data that relates to spatial features: time series of discharge measurements at gauge locations, water quality sampling results, climate data records, calibration parameters for hydrological models. The power comes from linking these tables to feature classes through relationships and joins.

**Relationships** in a geodatabase formalize the connections between tables and feature classes. A one-to-many relationship between a gauge feature class and a discharge measurement table says: "each gauge has many measurements, and each measurement belongs to one gauge." This relationship allows you to click on a gauge in the map and immediately retrieve its entire measurement history, or to query the measurement table and highlight the corresponding gauges on the map. For water resources databases, relationships are essential -- the physical system is a network of interconnected entities, and the data model should reflect that.

### 4.3.4 Geodatabase Formats

In practice, water professionals encounter several geodatabase formats, each suited to different contexts.

The **file geodatabase** (a folder with a `.gdb` extension) is the most common format for desktop work. It stores all data types -- feature classes, raster datasets, tables, relationships -- in a proprietary but efficient binary format. File geodatabases have no practical size limit for individual datasets (up to 1 TB per table by default, expandable to 256 TB), support sophisticated data types, and offer good performance for single-user access. For most water resources projects, the file geodatabase is the default choice.

The **GeoPackage** (a single file with a `.gpkg` extension) is the OGC-standard alternative. Based on SQLite, it stores vector features, raster tiles, and attribute data in a single, portable file. GeoPackage is the native format of QGIS and is supported by virtually all GIS software, including ArcGIS Pro. For projects that need to work across software platforms -- sharing data between ArcGIS Pro and QGIS users, for example -- GeoPackage is the safest choice. Its main limitation compared to the file geodatabase is the lack of support for some advanced features like topology rules and geometric networks.

The **enterprise geodatabase** (backed by PostgreSQL/PostGIS, Microsoft SQL Server, or Oracle) serves multi-user environments where dozens of analysts need concurrent access to the same datasets. Water management agencies and national hydrological services typically use enterprise geodatabases for their operational data. The PostGIS extension for PostgreSQL is particularly popular in the open-source world, as it provides full spatial database capabilities at no licensing cost.

The **shapefile** deserves mention despite being a legacy format, because it remains ubiquitous in data exchange. A shapefile is not a single file but a collection of files (`.shp` for geometry, `.dbf` for attributes, `.shx` for index, `.prj` for projection information, and several others). Shapefiles have significant limitations -- field names are truncated to 10 characters, file size is capped at 2 GB, there is no support for null values, date-time handling is poor, and there is no way to store multiple geometry types in one file. Despite these limitations, shapefiles are understood by every GIS application ever written, which ensures their continued use for data exchange. The professional recommendation is clear: use GeoPackage or file geodatabase for your working data; export to shapefile only when the recipient requires it.

## 4.4 Vector and Raster: Two Views of the Water World

Water exists in the landscape in two fundamentally different ways, and GIS represents them with two fundamentally different data models. Rivers, lakes, and watershed boundaries are discrete objects with definable edges -- they lend themselves to vector representation. Elevation, rainfall, soil moisture, and temperature are continuous fields that vary across space without sharp boundaries -- they lend themselves to raster representation. Understanding when to use each model, and how they work together, is essential for water resources GIS.

### 4.4.1 Streams as Lines

A stream network is naturally represented as a collection of polyline features. Each reach -- the segment of stream between two confluences, or between a source and the first confluence -- is a single feature with a defined geometry (a sequence of coordinate vertices tracing the channel path) and a set of attributes (name, length, Strahler order, mean discharge, slope, contributing area).

The topology of a stream network matters as much as its geometry. Topology describes connectivity: which reaches connect to which, where confluences occur, and in what direction water flows. In a well-constructed stream network, every upstream reach connects precisely to its downstream reach at a shared node. This connectivity enables network tracing -- the ability to start at any point in the network and trace upstream to find all contributing reaches, or trace downstream to find all reaches that receive water from a given point. Network tracing is fundamental to water resources analysis: it is how you determine what is upstream of a water supply intake, what is downstream of a pollution source, or how a flood wave propagates through a channel network.

The NHDPlus dataset in the United States is the canonical example of a hydrologically connected stream network. Every reach has a unique identifier (NHDPlusID), a from-node and to-node defining connectivity, a flow direction, and estimated flow attributes. The EU-Hydro dataset provides similar functionality for Europe, with connected river network features derived from Sentinel-2 satellite imagery and Copernicus DEM data. Globally, the HydroRIVERS dataset (part of the HydroSHEDS family) provides a vectorized stream network for the entire planet, with attributes including discharge estimates, Strahler order, and upstream drainage area.

### 4.4.2 Watersheds as Polygons

A watershed -- the area of land that drains to a common outlet point -- is naturally represented as a polygon feature. The boundary of a watershed follows ridgelines and drainage divides across the terrain, enclosing all the land from which water flows to the outlet. In a GIS, a watershed dataset is a polygon feature class where each polygon represents a drainage area, with attributes describing its physical and hydrological characteristics.

Watershed polygons form nested hierarchies. A large river basin contains sub-basins, which contain sub-sub-basins, and so on. In the United States, this hierarchy is formalized as the Hydrologic Unit Code (HUC) system: HUC2 regions, HUC4 sub-regions, HUC6 basins, HUC8 sub-basins, HUC10 watersheds, and HUC12 sub-watersheds. Each level nests within the level above -- every HUC12 polygon lies within exactly one HUC10 polygon, every HUC10 within a HUC8, and so on. In the European Union, the Water Framework Directive defines a parallel hierarchy of river basin districts, sub-basins, and water bodies. Hungary's 12 vízgyűjtő-gazdálkodási tervezési alegység (water management sub-units) map to this EU framework. Globally, HydroBASINS provides 12 levels of nested watershed boundaries for the entire planet, derived from the HydroSHEDS framework.

The critical GIS property of watershed polygons is that they partition the landscape without gaps or overlaps. Every point on the land surface belongs to exactly one watershed at each level of the hierarchy. This means that any spatially distributed quantity -- rainfall, land cover, soil type, population -- can be unambiguously aggregated to watershed units by spatial overlay. This overlay capability is what makes GIS indispensable for watershed-scale analysis: it provides the mechanism for translating gridded or point data into the lumped watershed parameters that hydrological models require.

### 4.4.3 DEMs as Grids

The digital elevation model is the most important raster dataset in hydrology. A DEM is a regular grid of elevation values -- each cell stores a single number representing the height of the terrain surface at that location. From this apparently simple data structure, an extraordinary amount of hydrological information can be derived through geoprocessing.

The spatial resolution of a DEM determines both what it can resolve and how much data it contains. At 90-metre resolution (the original public SRTM data), a 50,000 km$^2$ watershed contains approximately 6.2 million cells -- manageable on any modern computer but too coarse to resolve small tributaries or urban drainage features. At 30-metre resolution (SRTM 1-arc-second, Copernicus GLO-30, or the US National Elevation Dataset), the same watershed contains about 55.6 million cells -- still manageable but requiring attention to processing efficiency. At 1-metre resolution (LiDAR-derived), the cell count explodes to approximately 50 billion cells -- requiring specialized data management, tiled processing, and significant computational resources.

The vertical accuracy of a DEM matters as much as its horizontal resolution for hydrological applications. A DEM with 10-metre horizontal resolution but 5-metre vertical error will produce unreliable flow directions in flat terrain, because the errors are larger than the actual elevation differences that drive drainage patterns. Modern DEMs achieve vertical accuracies of 1-2 metres (Copernicus DEM, ALOS World 3D) to 10-15 centimetres (LiDAR). The MERIT DEM specifically addresses this by removing systematic error components from SRTM data, producing a cleaner DEM for hydrological applications even at relatively coarse resolution.

For Hungarian applications, several DEM options exist at different resolutions. The DDM-5, produced by the Lechner Knowledge Centre, provides 5-metre resolution coverage of the entire country in the Hungarian EOV (Egységes Országos Vetületi rendszer, EPSG:23700) projection -- the highest-resolution nationally consistent DEM available, though it must be ordered through Lechner's data services rather than downloaded freely. The Copernicus EEA-10 provides 10-metre resolution coverage for 39 European countries including Hungary, available to qualified users. For quick analyses, the freely downloadable Copernicus GLO-30 (30-metre resolution) provides adequate quality for most watershed-scale studies.

### 4.4.4 When Vector Meets Raster

In practice, water resources analysis constantly moves between vector and raster representations. A typical workflow might begin with a raster DEM, derive a vector stream network from it, delineate vector watershed boundaries, then overlay those vector watersheds on raster rainfall data to compute mean areal precipitation for each watershed. The ability to convert between representations -- rasterizing vector features, vectorizing raster zones -- and to perform overlay operations across representations is one of GIS's core strengths.

The conversion from raster to vector is common in hydrology. After computing flow accumulation on a DEM grid, cells with accumulation above a threshold are flagged as stream cells. These raster stream cells are then converted to vector polylines to create a stream network that can carry attributes and participate in network analysis. Similarly, watershed zones delineated as raster grids (where each cell is labeled with its watershed ID) are converted to vector polygons for attribution, area calculation, and spatial overlay.

The conversion from vector to raster is equally common. A land cover polygon dataset might be rasterized to match the cell size of a DEM, so that both can be used together in a raster-based hydrological model. Watershed boundaries might be rasterized to create a mask for extracting DEM subsets. The key parameter in any rasterization is the cell size: it must be chosen to match the analysis resolution, which is usually determined by the DEM cell size.

The old adage "vector is corrector, raster is faster" captures a real trade-off. Vector features preserve geometric precision -- a stream channel follows its true path, a watershed boundary traces the exact ridgeline. Raster features approximate geometry as collections of square cells -- a stream becomes a staircase pattern, a curved watershed boundary becomes a jagged edge. But raster operations are computationally simple (apply the same operation to every cell) and naturally parallel, while vector operations (computing intersections, overlays, buffers) require complex geometric algorithms. For large-scale hydrological modeling, where millions of cells must be processed through dozens of geoprocessing steps, raster efficiency often outweighs vector precision. The water professional's task is to choose the appropriate representation for each stage of the analysis.

## 4.5 Seeing the Data: Symbology and Visualization

Having data in a geodatabase is only the beginning. The data must be rendered visually before it becomes useful for interpretation, communication, and decision-making. **Symbology** (szimbologia) -- the mapping of data values to visual properties like color, size, shape, and transparency -- is the mechanism that transforms raw GIS data into meaningful maps. For water professionals, effective symbology is not an aesthetic concern -- it is an analytical tool that can reveal spatial patterns, highlight anomalies, and communicate risk.

### 4.5.1 Single Symbol and Categorical Display

The simplest symbology assigns a single symbol to all features in a layer. Every stream reach gets the same blue line, every watershed gets the same green polygon outline, every gauge gets the same red dot. Single-symbol display is appropriate when the purpose is simply to show where features are -- the locations of all monitoring stations in a network, for instance, or the extent of a stream network within a study area.

Categorical display (called "unique values" in most GIS software) assigns a different symbol to each distinct value of a chosen attribute field. For water resources, this is useful for distinguishing perennial from intermittent streams (based on a flow permanence attribute), for showing different watershed management units in different colors (based on a watershed code), or for classifying land cover types (forest, agriculture, urban, wetland) with intuitive colors. The choice of color scheme matters: conventional expectations -- blue for water, green for vegetation, brown for bare ground, red for urban -- should be respected unless there is a compelling reason to deviate. Both ArcGIS Pro and QGIS provide predefined color ramps and schemes that follow cartographic conventions.

In QGIS, categorical symbology is configured through the Layer Properties dialog under the Symbology tab, where the "Categorized" renderer serves the same function as ArcGIS Pro's "Unique Values." QGIS additionally offers a "Rule-based" renderer that allows more complex classification logic -- for example, displaying streams with different line widths based on Strahler order AND different colors based on water quality status, using compound rules.

### 4.5.2 Graduated Symbology: Turning Numbers into Visual Patterns

Graduated symbology maps a continuous numeric attribute to a visual variable -- color, size, or both. This is where symbology becomes a genuine analytical tool. Consider a dataset of stream gauges, each with a mean annual discharge value. Displaying them as graduated symbols -- small circles for low-flow stations, large circles for high-flow stations -- immediately reveals the spatial structure of the flow regime: which tributaries contribute the most water, where the main stem grows, where the dry margins of the basin lie. The same data in a spreadsheet would take careful examination to extract this spatial pattern; on a map, it is visible at a glance.

For polygon features like watersheds, graduated color (choropleth) maps are the standard approach. Coloring sub-watersheds by mean annual precipitation, with a sequential color ramp from yellow (dry) through green to blue (wet), reveals the spatial precipitation gradient across a basin. Coloring them by percent impervious surface reveals the urbanization pattern. Coloring them by specific discharge (discharge per unit area) reveals which parts of the basin generate the most runoff per unit area -- information that is not obvious from the raw discharge values alone.

The classification method matters. Equal interval classification divides the data range into equal-width classes -- simple but often ineffective when data are skewed. Quantile classification puts the same number of features in each class -- good for visual balance but potentially misleading when it splits natural groupings. Natural breaks (Jenks) classification identifies natural clusters in the data -- usually the best default choice for exploratory mapping. Standard deviation classification shows departure from the mean -- useful for identifying anomalies. The water professional should experiment with different classification methods on the same data to see which reveals the most useful pattern, rather than accepting the default.

### 4.5.3 Raster Symbology: Visualizing Continuous Surfaces

Raster datasets require their own symbology approaches. A DEM can be displayed with a continuous color ramp from green (low elevation) through yellow and brown to white (high peaks), making the terrain structure immediately visible. Adding a hillshade layer -- a simulated illumination of the surface based on slope and aspect -- beneath a semi-transparent elevation color ramp produces the familiar shaded relief visualization that makes drainage patterns, ridgelines, and valley floors jump off the screen.

For analytical rasters like flow accumulation (which shows how many upstream cells drain through each cell), logarithmic color scales are often necessary because the values span many orders of magnitude -- headwater cells have values near 1, while the main river channel might have values of several million. Displaying flow accumulation with a linear color ramp produces a map that appears uniformly dark except for the main channels; a logarithmic or quantile classification reveals the full hierarchy of the drainage network.

Land cover rasters and other classified (categorical) rasters use unique-value symbology, where each integer class receives a specific color. International standards for land cover color schemes -- such as the CORINE Land Cover color palette used across Europe -- ensure consistency across maps and studies.

Both ArcGIS Pro and QGIS provide raster visualization tools including stretch types (minimum-maximum, standard deviation, percent clip), color ramps, hillshade generation, and transparency control. QGIS offers the additional ability to create virtual raster layers that combine multiple visualizations (e.g., hillshade and elevation color) without modifying the underlying data, through its raster rendering pipeline.

## 4.6 Selecting and Querying: Asking Questions of the Data

A GIS is ultimately a question-answering machine. The data is the knowledge base; queries are the questions. For water professionals, the ability to select specific features and query attribute values is not just a convenience -- it is the mechanism by which general datasets are filtered to produce project-specific information. Starting with a national stream network containing millions of reaches, you need to extract just the reaches within your study watershed. Starting with a national gauge database containing thousands of stations, you need to find the ones that have at least 30 years of daily discharge records within 50 kilometres of your project site.

### 4.6.1 Selection by Attributes

Attribute selection (or attribute query) selects features based on the values in their attribute table, using SQL-like expressions. The logic is straightforward: specify a field, an operator, and a value. In ArcGIS Pro, this is done through the "Select by Attributes" tool; in QGIS, through the "Select by Expression" dialog.

Simple queries test a single condition. "Select all watersheds where AREA_KM2 > 1000" retrieves the large sub-basins. "Select all gauges where RECORD_LENGTH >= 30" retrieves stations with adequate historical records. "Select all stream reaches where STRAHLER_ORDER = 1" retrieves headwater streams. "Select all water quality stations where NITRATE_MG_L > 50" retrieves stations exceeding the EU Nitrates Directive threshold of 50 mg/L.

Compound queries combine conditions with AND, OR, and NOT. "Select all gauges where RECORD_LENGTH >= 30 AND DRAINAGE_AREA_KM2 > 500" retrieves large-basin, long-record stations -- the gold standard for flood frequency analysis. "Select all stream reaches where FLOW_CMS > 0 AND STRAHLER_ORDER >= 3" retrieves perennial streams of moderate or larger size. Parentheses control evaluation order: "Select where (TYPE = 'Urban' OR TYPE = 'Industrial') AND FLOOD_ZONE = 'AE'" retrieves developed land in the regulatory floodplain.

Wildcard queries enable pattern matching. In the US HUC system, "Select where HUC12 LIKE '16010203%'" selects all HUC12 sub-watersheds within a specific HUC8 basin, because the HUC system is hierarchically encoded -- the first 8 digits of every HUC12 code identify its parent HUC8. This kind of query exploits the structure of hydrological coding systems to perform hierarchical selections efficiently.

### 4.6.2 Selection by Location

Spatial selection (or spatial query) selects features based on their geographic relationship to other features. This is distinctly a GIS capability -- no spreadsheet or database can do it. The most common spatial relationships used in water resources work are:

**Intersect**: Select all features in layer A that share any area or boundary with features in layer B. Example: select all land parcels that intersect a flood zone polygon. This identifies all properties with any portion in the flood zone, even if only a corner is affected.

**Within**: Select all features in layer A that are completely inside features in layer B. Example: select all stream gauges within a specific watershed polygon. This extracts the monitoring network for a study area.

**Within a distance**: Select all features in layer A that are within a specified distance of features in layer B. Example: select all wells within 500 metres of a contaminated site. This identifies potentially affected water supply sources.

**Have their center in**: Select features in layer A whose centroid falls inside features in layer B. Example: select all stream reaches whose center is within a watershed. This is a practical shortcut for extracting stream networks within a watershed, avoiding the edge cases where a reach crosses the watershed boundary.

Spatial selection is the tool that links datasets together geographically. A national gauge network and a project watershed exist as separate layers; spatial selection is what connects them. It answers the question "what is near what?" that is at the heart of geographic analysis.

### 4.6.3 Attribute Table Operations

Beyond selection, several attribute table operations are essential for water resources analysis.

**Field calculation** allows you to compute new attribute values from existing ones. Adding a field called AREA_KM2 and calculating it as Shape_Area / 1,000,000 converts the automatic area field (in square metres, if the feature dataset uses a projected coordinate system) to a more interpretable unit. Calculating SPECIFIC_Q as DISCHARGE / AREA_KM2 produces specific discharge values that allow meaningful comparison of runoff generation across sub-basins of different sizes.

**Table joins** attach attributes from one table to another based on a shared key field. Joining a table of mean annual discharge values (keyed by gauge ID) to a gauge feature class (also keyed by gauge ID) makes the discharge values available for spatial display and analysis. Joins are temporary -- they do not modify the underlying data -- which means you can join, analyse, and remove the join without altering either table.

**Summary statistics** aggregate attribute values across groups. Summarizing rainfall by watershed code produces mean, total, minimum, and maximum rainfall for each watershed. Summarizing land cover area by watershed and land cover type produces the land use composition needed for hydrological model parameterization. In ArcGIS Pro, this is done through the Summary Statistics tool; in QGIS, through the Statistics by Categories function or the Group Stats plugin.

## 4.7 Building a Watershed Base Map

The practical test of GIS capability is building something useful. The watershed base map -- a collection of thematic layers assembled for a specific study area, in a common coordinate system, with consistent attribution -- is the foundation of any water resources GIS project. Everything that follows in later chapters -- DEM processing (Chapter 5), terrain analysis (Chapters 6-7), watershed delineation (Chapters 9-11), flood mapping (Chapter 15) -- builds on this base map. This section walks through the process at a conceptual level, demonstrating how the geodatabase, visualization, and query capabilities described above come together in practice.

### 4.7.1 Defining the Study Area

Every watershed project begins with a question that defines the study area. "What is the flood risk for the city of Szolnok?" defines the study area as the contributing watershed of the Tisza River above Szolnok. "How will land use change affect water quality in Lake Balaton?" defines the study area as the Balaton catchment, roughly 5,775 km$^2$ of western Hungary. "What is the water balance of the Upper Bear River basin?" defines the study area as the HUC8 watershed 16010203 in Utah and Wyoming.

The study area determines everything: which DEM tiles to download, which stream reaches to extract, which gauges to include, which climate data to obtain. In GIS terms, the study area is a polygon -- the watershed boundary -- that serves as the spatial filter for all subsequent data acquisition.

For the United States, study area boundaries are readily available through the Watershed Boundary Dataset (WBD), which provides the complete HUC hierarchy as pre-delineated polygons. For the EU, the Water Framework Directive river basin districts and sub-basin boundaries provide equivalent coverages, available through the EEA and national agencies. Globally, HydroBASINS provides 12 levels of nested watershed boundaries for the entire planet. The starting point is always: find or create the watershed polygon.

### 4.7.2 Setting Up the Geodatabase

With the study area defined, the next step is creating the project infrastructure. In ArcGIS Pro, this means creating a new project (which automatically creates a project folder, a default geodatabase, and a default toolbox) and then creating a feature dataset within the geodatabase with an appropriate projected coordinate system. In QGIS, this means creating a new project, creating a GeoPackage file for the project data, and establishing the project CRS (coordinate reference system).

The choice of coordinate system deserves careful thought. For a project in Hungary, the natural choices are EOV (EPSG:23700) for consistency with national datasets, or UTM Zone 34N (EPSG:32634) for compatibility with European and global datasets. For a project in the central United States, UTM Zone 14N (EPSG:32614) or a relevant State Plane zone would be appropriate. The key requirement is that the coordinate system must be projected (not geographic) so that distances, areas, and other geometric measurements are computed in linear units (metres) rather than angular units (degrees). As noted in Chapter 2, computing area from geographic coordinates produces values in square degrees, which are meaningless for engineering purposes.

### 4.7.3 Assembling Thematic Layers

With the geodatabase and feature dataset in place, the assembly process follows a systematic pattern. For each thematic layer -- watershed boundaries, stream network, gauge locations, DEM, land cover -- the steps are:

1. **Obtain the data** from the appropriate source (Chapter 3 catalogued these sources in detail).
2. **Add it to the GIS project** by opening or connecting to the data source.
3. **Inspect it** by examining the attribute table and the spatial extent. Does it cover your study area? Are the attributes what you expected?
4. **Select the study area subset** using attribute queries (e.g., selecting by watershed code) or spatial queries (e.g., selecting all features within the study area polygon).
5. **Export the selection** to the project geodatabase or GeoPackage, which reprojects the data to the project coordinate system.
6. **Symbolize** the imported layer with appropriate colors, line widths, and labels.

Consider a concrete example: building a base map for the Zala River watershed, which drains into Lake Balaton in western Hungary. The steps might proceed as follows. Download the EU-Hydro river network for Hungary from the Copernicus Land Monitoring Service. Download the Copernicus GLO-30 DEM tiles covering western Hungary. Obtain the watershed boundary from the OVF (General Directorate of Water Management) or delineate it from HydroBASINS. Obtain gauge locations from the OVF hydrographic database (data.vizugy.hu). Download the CORINE Land Cover dataset for Hungary from the EEA. Create a GeoPackage with UTM Zone 33N or EOV projection. Import each dataset, clipping or selecting to the Zala watershed extent. Symbolize streams as blue lines (width proportional to Strahler order), the watershed boundary as a thick black outline, gauges as red triangles, the DEM as a hillshaded elevation color ramp, and land cover in standard CORINE colors. The result is a base map that provides geographic context for all subsequent analysis.

The parallel US example -- the Bear River watershed in Utah/Wyoming -- would follow exactly the same logic with different data sources: NHDPlus for the stream network, the Watershed Boundary Dataset for HUC boundaries, USGS NWIS for gauge locations, USGS 3DEP for the DEM, and NLCD for land cover.

### 4.7.4 Table Joins and Enrichment

A base map becomes genuinely useful when spatial features are enriched with non-spatial attributes through table joins. Stream reaches, on their own, are just blue lines on a map. Joining the NHDPlus EROM (Enhanced Runoff Model) table to the reach feature class adds estimated mean annual flow, mean annual velocity, and temperature to each reach -- suddenly, the blue lines carry hydrological meaning. Joining a gauge metadata table to gauge point features adds record length, drainage area, and station type -- enabling attribute queries like "show me all USGS gauges with more than 50 years of record and drainage areas between 100 and 1000 km$^2$."

In the Hungarian context, joining discharge statistics from the OVF hydrographic database to gauge point features, or joining water quality monitoring results from the WISE (Water Information System for Europe) database to sampling station locations, performs the same enrichment. The data sources differ; the GIS workflow is identical.

The join operation requires a common key field -- a field that exists in both the spatial feature class and the attribute table, containing matching values. For NHDPlus, this is typically the NHDPlusID or ComID. For Hungarian gauging stations, it might be the station identifier used by OVF. Getting the join field right -- ensuring that the field types match (both text, or both integer) and that the values correspond exactly -- is a common source of frustration for beginners. A join that matches zero records usually means a type mismatch or a naming inconsistency, not a GIS bug.

## 4.8 The Arc Hydro Data Model

The geodatabase capabilities described above are generic -- they apply to any spatial data, not just water data. The **Arc Hydro data model** takes the geodatabase framework and specializes it for water resources. Developed by David Maidment and colleagues at the University of Texas at Austin (whose contributions are discussed in Section 1.3), in collaboration with ESRI, Arc Hydro defines a standardized schema -- a set of feature classes, tables, and relationships -- designed to represent the components of a hydrological system and their interconnections.

### 4.8.1 What Arc Hydro Provides

At its core, Arc Hydro defines a data model for surface water hydrology. The model includes feature classes for waterbodies (lakes, reservoirs), watersheds (drainage areas at multiple scales), the drainage network (stream reaches with connectivity), and monitoring points (gauges, sampling stations). It also includes tables for time series data -- discharge measurements, water levels, water quality parameters -- linked to monitoring points through relationships. The key innovation is the HydroID: a unique identifier assigned to every feature in the model, enabling unambiguous cross-referencing between features and between features and time series.

The Arc Hydro data model is not software -- it is a schema, a blueprint for how to organize water data in a geodatabase. The accompanying Arc Hydro Tools (a free ArcGIS Pro add-in) provide geoprocessing functions that operate on data organized according to this schema: terrain preprocessing, flow direction computation, catchment delineation, network generation, and attribute assignment. These tools are designed to work together as a workflow, with each step producing output in the format expected by the next step.

For water professionals, Arc Hydro provides two things. First, it offers a standardized way to organize water data that has been tested across hundreds of projects worldwide. Instead of inventing your own database schema for each project, you adopt a proven template. Second, the Arc Hydro Tools provide a streamlined workflow for the terrain-to-watershed processing chain that is central to Chapters 9 through 12 of this book.

### 4.8.2 Arc Hydro in Context

Arc Hydro is not the only water-specific data model. The Arc Hydro Groundwater data model (discussed in Part V) extends the concept to three-dimensional subsurface features -- wells, boreholes, aquifers, and geological layers. The European INSPIRE Hydrography data specification defines a comparable (though not identical) schema for water features across EU member states, emphasizing interoperability and standardized feature types. The OGC WaterML 2.0 standard defines a data model for hydrological time series observations that complements spatial data models.

In the open-source world, there is no direct equivalent to Arc Hydro as an integrated data model plus toolset. However, the combination of HydroSHEDS data (which follows its own well-defined schema), GRASS GIS hydrological tools, and the WhiteboxTools terrain analysis library provides comparable functionality without the proprietary data model. The practical takeaway is that organizing water data in a structured, relational schema -- whether you call it Arc Hydro or something else -- is always better than ad hoc file management. The discipline of defining feature classes, establishing relationships, and enforcing spatial consistency pays dividends throughout the life of a project.

## 4.9 Cloud-Based Watershed Delineation

One of the most striking demonstrations of how cloud GIS changes water resources work is the ability to delineate a watershed from a web browser, without downloading any data or running any local software. This capability -- available through ArcGIS Online, Google Earth Engine, and several other platforms -- encapsulates a workflow that traditionally required hours of data acquisition, preprocessing, and geoprocessing into a single click.

### 4.9.1 How It Works

Cloud-based watershed delineation relies on pre-processed global elevation data stored on server infrastructure. The provider (ESRI, Google, or others) has already downloaded global DEM data, filled sinks, computed flow directions, and generated flow accumulation grids -- the entire terrain preprocessing chain described in Chapters 9 and 10. This preprocessed data lives on the server, ready to respond to queries.

When a user clicks a point on the map, the system identifies the nearest stream cell (using the pre-computed flow accumulation grid), then traces upstream through the flow direction grid to find all cells that drain to that point. The boundary of this upstream area is converted to a polygon and returned to the user. The entire computation takes seconds because the expensive preprocessing has already been done -- only the final tracing step executes in real time.

In ArcGIS Online, this functionality is available through the Watershed widget or the Trace Downstream service. You click on any location in the world, and the system returns the upstream watershed boundary as a polygon, along with attributes like area, mean elevation, and land cover composition. The underlying data is the ESRI global hydrological database, derived from 90-metre and 30-metre DEMs. For reconnaissance-level studies -- "roughly how big is the watershed above this proposed dam site?" -- this is remarkably powerful.

In Google Earth Engine, watershed delineation requires a few lines of JavaScript or Python code, but the logic is the same: load a pre-processed DEM (typically HydroSHEDS or the MERIT DEM), identify the pour point, and trace the upstream contributing area. The advantage of GEE is flexibility -- you can customize the DEM, the tracing algorithm, and the output attributes. The disadvantage is the need to write code.

### 4.9.2 Limitations and When to Go Local

Cloud-based watershed delineation is fast and convenient, but it has limitations that water professionals must understand. The underlying DEM is typically 30 to 90 metres in resolution -- adequate for large watersheds but too coarse for small urban catchments or detailed engineering studies. The preprocessing decisions (sink filling parameters, stream threshold values, burn-in of known channels) are made by the provider and cannot be customized. The results may not match locally derived watersheds, particularly in flat terrain where flow direction is ambiguous.

For preliminary studies, teaching, and rapid assessment, cloud-based delineation is excellent. For engineering design, regulatory compliance, or detailed research, the water professional should download the best available DEM, perform the preprocessing with full control over parameters, and delineate watersheds locally using the workflows described in Chapters 9-11. The cloud tool tells you approximately what the watershed looks like; the local workflow gives you defensible, reproducible results.

## 4.10 QGIS as the Open-Source Alternative

Throughout this chapter, QGIS has been mentioned alongside ArcGIS Pro for each capability area. This section consolidates the practical guidance for readers who will use QGIS as their primary platform, whether by choice (preferring open-source software) or by necessity (lacking access to an ESRI license).

### 4.10.1 The QGIS Workflow for Water Resources

QGIS provides a complete workflow for water resources GIS, from data management through analysis to cartographic output. The key components are:

**Data management** centers on the GeoPackage format rather than the file geodatabase. A QGIS project (`.qgz` file) references data stored in GeoPackages, shapefiles, PostGIS databases, or any of the 80+ formats supported through GDAL/OGR. While QGIS lacks the feature dataset concept (which enforces a common coordinate system for grouped layers), the same organizational discipline can be achieved by consistently using the "Reproject Layer" tool when importing data, and by setting the project CRS explicitly.

**Geovisualization** in QGIS is comparable to ArcGIS Pro in most respects. The symbology system supports single symbol, categorized (unique values), graduated, rule-based, and many other renderer types. The Print Layout (analogous to ArcGIS Pro's layout view) provides full cartographic composition tools including map frames, legends, scale bars, north arrows, and text elements. QGIS's color ramp and symbol library is extensive, and users can import additional symbols.

**Geoprocessing** is handled through the Processing framework, which unifies tools from QGIS native algorithms, GRASS GIS, SAGA GIS, GDAL/OGR, and any installed plugins into a single searchable interface. For hydrological analysis specifically, the GRASS tools (r.watershed, r.stream.extract, r.water.outlet) and the WhiteboxTools plugin (breach depressions, D8 flow direction, Strahler stream order) provide comprehensive terrain analysis capabilities. The Processing Graphical Modeler allows these tools to be chained into automated workflows, equivalent to ArcGIS Pro's ModelBuilder.

**Python scripting** through PyQGIS provides programmatic access to all QGIS capabilities. For repetitive tasks -- processing DEMs for 50 sub-watersheds, for instance -- Python scripts can automate the entire workflow. The QGIS Python console and the ability to write standalone scripts make automation accessible to users with basic Python skills.

### 4.10.2 Key Differences for Hydrological Work

Several differences between QGIS and ArcGIS Pro are particularly relevant for water resources applications.

**Coordinate system handling** differs in philosophy. ArcGIS Pro's feature datasets enforce coordinate system consistency at the container level; QGIS relies on on-the-fly reprojection, which displays layers correctly regardless of their native CRS but can mask underlying inconsistencies. The practical implication is that QGIS users must be more disciplined about explicitly reprojecting data before analysis, rather than relying on the container to handle it.

**Raster analysis** toolsets differ in detail. ArcGIS Pro's Spatial Analyst provides a tightly integrated set of hydrological tools (Fill, Flow Direction, Flow Accumulation, Watershed, Stream to Feature, etc.) that work together seamlessly. In QGIS, the equivalent tools are spread across GRASS, SAGA, and WhiteboxTools, each with slightly different conventions for input/output formats, NoData handling, and parameter naming. The results are equivalent, but the user must navigate a more fragmented toolset.

**The Arc Hydro data model and tools** have no direct QGIS equivalent. QGIS users can organize their data according to Arc Hydro principles (the schema is documented and can be replicated in GeoPackage), but the automated Arc Hydro Tools workflow must be replicated manually using individual geoprocessing tools. This requires more effort but also provides more transparency about what each step does -- which can be an advantage for learning.

**Enterprise integration** is an area where ArcGIS retains a clear advantage. Organizations with ArcGIS Enterprise infrastructure -- web portals, feature services, enterprise geodatabases -- find that ArcGIS Pro integrates seamlessly while QGIS requires additional configuration. For individual researchers or small teams, this difference is irrelevant; for large agencies, it can be decisive.

## 4.11 Practical Walkthrough: From Data to Watershed Map

To make the concepts concrete, this section presents a simplified end-to-end walkthrough of building a watershed base map. The workflow is described at a conceptual level applicable to both ArcGIS Pro and QGIS; platform-specific differences are noted where relevant. The example uses the Zala River watershed in western Hungary, which drains approximately 2,620 km$^2$ into Lake Balaton -- Hungary's largest lake and a system of enormous ecological and economic importance.

### 4.11.1 Step 1: Obtain the Watershed Boundary

The first data layer is the watershed boundary itself. For the Zala catchment, several sources are available. The OVF provides watershed boundaries for Hungarian water management planning units. HydroBASINS provides pre-delineated watershed polygons at multiple scales globally -- the Level 07 or Level 08 boundaries approximate the Zala catchment. Alternatively, the watershed can be delineated from a DEM using the methods described in Chapter 11.

For this walkthrough, assume we download the relevant HydroBASINS polygon from hydrosheds.org. The data arrives as a shapefile in geographic coordinates (WGS 84, EPSG:4326). We create a new GeoPackage (or file geodatabase) with a projected coordinate system -- UTM Zone 33N (EPSG:32633) is appropriate for western Hungary, as is EOV (EPSG:23700). We import the watershed boundary, which is reprojected to the target CRS during import.

### 4.11.2 Step 2: Add the Stream Network

The EU-Hydro River Network dataset, available from the Copernicus Land Monitoring Service, provides a vector stream network for all of Europe. We download the dataset for the Danube basin (which includes the Zala), add it to the project, and use a spatial selection to extract only the reaches within the Zala watershed boundary. The selected reaches are exported to the project GeoPackage, reprojected to the project CRS.

We symbolize the streams using graduated line widths based on the Strahler order attribute: thin lines (0.5 pt) for order 1-2 headwater streams, medium lines (1.5 pt) for order 3-4, and thick lines (3 pt) for order 5+. All lines are colored in a standard hydrological blue. The visual hierarchy immediately communicates the drainage structure.

### 4.11.3 Step 3: Add the Digital Elevation Model

We download Copernicus GLO-30 DEM tiles covering the Zala watershed extent from the Copernicus Data Space. The tiles arrive as GeoTIFF files in geographic coordinates. We mosaic the tiles into a single raster (using the Mosaic to New Raster tool in ArcGIS Pro or the gdal_merge/Build Virtual Raster tool in QGIS), then clip the mosaic to the watershed boundary with a small buffer.

For visualization, we apply an elevation color ramp (terrain colors from green lowlands through brown hills) and generate a hillshade layer. Setting the DEM color layer to 50% transparency over the hillshade produces the classic shaded relief visualization that reveals the terrain structure of the watershed: the flat Zala valley floor, the gentle hills to the north and south, and the abrupt shoreline of Lake Balaton at the eastern outlet.

### 4.11.4 Step 4: Add Monitoring Stations

Gauge locations come from the OVF hydrographic database. For the Zala watershed, the key stations include the gauge at Zalaegerszeg (upper Zala), Zalabér (middle Zala), and Zalaapáti (lower Zala, near the lake). We create a point feature class from the station coordinates (either from a downloaded table or manually digitized) and symbolize the gauges as red triangles with labels showing station names.

If discharge statistics are available as a separate table, we perform a table join to attach mean annual discharge, maximum recorded discharge, and record length to each gauge point. This allows us to display the gauges with graduated symbols -- larger triangles for higher-flow stations -- or to label them with discharge values directly on the map.

### 4.11.5 Step 5: Add Land Cover

The CORINE Land Cover dataset (discussed in Section 3.4.3), produced by the European Environment Agency at approximately 100-metre resolution, provides standardized land cover classification for all of Europe. We download the most recent CORINE dataset for Hungary, clip it to the watershed boundary, and display it using the standard CORINE color scheme. The result shows the land cover composition of the Zala catchment: predominantly agricultural land (cropland and pasture) in the valley floors, forest on the steeper hillslopes, and the urban areas of Zalaegerszeg and smaller towns.

For the parallel US example, the equivalent step uses the NLCD (National Land Cover Database) at 30-metre resolution with its 16-class system. Both products arrive as raster grids that must be clipped to the watershed extent and reclassified or symbolized according to their respective colour palettes. A useful addition at this stage is to compute zonal statistics -- calculating the percentage of the watershed in each land cover class -- which provides the lumped parameters needed by many hydrological models. In both CORINE and NLCD, the ability to compare multiple epochs (e.g., 2006 vs. 2018) reveals land cover change that may explain shifts in hydrological response. For global coverage, ESA WorldCover at 10-metre resolution provides an alternative (Section 3.4.4).

### 4.11.6 The Assembled Base Map

With all five layers assembled -- watershed boundary, stream network, DEM with hillshade, gauge locations, and land cover -- we have a watershed base map that provides the geographic foundation for any subsequent analysis. The map can be composed as a formal layout with title, legend, scale bar, north arrow, and data source credits, then exported as a PDF or image for reports.

## 4.13 What GIS Cannot Do (and What Fills the Gap)

It is important to be honest about the boundaries of GIS capability. GIS excels at spatial data management, visualization, and geometric analysis. It does not, by itself, solve hydrological equations, run hydraulic simulations, or optimize water system operations. The water professional must understand where GIS ends and specialized modeling software begins.

### 4.13.1 The GIS-Model Interface

GIS prepares the inputs that hydrological and hydraulic models need, and it visualizes the outputs those models produce. The model itself -- whether it is HEC-HMS for rainfall-runoff modeling, HEC-RAS for hydraulic simulation, MODFLOW for groundwater flow, or SWAT for watershed-scale water balance -- runs its own computational engine. GIS does not replace these models; it complements them.

The interface works in both directions. From GIS to model: terrain analysis produces watershed boundaries, drainage areas, channel slopes, and reach lengths that become model parameters. Land cover and soil type maps, processed through GIS overlay operations, produce the curve numbers or infiltration parameters that drive runoff calculations. DEM-derived cross-sections define channel geometry for hydraulic models. From model to GIS: simulated water surface elevations become flood inundation maps when subtracted from the DEM. Predicted groundwater levels become contour maps when interpolated across the model domain. Forecast flood hydrographs become real-time visualizations when displayed at gauge locations on a web map.

This bidirectional interface is the practical reality of hydroinformatics: GIS and models work together, each doing what it does best. Chapters 15 and 16 explore the GIS-model interface for flood mapping and forecasting in detail; Chapters 18-21 do the same for groundwater.

### 4.13.2 Beyond Desktop GIS: The Programming Frontier

As water resources problems grow in scale and complexity, the limitations of point-and-click desktop GIS become apparent. Processing 10,000 sub-watersheds through a parameter estimation workflow, running a sensitivity analysis that requires 500 model executions, or producing daily flood inundation maps for an entire river basin -- these tasks demand automation beyond what a graphical modeler can efficiently provide.

This is where programming enters the picture. Python, with its geospatial libraries (GDAL, Fiona, Shapely, Rasterio, GeoPandas, xarray), provides a scripting environment that can do everything a desktop GIS does, plus orchestrate complex workflows that would be impractical through a graphical interface. The chapters in Part VI (The Intelligent Water System) explore how Python scripting, machine learning, and AI-based approaches extend GIS capabilities into territory that traditional point-and-click tools cannot reach.

For the water professional who is not a programmer, the key message is this: GIS provides the foundation, and it is sufficient for a vast range of practical water resources tasks. But the frontier of hydroinformatics -- real-time forecasting systems, AI-assisted modeling, continental-scale analysis -- requires moving beyond the GUI. The good news is that both ArcGIS Pro (through ArcPy) and QGIS (through PyQGIS) provide gentle on-ramps from point-and-click to scripting, allowing the transition to happen gradually as the complexity of your work demands it.
