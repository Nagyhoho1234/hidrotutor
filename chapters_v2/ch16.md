# Chapter 16: Forecasting Floods in Real Time

A flood forecast that arrives after the water has already entered the living room is not a forecast -- it is a post-mortem. The entire value of flood prediction lies in the lead time it provides: hours or days during which people can evacuate, emergency managers can position resources, and dam operators can open gates to make room for the incoming wave. The preceding chapters built the spatial foundations -- terrain analysis (Chapters 9--11), drainage networks (Chapter 11), HAND computation (Chapter 14), and inundation mapping (Chapter 15) -- that make such predictions possible. This chapter puts those foundations to work in operational systems that run continuously, ingesting weather forecasts, transforming precipitation into runoff, routing flow through river networks, and delivering warnings to the people who need them.

The chapter follows three parallel threads. The first traces the development of the United States **National Water Model** (NWM), the most computationally ambitious national flood forecasting system in the world, and the **Texas Flood Response System** that translates its outputs into actionable intelligence. The second examines the **European Flood Awareness System** (EFAS) and its global counterpart **GloFAS**, which take a fundamentally different architectural approach -- ensemble-based, multinational, and built on different hydrological models -- yet solve the same problem. The third thread addresses Hungarian flood management, a domain shaped by the country's unique hydrological position as a downstream nation where 95% of surface water originates beyond its borders, and where the Tisza River has produced some of Europe's most devastating floods in the 21st century. These three threads converge on a common set of questions: how do we move from weather data to flood warnings at continental scale? What observational infrastructure validates and improves the forecasts? And how do we deliver the right information to the right person at the right time?

## 16.1 The Case for National-Scale Flood Forecasting

### 16.1.1 What Came Before

Before the era of continental-scale hydrological models, flood forecasting was a fragmented enterprise. In the United States, Regional River Forecast Centres operated by the National Weather Service produced predictions for major river basins within their jurisdictions. The West Gulf River Forecast Centre in Fort Worth, Texas, for example, generated 48- to 72-hour forecasts for large rivers in its region. In Europe, each nation operated its own forecasting service independently: the United Kingdom's Flood Forecasting Centre covered England and Wales, France's SCHAPI coordinated river forecasting across the French network of 19 Services de Prevision des Crues, and Hungary's OVF (Orszagos Vizugyi Foigazgatosag -- General Directorate of Water Management) operated forecasting for the Danube, Tisza, and their tributaries.

These systems shared a common limitation: they could only forecast rivers where gauges already existed. In the United States, approximately 4,000 stream locations received forecasts, out of 3.2 million miles of rivers and streams. Small streams, urban channels, and ungauged tributaries -- precisely the locations where flash floods kill people -- received no forecasts at all. In Hungary, hydroinfo.hu provided real-time water levels and forecasts for 98 sections of the major river systems, but thousands of smaller watercourses in the hills and lowlands went unmonitored. In developing countries, the situation was far worse: entire river basins covering millions of people had no forecasting capability whatsoever.

The fragmentation was not just geographic but institutional. Weather services produced precipitation forecasts. Hydrological services translated precipitation into river flow. Emergency management agencies translated river flow into evacuation orders. These organizations used different data formats, different spatial reference systems, and different communication protocols. Information flowed slowly across institutional boundaries, and the delay cost lives.

### 16.1.2 Catastrophic Failures That Drove Change

Several flood disasters in the early 21st century exposed the inadequacy of the traditional approach with brutal clarity.

The **2013 Onion Creek flood in Austin, Texas**, exemplified the problem of ungauged small streams. Heavy rain fell in the upper Onion Creek watershed around midnight. Emergency responders mobilized, then stood down when the immediate local threat seemed to pass. But the flood wave was propagating downstream through an area with no forecasting capability. It arrived in residential neighborhoods around 5:00 AM with almost no warning. By 6:30 AM, 500 houses were flooded and five people had drowned. The creek existed in databases -- it had a reach identifier in the National Hydrography Dataset -- but no model was producing discharge predictions for it.

**Hurricane Harvey** in August 2017 demonstrated the ultimate extreme. The storm produced the worst recorded precipitation in United States history, exceeding all previous storms by 280 mm over five days, with a return period estimated at 20,000 years. Of the 210,000 buildings flooded in Houston, 58% were outside any identified flood zone. This was a flood "from the sky" -- direct rainfall inundation rather than riverine flooding -- that overwhelmed all existing mapping and forecasting systems. The economic damage exceeded $125 billion, making Harvey the costliest natural disaster in US history at the time.

In Europe, the **June 2013 Danube flood** shattered records that had stood for centuries. At Passau, where the Danube, Inn, and Ilz rivers converge, the water reached its highest level since 1501 -- a record spanning more than five hundred years. Downstream in Hungary, the Danube at Budapest crested at 891 cm on June 10, 2013, the highest level in the 200-year instrumental record. The Hungarian flood defense system held, but the margin was uncomfortably thin. Emergency managers needed not just the crest level but a reliable prediction of when it would arrive, how long it would last, and whether the levees could withstand sustained high water. The national forecast from OVF was good for the Danube main stem, where gauges were dense and the system was well-calibrated. But for smaller tributaries feeding the Danube -- watercourses that could produce dangerous local flooding while attention was focused on the main river -- forecasting capability was limited.

The **Tisza floods of 2001 and 2006** in Hungary carried an additional lesson. The Tisza's catchment extends across five countries: Ukraine, Romania, Slovakia, Hungary, and Serbia. The 2001 flood, which inundated 1,070 km$^2$ and caused EUR 120 million in damage, originated primarily from rainfall and snowmelt in the Ukrainian and Romanian Carpathians -- territory beyond the reach of the Hungarian forecasting system. The 2006 spring flood set new records at multiple gauging stations along the Upper Tisza. In both cases, the flood wave was already large and dangerous by the time it crossed the Hungarian border. Effective forecasting required not just national capability but international data exchange and transboundary modeling -- a problem that no single country could solve alone.

### 16.1.3 The Vision: Atmosphere to Oceans, Coast to Coast

The response to these failures was not incremental improvement but a fundamental rethinking of how flood forecasting should work. The key insight was that the necessary ingredients already existed in scattered form: weather models could predict precipitation at kilometre resolution; land surface models could compute how much of that precipitation became runoff; river network datasets (NHDPlus in the US, EU-Hydro in Europe, HydroSHEDS globally) described how rivers were connected; and routing models could propagate flood waves through those networks. What was missing was the integration -- a system that linked all these components into a continuous pipeline running in near real time.

In the United States, this vision crystallized in 2014 when a National Water Center was established on the campus of the University of Alabama in Tuscaloosa, jointly operated by the National Weather Service, USGS, Army Corps of Engineers, and FEMA. At the first meeting held at the new facility, David Maidment proposed to "bring in the academic community and rapidly prototype a new national flood forecasting system in one year -- atmosphere to the oceans, coast to coast, near real time, high spatial resolution." The proposal was adopted, and what began as an academic "Summer Institute" led directly to the operational National Water Model.

In Europe, the vision emerged earlier but through a different institutional path. Following the devastating Elbe and Danube floods of 2002, the European Commission tasked the Joint Research Centre (JRC) in Ispra, Italy, with developing a pan-European flood forecasting capability. The result was EFAS, which became operational in 2012 under the Copernicus Emergency Management Service and has since been extended globally as GloFAS. Where the American system was born from academic innovation moving rapidly into operations, the European system grew from institutional mandate and multinational cooperation.

Both approaches recognized the same fundamental truth: flood forecasting is a chain, and the chain is only as strong as its weakest link. A perfect weather forecast is useless if it cannot be translated into river discharge. A perfect hydrological model is useless if the river network it routes through is disconnected or incorrectly topologized. A perfect discharge forecast is useless if it cannot be translated into a water depth that means something to a homeowner or an emergency manager. The systems described in this chapter represent humanity's most ambitious attempts to forge that chain from end to end.

## 16.2 Architecture of the US National Water Model

### 16.2.1 System Overview

The National Water Model (NWM) is a coupled atmosphere-land surface-channel routing system that produces discharge forecasts for 2.7 million stream reaches covering 3.2 million miles of rivers and streams across the continental United States. Forecasts are updated every hour for short-range predictions and every six hours for medium-range predictions extending 10 days into the future. The system runs on NOAA supercomputing infrastructure and delivers its products through the NOAA Water website and a suite of web services.

The NWM represents a qualitative leap from the old River Forecast Centre system. Instead of forecasting 4,000 gauged locations, it forecasts 2.7 million reaches. Instead of operating independently for isolated basins, it produces a single, consistent, continental picture. Instead of updating once or twice daily, it updates hourly. The computational cost is enormous -- the system requires sustained supercomputer performance equivalent to running tens of thousands of simultaneous simulations -- but the benefit is proportional: every stream in the nation, no matter how small, receives a quantitative discharge forecast.

### 16.2.2 The Weather Input: HRRR and GFS

The first link in the NWM chain is the weather forecast. For short-range predictions (up to 18 hours ahead), the system ingests output from the High Resolution Rapid Refresh (HRRR) model operated by NOAA's Environmental Modeling Center. The HRRR is a convection-allowing atmospheric model that assimilates radar data, aircraft observations, satellite retrievals, and surface observations every hour to produce precipitation forecasts at 3-km horizontal resolution. Its rapid update cycle and high spatial resolution make it particularly valuable for predicting the intense, localized rainfall that drives flash floods.

The HRRR's strength is also its limitation: it can only see 18 hours ahead, because the chaotic nature of atmospheric dynamics degrades convection-scale forecasts rapidly beyond that horizon. For medium-range predictions (out to 10 days), the NWM switches to the Global Forecast System (GFS), a global atmospheric model running at coarser resolution but with better skill at longer lead times. The GFS uses ensemble techniques -- running multiple simulations with slightly different initial conditions -- to quantify forecast uncertainty, although the operational NWM currently uses only the deterministic GFS control run rather than the full ensemble.

The combination of HRRR and GFS gives the NWM two complementary windows into the future: a sharp, detailed view of the next 18 hours (where is the rain falling right now? where will it fall in six hours?) and a broader, less certain view of the next 10 days (is a major weather system approaching? will a river be above flood stage by the weekend?). The handoff between these two time horizons is one of the technical challenges of the system: the HRRR may predict a thunderstorm that the GFS misses entirely, or vice versa, creating discontinuities in the forecast hydrograph.

### 16.2.3 The Land Surface Model: Noah-MP

Once precipitation reaches the ground, it must be partitioned among several competing pathways: interception by vegetation, infiltration into the soil, surface runoff, evapotranspiration back to the atmosphere, and percolation to deeper groundwater. The Noah Multi-Physics (Noah-MP) land surface model performs this partitioning on the weather model grid.

Noah-MP is a descendant of the original Noah land surface scheme developed by NOAA, the Air Force, and several universities in the 1990s. The "MP" designation reflects its modular design: multiple physics options are available for each process (canopy radiation transfer, stomatal conductance, soil moisture movement, runoff generation, groundwater interaction), and the model can be configured to use different combinations. In the NWM configuration, Noah-MP operates on a 1-km grid across the continental United States, ingesting precipitation and atmospheric forcing from the weather models and producing gridded runoff -- the volume of water per unit area available for routing to streams.

The key output of Noah-MP is not the total precipitation but the partitioning between infiltration and runoff. On a hot August afternoon when the soil is dry and the vegetation is actively transpiring, most of a moderate rainfall event may infiltrate and evaporate, producing little runoff. On the same landscape in February, with frozen soil and dormant vegetation, the same rainfall intensity may run off almost entirely. Noah-MP captures these seasonal and antecedent-moisture effects through its representation of soil physics, vegetation dynamics, and snow processes. The model carries four soil layers (extending to 2 meters depth), a multi-layer snowpack, and a simple unconfined groundwater reservoir, all of which interact to determine how much water reaches the stream network and when.

### 16.2.4 Geographic Transformation: From Grid to Catchment

The output of Noah-MP is a regular grid of runoff values. But rivers do not flow on grids -- they flow through irregularly shaped catchments connected by a dendritic network. The NWM must therefore transform gridded runoff onto the NHDPlus catchment framework before routing. This transformation uses area-weighted averaging:

$$R_{\text{catchment}} = \sum_{i} \frac{A_{i,\text{overlap}}}{A_{\text{catchment}}} \cdot R_{i,\text{grid}}$$

where $R_{i,\text{grid}}$ is the runoff from grid cell $i$, $A_{i,\text{overlap}}$ is the area of intersection between grid cell $i$ and the catchment, and $A_{\text{catchment}}$ is the total catchment area. This is conceptually identical to the Thiessen polygon weighting used for distributing point rainfall measurements over a watershed (Chapter 8), except that the source data is already gridded rather than station-based.

The geographic transformation step is where the NWM fundamentally diverges from simpler approaches. Some national hydrological models -- including early versions of the UK Met Office system -- route water on the same regular grid used by the land surface model, treating each grid cell as both a land surface element and a routing element. The NWM instead maps runoff onto 2.7 million irregularly shaped NHDPlus catchments and routes it through the vector stream network that connects them. This approach is more computationally expensive and more complex to implement, but it produces discharge estimates at specific, identifiable stream reaches rather than at arbitrary grid points. The result is a forecast that can be tied to a named river at a named location -- the kind of information that emergency managers and the public actually need.

### 16.2.5 Channel Routing: RAPID

The final link in the NWM chain is the RAPID (Routing Application for Parallel computatIon of Discharge) model, which propagates water through the NHDPlus channel network using a Muskingum routing scheme. The Muskingum method is a storage-based approach that relates the outflow from a river reach to the inflow and the storage within the reach:

$$S = K \left[ x \cdot I + (1 - x) \cdot Q \right]$$

where $S$ is storage in the reach, $I$ is inflow, $Q$ is outflow, $K$ is the travel time through the reach, and $x$ is a weighting factor (0 to 0.5) that controls the degree of attenuation. A value of $x = 0$ produces maximum attenuation (reservoir-like behavior), while $x = 0.5$ produces pure translation with no attenuation.

RAPID applies this scheme to every one of the 2.7 million NHDPlus reaches simultaneously, using the connectivity tables embedded in NHDPlus to propagate flood waves from headwater streams to river mouths. The computation is massively parallel: reaches that are not upstream or downstream of each other can be computed independently. RAPID was designed specifically for this parallelism, decomposing the network into independent subsets that can be distributed across thousands of processor cores on a supercomputer.

The Muskingum parameters $K$ and $x$ for each reach are estimated from the reach geometry in NHDPlus -- length, slope, and an empirical relationship to channel width -- rather than from calibration against observed hydrographs. This is a deliberate design choice: with 2.7 million reaches and limited gauge data (approximately 10,000 USGS gauges, or one gauge per 270 reaches), parameter calibration in the traditional sense is impossible. The system relies instead on the physical reasonableness of its parameterization and is progressively improved through systematic comparison against gauge observations during flood events.

### 16.2.6 The Critical Role of NHDPlus

The key innovation that enabled the US National Water Model was not any single model component but the NHDPlus geospatial framework that connects them all. NHDPlus provides:

- **2.7 million stream reaches**, each with a unique Common Identifier (COMID) that serves as a key linking to all other datasets
- **Corresponding catchments** with reach-to-catchment associations, enabling the grid-to-vector transformation
- **Connectivity tables** describing the upstream-downstream topology of the entire network
- **Physical attributes** including reach length, slope, stream order, and contributing drainage area

NHDPlus itself was built from four foundation datasets over approximately 20 years of national data integration: the National Elevation Dataset (Chapter 5), the National Hydrography Dataset (Chapter 11), the Watershed Boundary Dataset, and the National Land Cover Dataset. It took a decade to build these individual components and another decade to link them into the integrated NHDPlus framework. The National Water Model is, in a very real sense, the capstone application that justifies two decades of national geospatial data investment.

No other country has an equivalent dataset at the same level of integration and coverage. The European Union's EU-Hydro dataset is closest, providing a continental river network derived from Copernicus satellite imagery with associated catchment boundaries, but it lacks the depth of attribute data and the tight integration with elevation models that characterize NHDPlus. The global HydroSHEDS dataset, derived from SRTM elevation data, provides worldwide coverage but at coarser resolution and without the extensive quality control that NHDPlus has received through decades of state and federal agency review.

### 16.2.7 NWM Forecast Products

The National Water Model produces several categories of forecast products that serve different users and time horizons:

**Short-range forecasts** are updated hourly and extend 18 hours into the future. These are the primary products for flash flood warning: if an intense thunderstorm is producing heavy rainfall over a small urban watershed right now, the short-range forecast shows when and where the resulting flood wave will arrive. The one-hour update cycle means that the forecast tracks the storm as it evolves, incorporating the latest radar observations through the HRRR data assimilation.

**Medium-range forecasts** are updated every six hours and extend 10 days into the future. These serve the strategic planning needs of emergency managers: will a landfalling hurricane produce major river flooding next week? Should pre-positioned supplies be moved to a different region? Should reservoir operators begin pre-emptive releases to create storage capacity? The longer forecast horizon comes with greater uncertainty, but even an imperfect 5-day flood forecast provides enormously more value than no forecast at all.

**Streamflow anomaly maps** compare forecast flows to historical norms at each of the 2.7 million reaches, highlighting areas where flows are expected to be unusually high or unusually low. These maps provide a quick visual summary of the national flood picture and are particularly useful for identifying emerging flood threats that might be missed when attention is focused on a specific storm or region.

All products are accessible through the NOAA Water website and associated web services:

## 16.3 The Texas Flood Response System

### 16.3.1 From Discharge to Impact: A Four-Step Pipeline

A discharge forecast -- "the Onion Creek at US 183 will reach 3,500 cubic feet per second at 4:00 AM" -- is precise but not actionable for most people. What the homeowner on Bluff Springs Road needs to know is whether her house will flood. What the emergency manager needs to know is how many buildings will be affected and in which neighborhoods. The Texas Flood Response System bridges this gap through a four-step pipeline:

1. **Discharge**: Take the discharge forecast from the National Water Model for each stream reach
2. **Depth**: Transform discharge to water depth using HAND-derived rating curves (Chapter 14) or synthetic rating curves computed from channel geometry
3. **Inundation**: Create a flood inundation map by identifying all cells in the HAND raster with elevation above the nearest drainage less than the computed flood depth
4. **Impact**: Overlay the inundation map on building footprints, address points, and population data to estimate the number of affected structures and people

This pipeline was developed by researchers at the University of Texas at Austin, Utah State University, and the University of Illinois, with support from ESRI and state agencies. Its critical innovation is that steps 2, 3, and 4 can be pre-computed. For each NWM reach, a library of inundation maps is generated in advance for a range of possible flood depths -- say, 1 foot, 2 feet, 3 feet, and so on up to the probable maximum flood. When a real-time NWM forecast arrives, the system simply looks up the closest pre-computed depth and retrieves the corresponding inundation map and impact estimate. This lookup takes milliseconds, enabling the system to process all Texas stream reaches in near real time.

### 16.3.2 Validation During Hurricane Harvey

The Texas Flood Response System faced its ultimate test during Hurricane Harvey in August 2017. The National Water Model forecast was produced before the hurricane made landfall. Before the storm reached Corpus Christi on the evening of Friday, August 25, the system predicted the following impact by Texas disaster region:

**Table 16.2**: Texas Flood Response System pre-landfall predictions for Hurricane Harvey, generated from NWM medium-range forecast at 3:00 PM CDT, Friday August 25, 2017

| Disaster Region | Predicted Address Points at Risk | Primary Urban Area |
|-----------------|--------------------------------|-------------------|
| Region 2 (Southeast Texas) | 238,000 | Houston |
| Region 6 | 22,000 | -- |
| Region 1 (North Texas) | 3,000 | Dallas |
| Other regions | < 1,000 each | -- |

The overwhelming concentration in Region 2 provided clear, actionable intelligence before the hurricane even reached the coast: Houston was going to be severely impacted. This prediction was available to emergency managers a full 36 hours before the worst flooding began.

Post-event comparison with Texas Division of Emergency Management records (152,800 buildings requesting public assistance) confirmed the forecast's value. The five counties with the highest predicted impact -- Harris, Fort Bend, Brazoria, Galveston, and Montgomery -- closely matched the five counties with the highest actual damage claims. The main discrepancy involved Orange and Jefferson counties in the Beaumont-Port Arthur area, which experienced a second, unanticipated deluge at the end of the storm that the initial forecast had not captured. Wind damage in the Corpus Christi area (Region 3) was also missed, since the model predicted only flood damage, not wind damage.

The critical finding was not that the forecast was perfect -- it was not -- but that it correctly identified the geographic center of gravity of the disaster before the hurricane made landfall. In the fog of a major disaster, when resources are scarce and decisions must be made under uncertainty, knowing that Region 2 would receive ten times the impact of any other region is enormously valuable. It allows pre-positioning of rescue teams, medical supplies, and shelter capacity in the right locations before the roads become impassable.

### 16.3.3 Real-Time Tracking: The 2018 Llano River Flood

While Harvey demonstrated the system's value for slow-moving hurricanes, the October 2018 Llano River flood tested its ability to track a fast-moving inland flood wave. A "rain bomb" in west Texas generated record flows that propagated 110 km downstream over approximately 24 hours, destroying the RM 2900 bridge at Kingsland and threatening the Highland Lakes reservoir system protecting Austin.

The system tracked the flood wave at multiple gauge locations along the Llano River:

- **Near Junction (upstream)**: The initial rise of the flood wave appeared on the NWM forecast hydrograph, validated against the USGS gauge.
- **Near Mason**: Water depth reached 8.2 m (27 feet), increasing to 10.1 m (33 feet) downstream of Mason.
- **At Llano**: Depth reached 12.2 m (40 feet), with a peak discharge of 7,816 m$^3$/s (276,000 cfs) -- the second-largest flow on record since 1939.

The flood water subsequently entered Lake LBJ, passed through to Lake Travis, which rose 10.4 m (34 feet) in two days. The margin between the rising lake level and the uncontrolled spillway at Mansfield Dam (elevation 217.6 m / 714 feet) narrowed to approximately 3.7 m (12 feet) -- a significant public safety concern for the city of Austin downstream. Four gates were opened at Mansfield Dam on October 18, 2018, releasing 801 m$^3$/s (28,300 cfs) into the lower Colorado River through the heart of Austin.

A TxDOT National Water Model Bridge Portal monitored bridge conditions across the affected area in real time, integrating NWM forecasts with bridge location data. Texas has 27,000 bridges on 15,700 stream reaches forecast by the National Water Model, and these bridges represent both infrastructure at risk and potential sensor platforms for future monitoring networks.

### 16.3.4 Hurricane Florence: Extending Beyond Texas

The National Water Model was also applied to Hurricane Florence, which made landfall on the Carolina coast in September 2018. The system tracked the evolving forecast from Tuesday, September 11, through landfall, producing river discharge forecasts that showed a predicted peak of 4,531 m$^3$/s (160,000 cfs) on Sunday evening. The medium-range forecast streamflow anomaly maps identified the areas of extreme flooding along the Carolina coast days before the worst impacts occurred.

Florence demonstrated that the NWM was not merely a Texas system but a national capability. The same architecture -- weather model to land surface model to catchment transformation to channel routing -- worked for the Atlantic coastal plain as well as it did for the Texas Hill Country, because the underlying NHDPlus framework provides the same level of reach-by-reach detail everywhere in the continental United States.

## 16.4 The European Flood Awareness System (EFAS)

### 16.4.1 Institutional Origins

While the United States built its National Water Model through a rapid academic-to-operations pipeline, Europe's approach emerged from a different institutional context. The trigger was the same -- catastrophic flooding -- but the response was shaped by the EU's multinational governance structure.

The devastating floods of August 2002, which struck the Elbe and Danube basins simultaneously, caused over EUR 20 billion in damage across Central Europe and killed more than 100 people. Germany, Austria, the Czech Republic, and Slovakia were all severely affected. The floods exposed a critical weakness: national forecasting services operated in isolation, and flood warnings did not cross borders in time to be useful. A flood wave forming in the Czech Republic that would arrive in Germany's Saxony within 24 hours was being forecast by two different systems with no operational linkage between them.

In response, the European Commission tasked the Joint Research Centre (JRC) in Ispra, Italy, with developing a pan-European flood forecasting system. The resulting European Flood Awareness System (EFAS) became operational in 2012 as part of the Copernicus Emergency Management Service and has since grown into Europe's primary continental-scale flood forecasting platform. Unlike the NWM, which is operated by a single national agency (NOAA), EFAS operates in a partnership model: the JRC and ECMWF run the forecasting system centrally, but real-time forecast data are shared exclusively with national hydrological services (EFAS partners), who integrate the European forecasts with their own national models and local expertise before issuing warnings to the public.

### 16.4.2 EFAS Architecture

The EFAS architecture differs from the NWM in several important respects, reflecting different philosophical choices about how to handle uncertainty, how to represent the landscape, and how to serve a multinational user base.

**Weather input: ECMWF ensemble forecasts.** Where the NWM relies primarily on a single deterministic HRRR forecast for the short range, EFAS is built on ensemble weather prediction. The European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts (ECMWF) produces a 51-member ensemble forecast twice daily, with each member representing a slightly different plausible evolution of the atmosphere. EFAS ingests all 51 members and runs them through the hydrological model independently, producing 51 different discharge forecasts for each river reach. The spread among these forecasts provides an explicit measure of uncertainty: if all 51 members predict flooding, confidence is high; if only 5 do, the probability is low but non-zero.

This ensemble approach is more computationally expensive than a single deterministic run, but it gives EFAS a capability the NWM lacks: probabilistic flood forecasting. EFAS can say not just "the Rhine at Cologne will reach 8.0 m" but "there is a 70% probability that the Rhine at Cologne will exceed 8.0 m within the next 5 days." This probabilistic framing is more honest about the inherent uncertainty in weather prediction and more useful for risk-based decision-making.

EFAS also ingests deterministic high-resolution forecasts from ECMWF (HRES, at approximately 9 km resolution) and from the DWD (German Weather Service) COSMO model for short-range predictions. Flash flood indicators are produced from sub-daily rainfall forecasts to capture rapid-onset events on small catchments.

**Hydrological model: LISFLOOD.** Where the NWM uses Noah-MP for land surface processes and RAPID for channel routing, EFAS uses a single integrated model called LISFLOOD, developed at the JRC. LISFLOOD is a spatially distributed, physically based model that operates on a regular grid (currently 5 km resolution across Europe). It simulates:

- Interception by vegetation canopy
- Snowmelt and snow accumulation
- Soil moisture dynamics (three-layer soil model)
- Surface and subsurface runoff generation
- Channel routing using a kinematic wave approach
- Reservoir and lake regulation (where data are available)
- Groundwater recharge and baseflow

Unlike the NWM, which routes water through an irregular vector network (NHDPlus), LISFLOOD routes water on its regular computational grid using a kinematic wave approximation. This means EFAS does not produce forecasts at named stream reaches with unique identifiers in the way the NWM does. Instead, it produces gridded maps of discharge, soil moisture, and runoff that can be examined at any grid point. The advantage is simplicity and consistency across all of Europe; the disadvantage is that the 5-km grid cells do not correspond to specific physical river reaches, making it harder to link forecasts to particular locations, gauging stations, or infrastructure assets.

**River network: EU-Hydro and Copernicus data.** EFAS defines its river network from the Copernicus EU-Hydro dataset and associated catchment boundaries. The static maps -- drainage density, land use, soil type, slope -- are derived from Copernicus land monitoring products, CORINE land cover, and the European Soil Database (ESDAC). These datasets are freely available and provide consistent pan-European coverage, although at coarser resolution than the US national datasets.

### 16.4.3 EFAS Operational Products

EFAS produces a range of products designed for different forecasting horizons and user needs:

**Medium-range flood forecasts** (up to 15 days ahead) are the core product. For each EFAS reporting point -- approximately 1,500 key river locations across Europe -- the system reports the probability and expected timing of threshold exceedances. Three thresholds are defined at each point, corresponding roughly to a 2-year (high), 5-year (severe), and 20-year (extreme) return period flow. A flood notification is issued to national partners when the probability of exceeding the severe threshold reaches a defined level in any of the 51 ensemble members.

**Flash flood indicators** are derived from sub-daily rainfall forecasts and provide early warning for small, fast-responding catchments where the medium-range river forecasts may be too coarse to capture the threat. These indicators compare forecast rainfall intensity against estimated soil infiltration capacity and catchment response time.

**Seasonal hydrological outlooks** extend the forecast horizon to four months using ECMWF's seasonal forecast system. These are not specific flood predictions but rather assessments of whether the coming season is likely to be wetter or drier than normal, providing strategic context for water resource managers.

**Soil moisture and snow water equivalent maps** provide a Europe-wide picture of hydrological conditions -- how wet the soil is, how much snow is stored in mountain catchments -- that helps interpret the flood risk associated with incoming weather systems.

EFAS products are accessible to partner national hydrological services (including Hungary's OVF) through a secure web portal. Archived forecast data are publicly available for research purposes through the Copernicus Climate Data Store.

### 16.4.4 EFAS in Action: The 2013 Central European Floods

EFAS proved its value during the June 2013 floods that devastated Central Europe -- the same event that set the 500-year record at Passau and the 200-year record at Budapest. The system issued its first flood notifications on May 28, 2013 -- more than a week before the peak -- when the ensemble weather forecasts showed a persistent pattern of heavy rainfall over the northern Alps and Bohemian highlands.

By May 31, EFAS was issuing high-probability alerts for severe flooding on the Danube and Elbe systems. National hydrological services in Germany, Austria, the Czech Republic, and Hungary received these warnings through the EFAS partner portal and incorporated them into their national forecasting workflows. The advance warning allowed dam operators on the Danube to begin pre-emptive releases, and civil protection agencies in Bavaria and Saxony to begin evacuations, before the worst flooding materialized.

The 2013 event also exposed limitations. EFAS operates at 5-km resolution, which is too coarse to capture flooding on small urban streams. Several towns experienced severe flooding from tributaries too small to appear in the EFAS grid. The ensemble spread was wide enough that, while the system correctly predicted a major event, the precise location and timing of the worst flooding remained uncertain until the short-range forecast window. Post-event analysis showed that the overall magnitude of the event was well-captured but that local details -- which levee would be overtopped, which bridge would be submerged -- required the higher resolution of national models.

## 16.5 GloFAS: Global Flood Forecasting

### 16.5.1 From European to Global Coverage

The success of EFAS for Europe naturally raised the question: could a similar system be extended to the entire world? The answer was GloFAS -- the Global Flood Awareness System -- which became operational in 2011 and is now part of the Copernicus Emergency Management Service alongside EFAS.

GloFAS uses the same philosophical approach as EFAS -- ensemble weather forcing, physically-based hydrological modeling, probabilistic flood forecasts -- but at global scale. The current version (GloFAS v4.0) operates at 0.05-degree resolution (approximately 5 km) and produces forecasts for all major rivers worldwide, extending up to 30 days ahead and with seasonal outlooks extending four months.

The architecture mirrors EFAS in concept but differs in execution:

- **Weather forcing**: ECMWF ensemble forecast (51 members), same as EFAS
- **Hydrological model**: LISFLOOD, extended to global coverage with parameters derived from global soil, land cover, and topographic datasets
- **River network**: Derived from HydroSHEDS and MERIT DEM (see Table 16.1)
- **Calibration**: Limited by the scarcity of global discharge observations; uses GRDC station data where available
- **Products**: Probabilistic flood forecasts, seasonal outlooks, rapid flood mapping triggered by satellite observations

GloFAS serves a fundamentally different user community than EFAS or the NWM. Many of the regions it covers -- sub-Saharan Africa, South and Southeast Asia, Central America -- have limited national forecasting capability. For these regions, GloFAS may be the only source of quantitative flood predictions available. The system has been instrumental in supporting the humanitarian response to major flood events in Mozambique (Cyclone Idai, 2019), Bangladesh (monsoon floods, 2020), and Pakistan (catastrophic flooding, 2022), providing lead times of several days that enabled pre-positioning of humanitarian supplies.

### 16.5.2 Limitations and the Resolution Gap

The fundamental challenge of global flood forecasting is the resolution gap. At 5-km grid spacing, GloFAS captures major river floods on basins larger than a few thousand square kilometres. But it cannot resolve flooding on small rivers, urban streams, or the kind of localized flash floods that kill the most people in developing countries. A 5-km grid cell in Bangladesh covers several square kilometres of densely populated floodplain; the model produces a single discharge value for that entire area, losing all the spatial detail that determines whether a specific village is flooded or spared.

This resolution gap is being addressed incrementally. GloFAS v4.0 improved resolution from 0.1 to 0.05 degrees. National implementations of the LISFLOOD framework in countries like India and South Africa run at higher resolution within their borders, nested within the global GloFAS domain. And the emerging generation of global HAND maps (see Chapter 14) provides a pathway from GloFAS discharge forecasts to inundation mapping at the global scale, using the same pre-computed library approach demonstrated by the Texas Flood Response System.

## 16.6 Hungarian Flood Management

### 16.6.1 Hungary's Hydrological Position

Hungary occupies a unique position in European hydrology. The country sits in the Carpathian Basin, the lowest-lying region surrounded by the arc of the Carpathian Mountains, the Alps, and the Dinaric Alps. This geography creates a hydrological paradox: Hungary is relatively dry by European standards (annual precipitation averages 600 mm), yet it is extraordinarily flood-prone because it receives the accumulated drainage of a much larger mountain-rimmed basin through the Danube and Tisza river systems.

The numbers are striking. Approximately 95% of Hungary's surface water originates beyond its borders -- in the Austrian and Swiss Alps (via the Danube), in the Ukrainian and Romanian Carpathians (via the Tisza), and in the Croatian and Slovenian mountains (via the Drava). Hungary has virtually no control over the generation of this water; it can only manage what arrives at its borders. Of the country's 93,030 km$^2$ territory, approximately 21,000 km$^2$ (23%) lies in designated floodplain areas protected by levees. The Tisza floodplain alone covers approximately 10,000 km$^2$ -- a vast area of agricultural land and settled communities that would be submerged in any major flood without the protection of nearly 3,000 km of flood levees.

This combination of downstream position, flat terrain, and extensive levee dependence makes flood management not merely a technical challenge but a fundamental aspect of Hungarian national life. The country has been building and maintaining flood levees since the mid-19th century, when the great Tisza Regulation (Tisza-szabalyozas) under the direction of Pal Vasarhelyi and later Istvan Szechenyi shortened the river by approximately 450 km through cutoff channels and confined it between levees. The engineering was transformative -- it opened the Great Plain for agriculture -- but it created a permanent obligation: the levees must be maintained, raised, and defended in perpetuity, because the river behind them is now higher than the land it once flooded freely.

### 16.6.2 The Institutional Framework: OVF and the Regional Directorates

Flood management in Hungary is coordinated by the Orszagos Vizugyi Foigazgatosag (OVF) -- the General Directorate of Water Management -- which operates under the Ministry of the Interior. OVF oversees twelve regional water directorates (vizugyi igazgatosagok) that are responsible for day-to-day water management, levee maintenance, and flood defense within their territories.

During flood events, the system operates through a well-defined escalation hierarchy:

1. **I. fokozat (First degree)**: Flood alert. Water levels are rising and approaching the first warning threshold. The regional directorate activates monitoring and begins inspecting levees.
2. **II. fokozat (Second degree)**: Flood danger. Water levels exceed the second threshold. Levee defense operations begin, with patrol teams walking the levees 24 hours a day to detect seepage, piping, or structural weakness.
3. **III. fokozat (Third degree)**: Extreme flood danger. The highest alert level. Military and civilian volunteers are mobilized for levee defense. Sandbagging operations, emergency heightening of levees, and potential controlled flooding of designated retention areas may be ordered.

This three-tier system applies independently at each gauging station, so different stretches of the same river can be at different alert levels simultaneously. The alert thresholds are defined empirically at each station based on the historical flood record and the local levee geometry.

During the most severe events, flood management transitions from OVF to the National Directorate General for Disaster Management (Orszagos Katasztrofavedelmi Foigazgatosag), which coordinates across all emergency services -- fire, police, military, civil protection -- and has authority to order evacuations, deploy the Hungarian Defence Force, and activate international assistance through the EU Civil Protection Mechanism.

### 16.6.3 Hydroinfo.hu: Real-Time Hydrological Information

Hungary's operational flood forecasting system centers on hydroinfo.hu, the public-facing web portal operated by OVF's Hydrological Forecasting Service. The portal provides:

- **Real-time water levels** at approximately 200 gauging stations on the Danube, Tisza, Drava, and their tributaries, updated every 15 minutes
- **Water level forecasts** for 98 river sections, extending 1 to 5 days ahead depending on the river and the forecast method
- **Alert status** (I., II., or III. fokozat) at each station
- **Ice conditions** during winter months, critical for ice-jam flood forecasting
- **Small watershed alerts** based on rainfall intensity monitoring

The forecasting methods used by OVF combine statistical approaches (stage-stage correlations between upstream and downstream gauges) with hydrological modeling. For the Danube main stem, where travel times are long (approximately 2 to 3 days from the Austrian border to Budapest) and the upstream gauge network is dense, forecasts are reliable out to 3 to 5 days. For the Tisza, where the catchment response is faster and the upstream territory spans multiple countries, forecast lead times are shorter and uncertainty is greater.

### 16.6.4 The Tisza: Hungary's Most Dangerous River

While the Danube at Budapest is the most visible symbol of Hungarian flooding -- images of the swollen Danube lapping at the Chain Bridge appear on international news during every major event -- it is the Tisza that has caused the greatest damage and the most lives lost in recent decades. The Tisza's characteristics make it uniquely dangerous:

**Rapid rise times.** The Tisza responds much faster than the Danube to upstream rainfall events. A major rainfall event in the Carpathians can produce a flood wave at the Hungarian border within 24 to 48 hours, compared to several days for the Danube. This shorter lead time compresses the window for preparation and defense.

**Massive volume.** Despite being a smaller river than the Danube, the Tisza drains a catchment of approximately 157,000 km$^2$ -- larger than all of Hungary. During the 2001 and 2006 floods, the Tisza carried volumes that exceeded the combined capacity of all existing retention areas, forcing continuous levee defense along hundreds of kilometres simultaneously.

**Flat gradient.** On the Hungarian Great Plain, the Tisza's gradient is extremely low -- approximately 2 to 5 cm per kilometre. Flood waves propagate slowly through this flat reach, and high water levels can persist for weeks. During the 2006 spring flood, portions of the Upper Tisza remained above the third-degree alert threshold for over three weeks, placing enormous sustained stress on levees and on the people defending them.

**Coincident peaks.** The Tisza's major tributaries -- the Bodrog, Sajo, Zagyva, Koros, and Maros -- can contribute flood waves that arrive at the Tisza main stem simultaneously, producing combined peaks that exceed any individual tributary flood. The 2001 flood was exacerbated by coincident high water on the Tisza and the Somes/Szamos, arriving from different parts of the Carpathian basin.

### 16.6.5 Three Tisza Floods: 2001, 2006, and 2010

The first two decades of the 21st century brought three major flood events to the Tisza basin, each teaching different lessons about the adequacy of forecasting and defense systems.

**The 2001 Upper Tisza Flood.** In March 2001, rapid snowmelt combined with heavy rainfall across the Ukrainian and Romanian Carpathians sent a massive flood wave down the Upper Tisza. At Vasarosnameny, the flood peaked at 1,031 cm, the highest level in the instrumental record. Approximately 1,070 km$^2$ of agricultural land was inundated, 3,500 people were evacuated, and damages exceeded EUR 120 million. The levee system was stressed to its limits: at several locations, emergency sandbag walls had to be built on top of the levees when water levels exceeded the designed crest elevation. OVF's forecast for the main Tisza was reasonably accurate, but the forecast for tributary contributions -- particularly from the trans-border Somes/Szamos River -- arrived too late to enable preventive action.

**The 2006 Spring Flood.** In March-April 2006, a combination of rapid snowmelt across the entire Carpathian arc and sustained rainfall produced the longest and most geographically extensive Tisza flood of the modern era. New records were set at multiple gauging stations along the Upper and Middle Tisza. Unlike the 2001 event, which was concentrated on the Upper Tisza, the 2006 flood affected the entire river system from the Ukrainian border to the Danube confluence in Serbia. The sustained duration -- three weeks above third-degree alert at some stations -- tested the human capacity for levee defense as much as the physical capacity of the levees themselves. More than 30,000 people participated in flood defense operations, including military units, firefighters, and civilian volunteers.

**The 2010 Late Spring Flood.** In May-June 2010, an unusual late-spring rainfall pattern produced severe flooding on the Tisza and its tributaries, particularly the Koros and the Bodrog. The event coincided with major flooding on the Danube (which also set records in May 2010), stretching national flood defense resources across two major river systems simultaneously. The 2010 flood demonstrated the importance of integrated national-level coordination: with both the Danube and the Tisza in flood simultaneously, decisions about resource allocation -- which levee section gets the sandbags, which region gets the military engineers -- required real-time situational awareness that only a centralized, GIS-based monitoring system could provide.

### 16.6.6 Vasarhelyi Further Development Plan (VTT)

In response to the recurring Tisza floods, Hungary launched the Vasarhelyi Terv Tovabbi Fejlesztese (VTT) -- the Further Development of the Vasarhelyi Plan -- a comprehensive flood management strategy that combines traditional levee strengthening with modern flood retention concepts. Named after Pal Vasarhelyi, the 19th-century engineer who designed the original Tisza regulation, the VTT represents a philosophical shift from pure levee defense ("keep all the water in the channel") to managed flood storage ("give the river room to spread where it does least harm").

The plan designates six large flood retention reservoirs (tározok) along the Middle Tisza, with a combined storage capacity of approximately 1.5 billion m$^3$. During extreme floods, controlled openings in the levees allow water to flow into these reservoirs, reducing the peak discharge downstream and buying time for levee defense operations. Between floods, the retention areas serve as agricultural land, nature reserves, or recreational areas.

Three of the six reservoirs -- at Tiszaroff, Cigand, and Hanyi-Tiszasulyi -- were completed by 2015 and were activated during subsequent high-water events. The remaining three are in various stages of planning and construction. The VTT has been partially funded by the European Union through the Cohesion Fund and the European Regional Development Fund, reflecting the EU's recognition that flood management on transboundary rivers is a shared European responsibility.

The VTT also includes investments in hydrological monitoring and forecasting infrastructure: upgraded gauging stations, improved data transmission systems, and enhanced computational modeling capability for the Tisza basin. These investments complement the information systems (hydroinfo.hu) and institutional frameworks (OVF, Katasztrofavedelem) that constitute Hungary's flood management system.

### 16.6.7 Hungary Within EFAS

As an EU member state, Hungary participates in the European Flood Awareness System as a partner nation. OVF receives EFAS medium-range and flash flood forecasts through the partner portal and integrates them with national forecasting products. This integration provides two significant benefits:

First, EFAS provides ensemble-based probabilistic forecasts that OVF's national system does not currently produce. When EFAS shows a 90% probability of severe flooding on the Tisza within 5 days, OVF can begin preparations even before the flood wave appears in the national gauge network.

Second, EFAS forecasts cover the entire upstream catchment, including territory in Ukraine, Romania, and Slovakia where OVF has limited direct access to hydrological data. While bilateral data-sharing agreements exist (Hungary participates in the International Commission for the Protection of the Danube River, ICPDR), the real-time exchange of hydrological data across borders remains imperfect. EFAS, which ingests ECMWF weather forecasts for all of Europe regardless of national borders, effectively bypasses this limitation by modeling the upstream catchment response from meteorological first principles.

The relationship between EFAS and national forecasting services is complementary rather than competitive. EFAS provides the large-scale, longer-range, probabilistic context; national services provide the local detail, the historical expertise, and the institutional authority to issue warnings and direct flood defense operations. During the 2013 Danube flood, for example, EFAS notifications alerted OVF to the developing situation days before the flood wave reached the Hungarian border. OVF then used its own models and gauge network to refine the forecast for specific Hungarian river sections and to guide levee defense operations at the local level.

## 16.8 The Observational Infrastructure

### 16.8.1 Gauge Networks: The Ground Truth

No forecast has value without validation, and the primary validation tool for flood forecasting is the stream gauge. The United States operates the world's largest national gauge network through the USGS, with approximately 10,000 gauges measuring stage height and (at most sites) discharge in real time, accessible through the National Water Information System (NWIS) and Water Markup Language (WaterML2) web services.

Europe's gauge infrastructure is distributed across national services. Germany operates approximately 3,300 gauging stations, France about 3,200, and the United Kingdom about 1,500. Hungary's OVF network includes approximately 200 stations on major rivers and tributaries, with real-time data transmitted to hydroinfo.hu. Data exchange between national networks remains incomplete, although the EU Water Framework Directive and the INSPIRE directive have driven significant progress in standardizing data formats and making hydrological data available across borders.

The Global Runoff Data Centre (GRDC), maintained by the German Federal Institute of Hydrology (BfG) under WMO auspices, collects and quality-assures discharge data from national services worldwide, providing the most comprehensive global archive of river discharge observations. GRDC data are critical for calibrating and validating global systems like GloFAS.

Regardless of the network, all gauging stations share a common limitation: they measure conditions at a point, while floods affect areas. The 10,000 USGS gauges in the United States provide coverage of about one gauge per 270 NWM reaches -- meaning that the vast majority of forecasted reaches have no direct validation data. In Hungary, the 200 OVF gauges cover the main rivers well but leave thousands of smaller watercourses unmonitored. This mismatch between the spatial density of forecasts and the spatial density of observations is the central challenge of operational hydrology.

### 16.8.2 The Internet of Water and WaterML2

The fragmentation of water data across agencies, formats, and access protocols has long been recognized as a barrier to effective flood management. The solution emerging in the 2020s is the "Internet of Water" -- not a single centralized database but a federated network of data providers using standardized web services to make their data interoperable.

The technical foundation of the Internet of Water is WaterML2, adopted as an international standard by the Open Geospatial Consortium (OGC) in 2012 and by the World Meteorological Organization (WMO) in 2016. WaterML2 provides a standardized XML-based format for encoding time series of water observations -- water levels, discharge, precipitation, water quality parameters -- along with the metadata needed to interpret them (station location, measurement method, datum, units, quality flags).

A WaterML2 data stream for a US gauge looks like this:

```xml
<wml2:MeasurementTimeseries>
  <!-- Colorado River at Austin, USGS 08158000 -->
  <wml2:point>
    <wml2:MeasurementTVP>
      <wml2:time>2018-10-18T12:45:00-05:00</wml2:time>
      <wml2:value>801.3</wml2:value>  <!-- cubic meters per second -->
    </wml2:MeasurementTVP>
  </wml2:point>
</wml2:MeasurementTimeseries>
```

The USGS makes such data streams available for all its gauges with a latency of less than one hour through its web services:

```
https://waterservices.usgs.gov/nwis/iv/?format=waterml,2.0&sites=08158000&period=P1D&parameterCd=00060
```

New Zealand has demonstrated the most complete implementation of the Internet of Water concept, integrating data from 16 regional water organizations and one national organization (NIWA) into a seamless national system using WaterML2. The data remain with their respective regional providers -- there is no central data warehouse -- but standardized web services make all data discoverable and accessible through a single interface. A hydrologist in Wellington can query water levels from any station in New Zealand using the same API call, regardless of which regional council operates the station.

In Europe, the OGC Sensor Observation Service (SOS) standard serves a similar function to WaterML2, and the INSPIRE directive mandates that EU member states provide standardized access to spatial environmental data, including hydrological observations. Hungary's data.vizugy.hu portal represents OVF's response to these mandates, providing free public access to historical and real-time hydrological data since July 2024.

The vision of a truly global Internet of Water -- where any authorized user can query water data from any station in any country through standardized services -- remains unrealized but is technologically feasible. The standards exist (WaterML2, SOS, OGC SensorThings API). The institutional frameworks exist (WMO, ICPDR, EU Water Framework Directive). What remains is the political will and sustained investment to implement them universally.

### 16.8.3 Sensor Networks and Bridge-Based Monitoring

Traditional gauging stations are expensive to build and maintain -- a full USGS gauge costs approximately $25,000 per year to operate -- which limits their spatial density. Closing the gap between forecast resolution and observation density requires cheaper sensors deployed at greater density.

One promising approach exploits existing infrastructure: bridges. Texas alone has 27,000 bridges spanning 15,700 stream reaches that are forecast by the National Water Model. Each bridge is a potential sensor platform, and each bridge is also an infrastructure asset that flood forecasting aims to protect. A collaboration between the University of Texas, TxDOT (Texas Department of Transportation), and NOAA is deploying radar measurement instruments on bridges along Interstate Highway 10 from San Antonio to the Louisiana border.

The instruments -- SOMA RQ-30, manufactured by an Austrian company -- use two radar beams:

- **A vertical beam** measures the distance from the bridge deck to the water surface, yielding water level
- **An angled beam** exploits the Doppler effect from water surface ripples to measure surface velocity

Combining surface velocity with cross-section geometry (from bathymetric surveys or ADCP measurements) yields continuous discharge estimates. Twenty sensors have been deployed along the I-10 corridor, and the concept is being extended to other major highway crossings.

The principle underlying this deployment is that "densified forecasting requires densified measurement." If the NWM produces forecasts for 2.7 million reaches but observations exist at only 10,000, the forecasts for the other 2,690,000 reaches are unvalidated. Every additional sensor reduces this validation gap and provides data for improving model parameters.

In Hungary, a similar philosophy drives the expansion of the hydroinfo.hu monitoring network. OVF has been upgrading its gauging stations with automated data loggers and real-time telemetry, reducing the latency between measurement and data availability from hours to minutes. The Hungarian network is dense for the major rivers but sparse for smaller watercourses -- precisely the streams where flash floods pose the greatest threat.

### 16.8.4 Bathymetric Survey and Channel Geometry

Translating a water level measurement into a discharge estimate -- or translating a HAND-derived flood depth into an inundation map -- requires knowledge of the channel geometry below the water surface. This below-water geometry (bathymetry) cannot be measured by standard topographic methods:

- **Acoustic Doppler Current Profilers (ADCP)** use sound waves to simultaneously measure water velocity and channel depth from a moving boat. A single ADCP survey can collect 50,000 depth-velocity measurement points in a few hours. ADCPs have become the standard method for high-quality discharge measurement at USGS gauging stations and at OVF stations in Hungary.

- **Green-beam LiDAR** uses a laser wavelength (approximately 532 nm) that penetrates clear water, unlike the near-infrared wavelength (approximately 1064 nm) used in standard topographic LiDAR. Where water clarity permits, green-beam LiDAR can map the entire channel bed from an aircraft, producing a seamless "topobathy" DEM that combines above-water terrain from standard LiDAR with below-water bathymetry from the green-beam system. This technology is particularly relevant for the Tisza and Danube in Hungary, where floodplain mapping requires knowledge of channel geometry at scales that bridge surveys cannot provide.

- **Remote-control boats** carrying ADCP instruments can survey rivers that are too hazardous for manned vessels during flood conditions, providing channel geometry data precisely when it is most needed.

Combining above-water LiDAR topography with below-water bathymetry creates the complete channel geometry that the HAND method (Chapter 14) requires for accurate flood inundation mapping. In Hungary, the OVF maintains cross-section surveys at key locations along the Tisza and Danube, but continuous bathymetric coverage of the kind available in parts of the US through the USGS remains a goal rather than an achievement.

## 16.9 Emergency Management Operations

### 16.9.1 The US Model: Texas Emergency Operations

In the United States, emergency management follows a tiered system analogous to Hungary's but with a different institutional structure. When a flood exceeds local capacity, cities and counties request assistance through inter-local agreements with neighbors. If the event exceeds regional capacity, the state is activated through its Division of Emergency Management.

In Texas, the State Emergency Operations Center (SOC) is located two floors underground in Austin, designed to survive extreme events. During Hurricane Harvey, the SOC operated at maximum capacity for over 10 days. The scale of the response illustrates why accurate forecasting matters so much:

- 16,000 National Guard troops were mobilized
- 85 helicopters operated simultaneously over Beaumont/Port Arthur
- All national Urban Search and Rescue teams were deployed to Texas
- Victims were rescued by helicopter hoist, transferred to staging areas ("lily pads"), and transported by Chinook helicopters to unaffected cities
- The financial burn rate during peak operations exceeded millions of dollars per hour

The GIS-based flood mapping from the Texas Flood Response System provided critical situational awareness during these operations. Maps showing which neighborhoods were flooded, which roads were passable, and where flood waters were still rising guided the allocation of rescue helicopters and ground teams. Without spatial intelligence, rescue operations would have been reactive rather than proactive -- responding to calls for help rather than deploying to areas of known need.

### 16.9.2 The Hungarian Model: Katasztrofavedelem

Hungary's emergency management system for floods operates through OVF during the early stages of a flood event and transitions to the National Directorate General for Disaster Management (Orszagos Katasztrofavedelmi Foigazgatosag -- Katasztrofavedelem) when the event reaches the third alert level or when multiple river systems are in flood simultaneously.

The Hungarian system has several distinctive features shaped by the country's geography and history:

**Levee defense as a national duty.** Hungarian law (the Water Management Act) establishes flood defense as a public obligation. During third-degree alerts, regional water directorates can requisition labor, equipment, and materials for levee defense. The Hungarian Defence Force (Magyar Honvedseg) deploys engineering units for sandbag filling, levee reinforcement, and emergency construction. In major events like the 2006 Tisza flood, tens of thousands of volunteers join the organized defense effort.

**Continuous levee patrol.** During second- and third-degree alerts, patrol teams walk the levees continuously -- day and night, in 12-hour shifts -- looking for signs of failure: seepage through the levee body (buzgárok), piping (internal erosion creating channels through the levee), surface erosion, and animal burrows that weaken the structure. This human observation system, while labor-intensive, catches failure modes that instrumental monitoring alone would miss.

**Controlled inundation.** The VTT flood retention reservoirs (Section 16.6.6) provide a managed option for extreme events: rather than allowing uncontrolled overtopping, operators can deliberately open specific sections of the levee system to fill retention areas, reducing downstream flood peaks. This requires real-time decision-making informed by accurate flood forecasts -- opening a retention area too early wastes capacity, while opening too late may not prevent downstream overtopping.

**Cross-border coordination.** Hungary participates in the International Commission for the Protection of the Danube River (ICPDR), which coordinates flood management across the 14 countries sharing the Danube basin. The ICPDR's Flood Action Programme includes transboundary flood forecasting, joint flood exercises, and harmonized flood risk mapping under the EU Floods Directive (2007/60/EC). Similar bilateral agreements exist with Ukraine and Romania for the Tisza basin, though operational data exchange remains less seamless than within the EU.

### 16.9.3 The EU Floods Directive

The EU Floods Directive (2007/60/EC) provides the legal framework for flood risk management across all EU member states. It requires each member state to:

1. **Preliminary flood risk assessment** to identify areas of potential significant flood risk
2. **Flood hazard and flood risk maps** for those areas, showing flood extent, water depth, and affected population and assets for different probability scenarios (high, medium, and low probability events)
3. **Flood risk management plans** setting objectives and measures for reducing flood risk, updated on a six-year cycle

Hungary completed its first revision of flood hazard and risk maps in 2022, covering all major river systems and their floodplains. The maps are accessible through OVF's geoportal (geoportal.vizugy.hu) and the national flood risk management plans through the vizeink.hu portal. The directive's requirement for common standards across EU member states has driven significant harmonization of flood mapping methods, data formats, and risk assessment approaches, although differences in national implementation persist.

At the European level, the JRC's river flood hazard maps provide pan-European coverage at 100 m resolution for return periods from 10 to 500 years, derived from EFAS/LISFLOOD hydrological modeling. These maps are freely available and complement the more detailed national maps required by the Floods Directive.

## 16.10 The Human Dimension: Preventable Tragedies

### 16.10.1 The Gap Between Capability and Delivery

The technology described in this chapter is impressive, but technology alone does not save lives. Between the model output and the person at risk lies a chain of communication, interpretation, and action that can fail at any link.

In August 2014, Jessica Hollis, a sheriff's deputy in Travis County, Texas, drove down Fritz Hughes Park Road at 2:00 AM to check on an isolated subdivision. She was washed off at a low-water crossing and drowned. She was 35 years old and had two sons. The creek at that crossing, though small and unnamed on most maps, exists as a reach in the National Water Model. A neighboring farmer had, on previous occasions, driven his car to the crossing with headlights illuminated to warn drivers of high water. He did not do so that night.

This tragedy exemplifies the "last mile" problem of flood forecasting. The technical capability to predict dangerous water levels at that crossing existed -- or could have existed with the NWM's reach-level forecasting. But no automated system translated that prediction into a warning that reached Deputy Hollis before she reached the water. The road has since been renamed in her honor, but the underlying gap -- between modeling capability and personal warning delivery -- remains the most important unsolved problem in operational flood forecasting.

In Hungary, a similar gap exists at the thousands of low-water crossings (gazlok) on minor roads in the Great Plain and the northern hills. These crossings become impassable during moderate floods, and drivers unfamiliar with local conditions may attempt to cross rising water. While hydroinfo.hu provides flood alerts for major river stations, the small streams that flood these crossings are often below the monitoring threshold.

### 16.10.2 Toward Real-Time Flood Navigation

The vision for closing the last-mile gap combines flood forecasting with personal navigation technology. If a vehicle navigation system could access real-time NWM or EFAS forecasts and HAND-based inundation maps, it could route drivers around flooded crossings before they arrive. The technical components exist: the NWM produces reach-level forecasts, HAND provides the depth-to-inundation translation, and vehicle navigation systems already reroute around traffic congestion using real-time data feeds. The missing link is integration -- connecting the flood data stream to the navigation data stream -- and the institutional agreements to make it happen.

In Europe, the European Commission's Connected and Automated Driving initiatives include provisions for weather and road-condition data integration into vehicle navigation systems. Hungary's national road authority (Magyar Kozut) maintains a real-time road closure database that includes flood-related closures, but integration with hydrological forecast data remains at the pilot stage.

The Waze application and similar crowd-sourced navigation tools already incorporate user-reported flood closures, but these are reactive (someone must encounter the flood and report it) rather than predictive (the flood is forecast before it happens). The combination of predictive hydrological modeling and connected vehicle technology could, in principle, eliminate preventable flood-related traffic fatalities -- but it requires a level of inter-system integration that neither the US nor Europe has yet achieved.

## 16.11 Reducing Uncertainty: Validation and Data Assimilation

### 16.11.1 Observed vs. Modelled Rating Curves

At locations where USGS or OVF gauges provide observed rating curves -- empirical relationships between stage height and discharge built from decades of direct flow measurements -- the HAND-derived synthetic rating curves can be directly validated. These comparisons are essential for understanding the accuracy of the automated approach and for identifying reaches where the assumptions break down.

Several complications arise in these comparisons:

**Datum inconsistency.** USGS stage heights use arbitrary local datums, sometimes set well below the channel bed, while HAND-derived water levels are referenced to the nearest drainage point. Aligning these two reference systems requires knowledge of the gauge datum and the channel bed elevation, which are not always consistently documented.

**Extrapolation limits.** Observed rating curves are calibrated from field measurements at specific discharges. At the highest flows -- the flows that matter most for flood forecasting -- direct measurements are rare because it is dangerous to deploy measurement equipment during extreme floods. ADCP measurements from bridges during major events are progressively filling this gap, but many observed rating curves are poorly constrained at extreme flows.

**Non-uniform flow.** The HAND approach assumes uniform flow conditions (Manning's equation), while real flows are non-uniform -- backwater effects from bridges, confluences, and downstream obstructions modify the stage-discharge relationship. These effects are particularly important in the flat reaches of the Tisza and Danube in Hungary, where backwater from the Danube can affect Tisza water levels hundreds of kilometres upstream of the confluence.

### 16.11.2 Field Validation During Flood Events

During the October 2018 Lake Travis event, researchers from the University of Texas physically marked water edge positions along Town Lake and Lake Austin while 801 m$^3$/s (28,300 cfs) was being released from Mansfield Dam. These GPS-marked positions, combined with the HAND raster, provided independent validation of modelled inundation extents. The "Pin to Flood Map" application allows anyone -- emergency responders, scientists, or citizens -- to record GPS coordinates at the water's edge during flood events, creating crowd-sourced validation data.

In Hungary, post-flood surveys conducted by OVF after major Tisza events provide similar validation data, documenting the maximum flood extent through debris lines, watermarks on buildings, and satellite imagery from the Copernicus Emergency Management Service. The Copernicus Emergency Management Service provides on-demand rapid mapping for flood events using Sentinel-1 SAR imagery, which can detect flood extent through cloud cover -- a critical capability during the rainy conditions that typically accompany major floods.

### 16.11.3 Systematic Improvement Through Data Assimilation

The discrepancies between modelled and observed conditions during floods are not merely indicators of error -- they are information that can improve the system. If a HAND-based flood map shows inundation that does not match satellite observations, the likely sources of error include:

- **Manning's roughness coefficient** -- too high or too low for the actual channel and floodplain conditions
- **Channel geometry** -- particularly subsurface bathymetry that determines how much water the channel can carry before overtopping its banks
- **Weather forecast inputs** -- errors that propagate through the entire modeling chain, producing incorrect discharge at the routing stage

Systematic comparison during multiple flood events allows progressive refinement of these parameters. The US National Water Model incorporates USGS gauge observations through data assimilation -- adjusting model states (soil moisture, channel storage) to be consistent with observed water levels -- which progressively reduces forecast error. EFAS performs similar assimilation using data from national gauge networks. In Hungary, OVF's forecasting system assimilates real-time water level observations from the hydroinfo.hu network to correct and update its predictions as a flood event unfolds.

## 16.12 The Broader Context: Design Storms, Climate Change, and Water Quality

### 16.12.1 When Risk Definitions Change

Flood risk maps are based on design storms -- the precipitation events that define, for example, the "100-year flood" used in insurance and planning. In the United States, these values come from NOAA Atlas 14. When Atlas 14 was recently updated for Texas, the 100-year, 24-hour rainfall increased from 305 mm (12 inches) to 381 mm (15 inches). This single change invalidated 15 years of flood insurance studies, of which only half the state had been completed using the old values.

In Europe, equivalent design storm values are defined nationally (in Hungary, the OMSZ provides csapadek-intenzitas -- precipitation intensity -- curves for engineering design). The EU Floods Directive requires member states to account for the effects of climate change in their flood risk assessments, but the methodology for doing so varies across countries and remains an active area of research.

The automated, scalable nature of the HAND approach (Chapter 14) offers a way to rapidly regenerate flood maps when risk parameters change. Rather than repeating years of manual hydraulic analysis, a new set of design storms can be propagated through the NWM or EFAS chain, transformed to flood depths via HAND, and mapped to inundation extents within hours. This scalability is one of the most important practical benefits of the computational approach to flood mapping.

### 16.12.2 Climate Change and Intensifying Flood Risk

Climate change is altering precipitation patterns in ways that directly affect flood risk. The Clausius-Clapeyron relationship -- which predicts that the atmosphere can hold approximately 7% more water vapor for each 1 degree Celsius of warming -- implies that extreme rainfall events will become more intense as temperatures rise. Observations confirm this: the frequency of record-setting rainfall events has increased measurably in both the United States and Europe over the past three decades.

For Hungary, climate projections suggest increasing flood risk on the Tisza from more intense spring rainfall events in the Carpathians, combined with more variable snow accumulation and melt patterns. The CARPATCLIM dataset (covering 1961-2010) and ERA5 reanalysis provide the historical baseline against which future changes can be assessed. EFAS seasonal outlooks, extended to incorporate climate projection scenarios, provide the longest-range view of evolving flood risk.

The ability to rapidly recompute flood risk at continental scale -- using updated climate-adjusted design storms, current land cover data, and the latest HAND maps -- is not a theoretical exercise but an operational necessity. The flood maps that guide building permits, insurance rates, and evacuation planning must evolve as the climate evolves. The computational infrastructure described in this chapter makes that evolution practical rather than prohibitively expensive.

### 16.12.3 Beyond Floods: Water Quality and Ecological Applications

While no national-scale water quality model of the same ambition as the NWM or EFAS currently exists for water quality, the underlying hydrological infrastructure provides the foundation for future development. The HAND-derived channel geometry -- volume, surface area, hydraulic radius as functions of depth -- contains exactly the information needed to estimate residence time and transport capacity for dissolved and particulate constituents. The NHDPlus network topology describes how contaminants would propagate downstream. And the NWM's continuous simulation of discharge at every reach provides the flow conditions under which water quality processes occur.

In Europe, the EU Water Framework Directive already requires member states to assess ecological and chemical status of all surface water bodies. The integration of hydrological modeling (EFAS/LISFLOOD) with water quality models is an active area of JRC research, with the goal of producing operational water quality forecasts analogous to the current flood forecasts.

## 16.13 Global Perspectives

### 16.13.1 Flood Forecasting in the Developing World

The systems described in this chapter -- NWM, EFAS, GloFAS -- represent the state of the art in affluent nations with well-developed institutional infrastructure. But the majority of flood fatalities occur in countries with far less capability: South Asia, sub-Saharan Africa, and Central America account for more than 80% of global flood deaths.

GloFAS provides the most accessible path to flood forecasting for these regions. Because it is driven entirely by ECMWF weather forecasts (which are global) and uses global datasets for its hydrological parameters (soil from HWSD, land cover from ESA WorldCover, topography from MERIT DEM), it can produce forecasts for rivers anywhere on Earth without requiring local gauge data. The forecasts are coarse (5 km resolution) and poorly validated in data-sparse regions, but they are far better than no forecast at all.

The World Meteorological Organization (WMO) Flash Flood Guidance System extends this coverage to flash floods, providing regional flash flood guidance products for Africa, Southeast Asia, and Central America. These products combine satellite-based rainfall estimates with soil moisture indices to identify areas where flash flooding is likely, providing lead times of several hours in regions with no ground-based forecasting capability.

India's flood forecasting system, operated by the Central Water Commission (CWC), represents an intermediate case: extensive gauge networks exist on the major rivers, but real-time data integration and model-based forecasting capability vary greatly across states. The India Water Resources Information System (India-WRIS) provides a national data portal, but operational flood forecasting remains fragmented. GloFAS forecasts for Indian rivers are increasingly used as a complementary source of early warning, particularly for the transboundary rivers (Brahmaputra, Ganges) where upstream data from China and Nepal is limited.

### 16.13.2 The Role of Satellite Earth Observation

Satellite data play an increasingly important role in both flood forecasting and flood response, particularly in regions with sparse ground-based monitoring:

**Sentinel-1 SAR imagery** can detect flood extent through cloud cover by measuring the backscatter difference between water and land surfaces. The Copernicus Emergency Management Service uses Sentinel-1 to produce rapid flood maps within hours of activation, providing critical information for humanitarian response.

**GPM/IMERG satellite precipitation estimates** provide near-real-time rainfall data at 10-km resolution globally, complementing ground-based rain gauges in data-sparse regions. While less accurate than ground-based measurements, satellite precipitation data are essential for driving hydrological models in regions where gauge networks are thin or non-existent.

**GRACE/GRACE-FO gravity measurements** monitor changes in terrestrial water storage at monthly timescales, providing information about antecedent moisture conditions that influence flood severity. Wet soil conditions before a rainfall event increase flood risk; GRACE data quantify this effect at continental scales.

These satellite capabilities are equally relevant for all three regional threads of this chapter. Hungary uses Copernicus data products extensively, the United States integrates satellite observations into NWM data assimilation, and developing countries depend on satellite data as their primary (sometimes only) source of spatially distributed hydrological information.

## 16.14 Looking Ahead: The Convergence of Systems

### 16.14.1 From Separate Systems to Integrated Intelligence

The three flood forecasting systems described in this chapter -- NWM, EFAS, and Hungary's OVF -- were developed independently and operate on different architectures, different data standards, and different institutional frameworks. Yet they are converging. The NWM is incorporating ensemble techniques that EFAS pioneered. EFAS is moving toward higher-resolution, reach-based routing that the NWM demonstrated. Both are exploring AI-based post-processing to improve forecast accuracy (Chapter 23). And both are pushing toward the same goal: translating raw discharge forecasts into impact-based warnings that tell people not just "the river will rise" but "your house will flood" and "this road will be impassable."

Hungary's position at the intersection of these developments is uniquely interesting. As an EU member state, Hungary participates in EFAS and benefits from the ensemble forecasting and transboundary modeling that the European system provides. As a country with a long and sophisticated tradition of flood management on the Tisza and Danube, it brings institutional expertise and historical data that enrich the European system. And as a downstream nation dependent on upstream transboundary flows, it has perhaps the strongest motivation of any European country to push for more seamless international data exchange and more integrated forecasting systems.

The Internet of Water vision -- standardized, interoperable, real-time water data exchange -- is the common thread that connects all these developments. Whether the standard is WaterML2, OGC SensorThings API, or a future protocol not yet defined, the principle is the same: water data should flow as freely as water itself, crossing institutional and national boundaries without friction. The technologies described in this chapter -- from HRRR weather models to HAND inundation maps, from RAPID channel routing to bridge-mounted radar sensors -- are only as powerful as the data infrastructure that connects them.

### 16.14.2 The Promise and the Obligation

The systems described in this chapter represent an extraordinary achievement: for the first time in human history, it is technically possible to forecast flooding at every river reach on a continent, hours to days in advance, with quantified uncertainty. But technical possibility is not the same as effective operation. The 2017 Harvey disaster, the 2013 Central European floods, and the recurring Tisza floods all demonstrate that even the best models cannot prevent damage if their outputs do not reach the people who need them, in a form they can understand, in time to act.

The transition from forecast to warning to protective action is a chain of human decisions, institutional procedures, and communication systems that is at least as important as the hydrological modeling chain. Building that human chain -- training emergency managers to interpret probabilistic forecasts, educating the public about flood risk, establishing clear authority for evacuation decisions, and ensuring that warnings reach vulnerable populations including the elderly, the disabled, and those without internet access -- is the work of a generation, not a software update.

The hydrologist's obligation in this context is clear: build systems that are not just accurate but understandable, not just fast but reliable, and not just technically elegant but operationally useful. The measure of success is not the correlation coefficient of a hindcast but the number of lives saved when the next flood comes.
