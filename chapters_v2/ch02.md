# Chapter 2: Mapping Water: From Paper to Pixels

Water does not respect the lines we draw on maps. A rainstorm crosses county borders without stopping. The Danube flows through ten countries before reaching the Black Sea, and the Tisza gathers tributaries from five nations before joining it. A groundwater aquifer beneath the Great Plains of the United States cares nothing for the boundary between Kansas and Nebraska. Yet the moment we try to measure, model, or manage water, we must place it on a map -- and every map rests on a set of invisible assumptions about how to represent the curved, lumpy, spinning Earth on a flat surface.

These assumptions matter enormously for water science. Get them wrong, and a watershed area calculation is off by ten percent. Confuse two coordinate systems, and a flood map shifts by hundreds of meters -- enough to change which neighborhoods appear to be at risk. Ignore the difference between an ellipsoid height from a GPS receiver and a true elevation above sea level, and a levee design can be fatally flawed.

This chapter is about the hidden infrastructure beneath every water map: the science of **geodesy** (geodeziai), the mathematics of **map projections** (terkepi vetuletek), and the practical coordinate systems that make spatial water data usable. The goal is not to turn the reader into a geodesist, but to provide a working understanding of why these things matter, what can go wrong, and how to get them right. (The data that depend on these foundations are catalogued in Chapter 3; the GIS tools that work with them are introduced in Chapter 4.) We begin with the shape of the Earth itself, move to the coordinate systems that describe positions on it, examine how the curved surface is flattened into maps, and conclude with the satellite missions that have revolutionized how we measure both the Earth's shape and the water stored on and within it.

## 2.1 Why Geodesy Matters for Water

### 2.1.1 The Hidden Foundation of Every Water Map

Every hydrological analysis begins with a deceptively simple question: where is the water? To answer it, we need to describe locations on Earth with enough precision that data from different sources -- a satellite image, a stream gauge record, a soil map, a weather radar sweep -- can be combined into a coherent picture. This is the domain of geodesy: the science of measuring and representing the Earth's shape, orientation, and gravity field.

For most daily work in hydroinformatics, the geodetic machinery operates invisibly. GIS software reprojects data on the fly, GPS receivers report coordinates in familiar formats, and elevation models come with metadata specifying their vertical reference. The problems arise at the boundaries: when data from different countries must be merged, when centimeter-level accuracy matters for flood mapping, when a legacy dataset from the 1970s must be combined with modern satellite observations, or when an analysis spans a continent and the choice of map projection determines whether area calculations are meaningful.

Consider a concrete example. Hungary's national water management datasets -- stream networks, gauge locations, flood risk maps -- are stored in the EOV coordinate system (EPSG:23700), based on the Hungarian Datum of 1972. European-wide datasets from Copernicus use ETRS89, the European Terrestrial Reference System. Global satellite products like GRACE terrestrial water storage anomalies use WGS84. A hydrologist studying water balance in the Tisza basin using all three data sources must understand how these systems relate to one another, or the analysis will contain systematic spatial errors that no amount of sophisticated modeling can correct.

The situation is analogous in other parts of the world. A flood forecaster in the United States works with NAD83-referenced gauge data, WGS84 GPS observations, and NAVD88 elevations. A researcher in India uses the Everest ellipsoid for historical survey data alongside WGS84 satellite products. In every case, the underlying geodetic framework determines whether spatial data can be trusted.

### 2.1.2 Three Questions That Drive This Chapter

The practical content of geodesy, for the water professional, reduces to three questions that recur throughout every chapter of this book:

First, what is the shape of the Earth, and how do we approximate it mathematically? This leads us to the geoid and the ellipsoid -- the two fundamental reference surfaces that underpin all coordinate systems. The geoid matters because it defines what we mean by "sea level" and therefore what we mean by "elevation." The ellipsoid matters because it provides the mathematical surface on which latitude and longitude are defined.

Second, how do we flatten the Earth onto a map without destroying the information we need? This is the problem of map projections. For hydrology, the critical property is usually area: if a map distorts areas, then every calculation of rainfall depth over a watershed, every estimate of runoff volume, every comparison of drainage densities becomes unreliable. But sometimes we need accurate shapes (for navigation and detailed mapping) or accurate distances (for travel time calculations in flood routing). No single projection preserves everything, and the choice of projection is a decision with real consequences.

Third, how do we ensure that data from different sources, different countries, and different decades can be combined correctly? This is the problem of datums and coordinate transformations. It is the most practically important question for the working hydrologist, and the one most often handled carelessly.

## 2.2 The Shape of the Earth

### 2.2.1 The Geoid: What Gravity Tells Us About "Level"

The Earth is not a sphere. It is not even a perfect ellipsoid. The actual shape of the Earth's surface -- with its mountains, ocean trenches, and continental shelves -- is far too irregular to serve as a reference for measurement. Instead, geodesists define an idealized surface called the **geoid**: the equipotential surface of Earth's gravity field that best approximates mean sea level.

Imagine that the ocean could flow freely through channels under the continents, responding only to gravity and the Earth's rotation, with no winds, tides, or currents. The surface of this hypothetical global ocean is the geoid. It is smooth but not regular: where dense rock lies beneath the surface, gravity is slightly stronger, and the geoid surface rises; where mass is deficient, the geoid dips. These undulations are subtle -- the geoid deviates from a best-fit ellipsoid by at most about 100 meters worldwide -- but they are physically meaningful. The geoid surface is everywhere perpendicular to the local direction of gravity, which is why a spirit level (a bubble level) defines a surface parallel to the geoid, not parallel to any mathematical ellipsoid.

For hydrology, the geoid is the natural reference for elevation. When we say a point is "200 meters above sea level," we mean it is 200 meters above the geoid. Water flows downhill in the gravitational sense -- from higher to lower gravitational potential -- so the geoid is the surface that defines the direction of "downhill." An ellipsoid, by contrast, is a mathematical convenience that does not necessarily align with the gravitational field. A lake surface is parallel to the geoid, not to an ellipsoid, which is why the geoid matters for any work involving water levels, hydraulic gradients, or flood elevations.

The geoid is determined from a combination of satellite gravity measurements (notably the GRACE and GOCE missions), surface gravity observations, and satellite altimetry over the oceans. Modern geoid models such as EGM2008 (Earth Gravitational Model 2008) provide the geoid height -- the separation between the geoid and a reference ellipsoid -- on a global grid with roughly 2.5-arc-minute resolution (approximately 5 km). Regional geoid models achieve sub-centimeter accuracy in well-surveyed areas. Hungary, for example, uses a national geoid model fitted to the GRS67 ellipsoid of the HD72 datum for precise leveling work.

### 2.2.2 The Ellipsoid: A Mathematical Approximation

While the geoid captures the physical reality of Earth's gravity, it is too complex to serve as the computational basis for coordinate systems. Instead, positions on Earth are defined relative to an **ellipsoid** (also called a **spheroid**) -- the three-dimensional surface generated by rotating an ellipse about its shorter (polar) axis. An ellipsoid is defined by just two parameters:

- The **semi-major axis** $a$ -- the equatorial radius
- The **semi-minor axis** $b$ -- the polar radius

From these, the **flattening** $f$ is derived:

$$f = \frac{a - b}{a}$$

For the Earth, the best-fit ellipsoid has $a \approx 6{,}378{,}137$ m and $b \approx 6{,}356{,}752$ m, giving $f \approx 1/298.257$. The polar radius is about 21 km shorter than the equatorial radius -- a difference of roughly one-third of one percent. This slight oblateness is caused by the centrifugal effect of Earth's rotation, which pushes material outward at the equator. To the eye, Earth looks perfectly spherical; mathematically, the difference matters.

The choice of ellipsoid has evolved as measurement technology improved. Early ellipsoids were fitted to local regions using ground surveys, while modern ellipsoids are global, fitted using satellite observations.

The GRS 1980 and WGS 1984 ellipsoids are nearly identical -- they share the same semi-major axis and agree in flattening to the sixth decimal place. The practical difference between them is negligible for all but the most precise geodetic work. This near-identity is why NAD83 (which uses GRS 1980) and WGS84 (which uses WGS 1984) are often treated as equivalent, though technically they are maintained by different organizations and diverge by about two meters due to differences in how each system accounts for tectonic plate motion.

### 2.2.3 From Ellipsoid to Datum: Anchoring the Math to the Earth

An ellipsoid is a mathematical shape; a **datum** is an ellipsoid that has been positioned and oriented relative to the actual Earth. The datum defines the reference frame in which coordinates have meaning. Two datasets may use the same ellipsoid but different datums, and their coordinates will not agree.

The conceptual distinction is important. An ellipsoid is like a custom-made glove: it has a definite size and shape, but it is not yet on a hand. A datum is the ellipsoid fitted onto the Earth -- positioned at a specific point and oriented in a specific direction. Historically, national datums were positioned to give the best fit to the geoid in a particular country or region: the ellipsoid was "pinned" to a fundamental point (often a national survey origin) where the ellipsoid surface was forced to coincide with the geoid. This meant that the same ellipsoid, positioned differently, would give different coordinates for the same physical location.

Modern datums are **geocentric** -- the center of the ellipsoid coincides with the Earth's center of mass, as determined by satellite observations. This is a fundamental shift. Geocentric datums are not optimized for any particular country; they provide a globally consistent reference frame. The two dominant geocentric datum families in use today are WGS84 (maintained by the U.S. National Geospatial-Intelligence Agency for the GPS system) and ITRS/ETRS89 (maintained by the International Earth Rotation and Reference Systems Service and its European realization).

The practical consequence is that older national datums -- NAD27 in the United States, HD72 in Hungary, the Pulkovo 1942 system across the former Soviet sphere -- give coordinates that differ from WGS84 by amounts ranging from meters to hundreds of meters. These offsets are not errors in the old datums; they are systematic differences that arise from positioning the ellipsoid differently relative to the Earth's center. Converting between datums requires transformation parameters (typically a 7-parameter Helmert transformation involving three translations, three rotations, and a scale factor) that are specific to each pair of datums.

## 2.3 Datums of the World: National, Continental, and Global

### 2.3.1 The Concept of a Datum: One Idea, Many Implementations

Every nation with a tradition of surveying and mapping has, at some point, defined its own geodetic datum -- a reference framework optimized for its territory. This proliferation is not carelessness; it reflects a practical reality. Before satellite geodesy, the only way to achieve high accuracy was to fit an ellipsoid to the local geoid as closely as possible over the country of interest. A datum that fits Hungary well will not fit Australia well, and vice versa, because the geoid undulates differently in each region.

The result is a world full of datums, each with its own ellipsoid and positioning. Understanding this landscape is essential for any international water project, because datasets from different countries will arrive in different datums, and combining them without proper transformation will produce spatial misalignments.

The modern trend is toward consolidation: most countries are adopting or transitioning to geocentric datums compatible with WGS84. But the legacy data -- decades of topographic maps, cadastral records, hydrographic surveys, and infrastructure databases -- remain in national datums, and will for years to come. A working hydrologist must be comfortable with at least the datums of their own country and the global systems used by satellite products.

### 2.3.2 WGS84: The Global Reference

The World Geodetic System of 1984 (WGS84) is the coordinate reference system used by the Global Positioning System (GPS). It is a geocentric datum: the origin is at the Earth's center of mass, the Z-axis points toward the IERS Reference Pole (essentially the North Pole), and the X-axis points toward the IERS Reference Meridian (essentially the Greenwich meridian). The ellipsoid is the WGS 1984 ellipsoid ($a = 6{,}378{,}137$ m, $1/f = 298.257223563$).

WGS84 is the closest thing to a universal coordinate system. GPS receivers report positions in WGS84 by default. Global satellite products -- DEMs, land cover maps, gravity measurements -- are typically distributed in WGS84 geographic coordinates (EPSG:4326). When someone says "the coordinates are 47.50 N, 19.04 E" without further qualification, they almost certainly mean WGS84.

However, WGS84 is not a single fixed frame. It is periodically refined to account for improved knowledge of the Earth's center of mass and orientation. The current realization (as of 2026) is WGS84 (G2296), which is aligned to the International Terrestrial Reference Frame (ITRF) at the centimeter level. For most hydrological work, these refinements are irrelevant -- the differences between WGS84 realizations are sub-centimeter. But for precise geodetic work, it matters.

### 2.3.3 NAD83: The North American Datum

The North American Datum of 1983 (NAD83) is the horizontal datum used for all federal mapping and spatial data in the United States, Canada, and Mexico. It uses the GRS 1980 ellipsoid, which is functionally identical to the WGS 1984 ellipsoid. NAD83 was designed to be geocentric, but its original realization (NAD83/1986) was based on Doppler satellite observations that placed the origin about 2.2 meters from the Earth's true center of mass. Subsequent adjustments -- NAD83(HARN), NAD83(CORS96), NAD83(2011) -- have improved the alignment but maintained continuity with the original system rather than jumping to a new frame.

For the international reader, the key facts about NAD83 are:

NAD83 and WGS84 agree to within about two meters. For any work at the meter level or coarser -- which includes most hydrological modeling -- they can be treated as identical. The differences matter only for surveying, precise engineering, and geodetic research.

NAD83 is tied to the North American tectonic plate. This means that NAD83 coordinates for a benchmark in California do not change as the Pacific Plate carries California northwestward at about 5 cm per year, whereas WGS84 coordinates for the same benchmark do change (because WGS84 is fixed to the Earth's center of mass, not to any plate). Over decades, this divergence accumulates.

The predecessor to NAD83 was NAD27, based on the Clarke 1866 ellipsoid and established from ground surveys. The offset between NAD27 and NAD83 varies spatially but is typically tens to hundreds of meters across North America. Historical data in NAD27 must be transformed to NAD83 using the NADCON grid shift files before it can be combined with modern data.

The United States is planning a modernization of its spatial reference framework, replacing NAD83 and the vertical datum NAVD88 with new frames aligned to ITRF. This transition, expected to be completed in the late 2020s, will bring U.S. coordinates into closer alignment with WGS84 and simplify international data exchange.

### 2.3.4 ETRS89: The European Terrestrial Reference System

The European Terrestrial Reference System 1989 (ETRS89) is the geodetic datum adopted by the European Union for all cross-border spatial data. It uses the GRS 1980 ellipsoid (the same as NAD83) and was defined to coincide with ITRF at the epoch 1989.0. Like NAD83, ETRS89 is fixed to a tectonic plate -- in this case, the stable part of the Eurasian plate. This means that ETRS89 coordinates for a benchmark in Budapest do not change due to plate motion, even though the benchmark is moving with the plate at about 2.5 cm per year relative to the Earth's center of mass.

ETRS89 is the mandatory reference system for EU spatial data infrastructure (the INSPIRE Directive). Copernicus data products, Eurostat geographic datasets, and cross-border water management data are distributed in ETRS89 or in projections based on it (such as ETRS89/LAEA, EPSG:3035, for pan-European mapping). For the Hungarian hydrologist, ETRS89 is the "bridge" datum: national data in EOV/HD72 must be transformed to ETRS89 for interoperability with European datasets, and ETRS89 is close enough to WGS84 that the transformation to global products is trivial (sub-meter differences).

The relationship between ETRS89 and WGS84 is very close -- at the epoch 1989.0, they were identical. Since then, the Eurasian plate has moved about 90 cm relative to the Earth's center, so ETRS89 now differs from the latest WGS84 realization by about that amount. For hydrological work, this difference is negligible.

### 2.3.5 HD72: The Hungarian Datum

The Hungarian Datum 1972 (HD72) is the national geodetic datum of Hungary. It uses the GRS 1967 (IUGG 1967) ellipsoid ($a = 6{,}378{,}160$ m, $1/f = 298.247$) and is the foundation for the EOV projected coordinate system (discussed in Section 2.6.4). HD72 was established using a national triangulation network adjusted in 1972, with the datum origin at the Szolnok base point.

HD72 is not geocentric. Like other national datums of its era, it was positioned to give the best fit to the geoid over Hungarian territory. The offset between HD72 and WGS84 (or ETRS89) is approximately 50-60 meters -- large enough that confusing the two systems will visibly misplace features on a map. The transformation from HD72 to ETRS89 is defined by EPSG transformation code 1449 (a 7-parameter Helmert transformation). For sub-meter accuracy, the Hungarian national grid shift file (available from the Lechner Knowledge Centre) should be used.

HD72 and its associated EOV projection remain the standard for Hungarian national datasets: the DDM-5 national DEM, cadastral maps, water management databases (data.vizugy.hu), and topographic mapping all use EOV/HD72 natively. Any project combining Hungarian national data with European or global datasets must include a datum transformation step.

### 2.3.6 Other National Datums: A Global Perspective

The pattern of national datums is universal. India uses the Everest ellipsoid (1830) for its national datum; China uses CGCS2000 (a geocentric system adopted in 2008); Japan uses JGD2011 (geocentric, following the 2011 earthquake that shifted the Japanese islands by up to 5 meters); Russia uses PZ-90 for its GLONASS navigation system and the Pulkovo 1942 datum for legacy mapping; most African countries inherited colonial-era datums (Clarke 1880 is common across former British and French territories) and are gradually transitioning to geocentric systems through the African Geodetic Reference Frame (AFREF).

For the international reader, the lesson is always the same: know the datum of every dataset, and transform before combining. The EPSG Geodetic Parameter Dataset (accessible at https://epsg.io) catalogs thousands of coordinate reference systems and transformation methods, and is the authoritative source for transformation parameters.

## 2.4 Geographic Coordinate Systems

### 2.4.1 Latitude and Longitude

Geographic coordinates describe positions on the ellipsoid using two angles: **latitude** ($\phi$) and **longitude** ($\lambda$). These are the most fundamental way of specifying location on Earth, and all projected coordinate systems are derived from them.

**Latitude** ($\phi$) is the angle between the equatorial plane and the line perpendicular (normal) to the ellipsoid surface at the point of interest. This is called **geodetic latitude** and is the standard meaning of "latitude" in all modern contexts. It ranges from $-90°$ (South Pole) to $+90°$ (North Pole), with $0°$ at the equator. Lines of constant latitude are called **parallels**.

A subtlety worth noting: because the Earth is an ellipsoid rather than a sphere, the normal to the surface does not pass through the Earth's center (except at the equator and poles). The angle measured to the center is called **geocentric latitude** and differs from geodetic latitude by up to about $0.19°$ (roughly 21 km on the surface). This distinction rarely matters in practice -- GIS software always uses geodetic latitude -- but it explains why formulae for a sphere give slightly different results from formulae for an ellipsoid.

**Longitude** ($\lambda$) is the angle between the plane of the Prime Meridian (through Greenwich, England) and the plane of the meridian through the point. It ranges from $-180°$ (west) to $+180°$ (east). Lines of constant longitude are called **meridians**, and they converge toward the poles.

A perennial source of confusion: latitude-longitude is in (Y, X) order -- latitude is a north-south measurement (Y direction) and longitude is east-west (X direction). GIS software typically expects input as (X, Y), meaning (longitude, latitude). Reversing these is one of the most common errors in spatial data handling. A dataset where Budapest appears at latitude 19.04, longitude 47.50 has the coordinates swapped -- and the point will land in the Caspian Sea instead of Hungary.

### 2.4.2 Converting Between Coordinate Formats

Geographic coordinates are traditionally expressed in degrees, minutes, and seconds (DMS), a base-60 system inherited from Babylonian mathematics -- the same system used for time. One degree contains 60 minutes ('), and one minute contains 60 seconds ("). Conversion to decimal degrees (DD) follows:

$$DD = D + \frac{M}{60} + \frac{S}{3600}$$

For western longitudes and southern latitudes, the result is negative.

**Worked example: Budapest coordinates**

The Hungarian Parliament building is located at 47° 30' 22" N, 19° 02' 43" E.

Latitude:
$$\phi = 47 + \frac{30}{60} + \frac{22}{3600} = 47 + 0.5000 + 0.0061 = 47.5061° \text{ N}$$

Longitude:
$$\lambda = 19 + \frac{2}{60} + \frac{43}{3600} = 19 + 0.0333 + 0.0119 = 19.0453° \text{ E}$$

**Worked example: Austin, Texas coordinates**

The Texas State Capitol is at 30° 16' 28" N, 97° 44' 14" W.

Latitude:
$$\phi = 30 + \frac{16}{60} + \frac{28}{3600} = 30 + 0.2667 + 0.0078 = 30.2744° \text{ N}$$

Longitude (western, therefore negative):
$$\lambda = -\left(97 + \frac{44}{60} + \frac{14}{3600}\right) = -(97 + 0.7333 + 0.0039) = -97.7372°$$

When performing calculations with trigonometric functions, angles must be in **radians**. The conversion is:

$$\text{radians} = \text{degrees} \times \frac{\pi}{180}$$

A useful check: $\sin(30°) = \sin(\pi/6) = 0.5$.

### 2.4.3 Distances on a Spherical Earth

For approximate calculations, the Earth can be treated as a sphere with mean radius $R = 6{,}371$ km. Two basic distance formulae apply:

**North-south distance** along a meridian:
$$L_{NS} = R \cdot \Delta\phi$$

where $\Delta\phi$ is the latitude difference in radians. This distance is the same everywhere on the globe for a given angular difference.

**East-west distance** along a parallel:
$$L_{EW} = R \cdot \cos(\phi) \cdot \Delta\lambda$$

where $\phi$ is the latitude and $\Delta\lambda$ is the longitude difference in radians. The $\cos(\phi)$ factor accounts for the convergence of meridians toward the poles: at higher latitudes, one degree of longitude covers less ground distance.

**Worked example: One degree at Budapest vs. one degree at the equator**

Budapest lies at approximately $\phi = 47.5°$ N. Converting one degree to radians: $\Delta = 1° \times \pi/180 = 0.01745$ rad.

North-south (same everywhere):
$$L_{NS} = 6{,}371 \times 0.01745 = 111.2 \text{ km}$$

East-west at Budapest ($\phi = 47.5°$):
$$L_{EW} = 6{,}371 \times 0.01745 \times \cos(47.5°) = 111.2 \times 0.6756 = 75.1 \text{ km}$$

East-west at the equator ($\phi = 0°$):
$$L_{EW} = 6{,}371 \times 0.01745 \times \cos(0°) = 111.2 \times 1.0 = 111.2 \text{ km}$$

At the latitude of Budapest, one degree of longitude covers only 75 km -- about two-thirds of what it covers at the equator. This is why grid cells defined in geographic coordinates (arc-seconds or arc-minutes) are rectangular, not square, at all latitudes except the equator. A 1-arc-second DEM cell at Budapest's latitude is approximately 31 m north-south but only 21 m east-west.

This convergence has practical implications for digital elevation models. Global DEMs distributed in geographic coordinates (such as SRTM, Copernicus GLO-30, and ALOS AW3D30) have cells that become increasingly narrow in the east-west direction at higher latitudes. Slope and aspect calculations on such grids must account for this asymmetry, or they will overestimate east-west gradients. Most GIS software handles this automatically when the DEM's coordinate system is correctly specified, but it is worth understanding why.

**Arc-second resolution and ground distance:**

| Resolution | Approx. ground distance (equator) | Approx. ground distance (47.5 N) |
|------------|-----------------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| 3 arc-seconds | ~90 m | ~90 m (N-S), ~61 m (E-W) |
| 1 arc-second | ~30 m | ~30 m (N-S), ~20 m (E-W) |
| 1/3 arc-second | ~10 m | ~10 m (N-S), ~7 m (E-W) |

### 2.4.4 Great Circle Distance

The shortest path between two points on a sphere follows a **great circle** -- the curve formed by the intersection of the sphere with a plane passing through both points and the sphere's center. The great circle distance is given by:

$$d = R \cdot \arccos\left[\sin\phi_A \cdot \sin\phi_B + \cos\phi_A \cdot \cos\phi_B \cdot \cos(\lambda_A - \lambda_B)\right]$$

This formula should be evaluated carefully to avoid numerical precision issues. In a spreadsheet, calculate each trigonometric term separately before combining.

**Worked example: Budapest to Debrecen**

Budapest: $\phi_A = 47.497°$ N, $\lambda_A = 19.040°$ E
Debrecen: $\phi_B = 47.532°$ N, $\lambda_B = 21.625°$ E

Convert to radians:
$\phi_A = 0.8290$ rad, $\lambda_A = 0.3323$ rad
$\phi_B = 0.8296$ rad, $\lambda_B = 0.3774$ rad

Compute the argument of arccos:
$$\sin(0.8290) \cdot \sin(0.8296) + \cos(0.8290) \cdot \cos(0.8296) \cdot \cos(0.3323 - 0.3774)$$
$$= 0.7372 \times 0.7376 + 0.6757 \times 0.6753 \times \cos(-0.0451)$$
$$= 0.5438 + 0.4563 \times 0.9990 = 0.5438 + 0.4558 = 0.9996$$

$$d = 6{,}371 \times \arccos(0.9996) = 6{,}371 \times 0.0283 = 180.2 \text{ km}$$

The great circle distance from Budapest to Debrecen is approximately 180 km. For comparison, the driving distance is about 230 km (roads do not follow great circles). The straight-line (planar) distance in a projected coordinate system like EOV would give essentially the same result at this scale, because the projection distortion is negligible over 180 km.

## 2.5 Elevation and Vertical Coordinate Systems

### 2.5.1 Three Ways to Measure Height

Measuring horizontal position is conceptually straightforward (though technically demanding). Measuring height is more subtle, because there are three distinct systems that do not automatically agree:

**Orthometric height** ($H$) is measured from the geoid -- the equipotential gravity surface that approximates mean sea level. This is the conventional meaning of "elevation" on topographic maps and in engineering. When a weather report says "Budapest is 96 meters above sea level," it means 96 meters above the geoid. Orthometric height is determined by precise leveling: starting from a defined sea-level reference point and measuring height differences along a series of benchmarks using spirit levels and calibrated rods.

**Ellipsoidal height** ($h$) is measured from the ellipsoid surface along the normal to the ellipsoid. This is what GPS receivers measure directly. Ellipsoidal height has no physical meaning in terms of water flow -- it does not tell you which way water will run, because the ellipsoid does not coincide with the gravity field. The relationship between the three surfaces is:

$$h = H + N$$

where $N$ is the **geoid undulation** (or **geoid height**) -- the separation between the ellipsoid and the geoid at that location. In Hungary, $N$ ranges from about 38 to 46 meters (the geoid is 38-46 meters above the GRS 1980 ellipsoid).

To convert a GPS-measured ellipsoidal height to a meaningful orthometric height, you need a geoid model. This is a routine operation in modern surveying and GIS, but it is a step that can be overlooked, leading to elevation errors of tens of meters.

**Tidal height** is measured relative to local sea level at a tide gauge. It is relevant for coastal flood risk and navigation. Different countries define "mean sea level" at different tide gauges, measured over different time periods, resulting in vertical datums that do not agree.

### 2.5.2 Vertical Datums: What "Sea Level" Means

A vertical datum defines the zero point for elevation measurements. Like horizontal datums, vertical datums vary by country and era.

**NAVD88** (North American Vertical Datum of 1988) is the current vertical datum for the United States and Canada. It is based on a single tidal benchmark at Father Point/Rimouski, Quebec, and a least-squares adjustment of roughly 625,000 km of leveling lines across North America. NAVD88 replaced NGVD29 (National Geodetic Vertical Datum of 1929), which was based on mean sea level at 26 tide gauges. The difference between NAVD88 and NGVD29 varies spatially, reaching more than 1.5 meters in some western states. Historical flood data, particularly Base Flood Elevations used in floodplain management, may be referenced to NGVD29 and must be converted before comparison with modern data.

**The Baltic Height System** (Balti rendszer in Hungarian) is the vertical datum used in Hungary and across much of Central and Eastern Europe. It is based on mean sea level at the Kronstadt tide gauge near Saint Petersburg, Russia (the "Baltic zero"). Hungary adopted this system under Soviet-era standardization, and it remains the national vertical reference. Elevations in Hungarian national datasets -- including the DDM-5 national DEM and all water management records -- are referenced to the Baltic Height System.

The difference between the Baltic Height System and NAVD88 is not a simple constant offset, because they are referenced to different sea-level points (Kronstadt vs. Rimouski) and use different geoid models. Direct comparison requires transformation through a common framework, typically using the EGM2008 global geoid model or a national geoid model.

**Other vertical datums** include the Amsterdam Ordnance Datum (NAP), used in the Netherlands and serving as the origin for the European Vertical Reference Frame (EVRF); the Ordnance Datum Newlyn (ODN), used in Great Britain; and the various local mean sea level datums used across Asia and Africa. The European Vertical Reference Frame (EVRF2019) is the pan-European effort to unify these disparate systems, enabling consistent cross-border flood mapping and infrastructure design.

For the working hydrologist, the practical message is: always check the vertical datum of your elevation data, especially when combining data from different sources. A 1-meter offset in vertical datum can move a flood boundary by hundreds of meters horizontally in flat terrain -- exactly the kind of terrain where floods are most dangerous.

### 2.5.3 GPS Heights, Geoid Models, and Practical Surveying

Modern field surveys use GNSS (Global Navigation Satellite Systems -- the collective term for GPS, GLONASS, Galileo, and BeiDou) to determine positions. A GNSS receiver directly measures three-dimensional coordinates in a geocentric datum (typically WGS84 or ETRS89), giving longitude, latitude, and **ellipsoidal height**. To obtain the orthometric height that engineers and hydrologists need -- the height above the geoid, referenced to the national vertical datum -- a geoid model must be applied.

In the United States, the GEOID18 model (maintained by the National Geodetic Survey) provides the geoid-ellipsoid separation across the country with centimeter-level accuracy. In Europe, the EGG2015 (European Gravimetric Geoid 2015) serves a similar function. Hungary maintains its own national geoid model fitted to the Baltic Height System and the HD72 ellipsoid.

The practical workflow for a GPS-based survey in Hungary would be:

1. Measure positions with a GNSS receiver, obtaining WGS84 ellipsoidal coordinates ($\phi$, $\lambda$, $h$)
2. Transform horizontally from WGS84 to HD72/EOV using the Helmert parameters or the national grid shift file
3. Convert the ellipsoidal height $h$ to an orthometric height $H$ by subtracting the geoid undulation $N$: $H = h - N$
4. The resulting coordinates are in the EOV system (Easting, Northing) with Baltic Height System elevation

This three-step process is handled automatically by professional surveying software and by GIS transformation tools, but understanding what happens behind the scenes is essential for diagnosing errors.

## 2.6 Map Projections: Flattening the Earth

### 2.6.1 The Fundamental Problem

It is mathematically impossible to represent a curved surface on a flat plane without distortion. This was proven by Euler in 1777 and is a consequence of the fact that a sphere (or ellipsoid) has positive Gaussian curvature while a plane has zero curvature. No stretching, cutting, or rearranging can make a curved surface lie flat without some property being distorted.

Map projections accomplish this inherently imperfect transformation by preserving certain properties at the expense of others. The choice of projection depends on what property matters most for the task at hand. For hydrology, this choice has quantitative consequences: use the wrong projection, and your watershed area calculation, your rainfall depth estimate, or your flood extent map will be systematically wrong.

The transformation from Earth to map conceptually involves two steps. First, the Earth is reduced to a small model (a globe) by a **representative fraction** or map scale -- for example, 1:100,000 means that 1 cm on the map represents 100,000 cm (1 km) on the Earth. Second, the globe is projected onto a flat surface, introducing an additional **scale factor** $k$ that varies across the map. On the globe, $k = 1$ everywhere; on the flat map, $k$ varies -- it may be exactly 1.0 along certain lines (the lines of true scale) and deviate from 1.0 elsewhere. The magnitude of this deviation is the projection distortion.

### 2.6.2 Projection Families

Map projections are classified by the shape of the surface onto which the globe is projected:

**Conic projections** place a cone over the globe and project the surface onto it. The cone is then unrolled into a flat surface. The cone intersects the globe along one or two **standard parallels** -- circles of latitude where the scale factor is exactly 1.0. Between the standard parallels, the map is slightly compressed; outside them, it is slightly expanded. Conic projections are best suited for mid-latitude regions that extend primarily in the east-west direction. Two conic projections are especially important for water resources:

The **Albers Equal Area Conic** projection preserves area. The scale factor along parallels is the inverse of the scale factor along meridians: $k_{parallel} \times k_{meridian} = 1$. Any stretching in one direction is exactly compensated by compression in the perpendicular direction, so areas are preserved everywhere on the map. This property is critical for hydrology: if you are computing rainfall volume over a watershed (rainfall depth times area), the area must be correct. The Albers Equal Area Conic is the standard projection for the U.S. National Atlas and for many continental-scale hydrological analyses.

The **Lambert Conformal Conic** projection preserves local angles and shapes (conformality). This makes it suitable for navigation, weather mapping, and applications where the visual appearance of features matters. It is used for the U.S. State Plane Coordinate System in states that are wider than they are tall.

**Cylindrical projections** wrap a cylinder around the globe. In the normal (equatorial) orientation, the cylinder is tangent along the equator; in the **transverse** orientation, it is tangent along a meridian. Transverse cylindrical projections are excellent for mapping strips of territory that extend primarily north-south. The most important transverse cylindrical projection is the **Transverse Mercator**, which is the basis for UTM coordinates and for many national grid systems, including the British Ordnance Survey National Grid and the Gauss-Kruger system used across Europe and Asia.

The **Web Mercator** projection, used by virtually all online mapping platforms (Google Maps, OpenStreetMap, ArcGIS Online, Bing Maps), is a normal cylindrical Mercator variant. It maps the entire globe into a square tile that can be subdivided for efficient web delivery. Web Mercator preserves local shapes (conformality) but grossly distorts areas away from the equator. Greenland appears the same size as Africa on a Web Mercator map, when in reality Africa is 14 times larger. For visualization and web display, Web Mercator is ubiquitous and effective. For any quantitative analysis -- area measurement, distance calculation, density estimation -- it should never be used.

**Azimuthal projections** project onto a plane tangent to the globe at a single point. All directions from the center point are shown correctly. Azimuthal projections are used for polar regions and for global thematic maps. The **Lambert Azimuthal Equal Area** projection is the basis for ETRS89/LAEA (EPSG:3035), the standard equal-area projection for pan-European mapping required by the INSPIRE Directive.

### 2.6.3 What Projections Preserve -- and What They Sacrifice

No projection can preserve all spatial properties simultaneously. The choice of projection is therefore always a trade-off:

**Table 2.3**: Properties of map projections and their importance for hydrology

| Property | Meaning | Example projection | Hydrological importance |
|----------|---------|-------------------|------------------------|
| Equal area (equivalence) | Areas on the map are proportional to areas on Earth | Albers Equal Area Conic, Lambert Azimuthal Equal Area | Critical. Watershed areas, rainfall volumes, runoff ratios, land use fractions all require accurate areas. |
| Conformal (shape-preserving) | Local angles are preserved; small shapes are correct | Lambert Conformal Conic, Transverse Mercator, Mercator | Important for navigation, visual clarity, and local measurements. Angles between rivers and roads are shown correctly. |
| Equidistant | Distances are correct along certain lines | Azimuthal Equidistant | Useful for travel time and flood wave routing calculations along specific paths. |
| Compromise | Minimizes overall distortion without perfectly preserving any one property | Robinson, Winkel Tripel | Used for global thematic maps. Not suitable for quantitative analysis. |

For most hydrological work, **equal-area projections are preferred**. The fundamental equation of hydrology -- that runoff volume equals rainfall depth times watershed area minus losses -- requires accurate area. A 5% error in area translates directly to a 5% error in volume. Conformal projections are used where local accuracy matters (detailed flood mapping, engineering surveys) or where standard coordinate systems mandate them (UTM, State Plane, Gauss-Kruger). In practice, most hydrological analyses at the watershed scale use an equal-area projection or a projected coordinate system where the distortion within the study area is negligible.

### 2.6.4 The Albers Equal Area Conic Projection: A Closer Look

The Albers Equal Area Conic projection deserves special attention because of its importance in hydrology. Its area-preserving property arises from a mathematical constraint: at every point on the map, the scale factor along parallels $k_p$ and the scale factor along meridians $k_m$ satisfy:

$$k_p \times k_m = 1$$

If the map stretches features by a factor of 1.02 in the east-west direction at some point, it simultaneously compresses them by a factor of $1/1.02 = 0.98$ in the north-south direction. The area of any region is preserved exactly, though its shape is slightly distorted.

The projection is defined by two standard parallels (where $k = 1$ in all directions), a central meridian, a latitude of origin, and false easting/northing values. Between the standard parallels, the scale factor is slightly less than 1 (the map is compressed); outside them, it is slightly greater than 1 (the map is expanded).

For the contiguous United States, the standard Albers parameters use standard parallels at 29.5 N and 45.5 N, a central meridian at 96 W, and a latitude of origin at 23 N. The maximum scale distortion within the U.S. is less than 1.25% -- meaning areas are exact everywhere, but shapes can be distorted by up to 1.25%. For most hydrological purposes, this is entirely acceptable.

For European mapping, the Lambert Azimuthal Equal Area (LAEA) projection centered at 52 N, 10 E is the INSPIRE-mandated equal-area projection (EPSG:3035). It serves the same purpose as Albers for the U.S.: providing accurate areas across the continent for thematic mapping and spatial analysis.

## 2.7 Projected Coordinate Systems in Practice

### 2.7.1 Components of a Projected Coordinate System

Every projected coordinate system is defined by:

1. **Datum** -- the ellipsoid and its positioning (e.g., NAD83, ETRS89, HD72)
2. **Projection type** -- the mathematical transformation (e.g., Transverse Mercator, Lambert Conformal Conic, Oblique Mercator)
3. **Projection parameters:**
   - Central meridian ($\lambda_0$) -- the longitude at the center of the projection
   - Latitude of origin ($\phi_0$) -- the latitude at the center of the projection
   - False easting ($E_0$) -- an offset added to all Easting (X) coordinates
   - False northing ($N_0$) -- an offset added to all Northing (Y) coordinates
   - Scale factor ($k_0$) -- applied at the central meridian or central point
   - Standard parallels (for conic projections)

The **false easting and false northing** exist to ensure that all coordinates within the area of interest are positive. Without them, points west of the central meridian would have negative Easting values, creating confusion in data handling and field operations. The UTM system, for example, uses a false easting of 500,000 m so that all Easting values within any zone fall between roughly 166,000 and 834,000 m -- always positive, always distinguishable from Northing values.

### 2.7.2 The Universal Transverse Mercator (UTM) System

The UTM system divides the Earth into 60 zones, each 6 degrees of longitude wide, numbered from 1 (starting at 180 W) to 60. Within each zone, a Transverse Mercator projection is applied with the central meridian at the center of the zone. The parameters are:

- Central meridian: center of the zone
- Latitude of origin: 0 (the equator)
- False easting: 500,000 m
- False northing: 0 m (Northern Hemisphere) or 10,000,000 m (Southern Hemisphere)
- Scale factor at central meridian: 0.9996
- Units: meters
- Datum: depends on application (WGS84 for GPS-derived data; NAD83 for U.S. federal data)

The UTM zone for a given longitude can be calculated as:

$$\text{Zone} = \left\lfloor\frac{180 + \lambda}{6}\right\rfloor + 1$$

**Worked example: UTM zones for selected cities**

| City | Longitude | Calculation | UTM Zone |
|------|-----------|-------------|----------|
| Budapest | 19.04 E | $\lfloor(180 + 19.04)/6\rfloor + 1 = \lfloor33.17\rfloor + 1 = 34$ | 34N |
| Debrecen | 21.63 E | $\lfloor(180 + 21.63)/6\rfloor + 1 = \lfloor33.61\rfloor + 1 = 34$ | 34N |
| Austin, TX | 97.75 W ($-97.75$) | $\lfloor(180 - 97.75)/6\rfloor + 1 = \lfloor13.71\rfloor + 1 = 14$ | 14N |
| New York | 74.00 W ($-74.00$) | $\lfloor(180 - 74.00)/6\rfloor + 1 = \lfloor17.67\rfloor + 1 = 18$ | 18N |
| Nairobi | 36.82 E | $\lfloor(180 + 36.82)/6\rfloor + 1 = \lfloor36.14\rfloor + 1 = 37$ | 37S |

Note that Budapest and Debrecen share UTM Zone 34N. In fact, all of Hungary falls within Zone 34N (Hungary spans roughly 16-23 E longitude, and Zone 34 covers 18-24 E), with only the western edge near Sopron falling into Zone 33N. This is convenient -- most Hungarian analyses can use a single UTM zone without zone boundary issues.

**Limitations of UTM**: The 6-degree zone width means that large countries or regions spanning multiple zones face discontinuities at zone boundaries. Texas spans three UTM zones (13, 14, and 15); a statewide analysis must either use a different projection or handle the zone transitions. Hungary's compact size makes this a non-issue for Hungarian work, but continental analyses (such as pan-European flood mapping) cannot use UTM.

The scale factor of 0.9996 at the central meridian means that distances measured along the central meridian are 0.04% too short. The scale factor increases away from the central meridian, reaching 1.0000 at two lines about 180 km east and west of it, and increasing further toward the zone boundaries. The maximum scale distortion within a zone is about 0.04% -- negligible for most hydrological applications.

### 2.7.3 The State Plane Coordinate System (United States)

In the United States, each state has one or more **State Plane** coordinate zones, designed to provide very high accuracy (scale error less than 1 part in 10,000) within each zone. The projection type depends on the state's shape:

- East-west oriented states use **Lambert Conformal Conic** (e.g., Texas, with five zones from north to south)
- North-south oriented states use **Transverse Mercator** (e.g., New York, with four zones)

State Plane coordinates are used primarily for surveying, engineering, and legal descriptions of property. They provide higher accuracy than UTM within their zones because the zones are smaller (less distortion). The trade-off is complexity: the U.S. has over 120 State Plane zones, each with its own parameters.

For the international reader, the State Plane system illustrates a general principle: when a country is large enough that a single projection introduces unacceptable distortion, the solution is to divide the country into zones, each with its own optimized projection. This is the same principle behind UTM, the Gauss-Kruger system, and (for a small country like Hungary) the decision to use a single national projection that covers the entire territory.

### 2.7.4 The Hungarian EOV Coordinate System

Hungary's national projected coordinate system is the **Egyseges Orszagos Vetuleti rendszer** (Unified National Projection System), universally abbreviated as **EOV**. Its EPSG code is **23700**.

EOV is based on the **Hotine Oblique Mercator** projection -- a cylindrical projection where the cylinder is tilted to be tangent along an oblique great circle rather than along a meridian (as in Transverse Mercator) or along the equator (as in normal Mercator). The oblique orientation was chosen to minimize distortion across Hungary's roughly east-west extent at about 47 N latitude.

**EOV parameters:**

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Datum | Hungarian Datum 1972 (HD72) |
| Ellipsoid | GRS 1967 (IUGG 1967) |
| Projection | Hotine Oblique Mercator (variant B) |
| Latitude of center | 47° 08' 20" N (47.1389° N) |
| Longitude of center | 19° 02' 54.8584" E (19.0486° E) |
| Azimuth of initial line | 90° |
| Scale factor at center | 0.99993 |
| False easting | 650,000 m |
| False northing | 200,000 m |

The resulting coordinate ranges for Hungarian territory are:

- **Northing (Y in Hungarian convention, X in mathematical convention)**: approximately 44,000 to 367,000
- **Easting (X in Hungarian convention, Y in mathematical convention)**: approximately 421,000 to 950,000

A critical practical note: **Hungarian datasets frequently swap the X and Y labels.** The convention in Hungarian geodetic practice historically uses Y for Northing and X for Easting, which is the opposite of the international mathematical convention. When encountering a Hungarian dataset with columns labeled X and Y, do not assume which is which. Instead, **identify coordinates by their value ranges**: values between 0 and 400,000 are Northing; values between 400,000 and 1,000,000 are Easting. This rule is reliable regardless of how the columns are labeled.

**Why EOV for a small country?** Hungary spans approximately 5 degrees of longitude (about 16 E to 23 E) and 3 degrees of latitude (about 45.7 N to 48.6 N). This is compact enough that a single projection can cover the entire country with very small distortion. The scale factor of 0.99993 at the projection center means the maximum scale error anywhere in Hungary is less than 0.01% -- far better than UTM's 0.04%. For any analysis confined to Hungarian territory, EOV provides excellent accuracy with no zone boundaries.

**EOV in the European context:** As Hungary is an EU member state, interoperability with European spatial data is essential. Hungarian national datasets in EOV must be transformed to ETRS89 (or a projection thereof, such as ETRS89/LAEA for pan-European analysis) before they can be combined with Copernicus products, EU-Hydro stream networks, or EFAS flood forecasts. The transformation is defined by EPSG code 1449 (HD72 to ETRS89 via a 7-parameter Helmert transformation). For sub-meter accuracy, the Hungarian national grid shift file provides better results than the 7-parameter approximation.

**Worked example: Identifying EOV coordinates**

A water management dataset from data.vizugy.hu contains the following gauge location:

```
Station: Szolnok, Tisza
X: 699,414
Y: 186,152
```

Following the value-range rule: 699,414 falls in the 400,000-1,000,000 range, so it is the **Easting** despite being labeled "X". 186,152 falls in the 0-400,000 range, so it is the **Northing** despite being labeled "Y". The station is in the central part of Hungary, consistent with Szolnok's location on the Tisza.

### 2.7.5 The Gauss-Kruger System

The **Gauss-Kruger** system is a Transverse Mercator projection system widely used across Europe, the former Soviet Union, and parts of Asia and Africa. It is functionally similar to UTM but with 3-degree zones (instead of 6) and a scale factor of 1.0000 at the central meridian (instead of 0.9996). The narrower zones and unit scale factor provide higher accuracy within each zone, at the cost of needing more zones to cover a given area.

Gauss-Kruger zones are numbered by the central meridian: Zone 5 has its central meridian at 15 E, Zone 6 at 18 E, Zone 7 at 21 E, and so on. Hungary falls primarily in Gauss-Kruger zones 5 and 6 (central meridians at 15 E and 18 E) or zones 6 and 7 (depending on the variant used). Before the adoption of EOV, Hungary used the Gauss-Kruger system; some legacy datasets may still be in Gauss-Kruger coordinates.

The Gauss-Kruger system remains in active use in Germany (where it is being replaced by ETRS89/UTM), Austria, and many countries of the former Soviet Union. International readers working with data from these regions should be aware that Gauss-Kruger and UTM are closely related but not identical, and conversion requires attention to the zone numbering and scale factor differences.

### 2.7.6 The Web Mercator Projection

The **Web Mercator** projection (EPSG:3857) deserves separate discussion because of its ubiquity in online mapping. Every major web mapping platform -- Google Maps, OpenStreetMap, ArcGIS Online, Bing Maps, Mapbox -- uses Web Mercator as its display projection. It maps the entire globe into a square tile system that enables efficient caching and zooming, and it preserves local shapes (conformality), making maps look "right" at any zoom level.

However, Web Mercator is emphatically **not suitable for quantitative analysis**. It distorts areas dramatically away from the equator. At the latitude of Hungary (47.5 N), areas are inflated by a factor of approximately $1/\cos^2(47.5°) \approx 2.18$ -- meaning a polygon that should be 100 km$^2$ would compute as 218 km$^2$ if measured directly in Web Mercator coordinates. At the latitude of northern Scandinavia (65 N), the inflation factor exceeds 5.5.

For hydroinformatics: never compute watershed areas, rainfall volumes, or drainage densities in Web Mercator. Use it for display and sharing only. When publishing maps through web platforms, data should be reprojected to Web Mercator for visualization, but all analysis should be performed in an appropriate projected coordinate system (UTM, EOV, Albers, or LAEA as appropriate for the study area).

## 2.8 The Revolution in Earth Measurement: GPS, GNSS, and Reference Networks

### 2.8.1 How Satellite Navigation Works

The Global Positioning System (GPS), developed by the U.S. Department of Defense and fully operational since 1995, fundamentally changed geodesy and all fields that depend on spatial data. For the first time, accurate three-dimensional coordinates could be obtained anywhere on Earth, at any time, without line-of-sight to a ground reference point.

The principle is **trilateration** -- determining position by measuring distances from known points (the satellites). A GPS constellation of at least 24 satellites (currently 31 operational) orbits at approximately 20,200 km altitude in six orbital planes. Each satellite continuously broadcasts a time-stamped signal. The receiver measures the time difference between signal transmission and reception, multiplies by the speed of light, and obtains the distance (pseudo-range) to the satellite. With distances to four or more satellites, the receiver's position (latitude, longitude, ellipsoidal height) and its clock offset can be determined simultaneously.

The raw accuracy of a standalone GPS receiver is approximately 3-5 meters horizontally. For hydrological fieldwork -- locating gauge sites, mapping channel cross-sections, surveying flood boundaries -- this is often insufficient. Higher accuracy is achieved through **differential correction**.

### 2.8.2 Differential GNSS and Reference Networks

**Differential GNSS** (DGNSS) exploits the fact that errors in satellite signals (atmospheric delays, orbital errors) are spatially correlated: two receivers within a few hundred kilometers experience nearly the same errors. By placing a reference receiver at a precisely known location, its observed errors can be computed and transmitted to nearby mobile receivers for real-time correction.

The most precise form of this technique, **Real-Time Kinematic** (RTK) positioning, achieves centimeter-level accuracy (1-2 cm horizontal, 2-3 cm vertical) in real time. RTK requires a base station within about 50 km or a network of reference stations.

National networks of continuously operating reference stations (CORS) provide this infrastructure:

- In the **United States**, the National CORS network comprises over 1,800 stations operated by NGS and partner agencies. CORS data enable post-processed corrections to sub-centimeter accuracy.
- In **Europe**, the EUREF Permanent GNSS Network (EPN) coordinates national networks across the continent. Hungary operates **GNSSnet.hu**, a network of permanent GNSS stations providing real-time corrections for surveying and GIS work across the country.
- The **International GNSS Service** (IGS) operates a global network of over 500 stations, providing the reference frame for all GNSS-based positioning worldwide.

The term **GNSS** (Global Navigation Satellite Systems) has replaced "GPS" in professional usage because multiple satellite constellations are now operational: the U.S. GPS, Russia's GLONASS, the European Union's **Galileo** (fully operational since 2024), and China's **BeiDou**. Modern receivers track all four constellations simultaneously, improving accuracy, reliability, and the speed of position fixes. Galileo is particularly relevant for European users -- it provides the highest-accuracy open service (sub-meter with its High Accuracy Service) and is the GNSS component of the EU's space program.

### 2.8.3 Tectonic Motions and Geodetic Reference Frames

GPS measurements revealed that the Earth's surface is in constant motion. Tectonic plates move at rates of 1-10 cm per year, and these motions accumulate: the position of a benchmark in California changes by about 5 cm per year relative to one in New York. Over decades, these shifts become large enough to matter for high-precision spatial data.

This is why modern datums are either **plate-fixed** (coordinates remain constant for points on the same plate, as with NAD83 and ETRS89) or **Earth-centered** (coordinates change as the plate moves, as with ITRF). For hydrological work at the watershed scale, tectonic motions are negligible -- the Tisza does not flow differently because Hungary is drifting east at 2.5 cm per year. But for precise surveying, for maintaining national geodetic networks, and for interpreting decades-long GNSS time series (such as those used to monitor subsidence in the Great Hungarian Plain), tectonic effects must be accounted for.

## 2.9 Vertical Datums and Sea Level Change

### 2.9.1 Why Vertical Accuracy Matters for Water

Vertical accuracy is arguably more important than horizontal accuracy for hydroinformatics. Water flows downhill -- even a few centimeters of elevation error can change the predicted direction of flow in flat terrain. Flood inundation maps are exquisitely sensitive to vertical accuracy: in a river floodplain with a 0.1% slope (1 meter drop per kilometer), a 0.5-meter error in elevation can shift the predicted flood boundary by 500 meters. In Hungary's Great Plain (Alfold), where vast areas have slopes below 0.05%, the sensitivity is even greater.

The vertical datum -- the definition of "zero elevation" -- is therefore critical. As discussed in Section 2.5.2, different countries use different vertical datums, and the differences are not negligible. A point recorded as "96.0 meters" in the Baltic Height System is not the same as "96.0 meters" in NAVD88 or NAP. For any cross-border analysis -- and the Danube basin spans ten countries with at least three different vertical datum traditions -- vertical datum harmonization is essential.

### 2.9.2 Sea Level Trends and Coastal Flood Risk

Tidal height records from coastal tide gauges reveal that "sea level" is not constant. Relative sea level trends vary dramatically by location, reflecting the combination of global sea level rise (currently about 3.6 mm/year, accelerating) and local land motion (subsidence or uplift):

**Table 2.5**: Relative sea level trends at selected tide gauges

| Location | Trend (mm/year) | Primary cause of local component |
|----------|-----------------|----------------------------------|
| Galveston, TX | +6.5 | Subsidence from groundwater and hydrocarbon extraction |
| Trieste, Italy | +1.3 | Mild tectonic subsidence |
| Charleston, SC | +3.3 | Moderate subsidence + global rise |
| Stockholm, Sweden | -3.8 | Post-glacial rebound (land rising) |
| Amsterdam, Netherlands | +1.8 | Mild subsidence + global rise |
| Juneau, AK | -12.7 | Rapid post-glacial rebound |

For inland countries like Hungary, sea level trends are not directly relevant, but the concept has an analogue: land subsidence. Parts of the Great Hungarian Plain are subsiding due to groundwater extraction and tectonic processes, and this subsidence affects the accuracy of historical elevation data. Leveling networks must be periodically re-measured to maintain the integrity of the vertical reference frame.

## 2.10 Seeing Water from Space: The GRACE Satellite Mission

### 2.10.1 Measuring Water by Measuring Gravity

Perhaps the most remarkable application of geodesy to water science is the Gravity Recovery and Climate Experiment (GRACE), a joint NASA-German Aerospace Center (DLR) mission launched in 2002 and succeeded by GRACE-FO (Follow-On) in 2018. GRACE measures changes in Earth's gravity field with extraordinary precision, and because water is heavy, these gravity changes directly reveal changes in water storage -- in aquifers, in soil moisture, in snow and ice, and in surface reservoirs.

The concept is elegant. Two identical satellites fly in the same orbit, 220 km apart, at an altitude of about 500 km. A microwave ranging system measures the distance between them to an accuracy of approximately 1 micrometer -- one-hundredth the width of a human hair. When the leading satellite passes over a region of slightly higher gravity (due to more mass below, such as a full aquifer or a large lake), it accelerates slightly, and the distance between the satellites increases. When the trailing satellite reaches the same region, it accelerates too, and the distance decreases. By tracking these minute distance changes as the satellites orbit the Earth, the gravity field can be mapped globally each month.

The gravity data are processed into maps of **terrestrial water storage anomalies** -- deviations from the long-term average water storage in each region, expressed as an equivalent depth of water (centimeters) spread over the area. The spatial resolution is coarse (about 300-400 km, limited by the satellite altitude and the physics of gravity), but the temporal resolution is monthly, and the ability to measure the total water column -- surface water, soil moisture, and groundwater combined -- is unique to GRACE. No other observing system can do this.

### 2.10.2 GRACE and the 2011 Texas Drought

The power of GRACE was dramatically demonstrated during the extreme Texas drought of 2011 (discussed as a hidroinformatics case study in Section 1.8), the most severe single-year drought in Texas's recorded history. GRACE data showed that approximately 100 km$^3$ of water was lost from the state during the drought -- equivalent to about three times the volume of Lake Mead, the largest reservoir in the United States.

The GRACE terrestrial water storage time series for Texas shows a clear seasonal cycle (wetter in spring, drier in late summer) superimposed on multi-year variations. The 2011 event appears as a sharp drop that took the water storage anomaly to values far below any previously observed in the GRACE record (2002-2012 at the time). When compared with independently measured reservoir storage data from the Texas Water Development Board, the GRACE signal and the reservoir data are highly correlated, confirming that GRACE is measuring real changes in the water balance.

This case study illustrates a fundamental geodetic concept come full circle: the gravity field of the Earth, which defines the geoid and underpins all elevation measurement, is itself changing in response to the movement of water. Geodesy is not merely the static foundation of mapmaking; it is a dynamic tool for monitoring the water cycle.

### 2.10.3 GRACE in the European and Global Context

GRACE has been applied to water storage monitoring worldwide. In the Danube basin, GRACE data have been used to evaluate the 2003 European drought and the subsequent recovery, revealing that the total water deficit across the basin was comparable in magnitude to several months of the Danube's discharge at Budapest. In the Indian subcontinent, GRACE revealed that groundwater depletion in northern India was far more severe than ground-based monitoring had indicated -- a finding that prompted a national reassessment of groundwater management policies.

The GFZ Potsdam (German Research Centre for Geosciences) provides the **Global Gravity-based Groundwater Product (G3P)** through the GravIS portal (https://gravis.gfz.de/gws), which separates the GRACE total water storage signal into its components -- soil moisture, surface water, and groundwater -- using hydrological models. This product is particularly valuable for European water management, providing monthly groundwater storage anomalies at regional scales for the entire continent.

For the Hungarian hydrologist, GRACE/G3P data provide a unique perspective on water storage changes in the Tisza basin and the Great Hungarian Plain, where groundwater is a critical resource for agriculture. While the spatial resolution is too coarse for local management decisions, GRACE data are invaluable for basin-scale water balance validation and for detecting long-term trends in total water storage that ground-based networks, however dense, cannot capture.

## 2.11 Working with Coordinate Systems in Practice

### 2.11.1 The Essential Rule: Know Your Data's Coordinate System

The single most important practical skill in spatial data handling is ensuring that every dataset's coordinate system is correctly specified and that all datasets in a project are in a compatible system. This sounds obvious, but violations cause more GIS errors than any other single factor.

Modern GIS software (ArcGIS Pro, QGIS, and their cloud counterparts) can reproject data **on the fly** for display purposes. When layers with different coordinate systems are added to a map, the software transforms them into the map's coordinate system dynamically. This makes mismatched data appear to align correctly on screen -- which is both a convenience and a trap. The data still exist in their original coordinates, and geoprocessing operations (intersect, buffer, area calculation, zonal statistics) may fail or produce incorrect results if the underlying coordinate systems are inconsistent.

**Best practice:** For any geoprocessing analysis, ensure that all input datasets are in the same coordinate system. Reproject all data to a common system at the beginning of a project, not on the fly during analysis.

### 2.11.2 Define vs. Project: The Most Common Mistake

Two fundamentally different operations exist for handling coordinate systems in GIS:

**Define Projection** (or "Assign CRS") tells the software what coordinate system the data is already in. It adds or corrects the metadata (the `.prj` file for shapefiles, or the spatial reference property in a geodatabase) without changing any coordinate values. Use this when a dataset has no coordinate system defined, or when the existing definition is wrong.

**Project** (or "Reproject") transforms the data from one coordinate system to another, recalculating every coordinate value. Use this when you need to change a dataset's coordinate system -- for example, converting Hungarian EOV data to ETRS89 for combination with Copernicus products.

Confusing these two operations is a pervasive and serious error. If a dataset in EOV has no `.prj` file and you **project** it to WGS84 (assuming it is already in WGS84), the software will treat the 6-digit EOV coordinates as if they were WGS84 degrees -- and the result will be nonsensical (coordinates pointing to locations millions of degrees off the real Earth). The correct approach is to first **define** the coordinate system as EOV, then **project** to WGS84.

### 2.11.3 EPSG Codes: The Universal Language of Coordinate Systems

The EPSG Geodetic Parameter Dataset (maintained by the International Association of Oil and Gas Producers) provides a standardized numeric code for every coordinate reference system, datum, ellipsoid, and transformation in use worldwide. These codes are the universal language for communicating about coordinate systems, eliminating the ambiguity of names that vary by software vendor and language.

**Table 2.6**: Commonly used EPSG codes in hydroinformatics

| EPSG Code | Name | Type | Usage |
|-----------|------|------|-------|
| 4326 | WGS 84 | Geographic | GPS data, global datasets, web APIs |
| 4269 | NAD83 | Geographic | U.S. federal data |
| 4258 | ETRS89 | Geographic | European spatial data |
| 4237 | HD72 | Geographic | Hungarian national data (geographic) |
| 23700 | HD72 / EOV | Projected | Hungarian national data (projected) |
| 32634 | WGS 84 / UTM Zone 34N | Projected | Hungary in UTM (WGS84 datum) |
| 3035 | ETRS89 / LAEA | Projected | Pan-European equal-area mapping |
| 3857 | WGS 84 / Web Mercator | Projected | Web mapping display only |

When receiving data from any source, the first question should be: "What is the EPSG code?" If the data provider cannot answer, inspect the coordinate values. Six-digit values in the ranges described in Section 2.7.4 suggest EOV (EPSG:23700). Values between -180 and 180 suggest geographic coordinates (check which datum). Large values in the hundreds of thousands with a false easting of 500,000 suggest UTM (determine the zone from the context).

### 2.11.4 Length and Area Calculations: Why Projection Matters

Shape_Length and Shape_Area values computed by a geodatabase depend entirely on the coordinate system:

In **geographic coordinates** (degrees), Shape_Area is computed in square degrees -- a physically meaningless unit. A watershed might report a Shape_Area of 0.0023 square degrees, which conveys nothing about its actual size.

In a **projected coordinate system** (meters), Shape_Area reports square meters. The same watershed in UTM Zone 34N might report 228,800,000 m$^2$ (228.8 km$^2$), which is a meaningful and useful number.

In **Web Mercator**, Shape_Area reports square meters, but the values are inflated by the projection distortion. At the latitude of Budapest, the reported area would be approximately 2.18 times the true area. This is dangerous because the numbers look plausible -- they are in square meters, after all -- but they are systematically wrong.

**Worked example: Watershed area in different coordinate systems**

A small watershed in the Bukk Mountains of northern Hungary has a true area of approximately 42.5 km$^2$.

| Coordinate system | Reported Shape_Area | Units | Correct? |
|-------------------|-------------------|-------|----------|
| WGS 84 (EPSG:4326) | 0.0059 | square degrees | Not meaningful |
| EOV (EPSG:23700) | 42,480,000 | square meters (42.48 km$^2$) | Yes (error < 0.05%) |
| UTM Zone 34N (EPSG:32634) | 42,510,000 | square meters (42.51 km$^2$) | Yes (error < 0.1%) |
| Web Mercator (EPSG:3857) | 92,600,000 | square meters (92.6 km$^2$) | No -- inflated 2.18x |

The message is clear: always compute areas (and distances) in an appropriate projected coordinate system. For Hungarian work, EOV gives the best results. For broader European analyses, ETRS89/LAEA (EPSG:3035) provides accurate areas across the continent.

## 2.12 Putting It All Together: From Data to Map

### 2.12.1 A Practical Workflow for Multi-Source Water Data

The concepts in this chapter come together every time a hydrologist combines data from multiple sources. Consider a typical task: assessing flood exposure in the Tisza valley near Szolnok, Hungary, using data from national, European, and global sources.

**Step 1: Inventory the data and their coordinate systems**

| Dataset | Source | Native CRS | EPSG |
|---------|--------|------------|------|
| Stream network | data.vizugy.hu (OVF) | HD72/EOV | 23700 |
| Gauge locations and water levels | data.vizugy.hu (OVF) | HD72/EOV | 23700 |
| DEM (30 m) | Copernicus GLO-30 | WGS84 geographic | 4326 |
| Flood hazard zones | JRC River Flood Hazard Maps | ETRS89/LAEA | 3035 |
| Land cover | CORINE Land Cover 2018 | ETRS89/LAEA | 3035 |
| Settlement boundaries | KSH (Census 2022) | HD72/EOV | 23700 |
| Satellite imagery (Sentinel-2) | Copernicus Data Space | WGS84 geographic | 4326 |

**Step 2: Choose a working coordinate system**

For an analysis confined to Hungary, EOV (EPSG:23700) is the natural choice: it provides the best accuracy, it matches the majority of national datasets, and it avoids zone boundary issues. If the analysis were pan-European, ETRS89/LAEA (EPSG:3035) would be more appropriate.

**Step 3: Reproject all data to the working system**

The Copernicus DEM and Sentinel-2 imagery (in WGS84) and the JRC flood maps and CORINE land cover (in ETRS89/LAEA) must be projected to EOV. The national datasets (stream network, gauges, settlement boundaries) are already in EOV and need no transformation.

**Step 4: Verify alignment**

After reprojection, overlay the datasets in GIS and visually check that features align: do stream lines from OVF follow the valleys in the Copernicus DEM? Do gauge points fall on the stream lines? Do settlement boundaries match the built-up areas visible in Sentinel-2? Misalignment at this stage indicates a coordinate system error that must be resolved before any analysis proceeds.

This workflow -- inventory, choose, reproject, verify -- is the practical distillation of everything this chapter covers. It applies whether you are working in Hungary, the United States, Bangladesh, or Kenya. The datum names and EPSG codes change; the logic does not.

### 2.12.2 Global Perspective: Coordinate System Challenges in Developing Regions

In many parts of Asia, Africa, and the Middle East, coordinate system challenges are more acute than in the U.S. or Europe. Legacy data may be in colonial-era datums with poorly documented transformation parameters. National geodetic networks may be sparse or degraded. Multiple datums may be in simultaneous use without clear metadata.

The international reader should know that:

Global datasets (SRTM, Copernicus DEM, HydroSHEDS, CHIRPS, ERA5) are typically in WGS84 and can be used directly without datum transformation. This makes global datasets particularly valuable in regions where national spatial infrastructure is limited.

The **African Geodetic Reference Frame** (AFREF) and similar initiatives in Asia are working to establish continent-wide geocentric datums compatible with ITRF/WGS84, which will eventually resolve the patchwork of legacy systems.

For any analysis in a developing region, starting with WGS84 geographic coordinates or UTM and introducing national datums only where necessary (for integration with legacy data) is the safest approach.

## 2.13 Common Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them

### 2.13.1 The Datum Shift Trap

Combining data from different datums without transformation is the most consequential coordinate system error. The shift between HD72 and ETRS89 is about 50-60 meters -- enough to place a gauge location on the wrong side of a river, or to misalign a flood boundary with the buildings it is supposed to protect. The shift between NAD27 and NAD83 in the United States varies from about 10 to 300 meters, depending on location. In both cases, the features look plausible on screen (the shapes are recognizable), but they are systematically displaced.

The defense is simple: always check the datum of every dataset, and always transform before combining. GIS software will usually warn when data in different datums are combined without a transformation, but these warnings can be dismissed, and some software handles the transformation silently (which can mask errors if the transformation parameters are incorrect or ambiguous).

### 2.13.2 The Latitude-Longitude Swap

As mentioned in Section 2.4.1, latitude-longitude is in (Y, X) order, while GIS software typically expects (X, Y) -- meaning longitude first. Swapping these coordinates places a point at a mirror-image location: Budapest at (19.04, 47.50) lands in Hungary, but at (47.50, 19.04) lands in central Kazakhstan. The error is immediately obvious on a map, but it may go undetected in a database or a tabular calculation that does not involve visualization.

CSV files, API responses, and field-collected GPS data are common sources of this error. Always plot a sample of points on a map immediately after import to verify that they appear in the expected location.

### 2.13.3 The EOV X/Y Confusion

Hungarian datasets frequently label EOV Easting as "X" and Northing as "Y," or vice versa, with no consistent convention. This inconsistency originates from a difference between the Hungarian geodetic tradition -- which uses X for Easting and Y for Northing -- and the international mathematical convention, where X is the horizontal (Easting) axis and Y is the vertical (Northing) axis. Both conventions label Easting as "X," but in practice, Hungarian data files often reverse even the national convention, leaving the user with no reliable label-based identification.

The reliable identification method is by value range: values in the 0--400,000 range are Northing; values in the 400,000--1,000,000 range are Easting. For example, a record showing X=699,414 and Y=186,152 has the Easting in the "X" column and the Northing in the "Y" column, which happens to match the international convention -- but the next dataset from a different agency may reverse them. Any workflow that imports Hungarian spatial data should include a value-range check before assigning columns to coordinate axes. A simple validation script that flags values outside the expected Hungarian ranges (as discussed in Section 2.7.4) will catch these errors before they propagate into analysis.

### 2.13.4 The Ellipsoidal Height Confusion

GPS/GNSS receivers report ellipsoidal heights, not elevations above sea level. In Hungary, the difference between the WGS84 ellipsoidal height and the Baltic Height System orthometric height is approximately 40-46 meters. A raw GPS height reading of "150 meters" for a point in the Bukk Mountains does not mean the point is 150 meters above sea level -- it means it is 150 meters above the WGS84 ellipsoid. The true orthometric height would be approximately 150 - 43 = 107 meters (using a typical Hungarian geoid undulation of ~43 m).

This error is most dangerous when GPS heights are used directly in hydraulic modeling or flood mapping without geoid correction. The result would be a systematic 40+ meter offset in all elevations -- catastrophic for any analysis involving water levels or flow direction.

## 2.14 The International Coordinate System Landscape

### 2.14.1 An Equivalence Table for the Working Hydrologist

Throughout this chapter, we have seen that every country and region has its own coordinate system tradition, but the underlying concepts are universal. The following table summarizes the key systems that a working hydrologist is likely to encounter:

**Table 2.7**: Coordinate system equivalence table for hydroinformatics

| Function | United States | Hungary | European Union | Global |
|----------|--------------|---------|---------------|--------|
| Horizontal datum | NAD83 | HD72 | ETRS89 | WGS84 |
| Geographic CRS (EPSG) | 4269 | 4237 | 4258 | 4326 |
| National projection | State Plane (120+ zones) | EOV (1 zone) | ETRS89/LAEA (continental) | UTM (60 zones) |
| Projected CRS (EPSG) | Varies by zone | 23700 | 3035 | 326xx |
| Vertical datum | NAVD88 | Baltic Height System | EVRF2019 (emerging) | EGM2008 geoid |
| GNSS reference network | CORS (1,800+ stations) | GNSSnet.hu | EPN (250+ stations) | IGS (500+ stations) |
| Datum transformation to WGS84 | ~2 m (negligible for most work) | ~50-60 m (must transform) | ~0.9 m (negligible for most work) | identity |

This table reveals a practical asymmetry: data in ETRS89 or NAD83 can be treated as WGS84 for most hydrological work (the differences are at the meter level or below), but data in HD72 (or other legacy national datums) **must** be transformed before combination with global or European datasets. This is the central practical lesson of coordinate systems for Hungarian hydroinformatics.

### 2.14.2 Which Coordinate System Should I Use?

The answer depends on the scale and purpose of the analysis:

**For work confined to Hungary**: Use EOV (EPSG:23700). It provides the best accuracy, matches most national datasets, and avoids zone boundary issues. Use the Baltic Height System for elevations.

**For pan-European analysis** (e.g., Danube basin flood mapping, cross-border water management): Use ETRS89 geographic coordinates (EPSG:4258) for data storage and exchange, and ETRS89/LAEA (EPSG:3035) for mapping and area calculations. For elevation, use EVRF2019 where available, or EGM2008-referenced heights.

**For global analysis** (e.g., GRACE water storage trends, HydroSHEDS watershed delineation): Use WGS84 geographic coordinates (EPSG:4326) for data storage, and UTM or a suitable equal-area projection for calculations. For elevation, use EGM2008 geoid heights.

**For web publishing**: Use Web Mercator (EPSG:3857) for display, but never for analysis. Keep the analytical data in an appropriate projected system and export to Web Mercator only for the final visualization.

## 2.15 Conclusion: The Invisible Infrastructure

The geodetic concepts covered in this chapter -- the geoid, the ellipsoid, datums, coordinate systems, map projections -- are the invisible infrastructure on which all of hydroinformatics rests. They determine whether a watershed area calculation is accurate, whether a flood map aligns with the real world, whether data from different countries and different decades can be meaningfully combined.

The modern hydrologist need not master the mathematics of ellipsoidal geodesy, but must understand the key principles: that every dataset has a coordinate reference system that must be known and respected; that combining data from different systems requires explicit transformation; that the choice of map projection affects whether areas, shapes, or distances are preserved; and that vertical datums are at least as important as horizontal ones for any work involving water levels and flow.

These principles apply universally. Whether you are mapping floods along the Tisza, delineating watersheds in the Colorado Rockies, or monitoring groundwater depletion in the Indo-Gangetic Plain, the geodetic foundation is the same. The datum names change; the ellipsoid parameters vary; the EPSG codes differ. But the logic -- know your data's reference frame, choose an appropriate working system, transform carefully, and verify -- is always the same.

In the chapters that follow, we will rely on these concepts constantly. Chapter 3 explores where the data lives -- the global landscape of hydrological data sources, each with its own coordinate system conventions. Chapter 4 introduces GIS as a water tool, where coordinate system management is a daily operational concern. Part II begins the substantive work of terrain analysis and rainfall estimation, where the choice of projection and the accuracy of elevation data directly determine the quality of the results. The geodetic foundation laid here is the foundation for everything that follows.
